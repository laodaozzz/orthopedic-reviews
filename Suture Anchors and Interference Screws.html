Interactive Guide: Suture Anchors & Interference Screws in Sports Medicine

A comprehensive, evidence-based educational summary covering materials, biomechanics, clinical applications, and decision-making for modern fixation devices.

Introduction

This document provides a comprehensive analysis of two fundamental classes of fixation devices in modern sports medicine: suture anchors for soft tissue-to-bone repair and interference screws for graft fixation in bone tunnels. The evolution of these technologies reflects a broader shift in surgical philosophy from purely mechanical stability towards optimizing the biological environment for tissue healing.

Part I: Suture Anchors in Soft Tissue-to-Bone Repair

1.1 Fundamental Principles and Biomechanics Level V

Suture anchors are engineered to reattach soft tissues (ligaments, tendons, capsules) to bone, particularly when direct soft tissue repair is insufficient. Ref 1,3 They enable anatomic, minimally invasive repairs through a three-component system: anchor body, eyelet, and high-strength suture. Ref 5 Critical performance metrics include pull-out strength, ultimate load to failure, and cyclic displacement. Ref 2,7,9

Clinical Pearl: Material Selection

The choice between materials involves trade-offs. Metallic anchors offer maximal strength but compromise imaging and revision. Bioabsorbable materials solve imaging issues but risk inflammatory reactions. PEEK and All-Suture Anchors represent modern strategies prioritizing bio-inertness and bone preservation, respectively. 1.3 Classification by Design and Fixation Mechanism

Design dictates fixation method: Threaded (screw-in), Barbed/Impaction (press-fit), Knotless (mechanical locking), and novel concepts like Toggling. Ref 5,19,3 Knotless systems are highlighted for improving reproducibility and control of repair tension. Ref 8,16

1.4 Clinical Applications

Suture anchors are versatile across joints: Shoulder (rotator cuff, labral repair), Hip (acetabular labrum), Knee (meniscal root, MPFL), Elbow (distal biceps), Wrist, and Foot/Ankle (Achilles, lateral ligament). Ref 3,27

1.5 Complications and Failure Modes

Complications are categorized as mechanical (anchor pull-out, suture breakage, implant breakage), biological (inflammatory synovitis, osteolysis, cyst formation), and surgical/positional (implant migration, prominent implants). Ref 2,9,11,13 All-suture anchors commonly exhibit benign tunnel widening. Ref 8

Part II: Interference Screws in Graft Fixation

2.1 Fundamental Principles

Interference screws secure grafts (BPTB or soft tissue) within bone tunnels via aperture fixation—direct compression and friction. This provides rigid initial stability critical for early rehabilitation. Ref 33,36,37

2.2 Classification by Material

Material evolution mirrors that of anchors: from Metallic (Titanium) to Bioabsorbable/Biocomposite, PEEK, and emerging materials like Magnesium Alloys and Allograft Bone. Ref 32,40

Key Insight from Comparative Studies

Meta-analyses show no significant difference in functional outcomes (e.g., knee stability, range of motion) between bioabsorbable and metallic interference screws. However, bioabsorbable screws are associated with higher rates of tunnel widening, screw breakage, and prolonged effusion. Ref 37

2.3 Clinical Applications

Primary use is in cruciate ligament reconstruction (ACL, PCL) for both BPTB and soft tissue grafts. Ref 33,49 Also applied in tendon transfers (foot/ankle). Ref 51 Proper screw-tunnel alignment is critical; divergence significantly weakens fixation. Ref 36

2.4 Complications

Key complications: screw breakage (esp. bioabsorbable), graft laceration (esp. metallic), screw migration, tunnel widening, and foreign-body reactions. Ref 14,37,38

3.2 Framework for Implant Selection

Selection integrates Surgical/Anatomical Factors (bone quality, location, repair type), Patient Factors (age, activity, future surgery risk), and Implant Factors (material properties, surgeon experience). Ref 24,53

Direct Comparison Example: MPFL Reconstruction

Biomechanical study showed interference screw fixation is stiffer than suture anchor fixation, but both have comparable ultimate load to failure (exceeding native MPFL strength). Failure mode differs: screws fail by graft pull-out; anchors fail by anchor pull-out. Ref 7,29

Part IV: Future Directions

Innovation focuses on bioactive materials (e.g., citrate-based CitreGen), smart implants with integrated sensors, and advanced designs combining benefits (e.g., knotless all-suture anchors). Ref 30,41,55 The goal is implants that actively participate in healing and provide real-time feedback. Ref 41
