Management of Late Acute Culture-Negative PJI in Total Knee Arthroplasty

Interactive Educational Summary Based on Clinical Document

Introduction

Periprosthetic joint infection (PJI) following total knee arthroplasty (TKA) represents one of the most catastrophic complications in orthopedic surgery. A specific subset presents a clinical conundrum: the "late acute" PJI characterized by normal serological inflammatory markers and a "dry tap" yielding negative cultures. This clinical entity—often termed "occult" or "culture-negative" PJI—strips the clinician of the traditional "gold standard" confirmatory tests.

Key Challenge: The absence of elevated C-reactive protein (CRP) and Erythrocyte Sedimentation Rate (ESR) can falsely reassure the surgeon of an aseptic etiology, while the inability to obtain synovial fluid for analysis removes the most reliable preoperative diagnostic tool.

The prevalence of culture-negative PJI is non-trivial, with reported rates ranging from 7% to as high as 42% in some series. The increasing recognition of low-virulence organisms, such as Cutibacterium acnes and coagulase-negative Staphylococci, as causative agents has necessitated a paradigm shift in diagnostic and therapeutic strategies.

The Diagnostic Dilemma: Normal Markers and the "Dry Tap"

Pathophysiology of "Normal" Inflammatory Markers

The primary driver of seronegative PJI is infection with low-virulence organisms. Pathogens such as Cutibacterium acnes and coagulase-negative Staphylococci have evolved sophisticated mechanisms to evade host detection. Research indicates that median CRP levels in C. acnes PJI can be as low as 5.4 mg/L, and in CoNS PJI as low as 12.2 mg/L—values often below the standard diagnostic cutoff of 10 mg/L.

Clinical Significance of the "Dry Tap"

A "dry tap," defined as the failure to aspirate sufficient synovial fluid, is a frequent occurrence. Data from a comprehensive review of 275 attempted aspirations revealed that 16.0% of patients with a dry tap were subsequently confirmed to have PJI based on MSIS major criteria.

Critical Warning: A dry tap carries a significant risk of missed infection. The absence of fluid does not equate to the absence of pathology.

Diagnostic Utility of Saline Lavage

When a dry tap occurs, performing a saline lavage to obtain a sample for culture is recommended. A landmark study by Plummer et al. found that while saline lavage significantly lowers absolute WBC count, the PMN percentage remained robust (pre-lavage 87% vs. post-lavage 85% in septic patients).

Management Algorithm

Step 1: Diagnosis

High Index of Suspicion: Do not be reassured by a dry tap. Perform sterile saline lavage (10-20 mL).

Analyze PMN%: Prioritize PMN% >80% over absolute WBC count (due to dilution).

Biomarkers: Send for Alpha-Defensin (Lateral Flow or ELISA).

Step 2: Surgical Decision

DAIR is appropriate if: Symptoms <3-4 weeks duration, implant is stable, soft tissue envelope is viable, no sinus tract.

Two-Stage Revision is required if: Implant is loose, symptoms >4 weeks (chronic), failed prior DAIR.

Step 3: Surgical Technique (DAIR)

Modular Exchange: Mandatory if technically feasible. Ensure implant availability preoperatively.

Step 4: Medical Management (Culture-Negative)

Empiric Antibiotics: Vancomycin + Ceftazidime (or Meropenem).

Rifampin: Use with caution. Consider only if high suspicion of Staph and low risk of toxicity.

Duration: 12 Weeks total therapy (IV + Oral) per ICM 2025.
