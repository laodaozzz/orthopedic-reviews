Minimally Invasive Percutaneous Cannulated Screw Fixation for Calcaneal Fractures

An Exhaustive Evidence-Based Review: Surgical Indications, Technique, Anatomy, and Outcomes

1. Introduction and Historical Paradigm Shift

The calcaneus, the bedrock of the hindfoot, bears the immense burden of weight transmission and gait mechanics. Its fracture represents approximately 60% of all tarsal injuries and 2% of all fractures, disrupting the intricate architecture of the subtalar joint and the fundamental tripod of the foot. For over a century, the management of displaced intra-articular calcaneal fractures (DIACF) has remained one of the most polarized debates in orthopaedic trauma.

Historically, the Extensile Lateral Approach (ELA) emerged in the 1990s as the "gold standard" for DIACF management, offering surgeons an unparalleled panoramic view of the lateral wall and the posterior facet. However, this visualization came at a steep physiological price. The lateral soft tissue envelope of the heel, supplied by the angiosome of the lateral calcaneal artery, is notoriously prone to catastrophic failure. The incidence of wound complications following ELA—ranging from marginal necrosis to deep infection and osteomyelitis—has been reported as high as 25% to 30% Evidence Level III, particularly in high-risk cohorts such as diabetics and smokers.

In this context, the last two decades have witnessed a renaissance of minimally invasive surgery (MIS). Driven by advances in intraoperative fluoroscopy and a deeper understanding of fracture pathomechanics, percutaneous cannulated screw fixation has evolved from a niche salvage technique into a primary treatment modality, aiming to achieve the twin goals of restoration of anatomy and preservation of biology.

2. Pathoanatomy and Biomechanics of the Hindfoot

2.1 The Mechanism of Injury

Most DIACFs result from a high-energy axial load, typically a fall from height or a motor vehicle collision. The talus acts as a wedge, driving downward into the calcaneus. The force is concentrated at the crucial angle of Gissane, where the lateral process of the talus splits the calcaneus:

  1. Primary Fracture Line: This shear force creates a vertical fracture through the posterior facet, dividing the calcaneus into the anteromedial (sustentacular) fragment and the posterolateral (tuberosity) fragment.
  2. Secondary Fracture Lines: As the force continues, the tuberosity fragment is further comminuted, with the resulting pattern depending on the direction of the force and the position of the foot at impact.

2.2 The "Constant" Fragment

🔑 Key Concept: The Sustentaculum Tali The sustentaculum tali is the "constant fragment" in calcaneal surgery. Because it is tethered strongly to the talus by the deltoid ligament complex and the interosseous talocalcaneal ligament, it rarely displaces significantly. It serves as the stable foundation—the "docking station"—against which the mobile lateral wall and tuberosity fragments must be reduced. In percutaneous surgery, targeting this fragment blind requires an intimate knowledge of its 3D position.

2.3 Deforming Forces

Muscle forces act on the fracture fragments to perpetuate displacement:

  • Gastrocnemius-Soleus Complex: Pulls the posterior tuberosity proximally and into varus, creating loss of calcaneal height (flattening of Böhler's angle) and varus malalignment.
  • Abductor Hallucis / Quadratus Plantae: Can cause medial shortening.
  • Impact: The lateral wall blows out, causing heel widening, which can impinge on the peroneal tendons and the fibula (fibular abutment).

3. Surgical Indications and Classification Systems

3.1 The Sanders Classification: CT-Based Decision Making

The Sanders classification, based on coronal CT scans through the widest portion of the posterior facet, remains the lingua franca of calcaneal fracture management Evidence Level III.

Table 1: Sanders Classification and Suitability for Percutaneous Fixation
Type Description Suitability for Percutaneous Fixation
Type I Non-displaced fractures (< 2mm displacement). Non-operative. Fixation rarely indicated unless early weight-bearing is essential.
Type II Two articular fragments (one primary fracture line). IIA: Lateral; IIB: Central; IIC: Medial. Ideal Indication. The "sweet spot" for percutaneous fixation. Large fracture fragments amenable to manipulation. Type IIC fractures often leave a large lateral fragment attached to the tuberosity.
Type III Three articular fragments (two fracture lines). IIIAB, IIIAC, IIIBC variants. Controversial but Feasible. Recent evidence supports percutaneous fixation provided adequate reduction of the depressed central fragment, often requiring the "rafting" screw technique.
Type IV Four or more articular fragments (highly comminuted). Generally Contraindicated. Restoring articular congruity percutaneously is virtually impossible. Often treated with primary subtalar arthrodesis.

3.2 The Essex-Lopresti Classification: The Pathomechanical Guide

While Sanders guides prognosis, the Essex-Lopresti classification (based on lateral radiographs) guides the reduction maneuver, distinguishing fractures by the exit point of the secondary fracture line.

3.2.1 Tongue-Type Fractures

✅ Quintessential Indication for Percutaneous Management The secondary fracture line exits the posterior cortex distal to the Achilles insertion. The articular surface remains attached to the tuberosity fragment—manipulating the tuberosity ("the handle") directly reduces the articular surface. This is the mechanical basis for the Essex-Lopresti maneuver.

3.2.2 Joint Depression Fractures

The secondary fracture line exits superior to the articular facet but anterior to the Achilles insertion. The articular fragment (thalamic fragment) is separated from the tuberosity and often impacted deep into the calcaneal body. Manipulating the tuberosity will not reduce the joint surface—a "joystick" technique or localized intrafocal approach is required.

3.3 Host Factors and Soft Tissue Considerations

Percutaneous fixation is often the only safe option for patients with compromised soft tissue envelopes Evidence Level III:

  • Absolute Indications for MIS: Severe soft tissue swelling (blisters), Peripheral Vascular Disease (PVD), Diabetes Mellitus (especially with neuropathy), Chronic smokers, History of DVT necessitating early mobility.
  • The "Wrinkle Sign": While ELA requires waiting 10–21 days for edema resolution, percutaneous surgery can often be performed within 3–7 days, as small incisions do not disrupt lymphatic drainage or venous return to the same degree.

4. Preoperative Planning and Imaging

4.1 Imaging Modalities

  • Plain Radiographs:
    • Lateral View: Assessment of Böhler's angle (normal 20–40°) and the Critical Angle of Gissane (normal 100–130°).
    • Axial (Harris) View: Crucial for assessing calcaneal width, varus/valgus angulation, and sustentaculum tali integrity.
  • Computed Tomography (CT): Mandatory for Sanders classification. Coronal, sagittal, and axial views with 2D and 3D reconstructions are essential. 3D reconstructions are particularly helpful for understanding spatial relationships in tongue-type fractures.

4.2 Operating Room Setup

The patient is placed in a lateral decubitus position on a radiolucent table, allowing direct access to the lateral wall with free C-arm movement. Key fluoroscopic views must be verified before prep:

  • Lateral View: C-arm perpendicular to the foot.
  • Axial View: C-arm tilted ~45° plantarflexion to align with the long axis of the calcaneus.
  • Broden's View: C-arm rotated 10°, 20°, 30°, and 40° cephalad to visualize the posterior facet.

5. Step-by-Step Surgical Technique: The Reduction

5.1 The Essex-Lopresti Reduction Maneuver

For tongue-type fractures, this maneuver is legendary for its elegance and efficacy.

5.1.1 Conventional Method

  1. Pin Insertion: A heavy threaded Steinmann pin (4–5mm) or Schanz screw is inserted percutaneously into the tongue fragment, slightly lateral to the midline of the Achilles tendon insertion.
  2. Knee Flexion: The knee is flexed to 90 degrees to relax the gastrocnemius—failure to do so makes reduction difficult or impossible.
  3. The Maneuver:
    • Downward (plantar) force on the pin handle lifts the depressed articular surface dorsally.
    • Simultaneous valgus force corrects varus deformity of the tuberosity.
    • The pin is advanced across the fracture line into the stable anterior fragment to hold reduction temporarily.

5.1.2 Modified Essex-Lopresti Technique

⚠️ Osteoporotic Bone Modification In elderly or osteoporotic patients, the cancellous bone may be too weak, causing the pin to "cut out." The modification: aim the pin to engage the plantar cortical surface of the tongue fragment. By engaging the dense plantar cortex, the pin gains solid purchase, allowing powerful reduction even in osteopenic bone. This modification significantly improves restoration of Böhler's angle in Type IIC and osteoporotic fractures.

5.2 Reduction of Joint Depression Fractures

  1. Tuberosity Reduction First: A corkscrew or Schanz pin is placed in the tuberosity to pull it out of varus and restore length/height.
  2. Thalamic Fragment Elevation:
    • Lateral Window: Small stab incision over the sinus tarsi.
    • Instrument: Curved elevator (McDonald, Freer, or Cobb) inserted under the depressed fragment and pried upward.
    • Joystick: Alternatively, a K-wire drilled into the fragment acts as a joystick to rotate it back into congruity.

5.3 Reduction Verification

Before screw insertion, reduction must be confirmed in three planes: Lateral (Böhler's and Gissane's angles), Axial (varus correction and width), and Broden's (posterior facet congruity).

6. Fixation Strategies: "Rafting" and "Tripod" Configurations

6.1 The "Rafting" Screw Technique

Borrowed from tibial plateau fracture management, "rafting" is designed to support a comminuted or depressed articular surface that has lost its cancellous support Evidence Level V — Expert Opinion / Biomechanical Study.

  • Indication: Sanders Type III fractures or osteoporotic bone where a single screw may not prevent articular subsidence.
  • Technique: Multiple small-diameter screws (3.5–4.0mm) inserted from the lateral wall, immediately inferior to the subchondral bone, parallel to the joint surface, directed toward the sustentaculum tali.
  • Biomechanics: Distributes axial load across multiple fixation points, significantly increasing resistance to vertical depression. Some studies use 3D-printed guides for precision Evidence Level V. Washers prevent screw head penetration through the lateral cortex.

6.2 The "Tripod" (Spatial Weaving) Configuration

The "Tripod" configuration creates a 3D triangular frame within the calcaneus, offering superior stability compared to linear screw placement. Recent biomechanical studies refer to this as the Spatial Weaving Intra-calcaneal Fixator System (SWIFS) Evidence Level V — Finite Element Analysis.

Components of the Tripod

Table 2: The Three Pillars of the Tripod Configuration
Component Screw Type Path Function
The Sustentacular Screw (The Anchor) 6.5–7.3mm cannulated partial thread Lateral wall → Sustentaculum Tali Reconnects lateral to constant medial fragment; corrects widening
The Longitudinal Column Screw (The Beam) 6.5–7.3mm cannulated Posterior tuberosity → Anterior Process / Cuboid facet Maintains calcaneal length; counteracts bending moment
The Thalamic/Vertical Screw (The Strut) 4.0–6.5mm Posterior tuberosity → Superiorly toward Posterior Facet Maintains calcaneal height (Böhler's angle); supports articular reduction
🔬 Biomechanical Superiority Finite Element Analysis demonstrates that stress in the Tripod configuration is concentrated at the intersection of the screws deep within the bone, rather than at fracture lines or screw heads. This "internal locking" mechanism provides stiffness comparable to, and in some vectors superior to, lateral locking plates, with significantly less soft tissue disruption.

7. Anatomical Landmarks and Safe Zones

7.1 Lateral Safe Zones: The Sural Nerve Map

The sural nerve courses along the lateral hindfoot and is the structure most at risk during lateral pin insertion. Cadaveric studies have mapped "Safe Zones" to minimize injury Evidence Level IV.

Table 3: Lateral Safe Zone Descriptions for Sural Nerve Protection
Safe Zone Definition Risk Level
Mekhail et al. Posteroinferior region at 3/4 distance from lateral malleolus tip to heel Low
Santi & Botte Rectangular area in posterior aspect of calcaneal tuberosity Low
Licht Zone IA/IB (Anterior Process) Nerve often crosses here; requires "mini-open" visualization High
Posterior Tuberosity Safest area for pin insertion for Essex-Lopresti maneuver Low
🔴 Surgical Pearl For any screw inserted anterior to the posterior tuberosity, use a hemostat to bluntly dissect down to bone before inserting the drill guide. This "spread and drill" technique pushes the nerve aside rather than winding it around the drill bit. The sural nerve crosses the lateral border of the Achilles tendon approximately 9.8–10 cm proximal to the calcaneal tuberosity insertion.

7.2 Medial Safe Zones: Targeting the Sustentaculum Tali

Just medial to the sustentaculum lies the tarsal tunnel, containing the posterior tibial neurovascular bundle and the FHL tendon.

  • Trajectory: Lateral to Medial, approximately 15° anterior and slightly cephalad (parallel to the joint).
  • The "Backstop": The medial cortex of the sustentaculum is the limit. A too-long screw can tether the FHL tendon or impinge on the nerve.
  • Verification: The Axial Harris View is the only reliable way to judge screw length and confirm it has not penetrated the medial cortex significantly.

8. Clinical Outcomes: Percutaneous Fixation vs. Extensile Lateral Approach

8.1 The 2025 RCT: Fadle et al.

A landmark RCT published in 2025 compared the Sinus Tarsi Approach (representative MIS technique) versus ELA in 157 patients with Sanders II and III fractures Evidence Level I — RCT.

Table 4: Key Findings of the 2025 RCT (Fadle et al.) — MIS vs. ELA
Outcome Measure MIS (Sinus Tarsi) ELA P-Value Clinical Implication
Wound Complications 3.3% 18.9% 0.001 MIS drastically reduces wound failure
Deep Infection 1.1% 7.4% <0.05 MIS reduces osteomyelitis and reoperation risk
Subtalar Osteoarthritis 9.9% 32.6% 0.001 ELA's soft tissue stripping devascularizes fragments → cartilage death
AOFAS Score (1 yr) 83.49 68.62 0.000 Significantly better functional outcomes with MIS
Fracture Union (wks) 6.33 7.13 0.000 Biology preservation accelerates healing
Operative Time (min) 55.83 89.66 <0.05 MIS is faster and more efficient

Fadle et al. 2025 RCT: 157 patients, Sanders II–III fractures. STA = Sinus Tarsi Approach. ELA = Extensile Lateral Approach.

8.2 Meta-Analysis Data (2018–2024)

Broader systematic reviews corroborate the RCT findings Evidence Level I — Meta-Analysis:

  • Sanders Type II: MIS offers superior functional outcomes compared to ELA—simple enough anatomy for perfect percutaneous reduction with maximized soft tissue benefits.
  • Sanders Type III: Functional outcomes were equivalent between MIS and ELA, but wound complication benefit persisted for MIS, suggesting that biological advantage offsets the slightly more difficult reduction.
  • Pain Scores: Consistently lower VAS pain scores in the early postoperative period with MIS.
  • Hospital Stay: MIS associated with discharge within 24–48 hours vs. prolonged admission for ELA.

8.3 Screw-Only vs. Plate Fixation in MIS

A comparative study found the screw-only technique had a significantly lower implant removal rate (26% vs. high for plates), shorter operative time, and comparable radiological and functional outcomes Evidence Level III. This validates the "Tripod" and "Rafting" screw concepts as biomechanically sufficient without lateral plating in many Sanders II and selected Type III fractures.

Table 5: Postoperative Protocol for Percutaneous Calcaneal Fixation
Timeline Management
Immediate Post-Op Bulky Jones dressing and posterior splint. Foot elevation.
Week 2 Suture removal. Transition to removable CAM walker boot. Early ROM exercises for ankle and subtalar joint (key advantage of MIS).
Weeks 0–8 Non-weight bearing (NWB) — non-negotiable. Cancellous bone needs time to heal despite screw fixation.
Weeks 8–12 Progressive weight bearing if radiographs show callus formation.
Months 3–6 Full weight bearing and strengthening.
Return to Work Sedentary: 2–3 weeks; Heavy labor: 4–6 months.

10. Conclusion

The management of calcaneal fractures has undergone a decisive evolution. The era of the "one-size-fits-all" Extensile Lateral Approach is ending, replaced by a nuanced, biologically friendly approach rooted in percutaneous precision. The integration of the Essex-Lopresti reduction maneuver with modern "Tripod" and "Rafting" screw configurations allows surgeons to construct stable, weight-bearing architectures through keyhole incisions.

The anatomical risks are real—specifically to the sural nerve and medial neurovascular bundle—but manageable through strict adherence to the Safe Zone maps described by Mekhail, Santi, and others.

📊 Definitive Evidence Summary The 2025 RCT data demonstrates that for Sanders Type II and selected Type III fractures, percutaneous fixation is not just a "safer" alternative regarding wounds—it is a functionally superior one. By preserving vascularity of fractured fragments, MIS reduces post-traumatic arthritis rates and accelerates union. For the modern orthopaedic surgeon, percutaneous cannulated screw technique represents the convergence of biomechanical stability and biological preservation, establishing itself as the new standard of care for the majority of DIACF.
