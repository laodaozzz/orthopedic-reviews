1. Introduction: Reframing Gout as a Systemic Disease in Orthopedic Practice

Gout is a chronic, systemic metabolic disorder [27], not an intermittent disease of acute flares. It is defined by the deposition of monosodium urate (MSU) crystals in joints and soft tissues, a process driven by sustained hyperuricemia — typically defined as a serum urate (SU) level exceeding 6.8 mg/dL.[6] This crystal deposition underlies all gouty manifestations, from acute inflammatory attacks to chronic tophi and gouty arthropathy.[6]

The prevalence of gout is increasing globally, mirroring epidemics of obesity, hypertension, and chronic kidney disease (CKD) — all common comorbidities within the orthopedic surgical population.[6] Orthopedic surgeons now encounter gout not only as an acute monoarthritis in the emergency setting[18] but also as chronic gouty arthropathy requiring joint replacement, atypical spinal complaints[31], and unusual soft-tissue masses.[23]

The Central Clinical Problem There exists a critical disconnect between gout's identity as a chronic metabolic disease and its typical presentation as an acute, localized inflammatory event.[32] The acute flare is managed (e.g., with steroids or NSAIDs), but the underlying metabolic driver — hyperuricemia — is left unaddressed, leading to further crystal deposition, joint damage, and perioperative risk.[34]

Gout's ability to mimic other, more emergent conditions is a major theme in orthopedic practice. The acute flare is clinically indistinguishable from septic arthritis, a true surgical emergency.[17] In chronic tophaceous form, gout mimics spinal infection (spondylodiscitis)[20], malignant soft-tissue sarcoma[22], and other inflammatory arthritides.[35]

2. Pathophysiology

Gout results from the nucleation and deposition of monosodium urate (MSU) crystals in and around joints when extracellular urate concentrations exceed the saturation threshold of 6.8 mg/dL at physiological temperature and pH.[6] The process involves:

  1. Hyperuricemia: Sustained elevation of serum urate from overproduction (e.g., high purine intake, enzymatic defects) or, more commonly, underexcretion (e.g., CKD, medications) of uric acid.[6]
  2. Crystal Nucleation & Deposition: MSU crystals form in the synovial fluid, cartilage, tendons, and soft tissues. These deposits are the pathological hallmark of the disease.[6]
  3. Innate Immune Activation: Phagocytosis of MSU crystals by monocytes activates the NLRP3 inflammasome, triggering a massive release of Interleukin-1β (IL-1β) and initiating the acute inflammatory cascade.[13]
  4. Chronic Inflammation & Tissue Destruction: Persistent crystal deposition drives chronic low-grade inflammation, granulomatous tophus formation, cartilage erosion, and bony destruction.[6]
Clinical Relevance The acute gout flare is merely the most visible manifestation of a chronic, systemic depositional disease. Effective management requires addressing the underlying crystal burden, not just the inflammatory symptom.[34]

3. Current Diagnostic Guidelines & Advanced Imaging

3.1 The Gold Standard: Synovial Fluid Analysis

The definitive diagnosis of gout is established by identifying monosodium urate (MSU) crystals in synovial fluid or an aspirate from a tophus.[3] Under compensated polarized light microscopy, these crystals appear as needle-shaped and negatively birefringent.[18]

However, arthrocentesis has significant practical limitations: it is invasive, not always performed even when indicated[3], and its accuracy is highly operator-dependent.[4] False negatives can occur due to sampling error, crystal dissolution, or examiner inexperience — leading some to describe it as a "suboptimal gold standard" in real-world practice.[1]

3.2 The 2015 ACR/EULAR Gout Classification Criteria

The 2015 ACR/EULAR classification criteria[1] incorporate modern imaging and address limitations of older criteria. Key features include:

  • Entry Criterion: Applied only to patients with at least one episode of swelling, pain, or tenderness in a peripheral joint or bursa.[1]
  • Sufficient Criterion: Identification of MSU crystals in a symptomatic joint, bursa, or tophus is sufficient for classification as gout (no further scoring needed).[1]
  • Scoring System: If the sufficient criterion is not met, a scoring system combining clinical, laboratory, and imaging domains is used. A cumulative score of ≥ 8 (out of 23) classifies the patient as having gout.[2]
  • Performance: Sensitivity 92%, Specificity 89%.[1]

3.3 Advanced Imaging in Diagnosis

The integration of advanced imaging into the 2015 criteria represents a paradigm shift: from an event-based model (capturing an acute flare) to a deposition-based model (quantifying chronic crystal burden). This allows definitive diagnosis in the asymptomatic intercritical period.

Ultrasound (US)

  • "Double Contour" Sign (DCs): The pathognomonic finding — a hyperechoic band of MSU crystals depositing on the surface of articular cartilage, parallel to the bone cortex.[1] This is distinct from the pseudo-double contour of CPPD, which appears within the cartilage.[38]
  • Other Findings: Tophi (hyperechoic aggregates/"snowstorm"), crystal aggregates, bone erosions.[6]
  • Sensitivity / Specificity: 92% / 83% for gout diagnosis.[48] Doppler US can detect inflammatory signals more sensitively than clinical assessment.[6]

Dual-Energy Computed Tomography (DECT)

  • Mechanism: Uses two distinct X-ray energy levels to perform material-specific decomposition, definitively identifying and color-coding MSU deposits.[6]
  • Key Advantages: Detects subclinical urate deposition in asymptomatic hyperuricemic patients; quantifies total body urate burden as an objective metric to monitor ULT response.[5]
  • Clinical Utility: High sensitivity and specificity.[5] Emerging as the only non-invasive modality for identifying spinal urate deposits pre-operatively.[20]

4. Advancements in Pharmacotherapy: A Treat-to-Target Approach

4.1 2020 ACR Guideline-Directed Medical Therapy

The 2020 ACR Guideline for the Management of Gout[51] reflects a more aggressive, evidence-based approach.

Acute Flare Management

First-line agents include low-dose colchicine, NSAIDs, or glucocorticoids (oral, intra-articular, or intramuscular).[18] LOE: I

Urate-Lowering Therapy (ULT) Indications

  • Strongly Recommended: Patients with ≥ 1 subcutaneous tophus, radiographic damage from gout, or frequent flares (≥ 2/year).[7] LOE: I
  • Conditionally Recommended: Previous flare with infrequent flares (<2/year).[53] LOE: II
  • Conditionally Recommended (First Flare): First flare with high-risk features: CKD stage ≥ 3, SU > 9 mg/dL, or history of urolithiasis.[53] LOE: II

The Treat-to-Target (T2T) Strategy

Key Recommendation: Serum Urate Target < 6 mg/dL The 2020 guidelines strongly endorse the T2T strategy: regular monitoring and titration of ULT to achieve and maintain a serum urate level < 6 mg/dL indefinitely.[7] This promotes dissolution of existing MSU crystals and prevents new crystal formation. LOE: I

Flare Prophylaxis During ULT Initiation

Concomitant anti-inflammatory prophylaxis (low-dose colchicine or NSAIDs) is strongly recommended for ≥ 3–6 months when initiating ULT, to prevent paradoxical "mobilization flares" from crystal dissolution.[51] LOE: I

4.3 Biologics for Acute Refractory Flares: IL-1 Inhibitors

The acute gout flare is driven by NLRP3 inflammasome activation and IL-1β release.[13] IL-1 inhibitors are reserved for difficult-to-treat patients with severe flares who have contraindications to or fail first-line agents.[12]

  • Anakinra (IL-1 receptor antagonist): A scoping review of 551 patients found 94% response rate for acute flares, with safety demonstrated even in complex patients (active infections, on dialysis).[12] LOE: III
  • Canakinumab (anti–IL-1β monoclonal antibody): FDA-approved for gout flares; single 150 mg SC injection. Retreatment interval ≥ 12 weeks. Concerns include infection risk and cost.[62] LOE: II

4.4 Pegloticase for Refractory Chronic Tophaceous Gout

Strongly recommended by 2020 ACR guidelines for patients with severe, uncontrolled chronic gout who fail maximum-dose oral ULTs with ongoing frequent flares or non-resolving tophi.[7]

  • Administration: IV infusion with mandatory premedication (corticosteroids, antihistamines).[14]
  • Monitoring: SU measured before each infusion. A rise to > 6 mg/dL indicates neutralizing antibody formation — treatment must be discontinued immediately to prevent severe allergic reaction.[14]
  • Contraindication: G6PD deficiency (risk of life-threatening hemolysis).[14]
  • SEL-212: Investigational combination of pegloticase + immunosuppressive co-treatment (ImmTOR) to mitigate anti-drug antibody response.[57]

5. Differential Diagnosis in the Orthopedic Setting: High-Stakes Mimics

5.1 Gout vs. Septic Arthritis — The Critical Differential

⚠ High-Stakes Diagnostic Pitfall Septic arthritis is an orthopedic emergency requiring immediate antibiotics and often surgical débridement.[17] An acute gout flare can perfectly mimic this presentation. Most critically, gout and septic arthritis can coexist in the same joint.[18] The identification of MSU crystals does not exclude concomitant infection.
  • The Mandate: Any acute, hot, swollen joint — even in a patient with known gout — must be treated as septic until proven otherwise. Arthrocentesis is mandatory.[17]
  • Synovial Fluid Workup: Gram stain, cell count with differential, aerobic & anaerobic cultures, AND polarized light microscopy.[6]
  • Diagnostic Clues:
    • WBC > 50,000/μL is highly suspicious for infection, though severe gout can also reach this level.[18]
    • Clinical rule: A 7-variable score ≥ 8 has 95.3% PPV for gout; ≤ 4 has 94.8% NPV, supporting septic arthritis.[67]
    • Serum urate is unreliable in acute flares — may be paradoxically normal or low.[6]

5.3 Atypical Presentations: "The Great Mimicker"

Spinal Gout Mimicking Spondylodiscitis

MSU crystal deposition in facet joints, the epidural space, or ligamentum flavum can present with fever, leukocytosis, elevated CRP, and acute neurological deficits — indistinguishable from pyogenic infection.[20] In multiple case reports, patients underwent emergency spinal surgery for presumed infection; the diagnosis of gout was only made on postoperative histology.[20] DECT is the only non-invasive tool capable of pre-operative identification of spinal urate deposits.[20]

Soft Tissue Tophi Mimicking Sarcoma

Large tophi in atypical locations (thigh, forearm, intra-articular) can be clinically and radiologically indistinguishable from soft tissue sarcoma.[22] Patients are frequently referred to orthopedic oncology.[23] Ultrasound-guided biopsy yielding chalky white material with confirmed MSU crystals prevents unnecessary wide-resection oncologic surgery.[70]

Chronic Gout Mimicking RA / Psoriatic Arthritis

Chronic tophaceous gout can become polyarticular and symmetric, mimicking rheumatoid arthritis.[35] Digital tophi can mimic psoriatic arthritis dactylitis.[36] Patients with psoriasis may have both elevated uric acid and concurrent gout and PsA.[74]

Post-Traumatic / Postoperative Gout

Trauma and surgery are well-established triggers for acute gout flares.[63] In the post-traumatic setting, gout must be differentiated from traumatic effusion, hemarthrosis, and post-surgical septic joint.[65]

6. Gout Management in Orthopedic Surgery

6.1 Perioperative Management: A Critical Intervention Point

Surgery as a Gout Trigger The stress of surgery is a potent trigger for acute gout flares.[24] These flares are frequently misdiagnosed as periprosthetic joint infection (PJI), leading to aggressive workups, unnecessary antibiotics, delayed rehabilitation, and increased costs.[24]
  • Risk Factors for Postoperative Flare: Hyperuricemia at admission[24] and presence of tophi[76] are independent predictors of postoperative gout recurrence within one year.
  • Guideline-Based Approach: The 2022 ACR/AAHKS perioperative guideline recommends continuing maintenance medications (allopurinol, colchicine) through the surgical period without interruption.[77]
  • Managing Postoperative Flares: Standard first-line agents (colchicine, NSAIDs, systemic corticosteroids). However, intra-articular corticosteroid injection must be avoided if PJI is suspected.[18]
  • Initiating ULT During Acute Flares: Contrary to older dogma, moderate-quality evidence now shows that initiating ULT during an acute flare is safe and does not worsen pain or increase discontinuation.[34] LOE: II
Paradigm Shift: From Reactive to Proactive Management Gout should be regarded as a significant, modifiable risk factor — similar to diabetes (HbA1c) or smoking — requiring preoperative rheumatology co-management to achieve serum urate targets before elective surgery.[18]

6.2 Surgical Indications for Gout

Medical management with ULT is the cornerstone of treatment.[18] Surgery is reserved for managing complications of uncontrolled, chronic tophaceous disease.[79]

Procedure Selection

  • Tophysectomy (Debulking): Improves function and reduces pain, but aggressive soft-tissue shaving risks poor wound healing.[85]
  • 1st MTP Arthrodesis: Considered superior to simple tophi excision when MRI confirms intra-articular tophi and > 50% cartilage loss, as excision alone leads to progressive arthritis.[88] LOE: III

7. Gout and Total Joint Arthroplasty (TJA)

TKA and THA are valid treatments for end-stage gouty arthropathy.[18] However, a critical contradiction exists in the literature:

  • Older/Narrative Reviews: Suggested gout does not independently affect TJA outcomes, attributing complications to associated comorbidities (obesity, HTN, CKD).[18]
  • Recent Systematic Reviews/Meta-Analyses: Large-scale evidence now clearly identifies gout as an independent risk factor for poor outcomes after TJA.[25] LOE: III

Implications for Practice This Level III evidence that gout is an independent risk factor for catastrophic complications like PJI and revision has profound implications. The chronic, subclinical inflammatory state, metabolic disturbances, or physical presence of urate crystals creates an unfavorable environment for wound healing and implant integration. This elevates gout from a simple comorbidity to a major, modifiable preoperative risk factor.[25]

8. Conclusion & Future Directions

Key Findings

This systematic review confirms that gout is a complex, systemic metabolic disease[27] presenting unique and high-stakes challenges to the orthopedic surgeon. The diagnostic paradigm has shifted to objective confirmation driven by the 2015 ACR/EULAR criteria[1] and advanced imaging (US/DECT).[5] Therapeutic options have expanded, guided by a treat-to-target ULT strategy[7] and supplemented by novel agents and biologics for refractory disease.

Three Key Implications for Orthopedic Practice

  1. Septic arthritis is the critical, must-not-miss differential. MSU crystals do not exclude coexistent infection. Mandatory arthrocentesis with culture is the standard of care for any acute, inflamed monoarthritis.[18]
  2. Gout is the "great mimicker." It must be included in the differential for atypical spinal infections[20], soft tissue tumors[23], and other inflammatory arthritides. DECT and ultrasound-guided biopsy are essential to prevent misdiagnosis.
  3. Gout is an independent risk factor for TJA failure. Recent evidence confirms that gout independently increases the risk of PJI, revision surgery, and dislocation after TKA and THA.[25]

Future Directions

The most pressing need is the development and validation of preoperative optimization protocols for gout patients undergoing elective arthroplasty.[89] Future research must investigate whether achieving and maintaining a preoperative serum urate target of < 6 mg/dL can mitigate the documented high risk of postoperative complications. This will require a new standard of multidisciplinary collaboration between orthopedic surgery and rheumatology to manage this systemic disease before the patient reaches the operating room.[18]
