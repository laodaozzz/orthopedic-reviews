1. The AO Principles of Fracture Management and Biomechanics

For over half a century, the Arbeitsgemeinschaft für Osteosynthesefragen (AO Foundation) has established the foundational principles of fracture fixation, evolving from an early emphasis on rigid mechanical anatomical fixation to a sophisticated contemporary understanding of biological osteosynthesis. LOE: Strong

Anatomical Reduction

Precise restoration of anatomical relationships, paramount for intra-articular fractures to prevent post-traumatic osteoarthritis. For diaphyseal fractures, restoring correct length, axial alignment, and rotation suffices without perfect interdigitation of every comminuted fragment. I–II

Stable Fixation

Provision of absolute or relative stability carefully tailored to the specific fracture pattern and biological demands of the bone. The fixation construct dictates the mechanical environment and, consequently, the pathway of biological bone healing. I–II

Preservation of Blood Supply

Modern techniques must respect the soft-tissue envelope and the delicate periosteal and endosteal vascular networks. The shift towards minimally invasive percutaneous osteosynthesis (MIPO) and biological fixation preserves local vascularity. I–II

Early, Active Mobilization

Restoration of function through early, safe, active mobilization prevents systemic and local effects of prolonged immobilization: joint contractures, muscle atrophy, complex regional pain syndrome, and deep vein thrombosis. I–II

Key Paradigm Shift

The contemporary evidence-based approach dictates that the choice between absolute and relative stability must be governed entirely by the anatomical location of the fracture (articular vs. diaphyseal) and the specific morphology of the fracture pattern (simple vs. comminuted). LOE: II

2. Perren's Strain Theory and the Pathways of Bone Healing

Perren's strain theory describes how the mechanical environment dictates the biological mechanism of bone repair. Strain is defined as the relative change in the length of the fracture gap divided by the original gap length when subjected to a given physiological load. LOE: Strong

Table 1 — Tissue Tolerance to Strain and Implications for Fracture Healing
Tissue Type Strain Tolerance Clinical Implication
Granulation tissue Up to 100% Initial hematoma can bridge even highly mobile gaps
Fibrous tissue / Cartilage 10–15% Soft callus formation in relative stability environment
Mature lamellar bone ≤ 2% Only survives in absolute stability; required for primary healing

Absolute Stability

Implies virtually no micro-motion at the fracture site under physiological loading, reducing interfragmentary strain below the critical 2% threshold. Required for all intra-articular fractures and preferred for simple, two-part diaphyseal or metaphyseal fractures. Achieved through lag screws with neutralization plates, dynamic compression plating, or tension band wiring. Bone heals via primary (intramembranous) bone healing — direct remodeling by cutting cones without visible intermediate callus. I–II

Relative Stability

Permits controlled, elastic micro-motion, generating moderate strain (typically 2–10%) that stimulates robust bridging callus formation. Ideal for multi-fragmentary or highly comminuted fractures where anatomical reduction of every fragment would devitalize the bone. Achieved via intramedullary nails, bridge plating constructs, and external fixators. Bone heals via secondary (endochondral) bone healing: hematoma → inflammation → soft callus → hard callus (endochondral ossification) → remodeling (Wolff's Law). I–II

Clinical Pearl

The choice between absolute and relative stability is not surgeon preference — it is dictated by the fracture's anatomical location and morphology. Using rigid compression for a comminuted diaphyseal fracture risks devascularization and nonunion; conversely, relying on a bridge plate for an intra-articular fracture risks malunion and arthritis.

3. Systemic Considerations: Polytrauma and the "Second Hit"

Management of major extremity fractures must be integrated with the systemic physiological status of the trauma patient. The timing, duration, and extent of orthopaedic intervention must be calibrated against hemodynamic stability, coagulation profile, and immunological reactivity. LOE: II–III

Early Total Care (ETC) vs. Damage Control Orthopaedics (DCO)

Historically, ETC mandated definitive fixation of all long bone fractures within 24 hours of injury, based on retrospective data suggesting reduced fat embolism syndrome, ARDS, and mortality. However, immunological research revealed that in borderline or hemodynamically unstable patients, prolonged major surgery acts as a massive "second hit", pushing marrow fat and inflammatory mediators (e.g., IL-6) into the pulmonary circulation, potentially causing lethal ARDS and multiple organ failure. II–III

Damage Control Orthopaedics (DCO) was established to mitigate this risk: rapid provisional stabilization using modular external fixators to control hemorrhage and provide skeletal stability with minimum physiological insult. Definitive management is staged and delayed until the acute inflammatory window (typically peaking 2–5 days post-injury) has safely passed. II–III

Table 2 — Physiological Markers and Clinical Conditions Indicating DCO Over ETC
Parameter Threshold / Criterion for DCO
Injury Severity Score (ISS) ISS > 40 (without thoracic trauma) or ISS > 20 (with concurrent thoracic trauma) III
Neurological Status (GCS) GCS ≤ 8 (severe TBI; intraoperative hypotension risk for fatal secondary ischemia) III
Hemodynamic Status Hemorrhagic shock, severe unstable pelvic ring disruption, or abdominal trauma requiring massive transfusion III
Pulmonary Compromise Bilateral pulmonary contusions on initial radiographs/CT III
Systemic Biomarkers Hypothermia (<35°C), IL-6 > 500 pg/dL, or profound metabolic acidosis (lactic acidosis/base deficit) III
Fracture Burden Bilateral femoral shaft fractures (immense marrow/blood loss) III
Early Appropriate Care (EAC) / Safe Definitive Surgery (SDS)

Emerging protocols advocate continuous, dynamic monitoring of lactate clearance and base excess correction. If resuscitation markers normalize rapidly, the patient may safely tolerate definitive fixation within the early phase, capturing ETC benefits without the risks of the "second hit." II–III

4. Indications for Amputation vs. Limb Salvage

In catastrophic high-energy trauma involving massive crush injuries, degloving, or profound neurovascular compromise ("mangled extremity"), the decision between extensive limb salvage and early amputation is one of the most complex in orthopaedic surgery. II–III

Landmark Evidence: The LEAP Trial

The Lower Extremity Assessment Project (LEAP) trial demonstrated that: (1) the most reliable predictor of eventual amputation is soft-tissue envelope severity and viability, not skeletal complexity; and (2) total loss of plantar sensation upon presentation is not an absolute indication for amputation — peripheral nerve function may spontaneously recover or patients may functionally adapt. II

Table 3 — Primary Drivers Toward Amputation (OTA/AAOS Guidelines)
Indication Type Specific Criteria
Absolute (highly context-dependent) Total or "near total" anatomical amputation (limb connected by non-viable tissue bridge); absolute inability to re-vascularize a completely ischemic extremity; overwhelming sepsis/gas gangrene from the injured limb II–III
Relative Warm ischemia time > 6 hours; complete transection of sciatic/tibial nerve in an adult; severe ipsilateral crush to foot; massive un-reconstructible plantar soft-tissue loss II–III

The METALS study (military trauma) demonstrated that young, highly motivated patients who undergo early amputation and aggressive prosthetic rehabilitation may achieve better long-term functional outcomes, higher rates of return to duty, and higher sports participation than those undergoing protracted multi-surgical limb salvage. II

5. Upper Extremity Fractures

The upper extremity prioritizes mobility, spatial positioning, and fine motor function over load-bearing. Clinical guidelines for acceptable non-operative alignment are generally more forgiving than the lower extremity, with notable exceptions for forearm rotation and articular congruity. II–III

5.1 Clavicle Fractures

Midshaft clavicle fractures are commonly treated non-operatively. Sling immobilization is equivalent to figure-of-8 bracing in outcomes but with significantly better patient compliance, less pain, and fewer nerve compressions. II

Non-Operative Indications

  • Less than 2 cm gross shortening
  • Less than 100% cortical displacement

Operative Indications (ORIF with plate or IM device)

  • Absolute: Open fracture; skin tenting with impending full-thickness necrosis; subclavian vessel injury; floating shoulder variant II
  • Relative: >2 cm shortening; bilateral displaced clavicle fractures; polytrauma requiring early upper extremity weight-bearing II–III
Key Data Point

For highly displaced fractures, operative fixation achieves: union rate >94%, time to union 16.4 weeks (vs. 28.4 weeks non-operatively), and superior long-term shoulder endurance and strength. II

5.2 Scapula Fractures

The vast majority involve the scapular body, which is inherently stable due to its robust muscular envelope. Non-operative management with sling + early pendulum exercises is the undisputed standard. II–III

Operative indications (surgical fixation): Intra-articular glenoid step-off >2–4 mm; medialization of the lateral border >20 mm; wide lateral column displacement; severe double disruptions of the superior shoulder suspensory complex. III

5.3 Humeral Shaft Fractures

The humerus tolerates significant malalignment due to compensatory glenohumeral and radiocapitellar ROM. The vast majority are managed non-operatively with functional fracture bracing (Sarmiento brace). II

Table 4 — Acceptable Non-Operative Alignment for Humeral Shaft Fractures
Parameter Acceptable Threshold
Anterior angulation≤ 20°
Varus/valgus angulation≤ 30°
Rotational malalignment≤ 30°
Shortening≤ 3 cm

Non-operative management achieves an average union rate of 93.5%. Primary radial nerve palsy (e.g., Holstein-Lewis fracture) is not a contraindication to bracing — the vast majority represent neuropraxias that resolve spontaneously. II

Operative indications: Severe soft-tissue destruction/segmental bone loss, vascular injury, brachial plexus injury, compartment syndrome (absolute); polytrauma/DCO, floating elbow, bilateral humerus fractures, bracing failure (relative). II–III

5.4 Forearm Diaphysis (Both-Bone Fractures)

The adult forearm is a complex rotational joint — the radius rotates around the ulna for pronation/supination, relying on the precise radial bow and the interosseous membrane (IOM). I–II

Critical Rule

Non-operative management of displaced adult both-bone forearm fractures is universally contraindicated. Cast immobilization invariably leads to loss of the radial bow, IOM tension failure, and catastrophic permanent loss of forearm rotation. Strong

  • Absolute operative indication: All displaced, unstable radius and ulna fractures in adults I–II
  • Isolated radial shaft: >10° angulation or any PRUJ/DRUJ subluxation (Monteggia/Galeazzi) → surgery
  • Isolated ulnar shaft: >10° angulation → surgery
  • Standard technique: ORIF with dynamic compression plating for both bones (absolute stability → primary healing → immediate ROM) I

5.5 Distal Radius Fractures

Among the most frequently encountered injuries, with a bimodal distribution (young high-energy; geriatric low-energy falls). Management guided by comprehensive AAOS Clinical Practice Guidelines and Appropriate Use Criteria. I–II

Major Paradigm Shift — Geriatric Patients (≥65 years)

AAOS guidelines state with Strong Evidence that operative treatment in patients ≥65 does not yield improved long-term patient-reported functional outcomes or pain reduction compared to cast immobilization, despite better radiographic parameters. Non-operative management is the absolute standard unless an overriding specific rationale exists. I (Strong)

Table 5 — Radiographic Thresholds Dictating Operative Fixation in Non-Geriatric Adults (<65 years)
Parameter Threshold for Surgery Evidence
Radial shortening > 3 mm vs. contralateral wrist Moderate–Strong
Dorsal tilt > 10° (loss/reversal of normal volar tilt) Moderate–Strong
Intra-articular displacement > 2 mm step-off/gap at radiocarpal surface Moderate–Strong

Volar locking plate osteosynthesis is the most widely adopted and biomechanically superior technique. For extreme comminution where internal fixation is unviable, spanning external fixation (ligamentotaxis) is used as a salvage procedure. II

5.6 Metacarpal and Hand Fractures

Surgical thresholds are heavily digit-dependent due to variable CMC joint mobility. III

Table 6 — Acceptable Non-Operative Alignment for Metacarpal Fractures
Fracture Location Neck Angulation Shaft Angulation Shaft Shortening
Index & Long (rigid CMC) 10–15° 10–20° 2–5 mm
Ring (mobile CMC) 30–40° 30° 2–5 mm
Small / "Boxer's" (highly mobile CMC) 50–70° 40° 2–5 mm

Any fracture exceeding these thresholds or presenting with rotational deformity requires surgery — even a few degrees of malrotation causes finger crossover during fist closure. Bennett fracture with >1 mm articular step-off mandates fixation to prevent CMC arthritis. III

6. Lower Extremity Fractures

The lower extremity is engineered for weight-bearing, load transfer, and bipedal locomotion. Tolerances for malalignment are remarkably stringent — even minor angular deformities alter the mechanical axis, leading to eccentric joint loading and accelerated osteoarthritis. II–III

6.1 Proximal Femur (Hip) Fractures

Hip fractures are a leading cause of morbidity and mortality in older adults. AAOS guidelines emphatically support surgical intervention within 24–48 hours of presentation to minimize risks of pulmonary embolism, pneumonia, deconditioning, and decubitus ulcers. I (Strong)

Displaced Femoral Neck Fractures

  • Elderly (≥65): Primary arthroplasty (THA for active ambulators; hemiarthroplasty for lower demands). Eliminates AVN/nonunion risk; permits immediate full weight-bearing. I (Strong)
  • Young adults (<50): Urgent ORIF. Perfect anatomical reduction + absolute stability to maximize revascularization potential. True orthopaedic emergency. II

6.2 Femoral Diaphysis

Massive muscular forces (quadriceps, hamstrings, adductor magnus) cause significant displacement, shortening, and angulation. Non-operative management is essentially obsolete in adults — prolonged skeletal traction yields unacceptable pulmonary morbidity, DVT, angular malunion, and permanent knee stiffness. I–II

Type Description Stability
0Simple, no comminutionInherently stable
IMinimal comminution, insignificant butterfly fragmentStable
IISignificant comminution, >50% cortical contactModerately stable
IIISevere, <50% cortical contactRotationally/axially unstable
IVSegmental, no contact between main fragmentsCompletely unstable

Gold standard: Intramedullary (IM) nailing — load-sharing construct preserving fracture hematoma, avoiding periosteal stripping, enabling early weight-bearing and secondary bone healing. I

Retrograde IM Nailing Indications

  • Ipsilateral tibial shaft fracture (single knee incision for both nails)
  • Ipsilateral acetabular fracture
  • Bilateral femur fractures
  • Morbid obesity (hindered proximal access)
  • Pregnant patients (radiation field distance from uterus)

All indications sourced from the provided document. II–III

6.3 Periarticular Knee Fractures

Distal Femur Fractures

Bimodal distribution; mechanically difficult due to wide osteoporotic metaphysis and gastrocnemius deforming forces. ORIF with lateral locked plating or retrograde IM nailing is the standard. Dual plating is indicated for massive medial bone loss, low transcondylar fractures, or medial Hoffa fragments to prevent varus collapse. II

Tibial Plateau Fractures

Occur via axial loading + varus/valgus stress. Classified by AO/OTA into Type A (extra-articular), B (partial articular), C (complete articular). II

Non-operative management (protected weight-bearing in hinged locked brace) is exceptionally rare, reserved for strictly non-displaced fractures with inherent stability. Relative non-operative criteria include: articular step-off <2–5 mm, condylar widening <5 mm, and frontal/sagittal malalignment <5°. III

Critical Exception — Medial Plateau Fractures

Due to varus-loading mechanics during gait, even perfectly non-displaced medial plateau fractures (AO/OTA B3.2, B3.3) have a remarkable propensity to displace and collapse. They require acute rigid surgical stabilization regardless of initial appearance. II–III

For high-energy bicondylar plateau fractures (41-C2/C3), suprapatellar IM nailing has emerged as a biologically superior alternative to dual-plate ORIF, yielding equivalent joint-line maintenance while sparing the vulnerable soft-tissue envelope. II

6.4 Tibial Diaphysis

The most commonly fractured long bone. Its anteromedial subcutaneous location makes it uniquely susceptible to severe soft-tissue injury and open fractures. Soft-tissue severity in closed fractures classified by the Oestern and Tscherne system. II

Table 8 — Oestern and Tscherne Classification for Closed Tibial Fractures
Grade Clinical Description
0Indirect forces, negligible soft-tissue damage
ISuperficial contusions/abrasions, simple fracture patterns
IIDeep abrasions, significant muscle/skin contusions from direct high-energy trauma; high compartment syndrome risk
IIIExtensive skin contusions, crushed/necrotic skin, destroyed muscle, subcutaneous degloving (Morel-Lavallée), acute compartment syndrome, or major vessel/nerve rupture

Low-energy fractures (Tscherne Grade 0–I) may be managed with a long-leg cast, but alignment criteria are incredibly strict: ≤5° varus/valgus angulation. Even minor varus malunion significantly alters tibiotalar contact pressures, almost guaranteeing ipsilateral ankle arthritis. II

Operative indications: Displaced fractures, all open fractures, polytrauma, and any failure to achieve or maintain the 5° alignment criteria. IM nailing is the undisputed gold standard. Open compression plating is rarely indicated due to unacceptably high soft-tissue complication and infection rates. I–II

Unique Indication: "Dreaded Black Line"

Anterior tension-sided stress fractures have an exceptionally high likelihood of progressing to nonunion or complete fracture. Prophylactic IM nailing is specifically indicated. III

6.5 Ankle Mortise Fractures

The ankle is a highly constrained hinge joint. A mere 1 mm lateral talar shift reduces joint contact area by 42%, exponentially escalating peak cartilage stresses and leading to rapid post-traumatic arthrosis. II

Non-Operative Indications (Narrow)

  • Isolated medial or lateral malleolar fractures with no talar shift on weight-bearing or gravity-stress radiographs
  • Medial clear space must remain <4–5 mm under stress (intact deep deltoid ligament)
  • Isolated posterior malleolus fractures: <25% articular surface and <2 mm step-off

Absolute Operative Indications (ORIF)

  • Any talar displacement/widening/shift
  • All bimalleolar and trimalleolar fractures
  • Bimalleolar-equivalent fractures (lateral fracture + deep deltoid rupture)
  • All open fractures
  • Posterior malleolus >25% of joint surface
  • Syndesmotic instability: tibiofibular clear space >6 mm or dynamic widening → trans-syndesmotic fixation (screws or suture button)

Syndesmotic instability occurs in up to 50% of Weber B and >80% of Weber C fibula fractures. II

6.6 Foot — 5th Metatarsal Base Fractures

Avulsion fractures from the peroneus brevis tendon/lateral plantar fascia band are common. While minor avulsions are managed in a hard-soled shoe, operative fixation (tension-band wiring or IM compression screw) is indicated if displacement is >2 mm or the fracture involves >30% of the cuboid-metatarsal articular surface. III

7. Advanced Clinical Guidelines: Soft Tissue, Infection, and Quality Programs

Surgical Site Infection (SSI) Prevention

AAOS has established rigorous Quality Programs focused on SSI prevention and early diagnosis of acute compartment syndrome. I–II

Negative Pressure Wound Therapy (NPWT)

While NPWT may mitigate revision surgery or deep infection risk when applied over closed, high-risk incisions, Strong Evidence indicates that NPWT does not offer a statistically significant advantage over standard sterile sealed dressings when applied directly to open wounds of severe open fractures, failing to further decrease wound complications or amputation rates. I (Strong)

Appropriate Use Criteria (AUC)

The AAOS/OTA AUC represent a sophisticated evolution in guideline creation, utilizing the modified Delphi method to blend systematic literature reviews with multidisciplinary clinician expertise. This generates highly specific treatment recommendations based on complex, multivariate patient profiles (fracture mechanism, age, functional status, bone quality), ensuring decisions are never based on a single parameter but on a holistic clinical understanding. II
