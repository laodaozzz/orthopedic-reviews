📚 Foundational Literature: Top Three Standard Textbooks

The primary challenge for an orthopedic surgeon in learning IONM is bridging the gap between clinical anatomy and electrophysiological signals. These three texts form the "gold standard" for surgical education in IONM.

1. A Practical Approach to Neurophysiologic Intraoperative Monitoring Level IV – Expert Consensus

The second edition, edited by Aatif M. Husain, remains the most accessible yet thorough resource for day-to-day case management. [1] This text is uniquely valuable for orthopedic surgeons because it is co-authored by both physicians and technologists, ensuring content is grounded in operating room realities.

The book is structured into three primary sections:

  • Introduction to the Operating Room — orientation to the IONM environment
  • Clinical Methods — dedicated chapters for vertebral column surgery, spinal cord surgery, and lumbosacral procedures
  • Administrative Issues — training, billing, and legal aspects of running a neuromonitoring service
💡
The text explicitly details the challenges of monitoring patients with pre-existing myelopathy, where baseline signals may be attenuated or absent. [1]

2. Neurophysiology in Neurosurgery: A Modern Approach Level IV – Expert Consensus

Edited by Vedran Deletis and colleagues, now in its second edition with 2024–2026 updates, this is the definitive academic reference for deeper theoretical understanding. [4]

Standout features include:

  • A dedicated "Surgical Perspective" section covering spinal cord tumor resections and complex deformity corrections
  • In-depth description of how transcranial electrical stimulation activates the corticospinal tract
  • New chapters on evidence-based medicine supporting IONM for various clinical scenarios [4]

3. Operative Neurophysiology Forthcoming (Feb 2026)

Edited by Justin W. Silverstein, Randy S. D'Amico, and Jason A. Ellis, this forthcoming text fills the need for a manuscript focused on electroneurophysiologic techniques during complex, high-stakes procedures. [6]

The text places heavy emphasis on:

  • Interpreting bioelectrical signal changes when critical structures are at risk
  • Reviewing relevant anatomy and physiology alongside electrode placement and patient positioning
  • Neuromapping — identifying specific neural structures intraoperatively to avoid injury during dissection or instrumentation [6]

📋 Clinical Guidelines & Standards of Practice

ASNM Position Statements and Technical Standards Level II–III

The American Society of Neurophysiological Monitoring (ASNM) provides the most technically rigorous guidelines for IONM. Their 2024 update on SSEP monitoring offers detailed recommendations on stimulation rates, recording parameters, and interpretation. [18]

  • ASNM recommends "continuous, multi-nerve, interleaved SSEP stimulation" for fastest data acquisition [18]
  • Advocates for a professional model including an in-room technologist and a remote supervising professional (MD or PhD) independent of the surgical team [20]
  • This independent oversight ensures neurophysiological data interpretation is not biased by the surgeon's desire to proceed [20]

NASS Evidence-Based Guideline Updates Level I – Meta-Analysis/Systematic Review

NASS has published comprehensive evidence-based updates establishing that multimodal monitoring is effective in predicting the risk of adverse outcomes (paraparesis or quadriplegia) during spinal surgery. [3]

✅
IONM is mandatory for high-risk deformity and intramedullary tumors. It is optional but recommended for routine degenerative cervical fusions. [23]

SRS Safety Checklists and Protocols Level III – Cohort Studies

The SRS champions the use of safety checklists involving three-way communication between the surgeon, anesthesiologist, and neuromonitoring team. [24] The "preoperative sign-in" is a critical opportunity to discuss:

  • The anesthetic protocol for the case
  • Specific warning criteria
  • The planned response to a positive alarm [24]
Section 4

💉 Anesthesia Protocols: TIVA versus Inhalational Agents

The anesthetic regimen is perhaps the single most important factor (outside of the surgery itself) that determines IONM signal quality. The interaction between pharmacological agents and neurophysiological pathways requires close collaboration between surgeon and anesthesiologist. [25]

Total Intravenous Anesthesia (TIVA): The Gold Standard Level I/II – Large Cohort

TIVA (typically propofol + remifentanil) is the gold standard for IONM. [26]

  • Allows target-site concentration calculations based on age, gender, and weight, providing stable anesthetic state that minimizes signal interference [26]
  • Large-scale cohort studies demonstrate TIVA is associated with significantly lower in-hospital mortality and fewer postoperative complications vs. inhalational anesthesia [27]
  • Propofol's neuroprotective and antioxidant effects make it ideal for high-risk spinal procedures [25]
  • Lacks the dose-dependent suppression of alpha-motor neurons, allowing robust MEP signals [26]

Inhalational (Volatile) Anesthesia: Limitations Level II–III

Inhalational agents (sevoflurane, desflurane) cause a significant, dose-dependent reduction in MEP amplitude and increase SSEP latency. [26]

⚠️
At 1.0 MAC, MEP waveforms often become unrecognizable, rendering monitoring useless for motor tract protection. If inhalational agents must be used, keep below 0.5 MAC and supplement with narcotics. [26]

Neuromuscular Blockade Management Level III

While short-acting agents are used for intubation, subsequent doses should be avoided to allow TcMEP and spontaneous EMG monitoring. [31]

  • The "Train-of-Four" (TOF) response monitors depth of relaxation
  • For effective MEP monitoring, a TOF of at least 2/4 or 3/4 twitches is usually required [26]

Section 5

🚨 Warning Criteria for Spinal Deformity Surgery

The determination of a "true alert" is based on established thresholds for signal change. While criteria vary slightly between institutions, the following are the most widely accepted standards. [3, 31, 32]

Somatosensory Evoked Potentials (SSEP) Level I

SSEPs monitor the dorsal columns and are sensitive to mechanical distraction, compression, and ischemia. [3]

  • Amplitude Change: Decrease of ≥50% from baseline = standard warning threshold [31]
  • Latency Change: Increase of ≥10% from baseline = significant alert [31]
  • Key Limitation: SSEPs are a "trailing indicator" — they require averaging of many stimuli, which can take several minutes to reflect an acute injury [32]

Transcranial Motor Evoked Potentials (TcMEP) Level I

TcMEPs provide a near-instantaneous assessment of the corticospinal tract and are the most sensitive tool for preventing iatrogenic paralysis. [32]

  • Amplitude Change: Decrease of ≥80% from baseline = most common alert criterion [32]
  • Threshold Change: Increase of ≥100 V in stimulation threshold from baseline [32]
  • Adaptive Criteria (newer): Nerve root dysfunction alert at 50–65% reduction; cord dysfunction at 75–85% reduction [23]

Electromyography (EMG) Level II

EMG monitors individual nerve roots, particularly during pedicle screw placement and lateral lumbar approaches. [1]

  • Spontaneous EMG (sEMG): "Neurotonic discharges" or "trains" signify mechanical/thermal nerve root irritation [31]
  • Triggered EMG (tEMG): Threshold of <8 mA = probable pedicle wall breach; >12 mA = high likelihood of secure screw [20]

🔧 Intraoperative Troubleshooting Checklist

When an IONM alert is issued, the surgeon must follow a disciplined protocol to identify and reverse the cause. This "Response Algorithm" is a critical skill. [32]

1

Immediate Surgical Response

Assess the surgical field for mechanical causes. If the alert follows a specific maneuver (distraction, derotation, implant placement), immediately undo that maneuver. This is the "reversible phase" of injury where reducing compressive or distractive forces can restore signal function. [32, 33]

2

Anesthetic & Systemic Check

The anesthesia team must verify:

  • MAP Optimization: Increase MAP to >80 mmHg (or >90 mmHg) to ensure adequate spinal cord perfusion [32]
  • Pharmacological Review: Ensure no recent bolus of propofol or neuromuscular blocker ("false positive") [32]
  • Physiological Stability: Check temperature, oxygenation (SpO₂), and end-tidal CO₂ [32]
3

Technical Troubleshooting

The IONM team must check for technical failures:

  • Electrode Integrity: Check for dislodged/disconnected needles after patient rolling or aggressive surgical movements [32]
  • "Parallel Alerts": If signals lost in all four limbs (including control) → likely systemic/technical. If lost only distal to surgical site → likely focal iatrogenic injury [32]
4

Stagnara Wake-Up Test

If the alert persists despite all corrective measures, the Stagnara wake-up test remains the definitive clinical confirmation of motor function. [32]

  • If the patient can move their feet → surgery may continue
  • If no movement → abort procedure and discuss with patient's family [32]
🔑
Key Principle: The decision to stop surgery is a clinical judgment that integrates IONM data, the surgical context, and the patient's overall condition. IONM provides objective data, but the surgeon remains the final decision-maker.
Section 7

🔮 The Future of IONM: AI and Predictive Models

The field of IONM is moving toward more sophisticated, data-driven approaches. Recent NASS publications describe the integration of Artificial Intelligence (AI) and Machine Learning (ML) to enhance signal interpretation accuracy. [23]

  • Future models will use preoperative variables (degree of curvature, baseline neurological status) and real-time intraoperative data (MAP, blood loss, anesthetic depth) to create predictive injury models [23]
  • Systems will provide "diagnostic confidence probabilities" rather than simple amplitude percentages, enabling more nuanced decision-making [23]
  • Surgeon-directed automated platforms may reduce reliance on remote monitoring in low-risk cases, though expert human interpretation will remain necessary for complex deformity and tumor surgeries [36]
