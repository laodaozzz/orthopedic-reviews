Acromioclavicular Joint Dislocation: Temporal Definitions, Hook Plate Outcomes, and Ligament Reconstruction

Interactive Evidence-Based Educational Summary  ·  Source: Comprehensive Literature Analysis (2026)

§1 Introduction: The Clinical Imperative

Acromioclavicular (AC) joint injuries represent a prevalent and complex challenge within orthopedic sports medicine, accounting for approximately 9% to 12% of all shoulder girdle injuries LOE II [1]. These disruptions predominantly affect young, active males in their third and fourth decades of life. The typical mechanism involves a direct, high-energy impact to the superior aspect of the acromion with the arm in an adducted position, driving the scapula inferiorly and sequentially disrupting the static and dynamic stabilizers of the joint [1].

While management of low-grade injuries (Rockwood Types I–II) is universally conservative and high-grade injuries (Types IV–VI) generally necessitate surgical stabilization, Type III injuries and patients presenting in the "subacute" phase (2–6 weeks post-injury) remain intensely debated LOE II [2]. The dilemma is compounded when considering rigid fixation devices such as the Clavicular Hook Plate (CHP). Although the CHP provides robust mechanical stability, its efficacy as standalone treatment diminishes as the biological healing potential of native coracoclavicular (CC) ligaments wanes over time LOE III [4].

📋 Scope of This Report

This summary provides an exhaustive analysis of: (a) definitions of "acute" vs. "chronic" AC dislocation; (b) clinical outcomes of CHP fixation in the delayed/subacute setting; and (c) comparative analysis of CHP as monotherapy versus hybrid techniques incorporating ligament reconstruction, yielding an evidence-based algorithm for management.

§2 Anatomical & Biomechanical Substrate

2.1 Static Stabilizers

The Acromioclavicular (AC) ligaments control horizontal (anteroposterior) stability. The superior AC ligament is the strongest and provides the primary restraint against posterior clavicular translation [2]. Disruption of this complex (as in Rockwood Type II) results in horizontal instability.

The Coracoclavicular (CC) complex—consisting of the conoid (posteromedial) and trapezoid (anterolateral) ligaments—provides the primary restraint against vertical translation. The conoid ligament is the principal restraint against superior loading, while the trapezoid constrains axial compression and rotation [2]. In Rockwood Types III–V, both bundles are ruptured, leading to significant vertical displacement.

2.2 Dynamic Stabilizers: Deltotrapezial Fascia

Dynamic stabilization is provided by the deltotrapezial fascia, which blends into the superior AC ligaments and the periosteum of the distal clavicle. In high-energy trauma (Rockwood Types IV–V), this fascia is stripped from the clavicle and acromion—a "degloving" mechanism that contributes to gross instability and the classic sign of "skin tenting" [1]. Its integrity is crucial for rehabilitation potential; its disruption necessitates surgical repair or plication to restore the dynamic force couple [2].

2.3 Vascular Considerations

The primary blood supply derives from the acromial branch of the thoracoacromial artery and the suprascapular artery [2]. Surgical approaches must respect this vascularity to optimize healing, particularly in the subacute phase where biological potential is already compromised.

§3 Defining Chronicity: The Temporal Debate

The dichotomy between "acute" and "chronic" injury fundamentally dictates orthopedic decision-making. In the AC joint context, this definition determines the biological environment and whether simple fixation (repair) or reconstruction (grafting) is necessary.

3.1 Systematic Review of Definitions

A systematic review of 116 articles analyzing definitions of chronicity in orthopedic sports injuries found that for AC joint dislocations, definitions of "acute" ranged from less than 10 days to less than 6 weeks LOE II [6]. This variability complicates meta-analyses, as a study defining "acute" as <6 weeks might include patients at 3–6 weeks who have already lost ligamentous healing potential.

3.2 The 3-Week Physiological Cutoff

Despite variation, a consensus is emerging around the 3-week (21-day) threshold as the physiological limit of the acute phase LOE III [1].

🔬 Biological Rationale

Ligamentous healing follows three phases: inflammation (Days 0–7), proliferation (Days 7–21), and remodeling (Days 21+). The inflammatory and early proliferative phases are characterized by fibroblasts and organized hematoma capable of bridging ruptured ligament ends if mechanically reduced [8]. Beyond 3 weeks, ligament stumps retract and undergo resorption, transitioning to scar tissue lacking structural integrity [9].

3.3 The "Grey Zone" (3–6 Weeks)

The period between 3 and 6 weeks represents a critical "Grey Zone." Historically, "chronic" AC dislocation was defined as >6 weeks [6]. However, treating a 5-week-old injury as "acute" with simple fixation often fails because the biological healing needed to maintain reduction after hardware removal is absent. The ISAKOS Upper Extremity Committee suggests a trial of non-surgical treatment for Type III injuries, with surgical evaluation if pain or instability persists beyond one month Expert Consensus [2].

3.4 The 7-Day Hyper-Acute Window

⚠️ Critical Finding (Lee et al., 2023)

A pivotal retrospective analysis found that time from injury to surgery >7 days was a statistically significant risk factor for loss of reduction after hook plate fixation. Multivariate analysis: OR 3.36 (95% CI) for reduction loss in patients treated after 7 days vs. within the first week LOE III [4]. This suggests biological degradation begins almost immediately.

§4 Clavicular Hook Plate: Mechanics & Acute Outcomes

4.1 Design Principles

The CHP is a specialized fixation device acting as an extra-articular internal splint. It features a lateral extension shaped into a hook inserted under the posterior aspect of the acromion, while the plate portion is secured to the distal clavicle with screws [10].

  • Lever Arm Mechanics: The hook creates a lever arm counteracting the superior pull of the trapezius muscle and arm weight, maintaining the coracoclavicular distance (CCD) without direct ligament repair LOE III [13].
  • Rotational Constraint: Unlike trans-articular pinning, the CHP allows partial rotational motion during shoulder elevation. However, this motion creates significant point-loading stress on the subacromial bone, the genesis of its unique complication profile [12].

4.2 Acute Outcomes (<3 Weeks)

When employed within the strict acute window, the CHP demonstrates high efficacy:

  • Functional Scores: Constant-Murley scores typically 89–94; UCLA scores consistently >30/35 LOE II [13].
  • Pain Relief: VAS scores drop from pre-operative 7–8 to post-operative <2 [15].
  • Return to Activity: Light activities within 3 months, coinciding with planned hardware removal [17].
  • Radiographic Efficacy: CCD restored to near-anatomical levels (100% of contralateral side). Minor loss post-removal (110–120% of contralateral) is common but well-tolerated LOE III [15].

§5 Analysis of Delayed & Subacute Fixation (>3 Weeks)

5.1 Failure Rates & Loss of Reduction

📉 Critical Data: Standalone CHP Failure in Delayed Cases

Delayed repairs utilizing standalone hook plates demonstrate radiological failure rates (recurrent subluxation/dislocation) approaching 30%–50% after hardware removal LOE III [17]. In delayed cases, the hook plate functions purely as a mechanical stop—native ligaments have resorbed or scarred in an elongated position, forming no biological tether. Upon removal at 3–4 months, the clavicle is subject to the same deforming forces that caused the initial injury.

5.2 Comparative Outcomes: Acute vs. Delayed

  • Radiographic Disparity: Kim et al. found patients treated after 3 weeks had significantly greater CCD ratios at final follow-up vs. acute cases, indicating persistent subluxation LOE III [15].
  • Functional Lag: Delayed groups show slower ROM restoration, lower satisfaction, and longer time to above-shoulder-level ROM [20].
  • Independent Predictors of Failure: Beyond timing, female sex (OR 5.88) and Rockwood Type V are independent predictors of failure in delayed CHP cases LOE III [11].

5.3 The "Grey Zone" Pitfall

For patients in the 3–6 week window, applying an "acute" solution (standalone hook plate) to a "subacute" problem is a common clinical pitfall. The surgeon relies on the plate for immediate stability, but without addressing the biological deficiency, the procedure is destined for late failure. This necessitates a shift from fixation to reconstruction.

§6 The Role of Ligament Reconstruction

6.1 Rationale for Augmentation

When native CC ligaments are non-viable, they must be replaced or augmented to provide long-term stability after hardware removal:

  • Biological Augmentation: Autografts (semitendinosus, gracilis) or allografts for anatomical CC reconstruction—gold standard for chronic (>6 weeks) injuries LOE II [21].
  • Synthetic Augmentation: Suture tapes, woven polyester cords (LARS), or cortical button systems (TightRope) provide scaffold for scar formation and act as permanent internal splints LOE III [23].

6.2 Efficacy of Combined Techniques

✅ Key Evidence (Wang et al., 2019)

A comparative study found that CHP + CC ligament augmentation (suture anchors or tape) yielded significantly better ASES and UCLA scores at final follow-up compared to CHP-only. The combined group demonstrated significantly lower rates of residual subluxation and loss of reduction LOE II [16]. Combined fixation may also reduce subacromial osteolysis by sharing vertical load [13].

§7 Complications of CHP Fixation

7.1 Subacromial Osteolysis & Erosion

The most frequent complication—reported in 30% to 66% of cases LOE II [14]. The hook acts as a fulcrum: during arm elevation, the acromion rotates against the stationary hook. Erosion ranges from minor cortical depression (Grade I) to complete cut-through (Grade IV) [17]. Risk is proportional to implantation duration; manufacturers recommend removal at 3 months. Retention beyond 4–5 months drastically increases severe erosion and acromial fracture risk [26].

7.2 Rotator Cuff Impingement & Tears

The hook reduces subacromial space volume, causing mechanical impingement. Patients frequently report subacromial pain and inability to abduct past 90° while the plate is in situ. While typically reversible upon removal, long-term retention can lead to bursitis, tendon fraying, or full-thickness supraspinatus tears [12].

7.3 Periprosthetic Fractures

Clavicle fractures at the plate's medial end occur in approximately 1–3% of cases, attributed to the stress riser created by the rigid plate on the S-shaped clavicle LOE III [27].

7.4 Infection

Infection rates are approximately 1.9%–5% [27]. Deep infection necessitates immediate hardware removal, potentially catastrophic if before soft tissue healing is complete.

§8 Comparative Efficacy: CHP vs. Other Modalities

8.1 Hook Plate vs. Suture Button (TightRope)

Suture button fixation involves drilling tunnels through the clavicle and coracoid to pass a heavy suture/button construct. Meta-analyses indicate that while both techniques yield similar functional scores (Constant, UCLA), the CHP provides more reliable maintenance of vertical reduction (CCD), whereas the suture button group has a lower re-operation rate (no removal surgery) LOE II [10]. Both techniques require biological augmentation in subacute cases to prevent late failure [30].

8.2 Hook Plate vs. LARS (Synthetic Ligament)

LARS provides immediate, permanent stability using industrial-strength polyester ligament. It allows rapid return to sport and does not require removal—particularly useful in chronic cases LOE III [23]. However, it is technically more demanding and carries a risk of synovitis if the graft abrades.

§9 Rehabilitation Protocols

Phase I: Protection (0–4 Weeks)

  • Immobilization: Sling to support arm weight and reduce traction on repair.
  • Passive Motion: Early passive ROM encouraged—pendulum exercises, passive forward flexion [31].
  • Restrictions: Abduction strictly limited to 90 degrees to prevent hook engagement with acromion. Cross-body adduction is avoided Protocol-Based [31].

Phase II: Activation (4–12 Weeks)

  • Active-assisted and active ROM exercises commence.
  • Scapular stabilizer strengthening (retraction, depression) initiated.
  • Mechanical Block Awareness: Full abduction may not be mechanically possible while the plate is in situ. Forcing this motion risks acromial fracture or hardware failure.

Phase III: Post-Removal (>3 Months)

  • Hardware Removal: Second surgery at 10–12 weeks (3 months), balancing healing time against osteolysis risk [28].
  • Recovery: Rapid improvement in pain and ROM within 2 weeks of removal.
  • Return to Sport: Contact sports generally permitted at 3–6 months post-initial injury, with dynamic strength restored to within 90% of the contralateral side [31].

§10 Conclusion & Clinical Practice Guideline

🔑 Key Findings
  1. Temporal Definitions: The widely accepted cutoff for "acute" injury is 3 weeks. The 3–6 week period is a "subacute grey zone" where biological healing potential is compromised.
  2. Delayed Hook Plate Risks: Standalone CHP fixation delayed beyond 3 weeks is associated with 30–50% reduction loss after hardware removal. Mechanical stability cannot compensate for absent biological ligament integrity.
  3. Augmentation is Essential: For subacute and chronic cases, ligament reconstruction alongside CHP fixation is strongly recommended.
