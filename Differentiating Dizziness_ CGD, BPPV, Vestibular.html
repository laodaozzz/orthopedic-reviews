I. Foundational Principles of Vestibular & Sensorimotor Assessment

The clinical complaint of "dizziness" is one of the most common presentations in outpatient practice. It is an imprecise "umbrella term" used by patients to describe a wide array of sensations. Effective differentiation requires moving beyond this vague symptom and employing a systematic framework grounded in pathophysiology, symptom profiling, and precise physical examination.Narrative Review

A. Deconstructing "Dizziness": Beyond the Traditional Four Categories

Historically, clinicians categorized dizziness into four types: vertigo (spinning), disequilibrium (balance disturbance), presyncope (faintness), and lightheadedness (vague). However, this traditional approach is now considered "of limited clinical usefulness" because patients have difficulty describing the quality of their symptoms, and dichotomizing can lead to misleading diagnoses.Practice Guideline

The modern, Bárány Society-endorsed approach focuses on phenomenological descriptions:Consensus (Bárány Society)

  • Internal Vertigo: False sensation of self-motion (spinning, swaying, bobbing)
  • External Vertigo: False sensation that surroundings are moving
  • Unsteadiness: Instability without directional preference
  • Dizziness (non-motion): Non-motion sensation of disrupted spatial orientation

Because patients "can more consistently identify the timing and triggers" of their symptoms than the quality, the superior diagnostic strategy is the TiTrATE framework:Practice Guideline

📋 The TiTrATE Framework
  1. Timing: How long do episodes last (seconds, minutes, hours, days)? Constant or episodic?
  2. Triggers: What brings on the dizziness (position change, head movement, visual stimuli, spontaneous)?
  3. Associated Symptoms: Neck pain, hearing loss, tinnitus, headache, photophobia, weakness?

B. The Core Pathophysiological Conflict: Mechanical vs. Sensory

1. BPPV — A Mechanical Problem

BPPV is the most common vestibular disorder.Epidemiological data It is a mechanical disorder caused by displacement of calcium-carbonate crystals (otoconia) from the utricle into the semicircular canals — most commonly the posterior canal due to its anatomy. When a person changes head position relative to gravity, these free-floating canaliths create drag on the endolymph, deflecting the cupula and producing brief, intense vertigo with characteristic nystagmus.Pathophysiology review

2. Cervicogenic Dizziness (CGD) — A Sensory-Mismatch Problem

CGD is not an inner ear disorder. It is a sensory-processing disorder rooted in cervical spine dysfunction, and remains a "controversial diagnosis".Narrative Review The most convincing pathogenesis is a sensory mismatch: the cervical spine's dense proprioceptors provide head-position information to the CNS. When cervical pathology (trauma, spasm, degenerative disease) distorts this input, the CNS receives conflicting data from the eyes, vestibular system, and cervical proprioceptors, producing dizziness and unsteadiness.Narrative Review

🔑 Key Diagnostic Implication: Because BPPV is mechanical, its diagnostic test (Dix-Hallpike) is a mechanical provocation producing an objective sign (nystagmus) — a "rule-in" diagnosis. Because CGD is a sensory mismatch, its tests are sensory-isolation maneuvers producing a subjective response — a "diagnosis of exclusion."

II. Screening for Central Pathologies

Before performing any provocative maneuvers for benign disorders, the clinician's first priority is to screen for dangerous central pathologies that mimic peripheral vestibular disorders. A posterior circulation stroke can present with vertigo as the only symptom.Emergency medicine literature

A. Red Flag Symptoms: The "5 D's" & "3 N's"

🚨 Central Red Flags
  • 5 D's: Dizziness + Diplopia + Dysphagia + Dysarthria + Dystaxia
  • 3 N's: Severe Nausea/vomiting + Numbness (facial/unilateral) + Central-pattern Nystagmus
  • Thunderclap headache, limb weakness, drop attacks, facial paralysis
  • Sudden unilateral hearing loss with vertigo (possible AICA stroke)

Any of these → STOP outpatient exam → Urgent neurological referral.

⚠ Limitation: Many posterior circulation strokes do not present with classic findings. The stroke may present as an "acute pure vestibular syndrome," perfectly mimicking vestibular neuritis — hence the need for the HINTS exam.

B. The HINTS Exam (for Acute Vestibular Syndrome only)

The HINTS exam is a three-step bedside oculomotor test that is "more sensitive than early MRI diffusion-weighted imaging" for detecting stroke in patients with Acute Vestibular Syndrome (AVS).Emergency medicine literature

Indication: AVS = acute, sudden-onset, continuous vertigo/dizziness lasting hours–days. Not for brief episodic dizziness (BPPV) or CGD.

Table 2: HINTS Examination — Interpretation of Findings
Component Peripheral (Reassuring) Central (Dangerous)
H — Head Impulse Abnormal: Corrective catch-up saccade present (deficient VOR) Normal: Eyes stay locked on target (VOR intact — problem not peripheral)
N — Nystagmus Unidirectional, horizontal (follows Alexander's Law) Direction-changing (bidirectional) OR purely vertical/torsional
TS — Test of Skew Normal: No vertical re-fixation Abnormal (Skew): Vertical re-fixation movement observed

Overall: Any ONE central finding → central pattern → suspect stroke. All three must be peripheral for "reassuring."Emergency neuro-otology

🔍 HINTS Plus (AICA Stroke Caveat): An AICA stroke can damage both cerebellum/brainstem and the inner ear, producing a pathological Head Impulse — mimicking neuritis. The "Plus" = check hearing. AVS + peripheral HINTS + new unilateral hearing loss → suspect AICA stroke, not labyrinthitis → urgent referral.

III. Diagnosing Common Peripheral Vestibular Disorders

Once central pathologies are excluded, the clinician must rule out common peripheral vestibular disorders. CGD is a diagnosis of exclusion, and these "rule-in" diagnoses must be addressed first.

A. Benign Paroxysmal Positional Vertigo (BPPV)

Clinical profile: Brief episodes (typically <1 min, 5–20 seconds) of intense, room-spinning vertigo, triggered exclusively by changes in head position relative to gravity. No auditory or neurologic symptoms.Bárány Society diagnostic criteria

Dix-Hallpike Test (Posterior Canal BPPV — Gold Standard)

  1. Patient in long-sitting position; head rotated 45° toward the side to be tested
  2. Rapidly guide patient to supine with head hanging 20–30° below horizontal
  3. Observe eyes for ≥30–40 seconds (latency period)
  4. Return to upright; observe for reversal of nystagmus
  5. Repeat on contralateral side
✅ Positive Dix-Hallpike = Pathognomonic Pattern:
  • Latency: 2–5 sec delay (up to 40 sec)
  • Nystagmus: Torsional upbeating (upper pole beats toward dependent ear)
  • Duration: Fatigues within 5–20 sec (max 60 sec)
  • Reversal: Opposite nystagmus on sitting up
  • Affected side: The ear toward the floor

Contraindications: Cervical instability, acute neck trauma, disc prolapse, Arnold-Chiari malformation, vascular dissection, vertebrobasilar insufficiency.Clinical guidelines

Supine Roll Test (Horizontal Canal BPPV)

If Dix-Hallpike is negative or shows horizontal nystagmus: patient lies supine, head rapidly rotated 90° to each side. Positive = horizontal nystagmus (geotropic = canalithiasis; apogeotropic = cupulolithiasis).Clinical neuro-otology

Table 3: Diagnostic Maneuvers for BPPV
Test Procedure Positive Nystagmus Canal
Dix-Hallpike Sit → supine, 45° head rotation, 20–30° extension Upbeating-torsional (torsion toward down-ear) Posterior canal
Supine Roll Test Supine, rapid 90° head rotation L and R Horizontal (geotropic or apogeotropic) Horizontal canal

B. Vestibular Neuritis & Labyrinthitis

Neuritis: Sudden, severe, constant vertigo lasting days–weeks. Any motion worsens symptoms. No auditory symptoms.
Labyrinthitis: Identical presentation plus hearing loss and tinnitus.Clinical review

Both are diagnoses of exclusion after central causes are ruled out. In AVS, a "peripheral" HINTS profile points to neuritis/labyrinthitis.

C. Ménière's Disease (MD)

Diagnostic criteria require:Bárány Society criteria

  1. ≥2 spontaneous vertigo episodes lasting 20 min – 12 hours
  2. Audiometrically documented low-to-mid frequency sensorineural hearing loss (≥1 occasion)
  3. Fluctuating aural symptoms (hearing, tinnitus, fullness) in affected ear
  4. Not better explained by another vestibular diagnosis

D. Vestibular Migraine (VM) — The Great Imitator

⚠ VM is the single greatest confound in CGD differentiation.
  • VM can mimic BPPV, Ménière's, and CGD
  • 38% of VM patients report MD-like aural symptoms
  • 49% of MD patients report migrainous features
  • VM is "frequently misdiagnosed as Ménière's disease"
  • Neck pain is an extremely common symptom of migraine itself
🔑 Bárány Society 2022 Position: "Migraine, including vestibular migraine, is by far the commonest cause for the combination of neck pain and vestibular symptoms."Consensus (Bárány COSC, 2022) Therefore, in any patient with neck pain + dizziness, VM must be the primary differential. An exhaustive migraine history is mandatory before considering CGD.

IV. Isolating Cervicogenic Dizziness (CGD)

Only after BPPV, Ménière's, and (most importantly) Vestibular Migraine have been rigorously excluded can the clinician build a positive case for CGD.

A. The "Diagnosis of Exclusion" Mandate

CGD is a "diagnosis of exclusion." There is no single definitive test.Narrative review

📋 Bárány Society COSC Position (2022):
  • The committee "refrains from proposing any preliminary diagnostic criteria for clinical use outside a research study"
  • Evidence for a mechanistic link between neck pathology and true vertigo "is lacking"
  • Head movement aggravating symptoms is non-specific (applies to almost any vestibular condition)
Consensus (Bárány COSC, 2022)

B. The CGD Clinical Profile

  • Symptom quality: Non-rotatory dizziness — "floating," light-headedness, unsteadiness. True vertigo is rare.Narrative review
  • Temporal link (most important): Dizziness must be "closely related to neck pain in time" — provoked by cervical movements or sustained postures.Narrative review
  • Associated cervical symptoms (required): Neck pain and/or reduced neck mobility, with local muscle tenderness or tightness.Narrative review

C. Provocative Physical Examination Tests for CGD

These tests aim to reproduce dizziness by stimulating cervical proprioceptors without stimulating the vestibular system.

Table 4: Diagnostic Maneuvers for Cervicogenic Dizziness
Test Procedure Positive Finding
Cervical Flexion-Rotation Test (CFRT) Supine → maximal passive cervical flexion (locks C3-C7) → passive rotation L/R at C1-C2 Visually estimated >10° loss from normal (~44°) OR symptom reproduction
Head-Neck Differentiation Test (HNDT) Seated on swivel chair, eyes closed. Head stabilized in neutral by examiner. Patient actively rotates body L/R under stationary head. Reproduction of patient's characteristic dizziness
Joint Position Error (JPE) Test Seated, eyes closed. Patient rotates head and attempts to return to neutral starting position. Error measured. Mean error > 4.5° from true neutral (abnormal proprioception)
✅ Provisional CGD Diagnosis Is Defensible When:
  1. All other central and vestibular causes are excluded
  2. Clear temporal link between neck pain and non-rotatory dizziness
  3. Constellation of positive cervical signs (C1-C2 restriction, abnormal JPE, positive HNDT)
  4. Dizziness is reproduced on HNDT (cervical-specific afferent isolation)

V. Clinical Decision-Making Algorithm

1

Triage by Timing

Acute Vestibular Syndrome (AVS) — continuous, hours–days → Step 2
Episodic Dizziness — discrete spells → Step 3

2

AVS Pathway

2A. Screen for "5 D's" red flags → If present: Urgent referral
2B. Perform HINTS exam → Any central sign → Urgent referral (stroke)
2C. HINTS Plus: Peripheral HINTS + new unilateral hearing loss → Urgent referral (AICA stroke)
2D. All peripheral + no hearing loss → Vestibular Neuritis
All peripheral + hearing loss → Labyrinthitis

3

Episodic Pathway (TiTrATE-Based)

<1 min vertigo + positional trigger → Suspect BPPV → Step 4
20 min – 12 hr spontaneous vertigo + auditory triad → Suspect Ménière's → Audiometry
Minutes–hours + migraine features → Suspect Vestibular Migraine → Step 5
Minutes–hours + neck pain + neck movement trigger → Possible CGD → Step 5

4

BPPV Confirmation

4A. Dix-Hallpike positive → Posterior Canal BPPV → Treat (Epley). STOP.
4B. Supine Roll positive → Horizontal Canal BPPV → Treat (Gufoni/BBQ roll). STOP.
4C. All negative → BPPV excluded → Return to Step 3/5

5

VM vs. CGD Differentiation — The Great Confound

5A. Exhaustively exclude Vestibular Migraine: Ask about photophobia, phonophobia, aura, personal/family migraine history. Recall: neck pain is a common migraine symptom, and VM is the commonest cause of neck pain + dizziness.
Any migraine features → Treat as VM. CGD unlikely.

5B. Provisional CGD workup (only if VM and all others excluded):
Positive cervical dysfunction on exam → CFRT positive → JPE >4.5° → HNDT reproduces dizziness → Provisional CGD diagnosis → Manual therapy + vestibular rehabilitation.

VI. Conclusion

The differentiation of cervicogenic dizziness, BPPV, and other vestibular disorders is a rigorous, multi-step process built on the TiTrATE framework. Two fundamentally different pathologies must be distinguished: BPPV (mechanical inner ear disorder) and CGD (sensory-mismatch syndrome). BPPV is a "rule-in" diagnosis confirmed by the Dix-Hallpike test; CGD is a controversial "diagnosis of exclusion" lacking formal diagnostic criteria.

The assessment follows a strict hierarchy:

  1. First: Screen for dangerous central pathologies (HINTS exam for AVS)
  2. Second: Rule out BPPV (definitive positional testing)
  3. Third: Differentiate from Ménière's Disease (auditory-vestibular triad)
  4. Fourth: Rigorously exclude Vestibular Migraine — the "great imitator" and the most common cause of neck pain + dizziness
  5. Only then: Build a positive case for CGD using cervical-specific provocative tests (HNDT, CFRT, JPE)
