1. Introduction: The Evolving Landscape of Ankle Sprain Management

The clinical landscape surrounding the diagnosis and management of acute lateral ankle sprains (LAS) has undergone a profound transformation between 2020 and 2025. Historically dismissed as self-limiting "innocuous" injuries requiring minimal intervention beyond rest and ice, LAS is now recognized as a complex neuro-physiological event with significant long-term sequelae. The prevailing evidence underscores a critical paradigm shift: the injury is not merely a local ligamentous disruption but a catalyst for a cascade of sensorimotor, mechanical, and behavioral impairments that, if left unaddressed, lead to Chronic Ankle Instability (CAI) in approximately 40% of patients. Level I [1]

This report synthesizes the most current high-level evidence, including systematic reviews, meta-analyses, and clinical practice guidelines (CPGs) from the American Academy of Orthopaedic Surgeons (AAOS), the National Athletic Trainers' Association (NATA), the International Ankle Consortium (IAC), and the Academy of Orthopaedic Physical Therapy (JOSPT). Level I [1, 2, 19, 32]

1.1 Epidemiology & Socioeconomic Burden

Acute ankle sprains remain the single most prevalent musculoskeletal injury in physically active populations. Current epidemiological data indicates that ankle sprains account for up to 45% of all athletic injuries, with the highest incidence rates observed in court and indoor sports such as basketball, volleyball, and soccer. Level II–III [4]

  • In Western populations, the incidence approaches one sprain per 10,000 individuals daily, translating to millions of emergency department visits annually. [5]
  • The economic burden is substantial—driven by direct medical costs (diagnostic imaging, bracing, physical therapy) and significant indirect costs related to absenteeism and lost productivity.
  • A critical "treatment gap" persists: nearly 50% of individuals who sustain an acute LAS do not seek formal medical care, a behavior that significantly increases the risk of recurrent injury and long-term disability. Level II [6]

1.2 Pathophysiology of Chronic Ankle Instability (CAI)

A central theme in the 2020–2025 literature is the rigorous characterization of CAI. Updated CPGs define CAI as a condition characterized by perceived instability, recurrent sprains, and episodes of "giving way" that persist for more than 12 months following the initial injury. The development of CAI is best understood through the Hertel model, which bifurcates instability into two distinct but interacting components: [1]

Hertel Model of Ankle Instability

Mechanical Instability (MI): Pathological laxity resulting from structural damage to the lateral ligament complex—specifically the ATFL and CFL. Includes synovial changes, degenerative transformations, and altered arthrokinematics. Level I

Functional Instability (FI): Sensorimotor deficits such as impaired proprioception, delayed neuromuscular reaction times, and deficits in postural control. Crucially, these deficits are often bilateral, suggesting central nervous system reorganization following the peripheral injury. Level II [8]

The failure to address the functional component during the acute phase is a primary driver of chronicity. Consequently, modern guidelines emphasize that effective management must target both the mechanical restoration of ligament integrity and the neurophysiological restoration of motor control. [2]

2. Anatomy & Mechanism of Injury

Understanding the underlying anatomy is prerequisite to precise diagnosis and grading. The lateral ligamentous complex of the ankle consists of three primary structures, which are injured in a predictable sequence depending on the magnitude and vector of the applied force.

2.1 The Lateral Ligament Complex

  • Anterior Talofibular Ligament (ATFL): The weakest and most frequently injured ligament. It acts as the primary restraint to inversion in plantarflexion and is involved in approximately 85% of all ankle sprains. Level II [10]
  • Calcaneofibular Ligament (CFL): Stabilizes the subtalar joint and restricts inversion when the ankle is in neutral or dorsiflexion. Injury usually occurs in severe Grade II or Grade III sprains after the ATFL has failed. [11]
  • Posterior Talofibular Ligament (PTFL): The strongest of the lateral ligaments, rarely injured in isolation—typically ruptures only in frank ankle dislocation. [11]

The classic mechanism of injury involves excessive inversion and adduction of the foot combined with plantarflexion. This position unlocks the talocrural joint, maximizing reliance on the ATFL for stability. Conversely, syndesmotic (high ankle) sprains involve damage to the distal tibiofibular ligaments and typically result from forced external rotation and dorsiflexion. Level II [12]

3. Clinical Assessment & Diagnosis

The diagnostic process has been refined to prioritize the exclusion of fractures using validated decision rules and the early identification of impairments that predict poor outcomes. The International Ankle Consortium's "ROAST" consensus (Rehabilitation-Oriented ASsessmenT) serves as the contemporary framework. Level I [2]

3.1 The ROAST Consensus (2020–2025 Updates)

The ROAST framework was developed to standardize the clinical assessment of acute LAS, ensuring clinicians capture the multifaceted nature of the injury. It moves beyond simple ligament grading to identify specific mechanical and sensorimotor impairments. [2]

Core Components of ROAST:

  1. Injury Mechanism: Confirming inversion/supination vs. external rotation (syndesmosis)
  2. Pain Assessment: Quantifying pain intensity during activity and rest
  3. Swelling/Edema: Measuring the magnitude of inflammatory response (e.g., figure-of-eight measurement)
  4. Structural Integrity: Application of Ottawa Rules and ligament stress tests
  5. Function: Assessment of ankle ROM (specifically dorsiflexion) and arthrokinematics
  6. Sensorimotor Control: Static (Single Leg Stance) and dynamic balance assessments

3.2 The Ottawa Ankle Rules (OAR)

The OAR remain the gold standard clinical decision tool for determining the necessity of radiographic imaging. Their implementation is critical for reducing unnecessary radiation exposure and healthcare costs. Level I [15]

OAR Diagnostic Performance

Sensitivity: 99–100% | Negative LR: ~0.15 — A negative OAR finding virtually excludes the presence of a fracture. Specificity ranges from 13% to 50%; a positive finding indicates the need for X-ray, not confirmation of fracture. [15, 16]

Radiography of the ankle is indicated only if there is pain in the malleolar zone AND any one of the following:

  • Bone tenderness at the posterior edge or tip of the lateral malleolus (distal 6 cm)
  • Bone tenderness at the posterior edge or tip of the medial malleolus (distal 6 cm)
  • Inability to bear weight both immediately after the injury and in the emergency department (inability to take four complete steps) [18]

Similarly, foot X-rays are indicated if there is pain in the midfoot zone AND bone tenderness at the base of the 5th metatarsal or navicular, or inability to bear weight. [18]

3.3 Diagnosis of Syndesmotic (High Ankle) Injury

Injuries to the distal tibiofibular syndesmosis are notoriously difficult to diagnose and are often missed. They require a high index of suspicion, particularly in athletes involved in collision sports or those describing an external rotation mechanism. Level II [12]

Current evidence suggests that no single physical examination test has sufficient diagnostic accuracy to rule in or rule out a syndesmotic injury in isolation. Instead, a "cluster" approach is recommended. Level I [20]

Recommended Diagnostic Strategy

Initial screening with highly sensitive tests (palpation, dorsiflexion lunge) → if positive, highly specific tests (Squeeze test) to confirm → if equivocal, advanced imaging is warranted. [20]

3.4 Indications for MRI

Routine use of MRI for acute ankle sprains is discouraged. However, the ACR and AAOS identify specific "red flag" indications where MRI is usually appropriate: [18, 22]

  1. Diagnostic Uncertainty: Severe swelling prevents reliable physical examination, with suspicion of complex injury
  2. Suspected Syndesmotic Injury: MRI demonstrates 100% sensitivity and 93% specificity for AITFL tears Level II [24]
  3. Occult Pathology: Suspicion of osteochondral lesions (OLT), peroneal tendon dislocation/tear, or Lisfranc injury
  4. Recalcitrant Symptoms: Persistent pain, swelling, or instability failing to resolve after 6–8 weeks of appropriate conservative management [22]
  5. High-Demand Populations: Elite athletes—MRI may be used early to grade Grade III rupture for decision-making regarding surgical repair vs. accelerated rehabilitation [23]

4. Acute Management: The Paradigm Shift to PEACE & LOVE

One of the most significant developments in musculoskeletal medicine between 2020 and 2025 is the critical re-evaluation of the RICE protocol. While deeply ingrained in first-aid culture, the physiological rationale for RICE—specifically the aggressive use of cryotherapy and rest—has been challenged by emerging evidence supporting the biological necessity of inflammation. Level II [25]

4.1 Limitations of the RICE Protocol

⚠ Key Concerns with RICE
  • Cryotherapy: Aggressive cooling causes vasoconstriction, potentially inhibiting infiltration of neutrophils and macrophages essential for phagocytosis and release of IGF-1 (primary driver of collagen synthesis). [25]
  • Rest: Complete immobilization leads to rapid muscle atrophy, cartilage degradation, and adverse viscoelastic changes in ligaments.
  • Evidence Gap: Systematic reviews note a distinct lack of high-quality RCTs confirming that RICE improves long-term functional outcomes compared to active recovery strategies. Not specified in source [27]

4.2 The POLICE Protocol (Transitional Framework)

POLICE (Protection, Optimal Loading, Ice, Compression, Elevation) replaced "Rest" with "Optimal Loading"—promoting mechanical stress to stimulate mechanotransduction, promoting collagen fiber alignment and enhancing tensile strength of the healing ligament. Studies comparing POLICE to PRICE demonstrated significantly better functional scores (AOFAS, FADI) and faster edema resolution at 14 days post-injury. Level II [29, 30]

4.3 The PEACE & LOVE Protocol (Current Gold Standard)

The current gold standard, widely advocated in the 2020–2025 literature, is the PEACE & LOVE acronym. This comprehensive framework addresses the immediate acute phase (PEACE) and the subsequent subacute/remodeling phase (LOVE). Level I [25, 29]

PEACE — Immediate Care (Days 1–3)

P

Protect

Unload or restrict movement for 1–3 days to minimize bleeding and prevent distension of injured fibers. Use removable brace rather than absolute immobilization.

E

Elevate

Elevate the limb above heart level to promote interstitial fluid drainage and reduce hydrostatic pressure. Risk-to-benefit ratio remains favorable despite weak evidence.

A

Avoid Anti-inflammatories

NSAIDs inhibit COX enzymes required for prostaglandin production necessary for tissue repair. Use simple analgesics (acetaminophen) unless NSAIDs are strictly necessary.

C

Compress

External mechanical pressure (taping or elastic bandages) limits intra-articular edema and tissue hemorrhage.

E

Educate

Educate patients on active recovery benefits; set realistic expectations to minimize the "nocebo" effect and reduce reliance on passive modalities.

LOVE — Subacute Management (Day 4+)

L

Load

Active approach with movement and exercise. Mechanical stress added early; normal activities resumed as symptoms allow to build tissue tolerance.

O

Optimism

Psychological factors (catastrophization, depression, fear-avoidance) are strong predictors of poor outcomes. Encourage optimism to improve adherence and prognosis.

V

Vascularization

Pain-free aerobic exercise (e.g., stationary cycling) started early to increase blood flow, supporting metabolism and repair of injured structures.

E

Exercise

Structured program addressing strength, mobility, and proprioception is critical to restore function and prevent recurrence.

5. Conservative Management vs. Surgical Intervention

5.1 Immobilization vs. Early Functional Mobilization

Guidelines from NATA, AAOS, and JOSPT overwhelmingly support functional rehabilitation over rigid immobilization for Grade I and II sprains. Functional rehabilitation uses external supports (lace-up braces, semi-rigid orthoses, or taping) allowing early weight-bearing and controlled ROM. Level I [19]

Evidence Summary

Systematic reviews confirm functional treatment results in superior outcomes compared to cast immobilization: faster return to sport, quicker return to work, less persistent swelling, and greater ROM. Level I [6]

Grade III Sprains: Management is more nuanced. Current consensus suggests a short period of immobilization (e.g., 10 days in a cast or rigid walking boot) may benefit initial scar tissue formation. However, this must be immediately followed by functional rehabilitation. Prolonged immobilization (>4 weeks) is contraindicated as it leads to stiffness and weakness without improving ligament stability. [22]

5.2 The Role of Manual Therapy

Manual therapy is strongly recommended as an adjunct to exercise therapy. Techniques include talocrural posterior glide mobilizations (to improve dorsiflexion) and subtalar joint mobilizations. Acute sprains often result in an anterior positional fault of the distal fibula or talus restriction; manual therapy corrects these arthrokinematic faults, rapidly improving dorsiflexion ROM and reducing pain. Level I [1]

The JOSPT 2021 guidelines assign a high grade of recommendation to manual therapy combined with exercise, noting it is superior to exercise alone for reducing pain and improving function in the acute phase. [1]

6. Rehabilitation Progression & Timelines

Effective rehabilitation follows a criterion-based progression rather than strict time-based protocols. General physiological timelines provide a framework. Level II [19]

🔹 Phase 1: Acute / Protected Motion
Week 0–2

Goal: Control pain and edema, protect healing tissue, prevent muscle atrophy

  • PEACE Protocol: Protection (brace/tape), Compression, Elevation
  • Gait: Weight-bearing as tolerated (WBAT), potentially with crutches, progressing to full weight-bearing
  • ROM: Active ROM in pain-free ranges (ankle alphabet, towel scrunches). Avoid end-range inversion
  • Muscle Activation: Isometric strengthening of peroneals, tibialis anterior, and calf complex
🔹 Phase 2: Impairment Correction & Strengthening
Week 2–4

Goal: Restore full ROM, normalize gait, restore muscle strength

  • ROM: Joint mobilizations (Mulligan MWMs) to restore dorsiflexion; calf stretching (gastroc/soleus)
  • Strength: Progressive resistance exercises (bands/weights) for inversion, eversion, dorsiflexion, and plantarflexion. Emphasis on eccentric peroneal strengthening
  • Proprioception: Static balance training (Single Leg Stance on firm → foam surface; eyes open → eyes closed)
🔹 Phase 3: Functional Training & Neuromuscular Control
Week 4–8+

Goal: Restore dynamic stability, power, and sport-specific skills

  • Dynamic Balance: Star Excursion Balance Test (SEBT) training, targeting posteromedial and posterolateral reach directions
  • Plyometrics: Double leg hopping → Single leg hopping → Multi-directional hopping
  • Agility: Figure-of-8 runs, T-Test drills, cutting maneuvers
  • Sport-Specific: Drills mimicking the metabolic and mechanical demands of the patient's sport [35]

7. Return to Sport: Criterion-Based Decision Making

Historically, RTS decisions were subjective, based on time since injury or absence of pain. The 2020–2025 literature strongly advocates for criterion-based RTS testing to reduce the high rate of recurrence. Level I [37]

7.1 The PAASS Framework

To standardize the RTS decision-making process, an international multidisciplinary consensus developed the PAASS framework. [37]

Table 3 — The PAASS Framework for Return-to-Sport [37]
DomainComponents for Assessment
P — PainPain severity during sport-specific activity and over the last 24 hours
A — Ankle ImpairmentsROM (weight-bearing dorsiflexion), muscle strength, endurance, and power
A — Athlete PerceptionPsychological readiness (confidence) and perceived stability (e.g., ALR-RSI scale)
S — Sensorimotor ControlProprioception and dynamic postural control (e.g., SEBT)
S — Sport/Functional PerformanceHopping, jumping, agility testing, ability to complete a full training session

7.2 The Ankle-GO Score

A major advancement is the validation of the Ankle-GO score (2023–2024), a composite scoring system designed to objectively quantify functional recovery and predict the risk of CAI. Level II [38, 40]

Components of the Ankle-GO Score (Max 25 Points):

  1. Single Leg Stance (SLS): Static balance on firm surface, eyes closed
  2. Modified Star Excursion Balance Test (mSEBT): Anterior, Posteromedial, and Posterolateral reach distances
  3. Side Hop Test (SHT): Lateral ankle stability and endurance (reps in 30s)
  4. Figure-of-8 Test (F8T): Agility and speed assessment
  5. FAAM: Patient-reported outcome measure for ADLs and sport
  6. ALR-RSI: Scale measuring psychological readiness and fear of reinjury
Table 4 — Ankle-GO Score Interpretation [36, 38, 39]
Score RangeRisk CategoryClinical Implication
< 8 Points High Risk Patients scoring below 8 at 2 months post-injury are unlikely to return to pre-injury sport level
8–11 Points Moderate Risk Continued rehabilitation required; monitor for development of CAI features
> 11 Points "Coper" Status Significantly more likely to become "copers"—full recovery without chronic instability or recurrent sprains

7.3 Functional Performance Benchmarks

In addition to the Ankle-GO score, specific quantitative benchmarks using the Limb Symmetry Index (LSI) are recommended. The LSI compares injured limb to uninjured contralateral limb. Level II [36]

Recommended RTS Criteria:

  • Strength: Isokinetic or handheld dynamometry for inversion/eversion → LSI > 90%
  • Hop Tests: Single Leg Hop for Distance and Side Hop Test → LSI > 90%
  • Vertical Jump: LSI > 90%
  • Psychological Readiness: Tampa Scale of Kinesiophobia (TSK-11) or ALR-RSI indicating high confidence and low fear [37]

8.2 The NSAID Controversy

A notable point of divergence involves the use of NSAIDs:

  • Traditional View (NATA/AAOS): Supports NSAIDs for 3–7 days to reduce pain and facilitate early mobilization, citing Grade A evidence for short-term functional improvement. Level I [32]
  • Physiological View (PEACE & LOVE): Discourages NSAID use due to prostaglandin inhibition necessary for collagen synthesis. While NSAIDs improve short-term pain, they may compromise mechanical quality of healed ligament. Level II [25]
  • Clinical Synthesis: Use NSAIDs judiciously. If pain is the limiting factor preventing mobilization, a short course (2–3 days) is acceptable. They should not be used prophylactically or for extended durations. Acetaminophen is the preferred first-line analgesic.

9. Prevention & Long-Term Health

Given the 40% chronicity rate, prevention is as critical as treatment. NATA guidelines highlight that secondary prevention (preventing recurrence in a previously injured ankle) is highly effective. Level I [32, 33]

9.1 Neuromuscular Training

There is strong (Grade A) evidence that completing a 3-month neuromuscular training program (balance boards, plyometrics) significantly reduces the risk of recurrent sprains. This effect is more pronounced in athletes with a history of sprain than in those without. [32]

9.2 Prophylactic Bracing and Taping

NATA recommends the use of lace-up braces or taping for athletes with a history of LAS when returning to sport. Evidence suggests bracing reduces the incidence of acute sprains but does not necessarily reduce severity if a sprain occurs. Crucially, there is no evidence that prolonged bracing weakens the ankle musculature, debunking a common myth. Level I [4]
