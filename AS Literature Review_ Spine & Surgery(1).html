Comprehensive Evidence-Based Synthesis: Management of Ankylosing Spondylitis

From Pharmacological Advances to Complex Orthopedic Reconstructions — Literature Review & Interactive Clinical Decision Support

1. Introduction to the Ankylosing Spondylitis Paradigm

Ankylosing Spondylitis (AS), the archetypal form of radiographic axial spondyloarthritis (axSpA), is a chronic immune-mediated inflammatory disease predominantly targeting the sacroiliac joints and axial skeleton. Its pathophysiology involves a complex interplay of genetic predisposition and aberrant cytokine signaling.

Genetic & Immunological Foundations

  • HLA-B27 is present in approximately 90–95% of affected patients Level II
  • Key pathogenic cytokines: TNF-α, IL-17, and IL-23 — driving systemic symptoms and localized joint destruction Level II

Pathophysiological Continuum

The disease oscillates between two destructive extremes: profound bone loss (osteoporosis) and aggressive bone formation (osteoproliferation). Initiation occurs as enthesitis and osteitis, progressing to pathological new bone formation with calcification of paraspinal ligaments, intervertebral discs, and facet joints — culminating in the classic "bamboo spine." Level IV

Clinical Paradox The remodeled, rigid "bamboo spine" is intensely osteoporotic and biomechanically fragile. It loses all segmental motion, acting as a long, continuous, brittle lever arm incapable of dissipating routine mechanical forces.

Two Major Clinical Trajectories Requiring Intervention

  • Acute Mechanical Failure: Exceedingly susceptible to unstable three-column fractures following trivial, low-energy trauma (e.g., ground-level fall). Most commonly at the cervical spine (C5–C7), frequently complicated by catastrophic neurological deficits. Level II
  • Chronic Structural Deformity: Severe fixed chin-on-chest thoracolumbar kyphosis and complete bony ankylosis of peripheral joints (especially the hip), necessitating complex orthopedic reconstructions. Level III

2. Surgical Management of Cervical Spine Fractures in AS

2.1 Pathomechanics, Epidemiology & Diagnostic Imperatives

Vertebral fractures affect approximately 10–15% of AS patients — a seven-fold increased risk versus healthy, age-matched individuals. Level II The cervical spine (C5–C7) and cervicothoracic junction are the most vulnerable sites, due to transition zone biomechanics where the mobile cervical spine meets the rigid, kyphotic thoracic spine. Level IV

  • Fractures are characteristically transcylindrical/transvertebral, universally involving all three columns — highly unstable (AO Type B3 or C) Level IV
  • Up to 26.5% of AS patients with cervical fractures present with concomitant Spinal Cord Injury (SCI) Level II
  • Short-term mortality rates range from 24.5% to 44.7% Level II
  • Delayed diagnosis is common — acute fracture pain frequently misattributed to inflammatory flares Level IV
Diagnostic Standard Whole-spine CT is the gold standard for fracture morphology. MRI is critical for identifying occult fractures, epidural hematomas, and assessing cord integrity prior to surgery.

2.2 Surgical Approach Selection

Non-surgical management with rigid orthoses is now considered contraindicated for three-column AS fractures due to unacceptable rates of non-union, progressive deformity, and fatal complications. Level II Surgical stabilization is universally imperative.

Anterior-Only Approach Level II

  • Indications: Stable fractures with minimal displacement (translation ≤ 2 mm), intact posterior tension band and posterior hinge
  • Advantages: Shortest operative time (mean ~81.4 min), lowest blood loss (mean ~87.5 mL), allows supine positioning avoiding perilous prone flip
  • Risks: Highest rate of late re-operations due to hardware failure and screw pull-out from osteoporotic bone

Posterior-Only Approach (Current Workhorse) Level II

  • Indications: Highly unstable fractures, severe kyphotic deformities, fractures at cervicothoracic junction requiring deep thoracic extension
  • Advantages: Superior mechanical stability against leverage forces, enables wide multi-level laminectomies for direct decompression, moderate operative time (mean ~124 min), blood loss ~714.7 mL; MIS percutaneous techniques increasingly utilized
  • Risks: Higher wound infection risk; prone positioning in rigid hyperkyphotic deformity is exceptionally dangerous, requiring awake fiberoptic intubation

Combined Anteroposterior (360-Degree) Approach Level II

  • Indications: Catastrophic, highly displaced fractures (translation > 3.7 mm), profound anterior column comminution, severe instability, pre-existing pseudoarthrosis (Andersson lesions)
  • Advantages: Absolute highest biomechanical stability, maximally reduces ROM across fracture site in all planes
  • Risks: Longest operative time (mean ~266.6 min), highest blood loss (mean ~912.7 mL), complication rate ~22.6% vs 7.5% for posterior-only — predominantly cardiopulmonary (pneumonia, ARDS, PE)

2.3 Long-Segment Fixation: The Absolute Standard

Standard short-segment fixation is destined for catastrophic mechanical failure in the ankylosed spine. The definitive standard of care mandates long-segment posterior fixation — instrumentation extending a minimum of two healthy vertebral levels above and two below the fracture site. Level II

  • Proximal anchorage: C2 pars/pedicle screws
  • Distal anchorage: Extended to C7 and upper thoracic (T1–T3) pedicle screws for rigid thoracic purchase Level IV
  • Cement augmentation: Polymethylmethacrylate-augmented pedicle screws increasingly utilized in severe osteoporosis to enhance pull-out strength Level IV

2.4 Mortality Paradox & Neurological Outcomes

Selection Bias Explained Raw data shows higher crude mortality in the posterior-only cohort (up to 24.5%) vs. combined (9.2%). This reflects confounding by indication: elderly, frail patients with severe cardiopulmonary disease are intentionally shunted to posterior-only approaches, while younger, robust patients receive combined approaches. The elevated mortality reflects baseline frailty, not failure of the posterior technique.

Neurological recovery shows no statistically significant difference across anterior, posterior, or combined cohorts. The primary determinant is rapidity of decompression and severity of initial cord insult — not the surgical trajectory. Level II

3. Pharmacological Advances in Early to Mid-Stage AS

The therapeutic armamentarium has evolved far beyond NSAIDs and csDMARDs (sulfasalazine, methotrexate — effective only for peripheral disease). The 2024/2025 EULAR/ASAS and BSR guidelines establish a clear algorithm prioritizing TNFi, IL-17i, and JAKi for persistently high disease activity. Level I

3.1 TNF-α Inhibitors (TNFi) — The Gold Standard First-Line

  • Agents: Adalimumab, infliximab, golimumab (monoclonal antibodies); etanercept (receptor fusion protein); certolizumab pegol (PEGylated)
  • Efficacy: Bayesian network meta-analyses confirm TNFi remain statistically superior to both IL-17i and JAKi in achieving rapid, profound disease control (ASAS20/ASAS40). Ideal for patients with rapid structural progression, elevated CRP, and severe functional limitations. Level I
  • Extra-musculoskeletal benefit: Highly effective for concomitant Inflammatory Bowel Disease (IBD)

3.2 Interleukin-17 Inhibitors (IL-17i)

The IL-23/IL-17 axis is the second critical independent pathway, particularly implicated in enthesitis and pathological osteoproliferation.

  • First-generation: Secukinumab, ixekizumab — selectively neutralize IL-17A only
  • Bimekizumab (dual IL-17A/F inhibitor): A profound pharmacological advance — synergistically suppresses inflammation to a significantly greater extent than IL-17A blockade alone Level I

BE MOBILE Trials (Bimekizumab) Level I

  • At 52 weeks: ~60% of axSpA patients achieved ASAS40 response
  • Significant improvements in morning stiffness, chronic fatigue, and axial pain
  • Long-term (3-year) extension data: MRI remission in >57%, sustained clinical remission in both biologic-naïve and TNFi-experienced patients
Treatment Sequencing Insight Retrospective cohort data (Korean NHIS) indicate that patients experiencing primary failure to a first-line TNFi exhibit significantly better long-term drug retention when switched to an IL-17i, rather than cycling to a second TNFi.

3.3 Janus Kinase (JAK) Inhibitors — The Oral Paradigm

  • Agents: Upadacitinib, tofacitinib, filgotinib — daily oral pill (dramatically improving compliance)
  • Mechanism: Intracellularly block the JAK/STAT pathway, simultaneously inhibiting signal transduction for >60 inflammatory cytokines. Strongly inhibit γδ T cells, ILC3, and MAIT cells upon which the IL-23/IL-17 axis relies Level II

SELECT-AXIS 2 Trials (Upadacitinib) Level I

  • Statistically significant improvements in disease activity, physical function, and MRI-detected spinal inflammation within 14 weeks
  • Robustly sustained over 2-year follow-up with acceptable safety profile — no significant MACE increase in axSpA cohort
  • Proven efficacy in bDMARD-refractory (bDMARD-IR) populations

3.4 Modern Treatment Sequencing (2024/2025 Guidelines)

  • Treat-to-Target (T2T) nuance (BSR): Caution against strictly treating to index-based targets — aggressive rapid cycling through the limited three effective classes can prematurely exhaust lifetime therapeutic options. Elevated scores may be falsely inflated by structural damage or fibromyalgia. Level I
  • Tapering vs. Withdrawal: For sustained remission (>6 months), dose tapering is recommended. Complete withdrawal is explicitly not recommended — almost universally results in severe, rapid flares recalcitrant to re-initiation. Level I
  • Comorbidity-driven selection:
    • Active IBD: Prefer monoclonal TNFi or JAKi; IL-17i contraindicated (lacks gut efficacy, can exacerbate IBD)
    • Severe psoriasis: Prioritize IL-17i

4. Orthopedic Surgical Interventions for Late-Stage AS

4.1 Spinal Osteotomy Techniques for Kyphosis Correction

End-stage AS results in severe, rigid thoracolumbar kyphosis — the devastating chin-on-chest deformity — restricting forward vision, compromising cardiopulmonary capacity, precluding supine positioning, and causing profound insomnia and psychological distress. Level IV

Two-Level Modified PSO Level III

For exceptionally severe round kyphosis requiring correction >60°, a single-level PSO is insufficient and risks cord kinking, foraminal stenosis, and vascular injury. The Two-Level Modified PSO distributes correction across two discrete levels (e.g., one thoracic + one lumbar), safely achieving total sagittal corrections averaging 69 degrees.

Predictive Thresholds for Two-Level PSO Preoperative Chin-Brow Vertical Angle (CBVA) > 63.9° and Global Kyphosis (GK) > 91.1° serve as independent predictors that a single osteotomy will fail. Level III

Surgical burden: Operative times average 550 ± 155.2 min (vs. 371.2 ± 90.8 min for single-level), requiring meticulous multidisciplinary perioperative management including continuous neuro-monitoring (SSEP/MMEP), controlled hypotension, and advanced blood resuscitation. Level III

4.2 Total Hip Arthroplasty (THA) in the Ankylosed Hip

Between 30–50% of AS patients experience aggressive chronic hip synovitis, and up to 25% progress to complete bilateral bony ankylosis — frequently presenting in their 30s–40s with hips fused in fixed flexion contracture. Level IV

The Spinopelvic Dilemma

The "bamboo spine" locks the pelvis in a fixed position. Because the pelvis cannot dynamically retrovert during sitting, the prosthetic femoral neck violently impinges against the anterior acetabular rim, forcefully levering the femoral head out of the socket. Level III

Dual Mobility (DM) Cups — Transformative Paradigm Shift Level II

  • Mechanism: "Bearing-within-a-bearing" design — a large polyethylene liner articulates freely within a fixed metal shell while capturing a smaller inner femoral head, exponentially increasing jump distance and allowing supraphysiologic ROM
  • Outcomes: Drives postoperative dislocation rate from 4–7% (standard implants) to 0.0–0.3%; significantly faster early functional recovery at 3–6 months Level II

Heterotopic Ossification (HO) Risk

HO occurs in approximately 15.11% of AS patients post-THA — significantly higher than in osteoarthritic populations. Severe HO (Brooker III–IV) can bridge femur to pelvis, causing complete re-ankylosis. Aggressive prophylaxis (localized radiation or prolonged indomethacin) is mandatory. Level II

Robotic-Assisted THA (RA-THA) Level II

  • Utilizes preoperative 3D CT mapping and intraoperative haptic feedback for precise patient-specific acetabular preparation
  • RA-THA adoption in the US: growing from 1.2% to 6.7% of all THA cases
  • Matched cohorts demonstrate significantly lower DVT risk, fewer mechanical complications, and shorter hospital stay vs. conventional THA Level II

6. Conclusion

The comprehensive management of Ankylosing Spondylitis requires a deeply integrated, multidisciplinary approach spanning the entire disease chronology:

  • Early/Mid-Stage: Pharmacological mastery to halt axial fusion. TNFi remain the gold standard; dual IL-17A/F inhibitors (bimekizumab) and oral JAK inhibitors (upadacitinib) provide powerful alternatives sustaining deep multi-year remission. Modern guidelines prioritize individualized therapy over rigid step-wise algorithms, endorse sustained dose tapering, and explicitly discourage complete withdrawal.
  • Acute Cervical Fractures: Long-segment posterior fixation is the definitive procedural workhorse. Combined 360° approaches are reserved for the most catastrophic fractures in patients with sufficient physiological reserve.
  • End-Stage Deformity: Two-Level Modified PSOs safely distribute the correction forces of extreme kyphosis. Dual Mobility cups with Robotic-Assisted 3D navigation for fused hips dramatically mitigate dislocation and complication risks.

By deeply acknowledging the unique spinopelvic kinematics and brittle biomechanics inherent to AS, clinicians and surgeons can safely execute these transformative interventions, restoring function, mobility, and dignity to the patient.
