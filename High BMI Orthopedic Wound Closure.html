1. The Challenge of Wound Healing in High-BMI Orthopedic Patients

1.1 Epidemiology of Obesity in the Orthopedic Population

The escalating global prevalence of obesity (BMI ≥ 30 kg/m²) has profound implications for musculoskeletal health and orthopedic surgery practice. Adiposity exerts a direct and deleterious effect on the musculoskeletal system, contributing to the pathogenesis of numerous conditions, most notably osteoarthritis.Level I [1,2]

This creates a challenging clinical paradox: the patient population with the highest demand for major reconstructive procedures such as total joint arthroplasty (TJA) is concurrently at the greatest risk for devastating postoperative complications. While institutions such as the AAOS have historically recommended a BMI threshold of < 40 kg/m² for elective procedures, this approach is increasingly recognized as insufficient.Level III [4]

⚖️ Clinical Paradox Weight reduction programs have limited long-term success for many patients. Withholding life-altering procedures from those in greatest need presents a significant ethical and clinical dilemma. The focus must evolve towards developing specialized techniques that safely accommodate this growing demographic.

1.3 Economic and Clinical Burden

Wound complications in this population represent a significant source of morbidity and economic burden:Expert Opinion [4,6]

  • Increased length of hospital stay and unplanned readmissions
  • Greater need for subsequent surgical interventions
  • PJI following TJA increases one-year mortality risk
  • Financial costs from prolonged hospitalization, imaging, antibiotics, and revision surgeries
  • Many complications are potentially preventable through optimized technique and perioperative management

2. Pathophysiological Basis for Increased Wound Complications

🧬 The Pathophysiological Triad The increased risk is attributable to three interrelated factors: compromised biology, adverse mechanical forces, and heightened bacterial challenge. An effective closure technique must be multi-modal, designed to simultaneously address all three components.

2.1 Unique Biology of Adipose Tissue

Subcutaneous adipose tissue in obese individuals presents a fundamentally challenging surgical environment:Expert Opinion [9,11]

  • Poor vascularity → local tissue hypoxia → impaired fibroblast proliferation and collagen synthesis
  • Impaired immune cell function → diminished capacity to combat bacterial contamination
  • Mechanical fragility → tissue lacks inherent tensile strength; prone to "cutting-through" with sutures
  • Fat necrosis risk → sutures under tension cause ischemia → necrotic tissue serves as avascular nidus for bacteria

2.2 The Pro-Inflammatory State

Modern understanding recognizes adipose tissue as a dynamic endocrine organ producing bioactive signaling molecules (adipokines).Expert Review [13]

  • In obesity: pro-inflammatory adipokines upregulated (leptin, resistin) and anti-inflammatory adipokines downregulated (adiponectin)
  • Creates chronic, low-grade, systemic pro-inflammatory state
  • Prolongs and amplifies the initial inflammatory phase of wound healing
  • Leads to excessive protease activity, degradation of extracellular matrix, impaired proliferative phase
  • Often compounded by comorbidities such as type 2 diabetes mellitus

2.3 Mechanical Factors: Dead Space and Wound Tension

The surgical challenges are profoundly mechanical:Expert Opinion [6,11,12]

  • Dead space: Surgical incision through thick fat creates substantial potential space between deep fascia and dermis. This space fills with serous fluid or blood, creating an ideal culture medium for bacteria.
  • Wound tension: Heavy adipose tissue flaps exert constant tensile force on the suture line, compromising blood supply to skin edges, causing ischemia, necrosis, and dehiscence.
⚠️ Vicious Cycle High tension exacerbates poor perfusion → fluid collections in un-obliterated dead space → bacteria overwhelm locally compromised immune response → infection and wound failure.

3. Foundational Principles of Multilayer Wound Closure

3.1 Meticulous Tissue Handling and Hemostasis

Gentle tissue handling is paramount in obese patients. Rough handling, excessive retraction, or indiscriminate electrocautery damages the already compromised vasculature, extending the zone of tissue necrosis.Expert Consensus [6]

Careful and complete hemostasis is a non-negotiable prerequisite — failure to control bleeding directly leads to hematoma formation, a primary precursor to dehiscence and SSI.

3.2 Layer-by-Layer Anatomic Restoration

Wound closure must restore normal anatomic planes: deep fascia → subcutaneous layers → dermis as distinct structures.Expert Consensus [6] This re-establishes structural integrity, allows appropriate tissue gliding, and contributes to cosmetically acceptable scarring.

3.3 Tension Management and Dead Space Obliteration

These are the most critical mechanical goals in high-BMI surgery:Expert Consensus [6]

  • Watertight closure leaving no potential space for fluid accumulation
  • Deeper, stronger tissues bear the tension of closure, allowing superficial layers to be gently approximated
📊 Practice Gap Identified A national survey of orthopedic surgeons in Spain revealed: 87% affirmed wound closure impacts outcomes, yet there was no consensus on the best method. Closure was performed by a resident in ~50% of cases. The most common technique was simple interrupted Vicryl — a significant disconnect between accepted principles and actual practice.Level II [7]

4. Evidence-Based Appraisal of Subcutaneous Fat Closure

4.1 Rationale for Subcutaneous Suturing

The primary rationale is mechanical: obliterate dead space to prevent seroma/hematoma formation and reduce wound breakdown and infection risk.Level I [12]

A randomized study in elective laparotomy demonstrated that subcutaneous sutures significantly reduced superficial SSI, hematoma, seroma, and total wound dehiscence vs. no subcutaneous closure.Level I [12]

Counterargument: Sutures through poorly perfused tissue can cause further ischemic injury, fat necrosis, and potentially increase complication risk through foreign body reaction.Level I [12]

4.2 Evidence from Obstetrics — The Landmark Meta-Analysis

🏆 Pivotal Finding (Evidence Level I) A landmark meta-analysis of RCTs in cesarean delivery found that for patients with subcutaneous fat thickness > 2 cm, active suture closure resulted in a 34% decrease in wound disruption risk (composite: infection, hematoma, seroma, separation). This was driven largely by significant reduction in seroma formation. For fat thickness ≤ 2 cm, suture closure provided no clinical benefit.[20]

4.3 The Critical Threshold: When Is Closure Indicated?

Based on strong analogous evidence from obstetrics, routine suture closure of the subcutaneous layer should be the standard of care when subcutaneous fat depth exceeds 2 cm (measured from deep fascia to dermis).Level I [20]

📐 Number Needed to Treat (NNT) = 16.2 A surgeon would need to close the subcutaneous layer in approximately 16 patients with > 2 cm of fat to prevent one significant wound disruption. This powerful statistic underscores the clinical effectiveness of the intervention.[20]

5. Suture Techniques for Subcutaneous Fat Closure

5.1 Conventional Approaches

Interrupted Suturing

Point-by-point approximation with individual knots. An Egyptian study in obese women undergoing cesarean section found interrupted technique superior to continuous technique, with reductions in wound gapping, seroma, erythema, and infection.Level II [21]

Continuous Suturing

Running stitch approximates tissue along the incision length. Provides continuous line of closure but can create a "purse-string" effect potentially compromising perfusion. Evidence is conflicting — some studies show lower SSI rates than interrupted technique.Level III [22]

5.2 Barbed Sutures

Feature tissue-anchoring barbs for knotless fixation. Proposed benefits: faster closure, even tension distribution, more watertight seal.Level II [2]

⚠️ Caution — Level I Evidence of Harm A prospective RCT by Matzkin et al. in TKA/THA found barbed sutures decreased closure time by 9.7 minutes and saved $549/case, BUT were associated with increased frequency and severity of wound complications. A plastic surgery review also found higher wound separation rates.Level I [25,28]

5.3 Quilting Sutures — A Novel Application for Orthopedics

Also known as progressive tension sutures, this technique from plastic/reconstructive surgery directly addresses both dead space and tension:Level II [29,30]

  • Multiple spaced absorbable sutures tack the subcutaneous flap to the underlying deep fascia
  • Completely obliterates dead space by physically adhering tissue planes
  • Distributes tensile forces across entire flap surface, away from skin incision
  • Consistently shown to significantly reduce seroma in abdominoplasty and mastectomy
  • High BMI is an independent risk factor for seroma — quilting is a significant protective factor in this populationLevel II [31]
🔬 Critical Finding The complete absence of quilting sutures in orthopedic literature represents a major opportunity for practice improvement. The mechanical principles are directly applicable to high-BMI orthopedic wound closure, particularly in TJA and spine surgery. The orthopedic community should adopt and formally study this technique.

6. Advanced Tension-Reducing Suture Methodologies

6.1 Retention Sutures for High-Tension Wounds (TKA)

Retention suturing provides a powerful external support system to reinforce primary wound closure. The technique uses heavy, non-absorbable monofilament suture (#1 Ethilon) on a straight needle with "far-far" bites passing deep through subcutaneous tissue. Bolsters (sterile infant feeding tube or rubber tubing) are placed under suture loops to prevent skin cutting.Case Series [11]

📈 Remarkable Results In a study of 27 obese TKA patients: zero instances of wound dehiscence, persistent discharge, localized skin necrosis, or suture cut-through over 14-month follow-up. This technique directly counteracts the tendency of sutures to necrose fat under pressure.Level III [11]

6.2 Multilayer Mattress Stitches for Complex Closures (Spine)

For deep wounds in posterior spine surgery, the multilayer mattress stitch achieves synergistic closure of deep and superficial layers with a single robust suture:Level III [33]

  • Thick monofilament suture passed in vertical mattress fashion incorporating full tissue thickness
  • Introduced ~4 cm from wound margin, passes through skin, entire subcutaneous fat, and takes a bite of paravertebral muscle/fascia
  • Reversed for a smaller superficial bite closer to wound edge
  • Silicone drain often used as bolster under superficial loop
  • Successfully used in patients with BMI > 36.4 kg/m² [33]

Force is effectively redirected from skin to deep musculofascial layer — a powerful tool for stable, tension-free closure in deep spinal wounds.

7.2 Absorption Profile

Wound healing in obese patients may be delayed due to poor vascularity and chronic inflammation. Sutures must maintain strength for a sufficient duration:Expert Opinion [34]

  • Rapidly absorbable (e.g., Monocryl) — may lose tensile strength too quickly for slow-healing patients
  • Slowly absorbable (e.g., PDS) — retain strength longer; preferred for deep, tension-bearing layers
  • For subcutaneous layer: a suture with prolonged strength retention is theoretically advantageous

7.3 Antimicrobial-Coated Sutures

The use of triclosan-coated sutures over non-antimicrobial-coated sutures is identified by an expert panel as a key intervention associated with lower wound complication and SSI rates.Expert Consensus [8] Local elution creates a zone of inhibition preventing bacterial adherence during the vulnerable post-closure period.

✅ Material Recommendation The optimal choice for subcutaneous closure in high-BMI patients is likely a slowly absorbable, antimicrobial-coated monofilament suture (e.g., triclosan-coated PDS), addressing both biological and mechanical challenges.

8. Adjunctive Therapies for High-Risk Wound Management

8.1 Prophylactic Incisional Negative Pressure Wound Therapy (NPWT)

NPWT involves a sealed dressing over the closed incision connected to a vacuum pump applying sub-atmospheric pressure. Mechanisms include:Expert Review [38,39]

  • Active removal of excess fluid and inflammatory exudate
  • Reduction of edema; prevention of seroma/hematoma
  • Increased local blood flow and tissue oxygenation
  • Mechanical splinting of wound edges; protection from external contamination
📊 Compelling Evidence — 360 Obese TKA Patients NPWT group vs. standard dressing:
• Wound dehiscence: 3.33% vs. 9.52% (50% reduction)
• Overall SSI: 11.33% vs. 25.24% (more than halved)
• Deep PJI: 4.0% vs. 10.0%
• Significantly fewer returns to OR for wound-related reasons
• Primary adverse effect: increased skin blistering (manageable)Level II [39]

8.2 The Controversial Role of Subcutaneous Drains

Evidence is highly conflicting and context-dependent:Level I–III

  • Supportive (abdominal/spine): Reduces seroma in obese abdominal surgery [49]; may reduce SSI in obese lumbar discectomy [51]
  • Against (TJA): Systematic review found drains do NOT reduce hematoma in arthroplasty; associated with increased blood loss, transfusion requirements, and potential retrograde bacterial conduit [52]
📋 Evidence Gap The powerful fluid-management capabilities of NPWT may represent a safer and more effective alternative to drains in many high-risk arthroplasty cases. A particularly promising strategy: combining quilting sutures (internal dead space elimination) with NPWT (fine-tuning fluid management and external splinting) — warrants formal clinical investigation.

8.4 Emerging Technologies

  • Bipolar sealant devices: Achieve meticulous hemostasis during dissection; useful in heavier patients to minimize blood loss and hematoma risk [2]
  • Advanced skin closure systems (e.g., DERMABOND PRINEO): Combine liquid adhesive with self-adhering mesh; provide additional tensile strength and microbial barrier; simplify postoperative care [6]

9. Synthesis & Evidence-Based Best Practice Recommendations

9.1 Multi-faceted Strategy for TJA (TKA/THA)

For high-risk patients (BMI > 35 kg/m², subcutaneous fat > 2 cm, or major comorbidities):

Intraoperative Layered Closure:

  1. Deep Fascia: Slowly absorbable, antimicrobial-coated monofilament suture (e.g., triclosan-coated PDS) [8]
  2. Subcutaneous Layer (if depth > 2 cm):
    • Recommendation 1: Quilting sutures — most effective method for dead space elimination and tension distribution [30]
    • Recommendation 2 (TKA): Retention sutures with bolsters — exceptional effectiveness preventing dehiscence [11]
  3. Skin Layer: Absorbable subcuticular sutures — meta-analysis demonstrates significantly higher infection risk with staples Level I [53]

Post-Closure Adjunctive Therapy:

  1. Recommendation 3: Prophylactic incisional NPWT immediately following skin closure for 3–7 days [39]

9.2 Specific Considerations

Spine Surgery:

  • Multilayer mattress stitch is particularly valuable for deep wounds with high tension [33]
  • Subcutaneous drain may be more justifiable in complex multi-level fusions [51]
  • Skin closure: conflicting evidence — sutures favored in arthroplasty, but one study found lower SSI with staples in obese lumbar fusion [54]

Trauma Surgery:

  • Principles of meticulous debridement, tension-free closure, and dead space management are paramount
  • Prophylactic NPWT strongly indicated for high-risk closed incisions after fracture fixation [45]

9.3 Decision Algorithm

1
Assess Risk: Is BMI > 30 kg/m² or are major risk factors present (diabetes, smoking, malnutrition)? → If yes, manage as high-risk patient.
2
Measure Intraoperative Fat Depth: After fascial closure, measure subcutaneous fat. Is depth > 2 cm? → If yes, mandatory subcutaneous closure. If no, closure optional with no clear benefit.[20]
3
Select Technique by Procedure & Risk:
  • TKA/THA (BMI 30–39): Coated monofilament fascia → Quilting sutures → Subcuticular skin → NPWT
  • TKA/THA (BMI ≥ 40): Coated monofilament fascia → Quilting + Retention sutures (TKA) → Subcuticular skin → NPWT
  • Complex Spine (high BMI): Coated monofilament fascia → Multilayer mattress or Quilting sutures → Consider drain + NPWT → Subcuticular suture or staples
  • High-Risk Trauma: Layered closure with dead space management → NPWT strongly recommended
