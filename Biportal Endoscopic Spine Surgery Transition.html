1. Introduction to the Endoscopic Paradigm Shift

The landscape of minimally invasive spine surgery (MISS) has undergone a profound transformation over the past decade. Driven by patient demand for reduced postoperative morbidity, faster recovery, and preservation of natural spinal biomechanics, techniques have evolved from traditional open laminectomies through microscopic tubular retractors to full-endoscopic procedures. II [1]

Within this evolution, Unilateral Biportal Endoscopy (UBE)—also called Biportal Endoscopic Spine Surgery (BESS)—has emerged as a revolutionary modality bridging conventional open spinal reconstruction and uniportal full-endoscopic surgery. II [3]

Early endoscopic applications began in the 1980s with intradiscal work by pioneers such as Forsts and Hausmann. [5] These evolved into uniportal systems where both optics and instruments share a single coaxial cannula, inherently limiting maneuverability and instrument size while steepening the learning curve by eliminating true bimanual tissue manipulation. III [6]

Key Innovation of UBE

UBE decouples optics from instrumentation by using two percutaneous portals on the same side of the spinous process: one for the endoscope (viewing channel) and one for surgical instruments (working channel). This restores panoramic high-definition visualization and enables unconstrained bimanual maneuvers using conventional open-spine tools. II [3,4]

Clinical evidence demonstrates that biportal interventions yield significant reductions in paraspinal muscle injury, intraoperative blood loss, lower postoperative inflammatory markers (C-reactive protein), and accelerated discharge while matching—and occasionally exceeding—the neural decompression achieved by open surgery. II [7]

⚠ Learning Curve Warning

The transition is governed by a steep, multiphasic, and potentially hazardous learning curve. II Without a structured, evidence-based framework, transitioning surgeons risk elevated rates of early-phase complications: dural tears, iatrogenic neurological injuries, and compressive epidural hematomas. [8]

2. The Synergistic Convergence of Arthroscopic & Open Spine Expertise

The transition to UBE is fundamentally a psychomotor and cognitive adaptation rather than a complete relearning. Orthopedic surgeons with extensive experience in both arthroscopic sports medicine and traditional open spine surgery arrive with highly synergistic, directly transferable skills. II [3]

2.1 The Arthroscopic Advantage: Triangulation & Spatial Adaptation

The most substantial barrier for open-trained surgeons is the visual-spatial disconnect of operating from a 2D monitor while manipulating instruments in a 3D epidural space. III [15] Surgeons with arthroscopic backgrounds have already achieved the neural and psychomotor adaptation required. [17]

  • Non-coaxial triangulation: UBE relies on identical principles used in complex knee, shoulder, or hip arthroscopy—the surgeon calculates the trajectory of the working instrument to intersect the endoscope's focal point without direct line-of-sight. III [14]
  • Functional ambidexterity: Experienced arthroscopists exhibit significantly less laterality and superior bimanual grasping efficiency. In UBE, the non-dominant hand must constantly adjust the endoscope while the dominant hand performs drilling or resection. II [20]
  • Fluid-medium proficiency: Familiarity with pressurized fluid environments for hemostasis, visual clarity management, and compensation for optical distortion in an aqueous medium. [4]
Technical Divergence Note

While knee/shoulder arthroscopists predominantly use angled (30°–70°) endoscopes, UBE relies heavily on a 0-degree arthroscope for direct, undistorted views of neural elements. This is a minimal adjustment for the seasoned arthroscopist. II [8]

2.2 The Open Spine Advantage: Haptic Feedback & Anatomical Architecture

Prior open spinal reconstructive experience provides the non-negotiable anatomical framework and tactile sensibility required for safety. UBE uniquely allows use of conventional, large-dimension spine instruments: standard rotating Kerrison rongeurs, high-speed diamond and cutting burrs, pituitary rongeurs, and angled osteotomes. II [22]

Critical haptic feedback remains consistent with open surgery: the sensation of a high-speed burr breaching cortical bone, the rubbery elasticity of the ligamentum flavum, and the delicate resistance of the dural sac must be physically experienced over hundreds of open cases. III [19]

✓ Convergence Advantage

The spatial and fluid mastery of the arthroscopist combined with the tactile and anatomical mastery of the open spine surgeon means the cross-trained orthopedic surgeon is optimally positioned to bypass fundamental psychomotor hurdles and focus immediately on technique optimization. II [3]

The learning curve has been rigorously quantified using Cumulative Sum (CUSUM) and Risk-Adjusted Cumulative Sum (RA-CUSUM) methodologies, plotting sequential cases against operative times, complication rates, and failure metrics. II [8]

3.3 Biportal vs. Uniportal Learning Trajectories

Systematic reviews indicate that the median case number for the primary learning threshold is marginally lower for uniportal procedures (~20 cases) vs. biportal (~37.5 cases). II [31] However, biportal demonstrates a significantly steeper improvement in operative time and surgical efficiency once the initial threshold is crossed, because UBE allows use of standard open instruments and true unconstrained bimanual manipulation. II [7,31]

4.1 "Red Screen" Phenomenon (Hemorrhage)

In the continuous water-medium environment, even microscopic capillary bleeding can obscure the entire optical field. Inappropriate reflexive responses include blindly increasing pump pressure or deploying instruments, both increasing morbidity risk. III [4]

🔴 Red Screen Protocol

1. Immediately halt all bone work and soft tissue resection.
2. Advance the endoscope directly toward the suspected bleeding source.
3. Use localized flushing action of saline inflow to visualize the specific vessel.
4. Apply low-voltage RF ablation to the identified bleeder.
5. Use transitory pressure increases for seconds only; ensure continuous outflow. [4]

4.2 Hydrostatic Complications

Documented fluid-related complications include retroperitoneal extravasation, severe paraspinal edema, postoperative head/neck pain, and—rarely—cerebral edema with elevated intracranial pressure. II [8]

Safe irrigation pressure: A mean of 30 mmHg is highly safe for continuous use in the epidural space. II [22] Automated pressure-controlled pumps must be used; gravity-fed bags are contraindicated. An adequate low-resistance outflow tract through a properly constructed cruciate fascial incision prevents dangerous pressure accumulation. III [4,32]

4.3 Epidural Hematoma

Continuous hydrostatic pressure tamponades epidural venous bleeding during surgery, creating a false sense of hemostasis. Upon fluid termination, suppressed venous plexuses can re-bleed. Unlike open surgery, the biportal technique preserves the muscular envelope intact, creating a constrained compartment where hematomas compress neural elements directly. III [4]

Mandatory safety practices:

  • Significantly lower irrigation pressure at the final stages to unveil occult bleeders. [22]
  • Aggressively treat identified bleeders with radiofrequency ablation prior to instrument withdrawal. [22]
  • Routine placement of a small-caliber suction drain for 24–48 hours, especially during the first 50–100 cases. III [4]

4.4 Dural Tears & Iatrogenic Neural Injury

These injuries typically occur during high-speed burring near the midline or when aggressively grasping the ligamentum flavum in severe chronic central stenosis where it is densely adherent to the thinned dural sac. II [32]

Critical Technique: Preserve the Ligamentum Flavum

Transitioning surgeons must resist the ingrained open-surgery habit of early flavum resection. The deep layer of the ligamentum flavum must be strictly preserved as a biological shield until all osseous resection is complete. Only then should it be carefully detached and removed. Use specialized low-voltage RF coagulators to prevent thermal neural necrosis. II [32,33]

5. The 10-Tier Difficulty Progression Framework

The global literature advocates for a strict, step-wise progression model to flatten the learning curve and prevent unnecessary patient morbidity. Surgeons must demonstrate outcome stability and low complication rates at lower tiers before advancing. II [19]

1 Ipsilateral Recess Decompression

Lumbar. Portal trajectory, spatial orientation, triangulation over spinolaminar angle, fluid outflow management. [19]

2 Lumbar Discectomy

Lumbar. Epidural space navigation, safe nerve root mobilization, controlled disc fragment resection. [19]

3 Bilateral Decompression (ULBD)

Lumbar. "Over-the-top" technique, safe midline crossing, facet joint preservation. [19]

4 Extraforaminal Approach

Lumbar. Paraspinal muscle dissection, complex extraforaminal vascular anatomy, L5-S1 junction. [19]

5 UBE-TLIF (Interbody Fusion)

Lumbar. Complete facetectomy, endplate preparation via haptic feedback, safe cage placement. [19]

6 Posterior Cervical Foraminotomy

Cervical. Low neural retraction tolerance, V-point identification, perineural membrane handling. [19]

7 Cervical Discectomy

Cervical. Delicate cervical nerve root mobilization, dense neurovascular anatomy navigation. [19]

8 Cervical Laminectomy

Cervical. Multilevel pathology, absolute spinal cord avoidance, OPLL management. [19]

9 Thoracic Decompression

Thoracic. Overlapping lamina orientation, narrow interlaminar spaces, thoracic OLF management. [19]

10 Thoracic Transforaminal Approach

Thoracic. Highest complexity; complex foraminal anatomy, zero margin for cord disturbance. [19]

⚠ Regional Safety Threshold

The lumbar cauda equina is inherently more forgiving of pressure fluctuations and transient retractions. The cervical and thoracic regions house the rigid, highly sensitive spinal cord, where even transient pressure or minor contusion can result in irreversible paralysis. Surgeons should complete a minimum of 30–50 complication-free lumbar cases before initiating Tier 6 cervical procedures. II [19,24]

6. Foundational Surgical Setup: The "10 Essentials" & BIPORTALS Mnemonic

The mnemonic BIPORTALS (Basic medium, Instruments/visualization, Placement of portals, Outflow of fluids, Route of approach, Technical/anatomical challenges, Location of lens and light source, and Start point to work) encapsulates the foundational principles. III [22]

  1. Patient Positioning: Prone on a radiolucent frame with abdomen hanging free to prevent elevated intra-abdominal pressure engorging Batson's plexus. Spine in kyphotic alignment to maximally open the interlaminar window. [4]
  2. Targeting & Portal Placement: Meticulously planned under orthogonal AP and lateral fluoroscopy. Longitudinal skin incisions recommended for early cases (facilitates rapid conversion). [4]
  3. Fascial Management for Outflow: Cruciate incision at the deep fascial layer to establish unhindered outflow tract. Restricted outflow is the primary preventable etiology of hydrostatic damage. [4]
  4. Triangulation Establishment: Instruments guided through the muscular envelope under direct fluoroscopic and endoscopic visualization to the spinolaminar junction. Avoid blind plunging. [4]
  5. Rigid Pathology Selection: Initial cases: single-level, soft, virgin ipsilateral lumbar disc herniations or mild lateral recess stenosis only. Avoid calcified discs, migrated herniations, revisions until Tier 3. [4]
  6. Laterality & Ergonomics: Right-handed surgeons: strictly select left-sided pathologies for initial cases (dominant hand holds working instrument, non-dominant manages endoscope). [4]
  7. Spatial Awareness: Constantly monitor distance between working instrument tip and endoscopic lens. Inadvertent collisions cause blurring, scratching, or catastrophic lens shattering. [4]
  8. Endoscopic Anatomical Mapping: Recalibrate visual anatomy to the magnified view. Actively seek micro-structures: facetal synovial tissues, meningovertebral ligaments, the J-point (lumbar), V-point (cervical), V-cleft of the ligamentum flavum. [4]
  9. Protocolized Hemorrhage Response: Strict team-wide protocol for "red screen" using flowable hemostatic agents, RF coagulation, and pressure management. [4]
  10. Terminal Hemostasis & Drainage: Protocolized fluid pressure lowering at conclusion; prolonged observation for occult bleeding; meticulous subfascial drain placement. [4]

7. The 5-Step "Z" Technique for Bilateral Decompression (ULBD)

The cornerstone procedure of UBE and the gateway to advanced pathologies is the Unilateral Laminotomy for Bilateral Decompression (ULBD), executed via the reproducible 5-step "Z" technique. II [33,35]

Table 4. The 5-Step "Z" Technique for ULBD
StepOperative PhaseTechnical Execution & Anatomical Goals
1 Landmark Identification Dissect soft tissue to expose the spinolaminar junction, inferior margin of the superior lamina, and superior margin of the inferior lamina. Establish a clear anatomical perimeter. [35]
2 Ipsilateral Laminotomy Using a high-speed diamond burr, drill the lower edge of the superior vertebra caudal-to-cranially until the superficial ligamentum flavum is widely exposed. Meticulously preserve the facet joint. [33]
3 Crossing the Midline Direct instruments medially. Undercut the spinous process base using burr and Kerrison rongeurs to access the contralateral side via the interlaminar space trajectory. [33]
4 Contralateral "Over-the-Top" Decompression Burr the undersurface of the contralateral lamina and facet joint. The deep ligamentum flavum is left completely intact as a biological shield between the burr and the dural sac. [33]
5 Flavum Resection & Neural Release After all osseous decompression is verified visually and radiographically, detach and remove the ligamentum flavum en bloc. Confirm ipsilateral and contralateral nerve roots are freely mobilized and pulsating with CSF. [33]

8. Educational Infrastructure & Step-by-Step Training Protocol

The traditional "see one, do one, teach one" paradigm is dangerously inadequate for UBE transition. II [19] A structured, multifaceted educational infrastructure is essential.

8.1 Simulation & Dry-Lab Training

  • High-fidelity VR systems: Bridging hand-eye coordination gaps with haptic feedback. [18]
  • Institutional simulation centers: UCLA Simulation Center at Rosenfeld Hall; Cedars-Sinai Medical Center Simulation Center. [40,41]
  • Low-cost dry box models: Using 0-degree ear endoscopes, webcams, Dremel rotary tools, and synthetic sawbones for daily repetitive triangulation practice. II [16]

8.2 Advanced Cadaveric Workshops

  • Amplify Surgical: 5th Annual Endoscopic Spine Symposium & Cadaver Workshop (March 2026, UC Irvine Surgical Science Laboratory; 120+ surgeons). [42]
  • joimax ESPINEA Academy: Tiered certification programs from beginner decompression to advanced cervical techniques. [44]
  • RIWOspine: Master Class Courses on advanced biportal lumbar fusion on cadaver specimens. [47]
  • NASS: Endoscopic labs at the 2026 Innovation NetWORK Summit (Rosemont, IL). [48]

8.3 Fellowships & Direct Clinical Mentorship

  • ESUBE (European Society of UBE): 1–2-week immersive fellowships blending live observation, case discussions, and supervised training. [49]
  • U.S. Fellowships: Hoag Orthopedic Institute, UCSF, and Cedars-Sinai Spine Center have integrated endoscopic modules. [50,51,52]
  • Proctored clinical cases: Strongly advocated for the first 10–15 live cases. An open surgical tray must be immediately available, and a predetermined time limit established—if adequate visualization is not achieved, convert unhesitatingly to open approach. II [8,22,29]

10. Conclusion

The transition to Unilateral Biportal Endoscopic Spine Surgery represents a highly advantageous evolution for the modern orthopedic spine surgeon. For the practitioner proficient in both arthroscopic sports medicine and traditional open reconstruction, UBE represents the ultimate synthesis of existing skill sets—non-coaxial triangulation and fluid management from arthroscopy, combined with tactile osseous resection and neural anatomy from open spine surgery.

However, the empirical learning curve demands a rigorous gauntlet of ~35–40 cases during which risks of hydrostatic complications, dural tears, and compressive epidural hematomas are significantly elevated. Safe integration requires unwavering commitment to:

  • The 10-Tier Difficulty Progression Framework, beginning with simple lumbar decompressions
  • Protocolized training through simulation, cadaveric workshops, and proctored mentorship
  • Standardized surgical sequences such as the 5-step "Z" technique
  • Meticulous adherence to the 10 Essentials / BIPORTALS setup principles

By following these evidence-based pathways, the surgeon can safely deliver a superior modality that minimizes collateral tissue damage, accelerates patient recovery, and preserves definitive neural decompression—the ultimate goal of spine surgery.
