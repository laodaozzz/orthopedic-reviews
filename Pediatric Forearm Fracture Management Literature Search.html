1. Epidemiology & Contemporary Trends

Pediatric both-bone forearm fractures are among the most prevalent osseous injuries in the skeletally immature population, accounting for an estimated 40% of all pediatric fractures, with a population-based incidence of up to 35.9 per 100,000 person-years.[1],[2] Level II

These injuries exhibit a distinct bimodal age distribution, peaking at ages 10–12 for females and 12–14 for males. Males are affected at a rate 2–3 times higher than females, reflecting greater exposure to high-energy recreational activities.[1]

📊 Paradigm Shift Toward Operative Management

Longitudinal epidemiological reviews document that the proportion of surgically managed pediatric forearm fractures has increased from 59.3% in 2000 to >70% by 2012, with a continued 4.27% aggregate increase through 2023. This trend is driven by the availability of elastic stable intramedullary nailing (ESIN), societal intolerance for prolonged immobilization, and diminishing tolerance for radiographic deformity.[3] Level III

Despite this trend, systematic reviews and meta-analyses published in JPO, JBJS, and JAAOS continuously reaffirm that nonoperative closed management remains extraordinarily safe and effective. Its success, however, depends on rigorous understanding of forearm biomechanics and precise execution of reduction and casting techniques.[5] Level I

2. Functional Osteology & Ligamentous Biomechanics

Osteological Architecture & the Radial Bow

The ulna functions as a straight, rigid central axis, while the radius possesses a critical structural curve—the radial bow—that acts as a mechanical cam to facilitate rotational clearance around the ulna.[1],[3] Loss of this radial bow through healing in a straight-line override (bayonet apposition) severely and permanently restricts forearm rotation.[4]

The physiological axis of rotation for pronation/supination passes obliquely from the ulnar head (DRUJ) to the radial head (PRUJ). Any translational or rotational malunion fundamentally alters this axis geometry, leading to subluxation forces and catastrophic functional loss.[7]

The Interosseous Membrane (IOM)

The IOM is a robust fibrous sheet spanning the diaphyseal space, with its central band running distally from radius to ulna. Its biomechanics are position-dependent: proximal fibers bear higher strain in neutral rotation/pronation, while distal fibers become maximally taut in extreme supination.[1],[7]

During casting, practitioners leverage IOM tension by molding the cast to physically push musculature into the interosseous space, thereby separating the radius and ulna, preventing radio-ulnar convergence (synostosis), and maintaining the space necessary for future rotation.[1]

The Pediatric Periosteal Sleeve

🔑 Critical Concept: The Intact Periosteal Hinge

The pediatric periosteum is exceptionally thick and mechanically robust. On the concave (compressive) side of a bending fracture, the periosteal sleeve typically remains completely intact. This "periosteal hinge" is the single most critical anatomical asset during closed reduction: it acts as a biological tension band that prevents over-reduction and provides immense intrinsic stability once the limb is casted.[1],[4]

3. Muscular Deforming Forces by Fracture Level

When diaphyseal continuity is disrupted, traversing muscles become unopposed deforming forces acting on free-floating fragments. The predictable direction of displacement is dictated by the specific anatomical level of the fracture relative to muscle insertion sites.[1]

4. Mechanism of Injury & Torsional Failure Dynamics

The overwhelming majority of pediatric forearm fractures result from an indirect mechanism—a fall onto an outstretched hand (FOOSH). "Falls from monkey bars" are cited as the single most common specific etiology.[1]

Torsional Determinants of Fracture Morphology

  • Supination injuries (forearm locked in supination at impact) → apex-volar angulation
  • Pronation injuries (forearm pronated during axial load) → apex-dorsal angulation

Failure Patterns Unique to Pediatric Bone

Due to the unique microstructural composition of pediatric bone (higher porosity, larger Haversian canals, greater collagen-to-mineral ratio), several distinct failure patterns occur:[1]

  1. Plastic Deformation: Sustained low-energy force induces microfractures → permanent bowing without visible fracture line. Requires heavy three-point bending to reverse.
  2. Torus (Buckle) Fractures: Pure axial compression → cortex buckles; periosteum intact. Inherently stable; excellent prognosis.
  3. Greenstick Fractures: Complete failure on convex (tension) side; intact but plastically deformed cortex on concave side. The intact cortex retains elastic memory and may need to be deliberately "completed" during reduction.
  4. Complete Fractures: Full 360° circumferential disruption of both cortices. Highly unstable; significant displacement and rotational malalignment.

5. Classification & the Biology of Remodeling

The entire premise of conservative management rests on Wolff's Law: malaligned pediatric bone subjected to normal physiological loads undergoes cellular remodeling—osteoblasts deposit bone along the concave aspect of deformity while osteoclasts resorb along the convex aspect.[2]

Variables Governing Remodeling Capacity

Table 2 — Remodeling Capacity Variables
Variable Impact on Remodeling Clinical Implication
Plane of Deformity Sagittal plane: excellent remodeling. Coronal plane: significantly less reliable. Rotational: zero spontaneous remodeling. Rotational malalignment must be fully corrected at reduction—any residual persists permanently.[2]
Proximity to Physis Distal radial physis contributes ~75% of radial length growth (~5.25 mm/year). Fractures nearer to active physes remodel better. Distal third fractures tolerate greater initial deformity than midshaft or proximal fractures.[4]
Remaining Skeletal Growth More remaining growth → greater remodeling potential. Younger children (<9 years) tolerate up to 15° angulation; older children require stricter alignment.[2]

⚠️ Key Principle: Rotational Malalignment Cannot Remodel

Regardless of patient age, rotational deformity exhibits zero spontaneous remodeling capacity. Any rotational malalignment left uncorrected at the time of initial reduction will persist permanently into adulthood, causing irreversible deficits in pronation and supination.[2]

7. Contemporary Re-evaluation of "Unacceptable" Alignment

Recent robust prospective trials (2024–2025) have begun to challenge the rigid strictness of historical Noonan and Price thresholds.[5],[6] Level II (Prospective cohort)

🔬 Key Findings from Re-evaluation Studies
  • Children up to 11.5 years of age with residual malunions of ≥15° (violating Noonan–Price thresholds) still exhibited massive, near-complete radiographic remodeling at minimum 2-year follow-up.
  • Every patient in the "unacceptably aligned" cohort achieved a highly functional range of motion without requiring secondary corrective osteotomies.
  • 23% showed a slight decrease in maximum pronation (≥10° vs. contralateral), but this did not translate into functional impairment or reduced PedsQL scores.
  • A preceding randomized trial found that 32.5% of fractures united in traditionally "unacceptable" alignment, yet 97% healed without surgical intervention, driving the institutional surgical conversion rate to just 2.6%.[5]

Clinical Implication: These findings support a modernized approach favoring continued conservative observation and serial casting over reflexive surgical conversion for fractures near or slightly above historical thresholds, specifically in older prepubescent cohorts up to 11 years.[5]

8. Advanced Closed Reduction Techniques

When displacement exceeds age-based acceptable alignment criteria, an acute closed reduction is indicated. The prevailing technique was originally elucidated by John Charnley (1950) and refined by Evans for rotational alignment.[12]

The Charnley Sequential Reduction Maneuver

Following adequate analgesia/procedural sedation, four distinct steps are executed:[12]

  1. Increase the Deformity: Deliberately accentuate the initial angulation to disengage interlocking cortical margins and unlock impacted fragments.
  2. Apply Distal Distraction: Sustained longitudinal traction (5–10 minutes) overcomes forearm muscular spasm and restores anatomical length. Caution: Attempting traction without first unlocking tightens the periosteal sleeve and blocks reduction.[4]
  3. Reverse the Mechanism of Injury: Manipulate the distal fragment in the exact opposite direction of the deforming force. The intact periosteal sleeve acts as a dynamic tension band preventing over-reduction.[12]
  4. Immobilize: Secure the aligned limb with a meticulously molded cast using three-point bending mechanics.

Specialized Maneuver for Greenstick Fractures

⚠️ The "Completion" Technique

Greenstick fractures retain massive elastic memory from the intact concave cortex. Simply straightening the fracture and casting will result in late loss of reduction as the intact cortex "springs back." Definitive treatment requires intentional "completion" of the fracture through a brisk over-correction maneuver, deliberately breaking the intact cortex to destroy its elastic memory, then reducing and immobilizing using standard principles.[1]

9. Casting Mechanics & Radiographic Predictive Indices

A simple cylindrical cast is insufficient. The cast must be aggressively molded to recreate the flattened, elliptical cross-section of the forearm. Firm molding pressure applied to volar and dorsal interosseous spaces forces soft tissues between the bones, tensioning the IOM to prevent radio-ulnar convergence.[1]

The Cast Index (CI)

The Cast Index is defined as the internal sagittal (AP) diameter ÷ internal coronal (lateral) diameter of the cast at the fracture level, measured on orthogonal radiographs.[12]

  • A perfectly circular cast yields CI = 1.0 (no rotational/translational control).
  • CI < 0.8 is highly predictive of successful reduction maintenance.
  • Casts with CI ≥ 0.8 were 2.97× more likely to suffer redisplacement (p = .020).[31] Level III
📐 Proximal Fractures — CI Limitation

Due to the massive muscle bellies in the proximal forearm, achieving CI < 0.8 is exceedingly difficult without risking compartment syndrome. Mean CI for successful proximal fractures is inherently higher (0.83) vs. distal fractures (0.76). The CI's strict application in proximal-third fractures is discouraged.[15]

💡 The Core Dilemma

While conservative management carries higher early mechanical instability (26% redisplacement vs. 3%), the ultimate long-term functional outcomes are virtually identical. The unassailable advantage of conservative management remains the total avoidance of surgical morbidity—no pin-site infections, no hardware migration, no obligate secondary surgery for nail removal, and fewer anesthetic exposures.[2]

Refracture Risk & Return to Sport (RTS) Protocols

Following prolonged immobilization, pediatric bone exhibits transient regional osteopenia, creating a highly vulnerable window. Registry data from Helsinki University Hospital (JBJS, 2024) reveals:[45] Level III (Registry-based cohort)

  • Overall pediatric refracture rate: ~0.48%
  • Diaphyseal both-bone forearm refracture rate: 3.76% (highest in pediatric skeleton)
  • Median time to refracture: 124 days post-initial injury
Table 8 — Return to Sport (RTS) Guidelines by Fracture Classification
Fracture Type Immobilization Duration Safe RTS Protocol Rationale
Distal Radius Buckle (Torus) 3–4 weeks 4 weeks — immediate RTS tolerated Intact periosteum; impaction mechanics
Simple Metaphyseal / Greenstick 4–6 weeks 8–10 weeks — avoid high-impact/contact sports Cortical consolidation required
Complete Diaphyseal Both-Bone 6–8 weeks 3–6 months — prolonged restriction mandatory Early RTS at 5 weeks → 6.7% refracture rate; RTS at 11+ weeks → 0.6%[46]

Rehabilitation priorities during the restriction window: Active and active-assisted range of motion, progressive isometric strengthening, and proprioceptive neuromuscular facilitation—targeting muscle atrophy and joint stiffness without prematurely loading the vulnerable, osteopenic diaphysis.[49]
