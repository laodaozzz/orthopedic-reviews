Perioperative Glycemic Management in Orthopedic Trauma & Ketoacidotic State Differentiation

1. Introduction: The Metabolic Crisis in Orthopedic Trauma

The management of orthopedic trauma patients presents a critical intersection of acute physiological stress and complex metabolic derangement.[1] In this high-risk population, hyperglycemia is not merely a common comorbidity but an active catalyst for poor outcomes, fundamentally compromising the pillars of surgical success: wound healing and bone integrity.[2]

This clinical challenge is defined by a dual threat:

  1. Stress Hyperglycemia: The profound stress of traumatic injury, compounded by surgical intervention, unleashes counter-regulatory hormones—catecholamines, glucagon, and cortisol—that drive severe insulin resistance and hyperglycemia.[4] This "stress hyperglycemia" is an independent predictor of complications, most notably surgical site infections (SSIs).[7] Level I–II
  2. Perioperative Ketogenesis: Standard nil per os (NPO) protocols create a starvation state that promotes ketogenesis.[9] This is dangerously amplified by sodium-glucose cotransporter 2 (SGLT-2) inhibitors, which can precipitate euglycemic diabetic ketoacidosis (eDKA)—a life-threatening metabolic emergency with normal blood glucose.[10,13]
⚠ Critical Diagnostic Dilemma: The perioperative team must rapidly and accurately differentiate benign starvation ketosis (SK) from life-threatening diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA) and the increasingly prevalent euglycemic DKA (eDKA)—a distinction perilously blurred by SGLT-2 inhibitor use.[9,13]

2. Pathophysiology: How Hyperglycemia Sabotages Orthopedic Outcomes

Hyperglycemia is not a passive bystander; it is an independent, active mediator of tissue damage.[2] Uncontrolled glucose levels systemically compromise recovery by attacking the two foundational elements of healing: immune integrity and bone regeneration.

2.1 Surgical Site Infections (SSIs): Immune Dysfunction Cascade

The association between perioperative hyperglycemia and SSI is robustly established:[16] Level I

  • Admission glucose ≥ 200 mg/dL is an independent risk factor for deep 90-day SSIs.[19]
  • Optimal postoperative glucose cutoff for predicting periprosthetic joint infection: as low as 137 mg/dL.[19]
  • AAOS Clinical Practice Guideline: poor glycemic control increases infection risk (high-quality evidence); postoperative glucose > 125 mg/dL increases risk (low-quality evidence).[21]
Causal Mechanisms: Hyperglycemia cripples host immune response through abnormal immune function, impaired white blood-cell phagocytic function, and reduced oxidative burst capacity.[4] Metabolic control is as fundamental to infection prevention as sterile technique and perioperative antibiotics.[22]

2.2 Impaired Fracture Healing: Cellular Sabotage of Bone

Poor glycemic control is directly linked to pseudarthrosis (non-union), hardware failure, and impaired wound healing through three major mechanisms:[2,3,23] Level II–III

  1. Advanced Glycation End Products (AGEs): Chronic hyperglycemia leads to non-enzymatic glycation of proteins, forming AGEs that accumulate in the bone collagen matrix, reducing its material quality and toughness.[23]
  2. Impaired Angiogenesis: Hyperglycemia suppresses Vascular Endothelial Growth Factor (VEGF), resulting in deficient microvascular invasion of the callus, elevated chondrocyte apoptosis, and compromised cartilage-to-bone transition.[3,23]
  3. The "Adipogenic Switch": The diabetic microenvironment causes osteoprogenitor cells to switch differentiation from bone-forming osteoblasts toward fat-forming adipocytes, producing a biomechanically weak callus infiltrated with fat tissue.[23]
Key Concept: Hardware failure in hyperglycemic patients often represents not surgical technique failure but biological substrate failure—the patient's body builds fat instead of bone around the implant.[23]

2.3 Stress-Induced Hyperglycemia (SIH) in Non-Diabetic Patients

SIH is defined as acute hyperglycemia (typically > 140 mg/dL) in patients with no prior diabetes history. It occurs in 20–40% of trauma and surgical patients.[6] Level II

  • This is not latent pre-diabetes—it is an acute, massive neuro-hormonal response: cortisol surge (hepatic glucose production), catecholamines (impaired insulin release), and inflammatory cytokines (TNF-α, IL-6).[4]
  • SIH patients have significantly higher odds of mortality than both non-diabetic patients with normal glucose and patients with known diabetic hyperglycemia.[29]
  • SIH is independently associated with SSIs in non-diabetic orthopedic trauma patients.[3]

The level of SIH is a direct, real-time reflection of the magnitude of systemic stress response. A non-diabetic trauma patient with glucose 220 mg/dL is in a more severe, catabolic state than a chronic poorly-controlled diabetic with the same glucose.[27]

3. Consensus Standards for Perioperative Glycemic Management

3.1 Preoperative Risk Stratification

For elective procedures, strong consensus exists to optimize glycemic control before scheduling: HbA1c target below 8–9% (68–75 mmol/mol).[16] Level I

In trauma, this optimization is a luxury not afforded.[1] HbA1c and Fructosamine serve as risk stratification tools, not barriers to surgery.[40]

High-Risk Thresholds (Source-Based):
• Admission HbA1c > 7.85%[41]
• Admission blood glucose ≥ 180–200 mg/dL[19]
• Day-of-surgery point-of-care glucose independently correlates with complications regardless of chronic HbA1c.[19]

3.2 Perioperative Glycemic Targets

Early enthusiasm for "tight" control (< 120 mg/dL) was tempered by increased hypoglycemia risk and associated mortality.[4] The modern consensus targets "safe control":[16] Level I

Unified Target Range: 100–180 mg/dL (5.6–10.0 mmol/L)
Hypoglycemia defined as blood glucose < 70 mg/dL.[3]

3.3 Inpatient Insulin Protocols: Basal-Bolus Therapy vs. Sliding Scale

The Failure of Sliding Scale Insulin (SSI)

SSI is a reactive, physiologically flawed strategy that treats hyperglycemia only after it occurs, failing to suppress hepatic gluconeogenesis.[50] It leads to wide glycemic variability associated with poor outcomes.[51] The ADA, Endocrine Society, and other bodies explicitly discourage SSI as the sole regimen.[40] Level I

The Standard of Care: Basal-Bolus Insulin (BBI)

BBI is the proactive, physiologic approach recommended by national guidelines:[40]

  1. Basal Insulin: Long-acting (glargine, detemir) once/twice daily to suppress excessive hepatic gluconeogenesis—the primary driver of fasting-state hyperglycemia.[16]
  2. Bolus (Prandial) Insulin: Rapid-acting (lispro, aspart, glulisine) before meals to cover carbohydrate intake.[55]
  3. Correctional Insulin: Small rapid-acting dose added to prandial dose to correct readings above target.
Key Evidence: The landmark RABBIT-2 trial demonstrated BBI significantly reduced postoperative complications vs. SSI alone.[22] In orthopedic surgery specifically, nurse-managed BBI protocols showed lower average blood glucose, reduced glycemic variability, and significant reduction in adverse events, infections, and hospital length of stay.[51] Level I
⚠ Critical NPO Management Error: Holding all insulin for NPO patients is a common and dangerous error. While prandial insulin must be held, basal insulin is crucial and must be continued—typically at 75–80% of the patient's normal home dose.[48]

3.4 Common Confounders

  • Glucocorticoid Therapy: Steroids are a known, predictable driver of hyperglycemia. Patients receiving steroids must be monitored closely and met with proactive insulin regimens.[40]
  • Anesthesia Type: General anesthesia has a more significant impact on blood glucose than epidural or combined general-epidural anesthesia.[40]

4. The Ketoacidosis Dilemma: A Primer for the Surgical Team

4.1 Foundational Pathophysiology

The body's switch between glucose utilization and fat breakdown (ketogenesis) is governed by the glucagon-to-insulin ratio—the "master switch."[11]

  • Insulin (Anabolic): Suppresses lipolysis and ketogenesis.
  • Glucagon (Catabolic): Promotes lipolysis and ketogenesis.

When insulin is low and glucagon high, hormone-sensitive lipase breaks triglycerides into free fatty acids (FFAs). FFAs are transported to the liver and converted via β-oxidation into ketone bodies: acetoacetate and β-hydroxybutyrate.[11]

The fundamental difference between physiologic ketosis and pathologic ketoacidosis is not the presence of ketones, but the failure of the regulatory "brake" on this process.

4.2 Profile: Diabetic Ketoacidosis (DKA)

  • Hormonal State: Absolute or profound relative insulin deficiency—the "brake" is completely gone.[11]
  • Pathophysiology: Without insulin, glucagon is unopposed → runaway lipolysis and ketogenesis. Simultaneously, impaired glucose uptake causes severe hyperglycemia, osmotic diuresis, and dehydration.[11,63]
  • Result: Ketone production (e.g., > 20–25 mM) overwhelms bicarbonate buffering, causing severe high-anion-gap metabolic acidosis.[11,63]

4.3 Profile: Starvation Ketosis (SK)

  • Hormonal State: Low insulin, but intact β-cell function—the "brake" remains functional.[9]
  • Pathophysiology: Fasting lowers insulin, permitting glucagon to rise and initiate ketogenesis. As ketone levels rise (to ~3–5 mmol/L), the ketones themselves stimulate a small, regulatory insulin release from the healthy pancreas.[67]
  • Result: Mild, non-progressive ketosis that does not overwhelm the bicarbonate buffer.[9]

5. Diagnostic Differentiation of Ketoacidotic States

5.1 Clinical Presentation

DKA: Acute medical emergency. Rapid onset (hours to a day).[65] Symptoms include polyuria, polydipsia, Kussmaul respirations, "fruity" acetone breath odor, abdominal pain, altered mental status.[11]

SK: Develops slowly over several days. Signs of chronic malnutrition may be present. Does not present with profound acidosis-driven symptoms.[67]

5.2 Laboratory Triad: The Definitive Diagnosis

Diagnostic Pitfall: Urine ketone (nitroprusside) tests are semi-quantitative and primarily measure acetoacetate, not β-hydroxybutyrate. Definitive diagnosis requires serum bicarbonate and quantitative serum β-hydroxybutyrate.[11,64]

6. The Modern Confounder: Euglycemic DKA (eDKA)

6.1 The SGLT-2 Inhibitor Paradigm Shift

SGLT-2 inhibitors (empagliflozin, canagliflozin, dapagliflozin) introduce significant perioperative risk:[83] Level II–III

  1. SGLT-2i blocks glucose reabsorption in the proximal tubule → massive glycosuria.[13]
  2. Lowered blood glucose → decreased insulin secretion + increased glucagon secretion.[63]
  3. High glucagon:insulin ratio → runaway ketogenesis.[13]

The result: a patient in a DKA-like hormonal state who appears euglycemic because excess glucose is being excreted in the urine.[13]

6.2 The "Perfect Storm" in Orthopedic Trauma

Three converging triggers for eDKA in the trauma setting:[13]
1. SGLT-2 Inhibitor Use — the underlying pharmacological driver
2. Surgical Stress / Trauma — counter-regulatory hormones exacerbate the high-glucagon state[10]
3. Fasting / NPO Status — depletes hepatic glycogen and promotes fat metabolism[10]

6.3 Case Analysis: A Stark Warning

A documented case: a man in his 60s with type 2 diabetes on Empagliflozin was admitted for hip fracture, NPO for several days. Capillary blood glucose remained normal (72–198 mg/dL).[14] Standard protocols—which only triggered ketone checks for hyperglycemia (> 252 mg/dL)—completely missed the developing crisis.[14]

Intraoperatively, the patient was found to have: pH 6.96, serum bicarbonate 9.2 mEq/L, serum ketones 4.9 mmol/L—life-threatening euglycemic DKA.[14]

⚠ New Diagnostic Paradigm for SGLT-2i Users:
Serum glucose is an unreliable and dangerous marker for DKA.
The diagnostic workup must shift from (Glucose + Ketones) to (Anion Gap + Bicarbonate + Serum Ketones).
Any NPO trauma patient on an SGLT-2i with an unexplained anion gap or bicarbonate < 18 mEq/L must be presumed to have eDKA until proven otherwise.[14]

7.2 Algorithm for Assessing Ketosis in the Perioperative Trauma Patient

Step 1 — Initial Assessment (All Trauma Patients):
On admission: blood glucose, serum electrolytes (anion gap), and quantitative serum β-hydroxybutyrate.[1]
▼
Step 2 — Is the anion gap elevated (> 12 mEq/L) AND/OR serum bicarbonate low (< 18 mEq/L)?
▼
If NO →
Not in significant ketoacidosis. Proceed with standard glycemic management (target 100–180 mg/dL).[16]
If YES →
Patient is in ketoacidosis. Proceed to differentiation.
▼
Step 3 — Check Serum Glucose AND SGLT-2 Inhibitor Status
▼
A) Glucose > 250 mg/dL
Diagnosis: DKA[12]
Management: Medical emergency → IV fluids + continuous IV insulin infusion.
B) Glucose < 250 mg/dL
→ Check SGLT-2i Status
B.i) On SGLT-2i → Presume eDKA[14]
Management: Emergency → IV insulin infusion + IV dextrose (D5/D10) simultaneously.[10]
B.ii) NOT on SGLT-2i → Most likely Starvation Ketosis[12]
Management: Administer IV dextrose to stimulate endogenous insulin and "turn off" ketogenesis.[9] Do NOT start insulin drip.
⚠ The "Treatment Paradox": The euglycemic eDKA patient needs both insulin (to stop ketosis) and dextrose (to prevent hypoglycemia), whereas the euglycemic SK patient needs only dextrose. The SGLT-2i history is the critical data point.[10,14]

7.3 Consolidated Perioperative Glycemic Management Pathway

  1. On Admission: All patients → blood glucose, HbA1c, electrolytes (anion gap), quantitative serum ketones. Identify and flag SGLT-2i users.[1]
  2. Risk Stratification: BG > 180 mg/dL, SIH, or high HbA1c → flag as high-risk for SSI/complications.[19]
  3. Pre-op/NPO Management: Subcutaneous BBI protocol. NPO: 75–80% basal dose; hold prandial. Avoid SSI-only regimens.[40,48,52]
  4. Ketoacidosis Check: Implement diagnostic algorithm (§7.2). If eDKA suspected → urgent endocrine/medical consultation.
  5. Intraoperative: Continuous IV insulin infusion, target 100–180 mg/dL.[46]
  6. Postoperative (Non-ICU): Transition from IV to scheduled subcutaneous BBI. Do not use SSI as sole therapy.[40]
  7. SGLT-2i Users (Postoperative): Continue to hold. Do not restart until stable, stress resolving, ketones negative, tolerating oral diet.[91]
