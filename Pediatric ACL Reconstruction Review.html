1. Introduction

The management of anterior cruciate ligament (ACL) injuries in skeletally immature patients at Tanner stages 1 and 2 represents one of the most intricate challenges in modern orthopedic sports medicine. This demographic—typically prepubescent girls under age 11 and boys under age 12—presents wide-open physes with significant remaining longitudinal growth potential. Level I

The intersection of increasing youth participation in high-demand pivoting sports and the vulnerability of the developing knee has driven a rise in injury incidence, necessitating rigorous re-evaluation of surgical paradigms. The last decade (2016–2026) has witnessed a decisive shift from whether to operate to how to operate safely and effectively.

Key Evidence

Delayed reconstruction (≥3 months) is associated with 2.82× higher odds of severe lateral meniscal tears compared to acute treatment. In prospective series, 33% of children treated nonoperatively eventually required reconstruction, with nearly half developing secondary meniscal lesions by the time of surgery. Level II

1.1 The Physiological Imperative

In Tanner 1–2 patients, the distal femoral physis contributes ~10 mm/year and the proximal tibial physis ~6 mm/year of longitudinal growth. Any surgical transgression poses risk of growth arrest manifesting as leg length discrepancy (LLD) or angular deformity (valgus/varus/recurvatum). Level II

The pediatric knee relies heavily on secondary stabilizers and neuromuscular control—both often underdeveloped—necessitating a reconstruction that is not only physeal-safe but robust enough for return to sport.

1.2 The Shift from Nonoperative to Operative Management

The rationale for early surgical intervention is now supported by robust longitudinal data:

  • Meniscal risk: Delay >3 months → 2.82× increased odds of severe lateral meniscal tears Level II
  • Nonoperative failure: 33% eventually require reconstruction; ~50% develop secondary meniscal lesions Level II
  • Poor compliance: Activity restriction compliance in children is inherently low

The contemporary objective is to restore kinematics sufficiently to protect the meniscus while respecting physeal vulnerability, driving the refinement of physeal-sparing techniques.

2. The Micheli-Kocher Technique

The Micheli-Kocher (MK) technique—a combined intra- and extra-articular reconstruction utilizing the iliotibial band (ITB)—has served as the gold standard for Tanner 1–2 patients, particularly in North America. By routing the graft "over the top" (OTT) of the lateral femoral condyle without femoral bone tunnels, it theoretically eliminates femoral physeal arrest risk. Level I

2.1 Classic Technique & Efficacy

The fundamental MK procedure involves:

  1. Harvesting an ITB strip, leaving it attached distally at Gerdy's tubercle
  2. Passing the graft over the lateral femoral condyle
  3. Routing through the intercondylar notch
  4. Passing under the intermeniscal ligament
  5. Fixing to the proximal tibia
Pooled Outcomes — Systematic Review (29 Studies, 1,177 Patients)
  • Return to preinjury activity: 93.4%
  • Negative pivot-shift test: 95.2%
  • Growth disturbance rate: 0.0%
  • Angular deformity rate: 0.0%
  • Graft failure rate: 6.6%

Source: ISAKOS systematic review, 2016–2024 Level I

2.2 Modern Modifications

2.2.1 Knotless All-Suture Anchor Fixation ("Modified MacIntosh")

Integration of knotless all-suture anchor technology (e.g., FiberTak) for tibial fixation replaces traditional staples or periosteal sutures. This creates a low-profile, high-strength fixation point that respects the physis and reduces bone volume removed. Level IV

2.2.2 Mini-Open & Arthroscopic-Assisted Approaches

Specialized tendon strippers now allow ITB harvest through small incisions while preserving anterior and posterior fibers. Graft passage is increasingly performed under arthroscopic visualization to ensure proper seating and reduce extension deficits. Level IV

2.2.3 Hybrid Procedures

  • Three-in-One: Combines ACL reconstruction, ALL reconstruction, and lateral extra-articular tenodesis (LET) using ITB in revision/complex cases Level IV
  • PARLET (Pedicled ACL Reconstruction and LET): Uses a pedicled hamstring graft to reconstruct both ACL and ALL without additional implants Level IV

2.3 The "Over-Constraint" Debate

A persistent criticism is potential over-constraining of the lateral compartment. Biomechanical studies show that while OTT/LET configurations significantly improve rotational stability, they may cause over-constraint at 15° flexion. However, current mid-term clinical data has not shown significant increases in degenerative changes or ROM deficits. Level V (biomechanical)

3. All-Epiphyseal (AE) Reconstruction

While MK prioritizes physeal safety via extra-osseous routing, the AE technique prioritizes anatomic restoration by placing grafts within the native footprint using tunnels drilled entirely within the epiphysis. Level II

3.1 Technique

  • Femoral Tunnel: Drilled via medial portal or outside-in approach, staying distal to the femoral physis; requires fluoroscopic guidance Level IV
  • Tibial Tunnel: Drilled at steeper angle (25–30°) or using retrograde drills (FlipCutter) within the tibial epiphysis Level IV

AE patients have a lower contralateral ACL tear rate (4.2% vs. 6.6% for MK), suggesting that anatomic restoration may better normalize kinematics and neuromuscular control, thereby protecting the contralateral limb. Level I

4. Regional Consensus: POSNA vs. ESSKA

A profound divergence exists between North American (POSNA) and European (ESSKA/EPOS) surgeons in managing Tanner 1–2 ACL injuries. Level III

Table 1: POSNA vs. ESSKA/EPOS Preferences for Tanner 1–2 ACL Reconstruction
Feature POSNA (North America) ESSKA / EPOS (Europe)
Early Surgery (<3 months) 70% recommend 17% recommend
Primary Goal Prevent meniscal/chondral damage Avoid growth disturbance ("Rehab first")
Preferred Technique ITB / Over-the-Top (46% preference) All-Epiphyseal / Transphyseal dominant
Graft Choice ITB or Hamstring autograft; allograft strictly avoided Hamstring autograft; increasing quadriceps interest
Transphyseal Drilling Generally avoided in Tanner 1–2 More accepted if "physically respectful" vertical tunnels

This divergence is partly attributable to training lineages: MK originated in Boston, embedding deeply in North American fellowship training, while European centers pioneered transphyseal instrumentation and techniques.

5. Biomechanics of Soft Tissue Fixation

5.1 The "Bungee Cord" Effect

The bungee cord effect refers to longitudinal oscillation of a soft tissue graft within a bone tunnel, particularly problematic with suspensory fixation:

  • Elasticity: Hamstring tendons possess inherent viscoelasticity; without aperture fixation, the graft acts as a spring Level V
  • Tunnel widening: Longitudinal pumping forces synovial fluid (containing osteolytic cytokines) into the tunnel interface Level V
  • Fixation distance: Cortical fixation far from the joint line increases effective graft length and total elongation

5.2 The "Windshield Wiper" Effect

Transverse graft movement at the tunnel aperture when the fixation point is distant, causing toggling during flexion-extension cycles. In the MK technique, the graft creates a fulcrum at the posterior femoral cortex—proper chamfering is critical to prevent graft abrasion. Level V

5.3 Rotational Stability: OTT Advantage

Biomechanical Data (Porcine Model)

OTT augmentation reduces internal tibial rotation to near-native levels: 8.5° vs. 18.9° for single-bundle reconstruction at 30° flexion. The OTT position provides robust resistance to anterior translation, functioning similarly to LET. Level V (biomechanical)

6. Graft Selection: The "Size Matters" Doctrine

6.1 Critical Diameter Thresholds

Multiple studies establish a deterministic relationship between graft diameter and failure risk. For every 1.0 mm decrease in diameter, the failure rate increases by 1.27×. Level II

Table 2: Graft Diameter and Failure Risk in Pediatric ACLR
Graft Diameter Risk Profile Recommendation
< 6 mm 🔴 High Risk (HR > 5.0) Contraindicated. Must augment or triple/quadruple strands.
6 – 7 mm 🟠 Moderate-High Risk Augmentation (InternalBrace) or tripling highly recommended.
7 – 8 mm 🟡 Low-Moderate Risk Standard of care, but >8 mm is preferred.
> 8 mm 🟢 Lowest Risk Ideal target for pediatric reconstruction.
Allograft (any diameter) ⛔ Extreme Risk (4–7.7× failure) Contraindicated in primary pediatric ACLR.

6.2 Solutions for Small Grafts

Tanner 1–2 patients often have hamstring tendons yielding only 6–7 mm diameter grafts. Strategies include:

  • 5- and 6-strand grafts: Tripling semitendinosus + doubled/tripled gracilis consistently achieves >8 mm Level IV
  • Partial-thickness quadriceps augmentation: Reliably augments hamstring graft size Level IV
  • ITB graft (MK technique): Inherently robust, avoiding the small-graft problem entirely

6.3 Allograft: Contraindicated

⛔ Critical Warning

Allografts in pediatric patients have failure rates 4 to 7.7 times higher than autografts. The MOON cohort data confirms that young age combined with allograft use is the single strongest predictor of graft failure. Level II

7. Augmentation Strategies

7.1 InternalBrace (Suture Tape Augmentation)

Reinforcement with high-strength suture tape (e.g., FiberTape) spans the joint as a "seat belt" load-sharing device, protecting the graft during early healing and preventing irreversible elongation. Level IV

⚠️ Critical Distinction: Augmentation vs. Repair
  • Augmentation of reconstruction: Promising results; some cohorts report 0% retear rate at 2 years Level IV
  • Primary ACL repair (standalone InternalBrace): Catastrophic failure rate of 52.6% within 2 years Level IV

InternalBrace must augment a robust graft—never standalone repair in this population.

7.2 Lateral Extra-Articular Tenodesis (LET)

LET is increasingly utilized to control rotational instability and protect the ACL graft. The MK technique is inherently a form of LET. For AE/transphyseal reconstructions, adding a modified Lemaire or ITB tenodesis significantly reduces rotatory laxity. Modified Ellison techniques pass ITB deep to the LCL without endangering the physis. Level IV

8.1 Growth Disturbance

  • Micheli-Kocher: 0.0% growth disturbance and angular deformity — the safety benchmark Level I
  • All-Epiphyseal: 1.5% growth disturbance, 1.3% angular deformity — often technical (drilling too close to physis) Level I

8.2 Graft Failure

  • Overall rates: 10–20% in pediatric populations Level II
  • MK vs. AE: 6.6% vs. 10.6% — attributed to robust ITB graft and rotational control Level I
  • Contralateral paradox: MK patients have higher contralateral tear rates (6.6% vs. 4.2%), suggesting non-anatomic reconstruction may alter gait/landing mechanics Level I

10. Conclusion

The management of ACL injuries in Tanner stage 1–2 patients has undergone significant maturation. The choice of technique involves a complex calculus balancing physeal safety, anatomic restoration, and graft durability.

🟢 Micheli-Kocher (OTT)

  • 0% growth disturbance — safest for physis
  • Lower graft failure (6.6%)
  • Ideal for youngest patients (Tanner 1)
  • Modern modifications address historical drawbacks
  • May increase contralateral tear risk

🔵 All-Epiphyseal (AE)

  • More anatomic reconstruction
  • Better contralateral knee protection
  • Higher technical risk (1.5% growth disturbance)
  • Higher graft failure rate (10.6%)
  • Favored by ESSKA; for Tanner 2–3 patients
The Irrefutable "Size Matters" Doctrine

Surgeons must ensure graft diameters >8 mm, utilizing tripling/quadrupling techniques or augmentation with InternalBrace if necessary. Allografts have no place in primary pediatric ACL reconstruction. Level I
