Surgical Management & Fixation Techniques for Inferior Pole Patellar Fractures

An Exhaustive Review of Biomechanical Outcomes, Clinical Complications & Therapeutic Consensus

1 Introduction & Epidemiology

Fractures of the patella — the largest sesamoid bone in the human body — represent approximately 1% of all skeletal injuries and play a pivotal role in disruption of the extensor mechanism of the knee.[1] Within this category, fractures involving the inferior pole (distal pole) constitute a distinct and clinically challenging subset, accounting for 9.3–22% of all patellar fractures.[1]

Unlike simple transverse fractures of the patellar body, inferior pole fractures are frequently characterized by extra-articular avulsion injuries where the bone is comminuted into multiple small fragments, often described in the literature as "eggshell" comminution.[3] The management of these fractures has historically been a subject of intense orthopaedic debate. The primary anatomical challenge lies in the size and quality of the distal fragments: they are often too small to accept standard screw fixation and too numerous to be anatomically reduced with simple wiring.

Clinical Significance This structural fragility creates a fundamental dichotomy in management strategies: the historical preference for partial patellectomy (excision of the fragments) versus the contemporary drive for osteosynthesis (fixation and preservation).[4]

1.1 Anatomy & Vascularity

The patella functions as a biomechanical fulcrum, increasing the lever arm of the quadriceps muscle group by displacing the patellar tendon anteriorly from the femorotibial contact point. The inferior pole serves as the origin of the patellar ligament (tendon), which inserts into the tibial tubercle. This region is subjected to immense tensile loads:

  • Walking: ~0.5× body weight[2]
  • Deep flexion / squatting: >7× body weight[2]

The vascular supply to the patella is derived from an anastomotic ring fed by the genicular arteries (superior, inferior, medial, and lateral). The inferior pole is particularly vulnerable; in transverse fractures or severe comminution, the intraosseous blood supply may be disrupted. However, the non-articular nature of the inferior pole implies that perfect articular reduction is less critical than in mid-body fractures, shifting surgical priority toward restoring the continuity and length of the extensor mechanism.[1]

1.2 Mechanism of Injury

Injuries to the inferior pole are predominantly indirect, resulting from violent eccentric contraction of the quadriceps against a flexed knee (e.g., stumble or fall). Direct trauma (e.g., dashboard injury) is also common, often resulting in greater comminution.[5]

2 The Historical Paradigm: Partial Patellectomy

For decades, the standard of care for comminuted inferior pole fractures was partial patellectomy: surgical excision of all distal bony fragments and reattachment of the patellar tendon to the remaining proximal patellar body, typically via transosseous tunnels or heavy non-absorbable sutures.[1]

2.1 Rationale for Excision

Historically, surgeons faced with "unfixable" comminution reasoned that excising fragments eliminated the risk of nonunion, avascular necrosis, and implant failure. Since the inferior pole is non-articular, removal was theoretically believed to have minimal impact on patellofemoral joint mechanics.[6]

2.2 Clinical Outcomes & Sequelae

Long-term follow-up studies have progressively dismantled the arguments favoring routine partial patellectomy. The procedure is now associated with significant biomechanical disadvantages and poorer functional outcomes compared to fixation.

Major Complications of Partial Patellectomy
  • Patella Baja (Infera): The most pervasive complication. Excision of the distal pole inevitably shortens the extensor mechanism unless the tendon is reattached at a strictly isometric point (surgically difficult). This fundamentally alters knee kinematics, leading to decreased lever arm efficiency and significantly increased contact pressures on remaining patellar cartilage.[7]
  • Quadriceps Weakness: Removing the inferior pole reduces the moment arm of the extensor mechanism. Patients require greater quadriceps force for full extension, leading to muscle fatigue, atrophy, and persistent extensor lag.[6]
  • Calcification & Ossification: Persistent irritation at the tendon-bone interface leads to heterotopic ossification. One long-term study observed calcification in 35.5% of patients following partial patellectomy.[5]

Comparative studies consistently demonstrate superior outcomes for fixation over excision. In a study comparing total/partial patellectomy with internal fixation, complication rates were lower in the patellectomy group, but functional outcomes (ROM, strength) were superior in the fixation group.[5] A systematic review reinforced that bony fragment excision is "no longer recommended" due to inferior functional results.[10]

2.3 Current Consensus on Excision

Current Orthopaedic Consensus Partial patellectomy is relegated to a salvage procedure only. It is indicated when the inferior pole is so severely pulverized that no distinct fragments remain for suture holding, or in cases of chronic nonunion where fixation has failed. The primary goal of modern surgery is preservation.[1]

Key Biomechanical Principle A fixation construct should ideally withstand 250–300 N to allow safe passive mobilization.[2] A gap of >3 mm at the fracture site under cyclic loading is considered clinical failure, leading to fibrous nonunion and extensor lag.[12]

4 Tension Band Wiring (TBW)

4.1 Principles & Technique

TBW is based on the AO principle of converting tensile forces on the anterior cortical surface into compressive forces at the fracture line during flexion. The standard technique involves placement of two parallel longitudinal Kirschner wires (K-wires) and a figure-of-eight cerclage wire looped over the anterior patella.[13]

4.2 Failure in the Inferior Pole Context

Mechanistic Flaws of TBW for Inferior Pole Fractures
  • Requirement for a Rigid Fulcrum: TBW relies on a solid articular hinge to convert tension to compression. In comminuted inferior pole fractures, the distal cortex is shattered — compression causes collapse or fragment tilting rather than stability.[2]
  • Fragment Size: K-wires (1.6–2.0 mm diameter) passing through small 10–15 mm distal fragments can cause iatrogenic fracture propagation.[2]

4.3 Clinical Complication Profile

  • Soft Tissue Irritation: 8.8% in TBW vs. 0% in SVW groups.[8]
  • Re-operation for Implant Removal: 100% of TBW patients eventually underwent implant removal; over one-third requested it specifically due to pain and irritation.[15]
  • Patella Baja: Incidence of 52.1% in TBW-treated inferior pole fractures — the highest among all techniques reviewed.[8]
  • Infection Rate: ~2.1%, comparable to other open techniques.[8]

5 Separate Vertical Wiring (SVW)

To address TBW limitations, Yang and Byun introduced and popularized the Separate Vertical Wiring (SVW) technique.[3]

5.1 Surgical Technique

  1. Proximal Tunnels: Longitudinal bony tunnels (2–3) drilled from the superior pole to the fracture site in the large proximal fragment.
  2. Distal Engagement: If distal fragments are large enough, corresponding tunnels are drilled. If too small/comminuted, wire is passed directly through the patellar tendon substance, hugging the inferior pole.
  3. Vertical Reduction: Separate wires are passed through tunnels and tightened at the superior pole, creating a vertical vector that pulls inferior fragments up without crushing.[2]

  • Union Rate: 100%, with healing typically achieved by 7–10 weeks.[3]

  • Hardware Irritation: Effectively 0% — wire knots buried in quadriceps tendon at superior pole.[8]

6 Suture Anchor (SA) Fixation

Suture anchors represent the modern, "biological" approach, eliminating transosseous metal wires entirely.[15]

6.1 Technique

Typically 2–3 anchors loaded with high-strength polyethylene sutures (e.g., FiberWire, Ethibond) are implanted into the cancellous bed of the proximal patellar fragment. Sutures are passed distally and woven through the patellar tendon using a Krackow or locking stitch. Advanced techniques use a "double-row" or "suture bridge" configuration for broad footprint compression.[15]

Key Advantage: Gap Resistance Under cyclic loading (simulating rehabilitation), SA constructs showed significantly less elongation (6.83 mm) compared to transosseous tunnel sutures (13.30 mm).[12] The rigid anchor point minimizes the "bungee cord" effect, maintaining fracture reduction more reliably over time.

6.3 Clinical & Economic Implications

  • Implant Removal Rate: 0% in SA groups vs. up to 100% in TBW groups.[15]
  • Soft Tissue Irritation: Zero cases reported.[15]
  • Cost-Benefit: While unit cost of an anchor exceeds K-wires, total cost of care is often lower when factoring in the avoided second surgery for hardware removal.[19]

7 Vertical Wiring vs. Separate Vertical Wiring

It is critical to distinguish between generic "Vertical Wiring" (VW) and the specific "Separate Vertical Wiring" (SVW) technique.

  • VW: Often refers to a continuous loop passed through vertical tunnels. A single loop may not provide uniform compression across a wide, comminuted fracture line.
  • SVW: Uses multiple independent loops. This modularity allows individual tensioning of each wire, accommodating the irregular fracture shape.

A meta-analysis comparing Transosseous (KT) suturing and VW found no significant difference in bone union complications (KT: 5.7% vs VW: 3.0%). However, supplementary fixation (augmentation) significantly reduced complications for both techniques.[14]

10 Postoperative Management & Rehabilitation

The choice of fixation dictates the rehabilitation protocol:

  • Rigid Fixation (Basket Plate, Combined SVW): Immediate passive ROM, often early active ROM. The high load-to-failure threshold (>300 N) provides a safety buffer against accidental quadriceps contraction.
  • Standard Fixation (SVW, SA): Immediate passive motion (CPM); hinged knee brace locked in extension for ambulation (4–6 weeks). Active extension delayed until radiographic healing evidence.
  • Comparison to Patellectomy: Fixation protocols are more demanding short-term (protection of repair) compared to patellectomy (immediate mobilization possible), but the long-term functional ceiling is significantly higher with fixation.

11 Consensus & Future Directions

11.1 Preservation is Paramount

The Orthopaedic Community's Firm Consensus Preservation of the inferior pole is the standard of care. Partial patellectomy should be abandoned as primary treatment for reconstructable fractures. The biomechanical penalties of patella baja and decreased extensor power are too high a price for surgical simplicity.[10]

11.2 The Shift from TBW to SVW and SA

There is a clear trend away from Tension Band Wiring for inferior pole fractures — the technique is biomechanically flawed for this fracture pattern (lack of compression) and carries unacceptable morbidity regarding soft tissue irritation.

  • SVW is the logical successor for surgeons preferring wire techniques, aligning with the biomechanical vectors of the extensor mechanism and significantly reducing patella baja and irritation.
  • Suture Anchors are rapidly becoming the preferred option for "biological fixation," offering a "fix and forget" solution with zero hardware removal rates and excellent gap resistance.

11.3 Recommendation

Evidence-Based Recommendation For the active patient with a comminuted inferior pole fracture, Separate Vertical Wiring (preferably augmented with cerclage) or Double-Row Suture Anchor fixation are the superior evidence-based choices. Both provide stability for early motion while minimizing complications. The Basket Plate remains valuable for the specific niche of osteoporotic fractures where screw purchase is limiting.

12 Conclusion

The management of inferior pole patellar fractures has evolved from a destructive philosophy of excision to a reconstructive philosophy of preservation. The literature unequivocally supports this shift, demonstrating that preserving the inferior pole prevents the debilitating sequelae of patella baja and quadriceps weakness.

Among fixation options, Separate Vertical Wiring and Suture Anchors have emerged as the dominant techniques, offering a distinct advantage over traditional Tension Band Wiring in terms of biomechanical stability, soft tissue safety, and the elimination of secondary surgeries. Surgeons treating these injuries must prioritize techniques that not only achieve union but also respect the delicate soft tissue envelope and the precise biomechanics of the patellofemoral joint.
