1. Embryology & Postnatal Development of the Hip Joint

1.1 Embryological Formation

The hip joint originates from a condensation of primitive mesenchymal cells in the developing limb bud. By the sixth week of gestation, cartilaginous precursors (anlagen) of the ilium, ischium, pubis, and femur are outlined.(Review)

A pivotal event occurs around the seventh week: a cleft forms via programmed cell death (apoptosis) within the mesenchymal inter-zone between femoral and pelvic cartilaginous models, creating the primordial joint cavity and establishing the ball-and-socket architecture.(Review)

By the eleventh week, the hip joint is macroscopically identifiable with a spherical cartilaginous femoral head, a cup-shaped acetabulum, and rudimentary stabilising structures (labrum, ligamentum teres, transverse acetabular ligament). At this stage, the joint is fully formed in cartilage and dislocation can theoretically occur for the first time.

The acetabulum is formed from the convergence of three pelvic bones at the triradiate cartilage (Y-shaped junction), which serves as the primary growth centre for the developing socket. Ossification of the femoral shaft begins around the eighth week, but the femoral head, neck, and trochanters remain cartilaginous until well after birth.

1.2 Postnatal Ossification & Growth of the Proximal Femur

At birth, the proximal femur is almost entirely cartilaginous. The secondary ossification centre of the capital femoral epiphysis does not typically appear on radiographs until 4–6 months of postnatal life — a critical milestone marking the transition from ultrasound to radiographic assessment.

Three distinct physes govern proximal femoral growth:

  • Capital femoral physis — longitudinal growth of the femoral neck
  • Trochanteric physis — development of the greater trochanter
  • Femoral neck isthmus — lateral width of the neck

A critical parameter is the caput-collum-diaphyseal (CCD) angle (neck-shaft angle, NSA). In the neonate, this angle averages approximately 150°. Through normal muscle forces and weight-bearing, it progressively decreases (physiological varusization) to the adult range of 125–135° by skeletal maturity.

1.3 Acetabular Development

The triradiate cartilage contributes approximately 70% of acetabular expansion in diameter and depth; the remaining 30% comes from appositional growth at the acetabular rim (acetabular ring epiphysis). This growth centre begins to fuse around 11 years in girls and 12 years in boys, after which the socket's capacity for intrinsic remodelling diminishes significantly.

1.4 The Imperative of Concentric Reduction

Key Principle — Mutual Induction: Normal, congruent growth of the femoral head and acetabulum are reciprocally linked. The femoral head acts as a dynamic mold, providing mechanical stimulus for acetabular deepening. Without concentric contact ("containment"), the acetabulum remains shallow and dysplastic. This is the "use it or lose it" paradigm of hip development.

2.3 Pathoanatomic Changes

Acetabular Changes: The socket fails to deepen and remains shallow with increased roof obliquity. In chronic dislocation, a fibrocartilaginous "false acetabulum" (neoacetabulum) may form on the iliac wing.

Soft-Tissue Changes:

  • Joint capsule — stretched, lax, redundant; may become hourglass-shaped due to iliopsoas tendon constriction
  • Labrum — may hypertrophy or become inverted into the joint ("limbus"), creating a mechanical block to reduction
  • Pulvinar — hypertrophied fibrofatty tissue filling the acetabulum
  • Ligamentum teres — elongated and thickened, obstructing reduction
Self-Perpetuating Cycle: Initial instability → failure of acetabular deepening → worse containment → greater displacement → secondary soft-tissue obstructions → potentially irreducible dislocation. This reframing of DDH as a progressive disease process is essential for understanding the rationale for early intervention.

3. Development of Coxa Valga in the Dysplastic Hip

3.1 Definition

Coxa Valga is defined as a pathological increase in the neck-shaft angle (NSA / CCD angle). In older children/adolescents, an NSA > 140° is considered pathologically valgus. Coxa Valga is characteristic of DDH and various neuromuscular conditions (e.g., cerebral palsy).

3.2 Mechanobiological Basis — The Hueter-Volkmann Law

The Hueter-Volkmann law states that sustained compression inhibits physeal growth, while reduced compression accelerates it. In DDH:

  1. Altered joint mechanics: Femoral head is subluxated superolaterally, shifting the joint reaction force vector
  2. Asymmetric stress distribution: Medial physis experiences high octahedral shear stress (growth promoter); lateral physis experiences high hydrostatic compressive stress (growth inhibitor)
  3. Differential physeal growth: Medial side grows faster than lateral side
  4. Valgus deformity: Progressive increase in NSA; normal varusisation is arrested and reversed
  5. Potential premature physeal closure in severe cases (high Tönnis angle, large Reimer's migration index)

3.3 Concomitant Deformity: Femoral Anteversion

DDH frequently coexists with excessive femoral anteversion (angle of femoral neck relative to condyles in the transverse plane). The combination of valgus + anteversion creates a highly unstable mechanical situation, often manifesting as an in-toeing gait. This deformity typically requires a derotational component added to the varus osteotomy for true 3D stability.

5. Principles of Surgical Management

5.1 Goals of Surgery

  1. Achieve concentric reduction (closed or open)
  2. Maintain stable reduction (osteotomies to address bony deformities)
  3. Promote normal development (reduced femoral head as template for acetabular remodelling)
  4. Prevent long-term sequelae (osteoarthritis)

5.2 Femoral Surgery: Varus Derotation Osteotomy (VDRO)

Indications: NSA > 140° (Coxa Valga) and/or excessive femoral anteversion compromising stability.

Technique:

  • Intertrochanteric/subtrochanteric osteotomy
  • Varus correction: Medially based wedge resected → NSA reduced to ~110–120°
  • Derotation: Distal fragment internally rotated to correct anteversion
  • Fixed with pediatric hip plate and screws

Biomechanical effect: Femoral head directed more medially/inferiorly → increased containment; improved abductor lever arm.

5.4 Combined & Staged Procedures

In many children of walking age, a single-stage combined procedure is preferred: anterior open reduction + pelvic osteotomy (Salter or Dega) + VDRO via separate lateral approach. In high dislocations, a femoral shortening osteotomy may be added to the VDRO to relax soft tissues and reduce the risk of avascular necrosis.

6. Long-Term Outcomes & Complications

6.1 Avascular Necrosis (AVN)

The most devastating complication. Incidence ranges from 6% to 63% depending on age, severity, and treatment type (source ref [9]).

Prevention strategies:

  • Gentle, tension-free reduction
  • Femoral shortening osteotomy when significant force required
  • Post-reduction immobilization in "human position" (90–100° flexion, 40–50° abduction)
  • Abduction limited to < 55° ("safe zone" of Ramsey)

6.3 Hip Survivorship & THA Conversion

A large retrospective study (mean follow-up 30.5 years) found 20% of surgically treated hips required total hip arthroplasty (THA).(IV, inferred)

Key predictors of THA conversion:

  • Age at treatment: Patients treated after age 4 had 50% survivorship at 35 years vs. 85% for those treated earlier
  • Severity of deformity: Need for femoral osteotomy = significant risk factor; 57% of hips requiring both pelvic + femoral osteotomies progressed to THA
  • Previous failed treatment = higher THA risk
Critical Insight from Source: For many patients with late-diagnosed DDH, THA in middle adulthood is a common and expected outcome. The primary objective of childhood surgery is better framed as long-term hip preservation — a "lifeboat" to delay, rather than definitively prevent, end-stage arthritis.

8. Conclusion

The relationship between DDH and Coxa Valga exemplifies how abnormal mechanical forces dictate pathological skeletal development. DDH disrupts the fundamental principle of mutual induction between femoral head and acetabulum. The resulting instability creates an asymmetric loading environment across the proximal femoral physis, where — governed by mechanotransduction principles — medial physeal growth is accelerated while lateral growth is inhibited, producing the progressive valgus deformity.

Diagnosis is age-stratified: ultrasound in infants, radiography in older children. Surgical management must address the 3D deformity on both sides of the joint (femoral VDRO + pelvic osteotomy) to achieve concentric, stable reduction. Long-term outcomes, however, must be viewed with realism: AVN remains a significant risk, and even successful surgery may only delay rather than prevent the eventual need for total hip arthroplasty.
