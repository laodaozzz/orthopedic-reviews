1. Introduction — The Diagnostic Conundrum

Cervical spondylosis is a ubiquitous, progressive degenerative cascade affecting intervertebral discs, uncovertebral joints, facet joints, ligamenta flava, and laminae of the cervical spine. Degenerative morphological changes manifest in the vast majority of individuals by the fifth decade of life. I

The cervical motion segments are uniquely susceptible because they must provide extensive multiaxial mobility while simultaneously protecting the spinal cord, cervical nerve roots, and ascending vertebral arteries.

Classical vs. Atypical Presentations

While axial neck pain, cervical spondylotic radiculopathy, and cervical spondylotic myelopathy (CSM) are universally accepted, a profound schism exists regarding atypical manifestations: Not specified

  • Severe vertigo and dizziness
  • Tinnitus and visual disturbances
  • Palpitations, vasomotor headaches
  • Gastrointestinal distress, atypical facial pain
Core Pathophysiological Debate Are symptoms caused by (a) mechanical compression of the vertebrobasilar system? (b) structural instability irritating the cervical sympathetic plexus? or (c) central sensory mismatch from corrupted proprioceptive afferents?

2. Historical Evolution & Taxonomical Frameworks

2.1 Eastern Structural-Autonomic Paradigm

The Chinese expert consensus (formalized 2018, updated 2024–2025) explicitly includes the vertebral artery and sympathetic nerve network as structures vulnerable to spondylotic pathology: II

  • Vertebral Artery Type Cervical Spondylosis (CSA): Degenerative morphological changes mechanically compromise the vertebral artery within the transverse foramina (C6–C1). Clinical hallmark: vertebrobasilar insufficiency provoked by dynamic cervical rotation/extension → rotational vertigo, drop attacks, diplopia, ataxia, nausea.
  • Sympathetic Cervical Spondylosis (SCS): Mechanical instability (C3–C5) or inflammatory mediators from disrupted discs chronically irritate the posterior cervical sympathetic chain or the sympathetic plexus enveloping the vertebral artery → autonomic dysregulation and reflex vasoconstriction.

2.2 Western Neuro-Otological Paradigm

Western literature recognizes four distinct hypotheses for cervical-origin vertigo: II

  1. Proprioceptive Cervical Vertigo — Sensory mismatch between corrupted cervical proprioceptive afferents and normal visual/vestibular inputs (most widely accepted in the West).
  2. Rotational Vertebral Artery Vertigo (Bow Hunter's Syndrome) — Direct equivalent of CSA; dynamic positional occlusion of the vertebral artery.
  3. Migraine-Associated Cervicogenic Vertigo — Shared central sensitization via the trigeminocervical nucleus.
  4. Barré-Lieou Syndrome — Direct Western counterpart of SCS; described in the 1920s by Barré and Liéou as "Posterior Cervical Sympathetic Syndrome."

2.3 Resurrection of Barré-Lieou Syndrome

Modern EBM literature indicates Barré-Lieou syndrome has been "resurrected recently by increased scientific evidence." Histological studies demonstrate that sustained mechanical stress and chronic inflammation in degenerative cervical tissues lead to increased local expression of nerve growth factor (NGF), triggering pathological sympathetic nerve sprouting within the posterior longitudinal ligament. This generates a state of autonomic overdrive with markedly increased norepinephrine levels, providing a biologically plausible mechanism for the phenomena observed historically. II

5. Advancements in Objective Diagnostic Modalities

5.1 Limitations of Static Imaging

Standard CTA and MRA are highly sensitive for fixed abnormalities (atherosclerotic plaques, fixed ostial stenoses, dissections) but inherently limited for motion-induced pathologies. A systematic review demonstrated: I

  • Static CTA for 50–99% vertebral artery stenosis: Sensitivity 100% (95% CI: 15.8–100), Specificity 95.2% (95% CI: 83.8–99.4), DOR 81
  • MRA for complete occlusion: Sensitivity 89.5%, Specificity 99.6%, DOR 429.7
  • However, in CSA / Bow Hunter's Syndrome, static supine imaging is frequently entirely unremarkable, yielding high false-negative rates.

5.2 Dynamic CTA and Dynamic MRA

Specialized dynamic protocols incorporate dual/multi-phase acquisitions: baseline scan in neutral position, then acquisitions with head rotation/flexion/extension to the "critical head position" triggering ischemic symptoms. These provide three critical diagnostic data points: II

  1. Hemodynamic Alteration: Real-time visualization of luminal narrowing, kinking, or signal void during provocative rotation.
  2. Structural Identification: Multi-planar depiction of the exact structure causing dynamic stenosis (uncovertebral osteophyte, herniated disc, ligamentous bands, atlantoaxial subluxation).
  3. Collateral Assessment: Evaluation of contralateral vertebral artery and posterior communicating arteries for compensatory cerebrovascular reserve.

Advanced 3D-printed anatomical models from dynamic CTA datasets provide invaluable hands-on insight for surgical planning.

5.3 Dynamic Catheter-Directed Digital Subtraction Angiography (dcDSA)

DcDSA remains the definitive gold standard for diagnosing dynamic vascular occlusion. A systematic review of 104 cases found: I

  • Definitive diagnosis established in 102/104 cases (98.1%)
  • Zero procedural complications directly related to dynamic maneuvers
  • Ability to synchronize exact moment of arterial occlusion with onset of clinical symptoms (nystagmus, vertigo, presyncope)

Among 102 successfully diagnosed cases, 38 were "atypical presentations" frequently missed by non-invasive imaging: I

  • 14 patients: dynamic occlusion during neck flexion/extension (not rotation)
  • 8 patients: bilateral simultaneous vertebral artery occlusion during a single maneuver
  • 3 patients: multiple independent compression sites along a single artery
  • 4 patients: anomalous vertebral artery entry above C6
  • 1 patient: paradoxical occlusion in neutral position with recanalization on contralateral tilt

5.4 Diagnostic Stellate Ganglion Block (SGB)

The stellate ganglion (formed by fusion of inferior cervical and first thoracic sympathetic ganglia in ~80% of individuals, measuring ~2.5 × 1 × 0.5 cm) is the primary conduit for sympathetic innervation to the head, neck, and upper extremities. II

A diagnostic SGB temporarily halts both preganglionic and postganglionic sympathetic transmission, causing a precipitous drop in regional norepinephrine. If atypical symptoms are mediated by cervical sympathetic hyperactivity, a well-placed SGB provides profound but temporary symptomatic resolution.

Technical Validation of SGB A complete, clinically valid SGB is confirmed by: (1) ipsilateral Horner's syndrome (ptosis, miosis, anhidrosis); (2) conjunctival injection; (3) measurable increase in regional skin perfusion index. If these objective autonomic signs are verified AND the patient reports >50% reduction in atypical symptoms, the diagnosis of sympathetically maintained SCS is robustly supported.

Safety Evolution: Ultrasound-Guided SGB

Historical landmark-based blind SGB carried formidable risks. A systematic review (1990–2018) identified 260 adverse events, including one death from massive hematoma and one case of quadriplegia from epidural abscess. I

Modern ultrasound-guided SGB revolutionized safety and reliability:

  • Real-time visualization of longus colli, fascial planes, thyroid gland, and critical vessels
  • Essentially eliminates intravascular injection risk
  • Commonly performed at C6 level (Chassaignac's tubercle) to avoid pneumothorax risk
  • Required anesthetic volume reduced from 6–8 mL (blind) to 2–4 mL (US-guided), maximizing diagnostic purity

6. Divergence in International Guidelines

6.1 Bárány Society Position (2022)

The premier international neuro-otology consensus body refrains from proposing any diagnostic criteria for clinical use outside strictly controlled research environments. Expert consensus

Exclusion criteria — cervicogenic dizziness is definitively excluded if:

  1. No accompanying neck pain, stiffness, or physical discomfort
  2. Dizziness ever occurs spontaneously (independent of head/neck movement)
  3. Dizziness is exclusively positional (gravity-related, suggesting BPPV rather than cervical rotation)

6.2 Chinese Association of Rehabilitation Medicine Guidelines

Chinese consensus fully embraces CSA and SCS via a holistic diagnostic triad: Expert consensus

  1. Clinical Symptom Scoring: Structured 0–54 point severity scale
  2. Provocative Physical Signs: Symptom reproduction upon forced cervical rotation/extension
  3. Advanced Imaging: Dynamic flexion-extension X-rays + functional MRA/CTA/TCD

Standard-of-care front-line treatments include targeted manual therapy (Tui Na), acupotomy, heat-sensitive moxibustion, acupoint catgut embedding at stellate ganglion, and herbal formulas.

6.3 JOA, NASS, and ASIPP

Major structural spine societies (JOA 2020/2024, NASS, ASIPP) focus predominantly on radiculopathy and CSM, offering little to no formal algorithmic guidance for isolated atypical autonomic/vascular symptoms absent hard cord/nerve root compression. Expert consensus

7. Evidence-Based Treatment: Conservative vs. Surgical

7.1 Conservative Management — Biomechanical Equilibrium Theory

The cervical spine's structural equilibrium depends on a static system (vertebrae, discs, facet joints, ligaments) and a dynamic system (layered cervical musculature). Chronic muscle spasticity and lactic acid accumulation cause dynamic disequilibrium and micro-level joint instability, irritating proprioceptive and sympathetic neural networks. II

Three-dimensional finite element analysis (FEA) modeling validates manipulative techniques: II

Systematic reviews demonstrate moderate-to-low quality evidence supporting manual therapy for significantly reducing cervicogenic dizziness intensity, frequency, and impact. Experimental models show cervical rotational manipulation improves autonomic function by reducing localized sympathetic tone and normalizing heart rate variability (HRV). I

7.2 Broad Systematic Reviews: Surgery vs. Conservative Care

A Cochrane-style systematic review of surgery vs. conservative care for generic neck pain concluded: I

  • "Very low-quality evidence" supporting general superiority of surgery
  • ACDF showed no statistically significant difference vs. physiotherapy or collar use for long-term pain/functional outcomes
  • Benefit of surgery not clearly demonstrated due to high risk of bias, high drop-out ratios, and lack of blinding
Contextual Caveat These broad reviews primarily aggregate data on non-specific axial neck pain and standard radiculopathy, fundamentally diluting specific data concerning patients selected explicitly for atypical autonomic or vascular symptoms.

7.4 ACDF vs. Cervical Arthroplasty (CA)

A 5-year retrospective comparison of 140 patients (78 ACDF, 62 CA) with cervical spondylosis and severe atypical symptoms: III

  • Both procedures showed equivalent improvement in generic PROMs (VAS, SF-36, NDI; p < 0.001)
  • ACDF achieved 97% solid radiographic fusion rate
  • ACDF showed statistically superior resolution of headache and vertigo vs. CA (p < 0.0001)
  • Supports the biomechanical instability-driven hypothesis: rigid arthrodesis eliminates micro-motion irritating the sympathetic plexus, while motion-preserving arthroplasty does not

9. Consensus Indications for Surgical Decompression

Surgery is indicated only when all of the following rigorous criteria are met: II

  1. Strict Refractory Nature: Symptoms unremitting, progressively worsening, completely non-responsive to minimum 6–12 weeks of comprehensive conservative management.
  2. Objective Diagnostic Confirmation: For CSA — dynamic CTA or dcDSA confirming dynamic vertebral artery occlusion/flow limitation. For SCS — successful US-guided diagnostic SGB providing profound transient symptom relief.
  3. Pathological Structural Correlation: Significant degenerative disc disease, severe uncovertebral osteophytosis, or dynamic angular/translational instability (typically C3–C5) on MRI and dynamic flexion-extension radiographs.
  4. Absolute Exclusion of Primary Pathologies: Documented exclusion of primary cardiac disease (for cervical angina), peripheral vestibular disorders (Ménière's, vestibular neuritis), and central neurological causes (posterior fossa stroke, MS) via multidisciplinary clearance.

10. Synthesis & Future Outlook

CSA and SCS — historically marginalized as nebulous "Cervicogenic Dizziness" or psychosomatic "Barré-Lieou Syndrome" — are increasingly validated by modern pathophysiological models and advanced diagnostics. II

Key advances:

  • Dynamic CTA and gold-standard dcDSA objectively capture transient motion-induced vertebrobasilar ischemia invisible to static imaging
  • US-guided SGB provides reliable, low-risk functional confirmation of sympathetically mediated autonomic overdrive
  • Neurochemical research (NGF-mediated sympathetic sprouting) provides biologically plausible mechanisms

Persistent challenges:

  • International guidelines remain distinctly fractured between Eastern and Western paradigms
  • High-quality, multi-center, randomized, blinded controlled trials are still urgently needed
  • The 1.6% absolute incidence difference, while statistically significant, demands careful patient selection

For the subset of patients with severe, intractable, objectively verifiable atypical symptoms, targeted evidence increasingly supports solid arthrodesis via ACDF as superior to both conservative management and motion-preserving arthroplasty — provided the most stringent, objective diagnostic and exclusionary criteria are meticulously applied.
