I. Introduction: The Digital Revolution in Orthopedic Trauma Surgery

Transforming fracture care from 2D approximation to 3D precision

A. The Paradigm Shift: From 2D Art to 3D Science

Historically, preoperative planning in orthopedic trauma relied on two-dimensional (2D) radiographs and acetate templates, demanding significant mental reconstruction and intraoperative improvisation. Not specified in source This traditional approach has been characterized as more of an "art" than a science, subject to the individual surgeon's spatial reasoning and tactile feedback. [2]

The advent of advanced medical imaging and computational software has catalyzed a paradigm shift towards Virtual Surgical Planning (VSP) — a three-dimensional (3D), data-driven science. [4] VSP leverages high-resolution CT or MRI data to create a patient-specific, interactive 3D model of the anatomy. This virtual replica allows the surgical team to:

  • Diagnose complex pathologies with enhanced spatial understanding
  • Simulate the entire surgical procedure in a pressure-free digital environment
  • Anticipate challenges before the patient enters the operating room [4]
📊 Clinical Benefits of VSP

Well-documented improvements include:

  • Enhanced accuracy of fracture reduction and implant placement [4]
  • Reduced surgical time and intraoperative blood loss [6]
  • Minimized fluoroscopy exposure [6]
  • More predictable procedure and potentially faster recovery [1]
⚠ Source limitation: The provided document is primarily a technical workflow guide rather than a clinical outcomes study. Evidence levels for clinical benefit claims are inferred from the referenced study designs (review articles, retrospective studies). No RCTs comparing VSP to traditional planning are explicitly cited within this source document.

B. The Materialise Mimics Ecosystem: An Integrated Platform Approach

A comprehensive orthopedic workflow is not performed within a single software program but across an integrated ecosystem of specialized tools. [8] The power of the platform lies in the seamless, bidirectional data exchange between its core components:

1
Mimics Core Foundational software for medical image processing. Handles segmentation — converting 2D DICOM stacks into patient-specific 3D models. Optimized for voxel-based image data (thresholding, region growing, mask editing). Includes anatomical measurement tools. [10]
2
3-matic Medical The virtual surgical and engineering workbench for "engineering on anatomy." Handles virtual fracture reduction, plate positioning/contouring, and design of patient-specific implants and surgical guides. Geared towards geometric operations on 3D meshes (STL files). [9, 12]
3
Mimics Flow Cloud-based collaborative backbone. Facilitates secure case storage, version control, team sharing, and access to AI-driven segmentation and extended reality (XR) visualization. [9, 15]
⚠ Critical Architectural Insight

The distinction between Mimics Core (interpreting pixel/voxel data) and 3-matic Medical (manipulating geometric/mesh data) reflects a fundamental difference in technology and purpose. Successful planning depends on leveraging each program's strengths and understanding the necessary data flow between them. [8]

II. Phase 1: From Patient Scan to Virtual Anatomy

Foundational segmentation — the most critical phase of the workflow

A. Data Acquisition and Import

The quality of the final 3D model is inherently limited by the source image quality. [16] For orthopedic applications, high-resolution CT scans are the standard.

📋 Best Practices for CT Acquisition
  • Thin axial slices: ideally ≤ 1 mm
  • No gaps between slices
  • Standard DICOM format import into Mimics Core [16]

Step-by-Step: Importing DICOM Data

1
Launch & New Project — From the File menu, select New Project Wizard. [11]
2
Navigate to DICOM Directory — Ensure all images for a single series are in one folder. [11]
3
Convert — Select the desired series and click Convert. [14]
4
Verify Orientation — Confirm correct anatomical orientation (Anterior/Posterior/Left/Right) in all four viewports. Adjust if necessary before segmentation. [14]

B. The Principle of Segmentation: Hounsfield Units (HU)

Tissue differentiation in CT is based on Hounsfield Units (HU), a quantitative scale of radiodensity. [19] Distilled water = 0 HU; air = −1024 HU.

Table 1: Typical Hounsfield Unit Ranges for Tissue Types
Tissue Type HU Range Relevance
Air −1024 HU Background / lung parenchyma
Fat ≈ −100 HU Medullary canal, soft tissue
Water / Soft Tissue 0 – 100 HU Muscle, organs
Trabecular (Spongy) Bone ≈ 200 – 700 HU Cancellous bone; often requires Fill Long Bones to capture
Cortical (Dense) Bone ≈ 700 – 2000+ HU Cortical shell; primary segmentation target
Metal Implants Very high (up to 3071 HU) Existing hardware; may produce artifacts

Data derived from source document [19].

C. The Segmentation Triad: Method Comparison

Mimics offers a spectrum of bone segmentation tools balancing speed, automation, and manual control. [20]

Detailed: Manual/Semi-Automated Method

  1. Thresholding: Segment → Thresholding → select "Bone (CT)" preset (lower bound ~226 HU, upper bound 3071 HU). Creates a mask of all pixels in this density range. [18]
  2. Region Growing: Click inside the target bone to isolate connected pixels. Creates a new, isolated mask. [19]
  3. Mask Editing & Refinement:
    • Morphology Operations: Open (removes small islands) and Close (fills small holes) [11]
    • Manual Editing: Multiple Slice Edit, Draw, Erase for slice-by-slice modification [19]

Detailed: CT Bone Wizard

  1. Launch via Segment → CT Bone Wizard [23]
  2. Place seed points within the target bone and in bones to be excluded [11]
  3. Adjust Seed Threshold and Sensitivity; enable Fill Long Bones for intramedullary canal [11, 23]
  4. Calculate and review the resulting mask [23]

Detailed: AI-Enabled Segmentation

Upload DICOM data to Mimics Flow → AI automatically identifies and segments predefined anatomical structures (e.g., femur, tibia, pelvis) with high accuracy → Download resulting mask to Mimics Core for review. [7]

D. Finalizing the 3D Model

Navigate to Segment → Calculate 3D from Mask. Select the desired mask and quality setting:

  • Optimal: Advanced smoothing and triangle reduction — recommended for final models [19]
  • Low/Medium: Faster calculation for quick previews [19]
🔒 Mandatory Quality Control

Before proceeding to fracture reduction, meticulously compare the 3D model against the original 2D DICOM images in all three planes. Make the model semi-transparent and overlay it on the scans. Any deviation represents an error that will compromise the entire downstream plan. [19]

III. Phase 2: Reconstructing the Puzzle — Virtual Fracture Reduction

Translating anatomical knowledge into a precise geometric plan

A. Isolating Fragments in Mimics Core

For complex, comminuted fractures, each fragment must become its own distinct 3D object:

  1. Duplicate the Mask for each fragment to be isolated
  2. Separate Fragments using Edit Mask tools (primarily Erase) or Split Mask for automatic non-contiguous separation [24]
  3. Calculate 3D Parts from each isolated fragment mask with descriptive names (e.g., "Femur_Shaft_Proximal") [13]

B. Exporting to 3-matic Medical

  1. Select all fragment parts in Mimics → export as STL files
  2. Launch 3-matic → File → Import Part → import all fragment STLs [13]

C. Anatomical Reduction Strategies

Method 1: Manual Reduction (Surgeon-Driven)

Relies on the operator's anatomical expertise. Primary method for bilateral fractures or when no contralateral template is available.

  1. Identify a large, primary fragment as the fixed anchor
  2. Use Interactive Translate and Interactive Rotate (Align menu) to position each moving fragment [13]
  3. Iteratively fine-tune alignment based on fracture lines, cortical contours, and rotational alignment

Method 2: Mirrored Contralateral Template (Anatomy-Driven)

Gold-standard technique for unilateral fractures — uses the patient's own healthy anatomy as an objective guide. Expert Opinion

  1. Import the 3D model of the uninjured contralateral bone → use Mirror tool to create an anatomical target [13]
  2. Use Global Registration for coarse, automated alignment of the main fragment to the template
  3. Use N-Point Registration or interactive manipulation for precise surface matching [13]
💡 Clinical Implication for Imaging Protocol

For complex unilateral fractures (especially articular fractures or malunion corrections), prospectively request that the CT scan includes the healthy contralateral anatomy. This provides the raw data for the most accurate, objective virtual reduction. [13]

D. Verifying and Unifying the Reduction

  1. Visual Inspection: Examine the assembled bone from all angles — no gaps, overlaps, or malalignments
  2. Boolean Union: Select all aligned fragments → Design menu → Boolean Union → merges into a single "healed" bone model [25]
  3. Mesh Fixing: Run the unified model through Fix Wizard (Fix menu) to repair imperfections, resulting in a clean, manifold mesh [25]

IV. Phase 3: The Fixation Strategy — Hardware Planning

Importing, positioning, and planning orthopedic implants

A. Building the Digital Implant Library

⚠ Common Misconception

Mimics/3-matic does not come pre-loaded with commercial implant libraries. Each institution must build its own library from manufacturer-provided CAD files. [26]

Table 3: Supported CAD Formats and Import Considerations
Format Type Common Source Best For Notes
STL Mesh 3D scanners, other medical software Anatomical models, simple geometry "Dumb" format — surface triangles only. Not ideal for precise implant models.
STEP (.stp, .step) B-Rep (Solid/Surface) CAD software, manufacturers Precise implant models (plates, screws) — Gold standard Rich geometric data → high-quality mesh after conversion. Use high-tessellation settings. [27]
IGES (.igs) B-Rep (Solid/Surface) CAD software, manufacturers Precise implant models — older alternative to STEP Similar quality to STEP. Use high-tessellation settings. [27]

Import Workflow in 3-matic

  1. Obtain CAD files from manufacturer (STEP/IGES preferred)
  2. File → Import Part → select the implant file [12]
  3. Set import options: high-resolution mesh conversion, enable Fix Normals and Automatic Stitching [12]

B. Virtual Plate Placement and Contouring

  1. Coarse Positioning: Interactive Translate/Rotate to approximate location
  2. Fine Alignment: Global Registration for best-fit; N-Point Registration for controlled alignment [30]
  3. Virtual Contouring: Advanced design tools to simulate bending the plate to conform to the patient's bone curvature

C. Virtual Screw Planning (Back in Mimics Core)

🔄 Critical Bidirectional Workflow Step

Screw trajectory planning must be performed in Mimics Core, where the screw trajectory can be overlaid on the original 2D CT scans. This enables precise assessment of bone quality and avoidance of critical structures. [14]

  1. Export reduced bone + plate from 3-matic as STL files
  2. Import back into the original Mimics project → register with original scan data
  3. In MedCAD module → Create Cylinder tool to represent intended screws [14]
  4. Define screw properties: click plate for head position, click second point for tip → edit diameter and length in Properties [31]
  5. Optimize trajectory in all three 2D viewports:
    • Bicortical fixation: Engage both near and far cortex
    • Length measurement: Precise screw length for bicortical purchase without excessive protrusion
    • Collision avoidance: Verify safe distance from nerves, vessels, and adjacent joint spaces [14]
  6. Repeat for every screw in the construct

V. Phase 4: Patient-Specific Implants & Surgical Guides

Beyond standard fixation for complex cases

A. Rationale

Complex fractures with unusual patterns, significant bone loss, or corrective osteotomies may require patient-specific solutions. [2] Standard plates may not fit unique bone contours; custom implants can bridge large defects; patient-specific surgical guides are the key to translating virtual plans into surgical reality with high fidelity. [16]

🎯 The Skill Paradigm Shift

With conventional procedures, surgical skill is weighted towards intraoperative freehand execution. With a patient-specific guide, the guide itself enforces the geometry — entry point, angle, and depth are physically predetermined. This shifts critical skill from hands-on execution to the intellectual process of creating the optimal virtual plan, potentially democratizing highly complex, accurate surgery. [16]

B. Designing a Patient-Specific Fracture Plate

Performed entirely within 3-matic [34]

  1. Define Footprint: Use Create Curve tool to draw desired plate outline on bone surface
  2. Create Surface: Generate a surface patch from the curve that conforms to bone anatomy
  3. Add Thickness: Offset tool → uniform thickness (e.g., 2–3 mm) to create a 3D solid
  4. Design Screw Holes: Create cylinders at desired locations → Boolean Subtraction to cut holes
  5. Refine: Chamfers/fillets for smooth, biocompatible edges

C. Designing a Patient-Specific Surgical Guide

The physical link between virtual plan and patient [14]

  1. Contact Surface: Mark the stable bone area with unique surface features for single-position seating. Copy to new part. [14]
  2. Guide Body: Offset to add thickness → main body [14]
  3. Integrate Drill Guides: Position virtual screw cylinders from planning → Boolean Subtraction to cut aligned drill sleeves [35]
  4. Finalize & Export: Add features (windows, labels) → export as STL for 3D printing in biocompatible material [14]

D. Automating Workflows with Python Scripting

3-matic incorporates a Python scripting engine to automate repetitive design tasks. [10] A script can take a reduced bone model and planned screw trajectories as input and automatically execute the sequence of operations (Offset, Boolean Subtraction, etc.) to generate the final device design. [35] Benefits:

  • Dramatically reduced design time per case
  • High consistency and standardization
  • Reduced human error and lower learning curve [10]

A data-driven feedback loop for continuous improvement

A. Finalizing and Communicating the Plan

The final output is a complete communication package: detailed surgical reports documenting measurements, implant models, and precise screw parameters. [38] This is disseminated via Mimics Flow and Mimics Viewer to align the entire surgical team before the operation. [9]

C. The Future Horizon

🤖 AI-Driven Planning

Future AI algorithms will propose entire surgical plans, learning individual surgeon preferences to provide high-quality initial plans requiring minimal adjustment. [39]

⚙ Biomechanical Simulation (FEA)

Integration of Finite Element Analysis will enable "virtual mechanical testing" — subjecting plans to simulated physiological loads before surgery to optimize implant choice and prevent hardware failure. [11]

🥽 Extended Reality (XR)

VR/AR will allow surgeons to step inside the plan — interacting with full-scale 3D anatomy and rehearsing complex steps in immersive environments. [9]

🔁 The Data-Driven Feedback Loop

VSP creates a powerful cycle: Plan → Execute → Measure → Learn. By comparing preoperative plans with postoperative scans, surgeons receive precise, quantitative feedback on accuracy — identifying tendencies to refine technique or customize future AI-assisted plans. This is the hallmark of a data-driven discipline. [39]
