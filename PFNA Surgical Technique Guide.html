1. Introduction: Evolution and Biomechanical Rationale

The management of unstable trochanteric and subtrochanteric femoral fractures remains a cornerstone challenge in orthopaedic trauma surgery. The demographic shift toward an aging population with reduced bone mineral density has necessitated a departure from traditional fixation methods that rely on screw thread purchase.

The Proximal Femoral Nail Antirotation (PFNA) system emerged as a response to the limitations of the Dynamic Hip Screw (DHS) and earlier generation intramedullary nails (Gamma nail, original PFN). The fundamental innovation lies in its helical blade—a single load-bearing element that achieves rotational and angular stability not by removing bone, but by compacting it. LOE II–III

Key Principle: The success of the PFNA is intrinsically linked to the surgeon's ability to execute a precise, mathematically driven implantation sequence. Failure to adhere to the strict geometric constraints of the targeting system leads to distinct failure modes—primarily cut-out and varus collapse.

1.1 The Biomechanics of Bone Compaction

Unlike a standard lag screw—which cuts a path through trabeculae and relies on shear strength between threads—the PFNA helical blade operates on volumetric compaction. As the blade is hammered into the femoral head, its geometry forces surrounding cancellous bone outward, densifying the bone-implant interface. This creates a circumferential mantle of denser bone that significantly enhances resistance to rotation and varus deviation. LOE II

Clinical and biomechanical studies indicate this compaction mechanism is particularly advantageous in osteoporotic bone, where trabecular structure is sparse. By preserving and compressing existing bone stock, PFNA improves anchorage in the presence of poor bone quality. LOE III

⚠ Critical Warning: The mechanical advantage imposes strict positioning requirements. The blade must be centered to compact bone uniformly. Eccentric placement—particularly ventral or superior positioning—creates stress risers and catastrophic failure. Biomechanical analysis shows ventral placement reduces fixation failure strength by up to 17%. LOE II

2. Preoperative Planning and Patient Positioning

2.1 Radiographic Templating and Implant Selection

Standard AP and lateral radiographs of the affected hip are insufficient; the contralateral, uninjured hip must also be imaged to determine the native Caput Collum Diaphyseal (CCD) angle. The PFNA is available in CCD angles of 125°, 130°, and 135°. Selecting a nail whose CCD angle closely matches the patient's anatomy ensures the helical blade travels along the femoral neck axis. A mismatch forces the blade into a superior or inferior trajectory, compromising the "center-center" position. LOE III

Nail diameter is determined by measuring the isthmus of the femoral canal. The surgeon must also account for the radius of curvature (ROC) of the femur. A significant mismatch between nail ROC and the patient's femoral bow—common in Asian populations—can lead to anterior cortical impingement or iatrogenic shaft fractures. In such cases, PFNA-II (Asian size) or nails with a modified ROC (1.0 m or 1.5 m radius) may be indicated. LOE IV

2.2 Patient Setup and Reduction Maneuvers

The patient is positioned supine on a fracture table. The setup must facilitate unobstructed fluoroscopic imaging in both AP and lateral planes. The unaffected leg is typically abducted and flexed (hemolithotomy) or extended and abducted ("scissor" position) to allow C-arm rotation.

🔑 Fundamental Tenet: The nail is an internal splint, not a reduction tool. Attempting to reduce a fracture using the nail often results in malalignment or comminution.

Closed Reduction Verification

  • AP view: Restore neck-shaft angle via traction and adduction; correct shortening.
  • Lateral view: Anterior cortices of head and neck fragments must be aligned. Sagging (posterior angulation) leads to posterior entry points and anterior blade placement. LOE IV
  • Internal rotation corrects anteversion.

Open Reduction

If closed reduction fails, a limited open reduction is mandatory. Techniques include percutaneous joysticks (Schanz screws), bone hooks, or finger/instrument introduction through the incision. LOE IV

3. Instrumentation Assembly: The Physics of Targeting

The precision of the PFNA procedure relies entirely on the rigidity and alignment of the external targeting device. The "targeting arm" acts as an external reference frame for the internal anatomy of the nail. Any laxity, misalignment, or assembly error propagates to the drill sleeve tip, potentially causing drill impingement on the nail, locking thread damage, or complete miss of the locking hole.

3.1 The Insertion Handle and Connecting Bolt

The insertion handle is typically radiolucent (carbon fiber reinforced PEEK) to allow proximal femur visualization without metal artifact obscuring the entry point or blade path. LOE IV

The Connecting Bolt Mechanism

The connection between nail and handle is mediated by a connecting bolt threading into the proximal end of the nail, pulling the nail surface flush against the handle's mating surface.

  • Orientation Keys: The proximal nail end features notches mating with corresponding keys on the handle. The surgeon must verify the arrow on the nail (R/L) aligns perfectly with the arrow on the handle barrel. LOE IV
  • Torque Application: The connecting bolt must be tightened firmly. A loose connection introduces "play" or toggle.
⚠ Consequence of Laxity: Even a few degrees of toggle at the handle can translate to several millimeters of deviation at the distal targeting arm tip. This can cause the guide wire to impinge on the locking hole or the drill to sheer off metal fragments from the nail, compromising the locking mechanism. LOE II

3.2 The Aiming Arm and Sleeve Assembly

The aiming arm (targeting bow) attaches to the insertion handle. Its geometry is fixed to correspond to the specific CCD angle of the chosen nail (e.g., 130° nail requires 130° aiming arm). Mixing components results in gross targeting error. LOE II

The Sleeve Assembly – "Click" Mechanism

The sleeve consists of three concentric tubes: Protection Sleeve (outermost), Drill Sleeve (middle), and Trocar (innermost).

  1. Screw the buttress nut onto the protection sleeve first.
  2. Insert drill sleeve and trocar into the protection sleeve.
  3. Pass entire assembly through the aiming arm.
  4. Push until a distinct "click" is heard and felt—confirming coaxial alignment with the locking hole.
  5. Spin the buttress nut down to contact the aiming arm for fine cortical compression adjustment.
⚠ Without the click: The sleeve may be angled obliquely, leading to a "skiving" effect where the guide wire glances off the nail rather than passing through it smoothly.

4. Surgical Approach and Entry Point

The entry point for PFNA differs from other nails due to its medial-lateral bend angle (typically 5° or 6°). The incision is made approximately 3–5 cm proximal to the tip of the greater trochanter, in line with the femoral axis. The fascia lata is split longitudinally, and gluteus medius fibers are split bluntly. LOE IV

5. Intraoperative Maneuvers for Helical Blade Positioning

Positioning the helical blade is the most technically demanding and consequential step. The goal is to maximize purchase in the femoral head and minimize cut-out risk.

5.1 Nail Depth and Rotation

Depth Adjustment (AP View)

Nail depth determines the craniocaudal blade position. The target is to align the blade hole axis with the center or slightly inferior aspect of the femoral neck.

  • Too deep → blade too inferior (potential calcar impingement)
  • Too proud → blade too superior (danger zone for cut-out)

Rotational Alignment (Lateral View)

The insertion handle acts as a joystick for internal/external rotation. The surgeon rotates until the nail's anteversion matches the femoral neck's anteversion.

Most PFNA handles have radiopaque alignment lines. When these lines appear parallel/symmetric relative to the protection sleeve in the lateral view, the handle is oriented perfectly lateral—a proxy for nail rotation. LOE IV

5.2 Guide Wire Placement Strategy: Center-Center and TAD

The "Center-Center" position is the gold standard for blade placement. LOE II

AP View Positioning

The guide wire should pass through the inferior half of the femoral neck and end in the center of the femoral head. Placement in the superior half (Cleveland zones 1, 2, 3) is strongly associated with cut-out.

Optimal Zone: Inferior-central position allows the blade to rest on the dense calcar femorale, providing a buttress against varus collapse.

Lateral View Positioning

The wire must be centered in the femoral neck and head (Cleveland zone 5). Recent biomechanical analyses highlight that ventral (anterior) placement is an independent risk factor for failure, reducing fixation strength by up to 17%. LOE II

5.3 Corrective Maneuvers: The Parallel Wire Technique

If the initial guide wire is malpositioned, simply removing it and re-drilling often fails because the new wire follows the old path. LOE IV

  1. Leave the malpositioned wire in place. The PFNA drill sleeve features multiple holes or a "correction guide" allowing a second wire at a defined offset (e.g., 5 mm).
  2. Rotate the correction sleeve around the first wire to dial in the new trajectory (e.g., anterior → central).
  3. Insert the new wire, verify position fluoroscopically in both planes.
  4. Remove the initial wire. This ensures the new tract is fresh and stable.

6. Helical Blade Insertion: Step-by-Step

6.1 Measuring and Opening the Cortex

  1. Measurement: Pass the Direct Measuring Device over the guide wire to the protection sleeve. Read the length directly—this accounts for necessary clearance.
  2. Open the Lateral Cortex: Use the 11.0 mm Cannulated Drill Bit over the guide wire. Limit drilling to the lateral cortex only (drill bit has a mechanical stop abutting the protection sleeve).
⚠ Why Limit Drilling? The biomechanical advantage of PFNA is compaction. Drilling the entire path removes cancellous bone, destroying the compaction effect—the blade would sit in a void rather than a densified bone mantle.

Exception: In young patients or extremely dense bone, full-length drilling or tapping may be required to prevent excessive impaction force that could distract the fracture. LOE II

6.2 Blade Assembly and Impaction

  1. Unlock the Blade: The helical blade is supplied locked. Attach to inserter and unlock (turn counter-clockwise) to allow free helix rotation during insertion. LOE IV
  2. Alignment: Slide blade-inserter assembly over the guide wire. Align specific markings on the inserter with the protection sleeve to ensure correct rotational orientation for the nail's locking hole.
  3. Impaction: Insert using a hammer. The surgeon does not rotate the handle—linear hammer blows cause auto-mechanical rotation of the helix, compacting bone as it advances.
  4. Feedback: Resistance should increase as bone compacts. Sudden loss of resistance may indicate a void or fracture gap.
  5. Stop Point: Impact until the inserter clicks or hits the mechanical stop on the protection sleeve.

6.3 Locking and Intraoperative Compression

Locking: Once fully seated, engage the locking mechanism by turning the impactor handle clockwise to the "LOCK" position. This fixes blade rotation relative to the nail. LOE IV

Intraoperative Compression (Optional):

  • Before removing instruments, turn the buttress nut counter-clockwise to retract the sleeve against the blade head, pulling the femoral head fragment laterally toward the nail shaft.
  • Monitor fluoroscopically—excessive compression can strip purchase or cause medial nail protrusion. LOE IV

7. Cement Augmentation

For patients with severe osteoporosis, the PFNA allows cement augmentation to increase pull-out strength. LOE IV

⚠ Absolute Contraindication: Any communication between the fracture site and the joint space—cement leakage into the joint causes rapid cartilage destruction. Always perform a contrast dye leakage test via the perforated blade and verify fluoroscopically in both planes before injecting cement. LOE IV
