1   Introduction & Epidemiological Context

The exponential increase in spinal instrumentation for degenerative, traumatic, neoplastic, and deformity-related pathologies has driven a parallel rise in postoperative spinal implant infections (PSII), which now rank as the third most common adverse event following spine surgery.[1,2] Level II

PSII encompasses superficial and deep surgical site infections (SSIs) as well as postoperative discitis (POD). These complications contribute disproportionately to patient morbidity, necessitating prolonged hospitalizations, extended intravenous antimicrobial courses, and complex revision surgeries.[1]

Key Risk Factors for PSII

  • Patient-specific: Uncontrolled diabetes mellitus, elevated BMI/obesity, active smoking, immunosuppression
  • Procedure-specific: Multi-level instrumented fusions, long operative time, extensive open exposures, revision surgery
  • Implant-specific: Presence of avascular metallic/polymeric foreign bodies (titanium screws, cobalt-chrome rods, PEEK cages)

2   Pathophysiology & Biofilm Biology

The lumbar spine's unique anatomy and vascular characteristics render it highly susceptible to infection once surgical intervention violates its structural integrity.[9]

2.1 Vascular Anatomy & Primary Infection Seeding

Vertebral arteries form critical end-arteries on either side of the intervertebral disc, granting circulating organisms direct access to the vertebral endplates. This typically leads to primary infection involving two adjacent vertebrae and the intervening disc space.[9] Level II

2.2 Biofilm Formation: The Central Pathogenetic Mechanism

The introduction of avascular implants fundamentally alters the local immunological landscape. Bacteria introduced intraoperatively exhibit a profound affinity for foreign surfaces. The biofilm life cycle proceeds through defined stages:

  1. Initial Reversible Attachment – Planktonic bacteria adhere to implant surfaces
  2. Irreversible Adhesion & Phenotypic Shift – Bacteria downregulate active metabolic pathways and begin synthesizing extracellular polymeric substance (EPS)
  3. Matrix Maturation – The EPS matrix (composed of polysaccharides, structural proteins, and extracellular DNA) completely encapsulates the colony[10]
  4. Persister Cell Formation – The hypoxic, nutrient-deprived core forces innermost bacteria into metabolically dormant "persister" states[6]
  5. Active Dispersion – Dissemination to adjacent sites

Level II

⚠️ Clinical Significance of Biofilms The biofilm architecture physically impedes diffusion of immune cells and antibiotic molecules. Because conventional antibiotics (beta-lactams, cephalosporins) target active cell wall synthesis or ribosomal translation, they are entirely impotent against dormant persister cells.[6] This explains the high failure rates when hardware-associated infections are managed solely with systemic antibiotics without physical biofilm disruption.

3   Consensus Definitions & Diagnostic Criteria (2024–2025)

3.1 Historical Limitations

For decades, clinical research relied on CDC/NHSN surveillance-oriented SSI definitions and ACS NSQIP criteria. These lacked the clinical nuance needed for deep-space and implant-associated infections, resulting in a fragmented research landscape and confounded treatment comparisons.[2,11]

3.2 The EBJIS/MSIS 2025 Consensus Framework

A multispecialty workgroup from the European Bone & Joint Infection Society (EBJIS) and the Musculoskeletal Infection Society (MSIS) established a new, universally accepted consensus definition for PSI. Published in late 2024 and widely adopted in 2025, this was developed via a rigorous modified Delphi consensus process informed by systematic literature reviews.[5] Level II

✅ Pathognomonic Criterion for "Definite" PSI The identification of a phenotypically indistinguishable microorganism from two or more independent deep operative site specimens is designated as a standalone, pathognomonic criterion confirming "definite" PSI without the need for further clinical justification.[5]

In the absence of this pathognomonic finding, specialized combinations of the remaining primary and secondary criteria are utilized to classify cases as "definite" or "probable" PSI. This structural hierarchy balances diagnostic sensitivity and specificity.[5]

4   Advanced Diagnostics: mNGS vs. Traditional Culture

The prompt identification of the causative pathogen is the absolute cornerstone of effective targeted antimicrobial therapy. Traditional tissue culture techniques (TCT) frequently yield false-negative results, particularly in chronic/late-onset infections, low-virulence biofilm-forming pathogens, or when empirical antibiotics have been administered prior to sampling.[6,12]

4.1 Metagenomic Next-Generation Sequencing (mNGS)

mNGS operates at the molecular level, extracting and sequencing all nucleic acids (DNA and RNA) directly from clinical samples, enabling rapid, unbiased identification of a vast spectrum of pathogens—including fastidious, unculturable, and rare organisms.[12] Level II

⚠️ Critical Caveat – Lower Specificity of mNGS The lower pooled specificity of mNGS represents a significant clinical limitation. False positives can originate from: (1) environmental contamination during processing, (2) amplification of transient commensal microbiota, and (3) detection of residual non-viable nucleic acids from eradicated bacteria.[12] Current guidelines strongly advise using mNGS as a powerful adjunct to—not replacement for—conventional culturing, particularly in culture-negative scenarios.

4.2 Hardware Sonication

When infected hardware must be removed, traditional swabbing often fails to capture the offending pathogen. Sonication of explanted hardware—using low-frequency ultrasound in a controlled fluid bath—physically disrupts the biofilm EPS matrix, dislodging embedded bacteria into the surrounding fluid for subsequent culture or mNGS analysis. 5   Clinical Practice Guidelines (2020–2025)

5.1 Infectious Diseases Society of America (IDSA)

The IDSA provides the foundational evidence-based framework for the medical management of vertebral osteomyelitis and complicated postoperative spinal infections. Developed via the GRADE process, key recommendations include:[15,16]

  • Obtain reliable microbiological samples before initiating empirical therapy – to avoid diagnostic masking and culture-negative infections Level I
  • Maintain high suspicion in patients with new/worsening axial pain plus fever, elevated CRP/ESR, or systemic bacteremia
  • Initial intravenous antibiotic induction of 2–6 weeks minimum due to high bacteremia incidence and the need for bone-penetrating concentrations Level I
  • Transition to highly bioavailable oral suppressive agents after the IV phase

5.2 North American Spine Society (NASS)

NASS focuses heavily on surgical prophylaxis, perioperative optimization, and multidisciplinary care frameworks:[18,19]

  • Modifiable risk factor optimization prior to elective instrumentation: strict glycemic control, BMI reduction, smoking cessation Level II
  • Routine broad-spectrum IV antibiotic prophylaxis prior to incision, tailored to institutional antibiogram
  • Integration of minimally invasive spine surgery (MISS) techniques as a preventative strategy—reduced soft tissue trauma, minimized dead space, shorter operative time correlate with lower deep infection rates[6]

5.3 EuroSpine – Structural Quality Assurance

The EUROSPINE Surgical Spine Centre of Excellence (SSCoE) certification mandates strict infrastructural requirements:[22]

  • True 24/7 multidisciplinary care availability
  • Immediate access to advanced imaging, ICUs, and emergency revision spine surgery capability
  • Ensures institutions can manage catastrophic infections rapidly to reduce mortality and morbidity

6   Surgical Strategies: DAIR vs. Implant Removal / Exchange

Definitive management is governed by a complex algorithm balancing source control (infection eradication) against biomechanical necessity (spinal stability). The two critical variables are infection chronicity (time since index surgery) and fusion status.[1] Level II

Decision Algorithm: Postoperative Spinal Implant Infection

  1. Confirm diagnosis using EBJIS/MSIS 2025 criteria (Section 3)
  2. Determine infection timing: Early (<30 days to ~3 months) vs. Late (>3 months post-op)
  3. Assess fusion status: Solid arthrodesis achieved vs. Pseudoarthrosis / No fusion
  4. If early infection + intact hardware + no fusion → DAIR Procedure
  5. If late infection + solid fusion achieved → Complete Implant Removal
  6. If late infection + no fusion → Implant Exchange (single-staged or two-staged)
  7. Initiate targeted antibiofilm antibiotic regimen (Section 9)

6.1 Early-Onset Infection: The DAIR Paradigm

The DAIR (Debridement, Antibiotics, and Implant Retention) procedure is the standard of care for early-onset PSII, when bacteria have not yet established mature biofilms and hardware loosening is absent.[1,6]

DAIR Protocol Components:

  • Aggressive, radical surgical debridement of all necrotic/devitalized/infected soft tissues
  • Copious high-volume pulsed lavage irrigation
  • Meticulous intraoperative collection of multiple deep microbiological specimens from peri-implant space
  • Manual evaluation of construct integrity; selective screw exchange if grossly loosened
  • Strict hardware retention to maintain alignment and facilitate eventual fusion

6.2 Late-Onset Infection: Hardware Removal or Exchange

By the late/chronic stage, mature biofilms completely encase hardware surfaces and are highly recalcitrant to all systemic antibiotics. Chronic inflammation induces osteolysis leading to hardware loosening. DAIR is associated with unacceptably high failure rates in this setting.[1]

7   Intrawound Vancomycin Powder Controversy

While systemic IV antibiotic prophylaxis is universally endorsed, the adjunctive use of intrawound local vancomycin powder (typically 1–2 g doses) has been the subject of intense, highly polarized debate throughout 2020–2025.[26,27]

7.1 Arguments in Favor

  • Achieves massive, supratherapeutic bactericidal concentrations at the surgical site
  • Circumvents systemic toxicity (nephrotoxicity, ototoxicity) and poor bone penetration of IV vancomycin
  • Early retrospective studies/meta-analyses demonstrated reduction in deep SSI rates (baseline rates to reduced rates)[26]
  • Popularized in high-risk procedures: adult spinal deformity (ASD) corrections, multi-level fusions

7.2 Arguments Against (Recent RCT Evidence)

  • Most recent, methodologically rigorous systematic reviews/meta-analyses of large RCTs (2023–2025) indicate no statistically significant decrease in superficial or deep SSI rates with vancomycin powder use in standard open spine surgery[26] Level I
  • Osteoblast cytotoxicity: High local molarities actively inhibit osteoblast proliferation, viability, and migration—threatening bone healing and increasing pseudoarthrosis risk[30]
  • Concerns about epidemiological shift toward Gram-negative or resistant culture-negative SSIs from selective pressure — however, pooled data show vancomycin powder does not increase Gram-negative infection risk (RR [data – source image], 95% CI [data – source image])[26]
🔒 Current Recommendation Given deeply conflicting efficacy data and real concerns regarding osteoblast cytotoxicity, routine ubiquitous use of intrawound vancomycin powder is no longer universally recommended. Its application remains surgeon-dependent and is generally reserved for exceptionally high-risk patient profiles or massive, highly complex exposures.

8   Novel Biofilm Eradication Strategies

8.1 Citrate-Based Advanced Surgical Irrigation (XPERIENCE™)

Standard saline or dilute betadine irrigation is frequently insufficient for mechanically dislodging deeply established biofilms from complex hardware. The XPERIENCE™ system leverages three synergistic biochemical components:[10] Level II

Table 6 – Components of Citrate-Based Irrigation System
ComponentConcentrationMechanism of Action
Citric Acid[Data – source image] g/LChelating agent: sequesters Ca²⁺ and Mg²⁺ cations essential for EPS cross-linking, destabilizing biofilm structure[10]
Sodium Citrate[Data – source image] g/LHigh-capacity physiological buffer: maintains optimal pH for antimicrobial activity while avoiding cytotoxicity to osteoblasts and fibroblasts[10]
Sodium Lauryl Sulfate[Data – source image] g/LPotent surfactant: reduces surface tension, disrupts bacterial aggregates, enhances penetrative depth into hardware crevices[10]

Clinical Efficacy: Up to 6-log reduction in planktonic bacteria and 4–8-log reduction in mature biofilms.[10]

Technique: Pre-implantation cleansing → periodic pressurized irrigation during procedure → unique "no-rinse" protocol before fascial closure (leave residual antimicrobial barrier on hardware).

Indications: Complex multi-level fusions, extensive deformity corrections, patients with compounding infection risk factors.[10]

8.2 Ultrafine-Grained (UFG) Stainless Steel Metallurgy

Materials science has increasingly focused on altering the fundamental physical topography of implants rather than relying on depletable pharmacological coatings. UFG stainless steel, processed under severe plastic deformation to possess nanoscale grain structures, creates an inherently unfavorable environment for bacterial binding.[7,8] Level II

  • Significantly delayed initial bacterial adhesion compared to conventional SUS316L
  • Drastically reduced biofilm maturation at early time points in murine models
  • Relies on physical physics rather than depletable pharmacology—may become standard in next-generation instrumentation

9   Targeted Antibiofilm Pharmacotherapy

In hardware-retention scenarios (post-DAIR or medically inoperable patients), systemic therapy must pivot to specialized antibiofilm combinations capable of penetrating the EPS matrix and killing dormant persister cells.[17,31]

9.1 The Rifampin Imperative

Rifampin remains the foundational, indispensable element of systemic antibiofilm therapy for Gram-positive hardware infections. It uniquely penetrates mature biofilms and directly targets bacterial RNA polymerase of dormant cells regardless of metabolic state.[33]

⚠️ Critical: No Rifampin Monotherapy Rifampin monotherapy rapidly induces single-step chromosomal resistance mutations within days. It must always be administered in strict combination with a highly active companion antibiotic.[32] Retrospective studies show that delaying rifampin introduction (e.g., waiting for drain removal) does not significantly alter outcomes, granting clinicians flexibility.[34]

10.2 Diagnostic & Therapeutic Algorithm

POD Management Algorithm

  1. Serial Serology: Immediate CRP and ESR measurement – reliably and sharply trend upward in active disc space infection[38]
  2. Advanced Imaging: Gadolinium-enhanced MRI (most sensitive/specific) → vertebral endplate edema, disc space narrowing, contrast enhancement[38]
  3. Pathogen Identification: CT-guided percutaneous transpedicular biopsy prior to antibiotics – pre-biopsy empirical antibiotics strictly discouraged[38]
  4. Conservative Management (Mainstay): Strict bed rest + rigid orthosis immobilization + [Data – source image]-week IV antibiotic course → oral transition → promotes spontaneous auto-fusion[38]
  5. Surgical Indications (Salvage): Refractory pain after [Data – source image] weeks of optimized conservative therapy; progressive neurological deficits (suggesting epidural abscess); radiographic worsening / progressive kyphotic deformity[38]
