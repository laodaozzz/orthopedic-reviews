Management of Persistent Intraoperative Medial Clear Space Widening During Ankle Fracture Osteosynthesis

Evidence-Based Algorithm for Diagnosis, Prevention, and Intraoperative Decision-Making — A Comprehensive Educational Summary

1. Introduction & Biomechanical Foundations

The ankle is a highly constrained, congruent synovial hinge joint whose kinematics depend on an intricate equilibrium between osseous architecture and ligamentous tensioning. The tibiotalar articulation transmits physiological loads from the appendicular skeleton to the foot throughout the gait cycle. The overarching objective of ankle fracture osteosynthesis is the exact anatomical restoration of the mortise to reconstitute native kinematics, distribute axial loads properly, and mitigate the risk of post-traumatic osteoarthritis.

Critical Biomechanical Threshold A lateral talar shift of merely 1 mm decreases tibiotalar contact surface area by a minimum of 40%, generating exponential increases in localized cartilage contact pressures — the primary mechanical pathway to rapid, irreversible osteoarthritis.Ref 1

Poor clinical and functional results are directly associated with syndesmotic width differences greater than 1.5 mm. The clinical and radiographic evaluation of medial stability revolves around the medial clear space (MCS) — the interval between the lateral border of the medial malleolus and the medial border of the talar dome. Under normal conditions, the mortise joint space should be perfectly uniform. An abnormal MCS widening is the primary radiographic surrogate for deep deltoid insufficiency, lateral talar subluxation, and syndesmotic instability.Refs 3, 4

Modern intraoperative fluoroscopic evaluation has repeatedly exposed high rates of residual syndesmotic instability following isolated lateral column fixation. Studies indicate that up to 45% of patients with stress-positive SER stage IV or Weber B injuries exhibit gross intraoperative syndesmotic instability following seemingly adequate fibular fixation.Ref 6

2. Anatomy & Pathomechanics of the Deltoid Ligament

The deltoid ligament is the primary medial stabilizer of the ankle — a robust, multifascicular structure extending from the medial malleolus to the talus, calcaneus, and navicular.Ref 7 It is divided into two functionally distinct layers:

  • Superficial Deltoid — composed of the tibionavicular, tibiocalcaneal, and superficial posterior tibiotalar ligaments; primarily resists hindfoot eversion and valgus angulation.Ref 8
  • Deep Deltoid — consists of the deep anterior and posterior tibiotalar ligaments; these intra-articular but extrasynovial fibers run horizontally and serve as the primary restraint to external talar rotation and lateral translation within the mortise.Ref 8

Deltoid ligament injuries occur in approximately 40% of all ankle fractures. While MRI demonstrates 96.3% sensitivity and 97.9% specificity for deep deltoid tears, recent studies caution against using preoperative MRI as the sole predictor of dynamic stability. The behavior of the MCS during dynamic intraoperative stress testing remains the definitive standard.Ref 9

3. Etiology of Persistent Intraoperative MCS Widening

3.1 Fibular Malreduction

The talus faithfully follows the distal fibula — the critical lateral buttress of the mortise. The leading cause of persistent MCS widening is fibular malreduction, manifesting as shortening and/or malrotation.Ref 10

Fluoroscopic Deception Alert A biomechanical study demonstrated that a mere 14.7° of caudal rotation of the fluoroscopic beam can obscure 2 mm of true fibular shortening, producing a "false positive" mortise view. Observers were up to 91% less likely to accurately judge alignment on false-positive images.Ref 11

On lateral radiographs, an anterior-to-posterior fibular gap (A-to-P FG) >1.0 mm yields 100% sensitivity and specificity for predicting MCS widening ≥5.0 mm.Ref 12

3.2 Deltoid Ligament Interposition & Soft Tissue Entrapment

High-energy external rotation forces can cause the deltoid ligament to avulse, retract, and "flip" directly into the medial gutter, creating an impenetrable physical barrier to closed reduction.Refs 10, 13 Additional structures may be incarcerated:

  • Posterior tibial tendon (PTT) dislocation — can physically dislocate from its retromalleolar groove and become incarcerated within the syndesmosis or medial clear space, creating an irreducible ankle.Ref 14
  • Medial malleolar "fleck sign" — a small avulsion fracture that is 99% specific for severe medial instability and should raise immediate suspicion for deltoid avulsion and potential interposition.Ref 2

3.3 Unaddressed Syndesmotic Instability

Epidemiological data indicates syndesmotic injuries occur in:

  • <10% of Weber A fractures
  • ~40–50% of Weber B fractures
  • >80% of Weber C fracture patternsRef 15

If the syndesmosis remains incompetent after fibular plate fixation, the fibula progressively diastases from the tibia, dragging the talus laterally. In Logsplitter injuries, failure to achieve anatomical reduction was associated with an 85.7% post-traumatic osteoarthritis rate versus 33.3% in those anatomically reduced.Ref 16

3.4 Posterior Malleolar Contributions

The posterior malleolus is increasingly recognized for its vital ligamentous contributions. The PITFL — the strongest syndesmotic ligament — inserts directly onto the posterior malleolar fragment. Unaddressed posterior malleolar fractures:

  • Leave the posterior syndesmosis entirely incompetent
  • Fragments as small as 5–25% of the articular surface can lead to radiographic osteoarthritis in up to 41% of cases if left with an articular step-offRef 2
  • Rigid anatomical fixation of the posterior malleolus restores up to 70% of native syndesmotic stiffness, often obviating the need for trans-syndesmotic screwsRef 2

5. Evidence-Based Prevention Strategies

5.1 Patient Positioning

Supine position with ipsilateral hip bump is standard for isolated injuries; the bump internally rotates the hip to direct the foot vertically, enabling true AP/mortise fluoroscopy without excessive C-arm rotation.Ref 17

For posterior malleolar fixation, the prone position is increasingly favored, providing direct visualization of the posterior plafond and enabling posterior antiglide plates — biomechanically vastly superior to anterior-to-posterior percutaneous lag screws.Refs 2, 15

5.2 The Critical Medial Incision

A liberal anteriorly placed medial incision is a critical surgical pearl. Whenever the MCS fails to close or the mortise feels "rubbery," blind lateral-to-medial clamping should be abandoned in favor of direct medial exploration. This serves dual purposes:Ref 10

  • Physical extraction of interposed deltoid ligament or dislocated PTT
  • Direct visualization of the medial talar dome for osteochondral lesions

5.3 Implant Selection Considerations

  • Posterolateral antiglide plating is biomechanically superior to lateral plating, particularly in osteoporotic boneRef 10
  • Fibular intramedullary nailing (IMN) shows 98% union rate but carries significantly higher risk of lateral column malreduction in Weber C and comminuted trimalleolar patterns — obsessive fluoroscopic assessment is mandatoryRef 2

6. The Comprehensive Intraoperative Management Algorithm

Persistent MCS widening must adhere to a strict chronological "Ladder of Fixation." Bypassing steps leads to compounding, unrecoverable malreductions.Ref 10

1

Anatomic Restoration of Fibular Length and Rotation

The lateral column is addressed first (except in rare gross lateral comminution cases). The surgeon must verify the Dime Sign and Shenton's Line under strict mortise fluoroscopy. Fixation is achieved via interfragmentary lag screw and neutralization plating, ensuring no construct shortening.Refs 10, 21

2

Medial Exploration and Debridement

Once the fibula is rigidly secured, the MCS is reassessed via stress mortise fluoroscopy. If the MCS remains widened (≥4 mm or wider than the superior clear space), the surgeon proceeds to an anteriorly placed medial incision. The medial gutter is inspected, invaginated deltoid tissue is retracted, and any medial malleolar fracture is anatomically reduced and fixed.Refs 10, 13

3

Posterior Malleolus Fixation

If a posterior malleolar fragment is present (trimalleolar fracture or bimalleolar equivalent), it is fixed via direct open reduction with a posterior buttress plate. This stabilizes the PITFL, directly tensioning the posterior syndesmosis. Often, once complete, the MCS returns to normal, obviating trans-syndesmotic screws.Ref 2

4

Syndesmotic Assessment and Trans-syndesmotic Fixation

If Steps 1–3 are complete and the MCS remains wide under dynamic stress, the syndesmosis is globally incompetent. The Hook Test, ER Test, and Tap Test confirm separation. Fixation is achieved via trans-syndesmotic screws (two 3.5 mm or 4.5 mm screws, three- or four-cortex purchase) or dynamic suspensory suture-button constructs. Suture buttons allow physiological fibular motion and can eliminate up to 50% of intraoperative malreductions through cyclical loading.Ref 2

5

Decision on Acute Deltoid Ligament Repair

The final, most debated step. If a massive, complete deep deltoid tear is identified during medial exploration, the surgeon must decide whether to acutely repair the ligament. See Section 7 for the evidence synthesis.Refs 5, 27

7. The Paradigm Shift: Acute Deltoid Ligament Repair

Historical consensus from the 1980s–1990s maintained that the deltoid ligament did not require primary repair provided the mortise was statically restored. The ligament was expected to heal via indirect fibrosis. However, high-resolution MRI has revealed that indirect healing often results in fibrotic lengthening, chronic medial instability, and subtle dynamic talar subluxation that accelerates chondral wear.Ref 5

7.1 Functional Equivalence vs. Radiographic Superiority Paradox

A consistent paradox emerges from the recent literature: while radiographic outcomes consistently favor DLR, short- to mid-term functional scores often show no statistically significant difference.

A definitive 2022 systematic review by James and Dodd analyzed eight clinical studies (three RCTs, five retrospective cohorts). Functional outcomes (AOFAS, VAS) were largely equivalent. However, five of six studies with rigorous radiographic assessment demonstrated significantly decreased postoperative MCS and reduced mortise malreduction rates within DLR groups.Ref 5

7.2 DLR as an Alternative to Trans-syndesmotic Fixation

A specialized subset of literature evaluates DLR as a complete alternative to trans-syndesmotic screws in bimalleolar equivalent fractures. The biomechanical rationale: repairing the primary medial restraint closes the mortise, naturally tensions the complex, and indirectly stabilizes the syndesmosis, rendering stiff hardware redundant.

Clinical Implication Anatomic DLR should be considered a highly viable, often superior alternative to trans-syndesmotic screw fixation for unstable ankle fractures with isolated MCS widening. DLR avoids high malreduction morbidity and eliminates secondary hardware removal surgeries.

8. Deltoid Ligament Repair — Surgical Techniques

  • Proximal avulsions (from medial malleolus): Suture anchors placed into the medial malleolar footprint; non-absorbable sutures passed through deep deltoid substance and tensioned against native bone.Ref 5
  • Distal avulsions (from talus): Suture anchor embedded into non-articular medial talar body; sutures threaded through transosseous drill holes in medial malleolus or tied over superficial layers.Ref 5
  • Mid-substance tears: Managed with direct end-to-end non-absorbable sutures; occasionally augmented with tissue turnover flaps or internal brace constructs.Ref 5
Technical Pearl The repair must be tied with the ankle in neutral dorsiflexion and slight inversion. Securing in excessive plantarflexion creates an anterior soft-tissue tether, limiting dorsiflexion and impacting gait mechanics.

9. Postoperative Rehabilitation & Future Directions

  • Early weight-bearing: Immediate or early weight-bearing as tolerated (within 2 weeks) in a CAM boot accelerates return to work by ~3 weeks and return to sport by ~5 weeks, without increasing complications.Ref 2
  • Range of motion: Early ROM improves 6-week scores but is associated with higher superficial wound complications; delaying aggressive ROM until incision healing is recommended.Ref 2
  • Syndesmotic screw management: Routine removal is no longer standard. The RODEO trial suggests "on-demand" removal for symptomatic screws is non-inferior. Patients with broken, retained screws often demonstrate improved outcomes, likely due to restored physiologic fibular motion.Ref 2
Future Directions Post-traumatic osteoarthritis from minor mortise malreduction typically requires 5–10 years to manifest clinically. Most current RCTs capture data at 12–24 months. As longitudinal data matures, cohorts treated with anatomic DLR — who demonstrably possess tighter MCS intervals and restored kinematics — are expected to exhibit significantly lower incidences of end-stage ankle arthritis.

10. Conclusions

Expert Consensus Summary

  1. Systematic Interrogation: Persistent MCS widening demands sequential rule-out of fibular malreduction (Dime Sign, Shenton's Line), soft tissue interposition (direct medial incision), posterior malleolar avulsion, and syndesmotic incompetence. Dynamic intraoperative stress testing is mandatory.
  2. Rigid Chronology: The "Ladder of Fixation" must be respected: fibular restoration → medial exploration → posterior malleolar stabilization → syndesmotic stabilization.
  3. Critical Role of DLR: Modern meta-analyses conclusively confirm that acute deltoid ligament repair decreases residual MCS widening, reduces syndesmotic malreduction rates, and significantly lowers overall complication profiles.
  4. DLR vs. Syndesmotic Screws: In bimalleolar equivalent fractures, anatomic DLR is a biomechanically sound, often superior alternative. It drops malreduction from 33.3% to 7.4% and eliminates the need for secondary hardware removal.

The medial clear space is not merely an empty radiographic void — it is the visual representation of the deep deltoid ligament's integrity and the survival of the articular cartilage.
