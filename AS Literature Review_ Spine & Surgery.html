1. Introduction to the Ankylosing Spondylitis Paradigm

Ankylosing Spondylitis (AS), the archetypal form of radiographic axial spondyloarthritis (axSpA), represents a clinical challenge requiring a highly integrated, multidisciplinary approach spanning rheumatology and orthopedic surgery. Characterized by chronic, immune-mediated inflammation predominantly targeting the sacroiliac joints and the axial skeleton, the disease is driven by a complex interplay of genetic predisposition and aberrant cytokine signaling.E-V

Key genetic and immunological markers:

  • HLA-B27 is present in approximately 90–95% of affected patients, representing the most notable genetic marker.E-V
  • The pathological inflammatory cascade is mediated by key cytokines: TNF-α, IL-17, and IL-23, driving both systemic symptoms and localized joint destruction.E-V

The Pathophysiological Paradox: Bone Loss Meets Bone Formation

The pathophysiological continuum of AS uniquely oscillates between two destructive extremes: profound bone loss and aggressive bone formation.E-V The disease initiates as enthesitis and osteitis — inflammation at tendon/ligament insertion sites. Over time, chronic inflammation triggers aberrant osteoproliferative responses, culminating in pathological new bone formation.E-V

Bamboo Spine: The End-Stage Structural Consequence Progressive calcification and ossification of the paraspinal ligaments (anterior and posterior longitudinal ligaments), intervertebral discs, and facet joints culminates in the classic "bamboo spine" — the entire spinal column fuses into a single, continuous, rigid segment.E-V

While extensive fusion creates a seemingly solid column, the remodeled bone is intensely osteoporotic, osteopenic, and biomechanically fragile.E-V Chronic systemic inflammation, coupled with lack of normal physiological stress across fused segments, leads to severe secondary osteoporosis.E-V The ankylosed spine acts as a long, continuous, and brittle lever arm incapable of dissipating routine mechanical forces.E-V

Two Major Clinical Trajectories

1. Acute Mechanical Failure The rigid, osteoporotic spine is exceedingly susceptible to highly unstable, three-column fractures following even trivial, low-energy trauma (e.g., ground-level fall). Most commonly occurring in the cervical spine, these fractures are frequently complicated by catastrophic neurological deficits.E-V
2. Chronic Structural Deformity Long-term progression frequently leads to severe, debilitating fixed deformities: chin-on-chest thoracolumbar kyphosis and complete bony ankylosis of peripheral joints (particularly the hip), necessitating complex orthopedic reconstructions.E-V

2. Surgical Management of Cervical Spine Fractures in AS

2.1 Pathomechanics, Epidemiology, and Diagnostic Imperatives

Vertebral fractures affect approximately 10–15% of the AS patient population, representing a seven-fold increased risk compared to healthy, age-matched individuals.E-V The cervical spine — particularly the lower cervical region and cervicothoracic junction (C5–C7) — is the most frequent and vulnerable site, dictated by the transition zone biomechanics where the mobile cervical spine meets the rigid thoracic spine, creating intense stress risers.E-V

Fractures are characteristically transcylindrical/transvertebral, universally involving all three columns simultaneously.E-V Traumatic forces are transmitted directly through brittle, ossified segments, resulting in devastating hyperextension shear or distraction injuries (AO Spine Type B3 or C).E-V

Spinal Cord Injury Incidence Up to 26.5% of AS patients with cervical fractures present with concomitant spinal cord injury (SCI), dramatically increasing morbidity and surgical complexity. Postoperative and one-year mortality rates range from 24.5% to 44.7%.E-III
Diagnostic Alert — Delayed Diagnosis Because AS patients suffer chronic inflammatory pain, acute fracture pain following minor trauma is often misattributed to a standard inflammatory flare. International clinical practice recommendations strongly endorse immediate whole-spine CT (gold standard for fracture morphology) and MRI for evaluating occult fractures, epidural hematomas, and cord integrity.E-V

2.2 Surgical Approach Selection

Conservative management with rigid orthoses is now considered largely obsolete and contraindicated for three-column AS fractures due to unacceptably high rates of non-union, progressive deformity, secondary neurological deterioration, and fatal halo-pin complications in osteoporotic skulls.E-V

Decision Algorithm: Surgical Approach for AS Cervical Fractures

1
Assess fracture displacement (translation): Use CT to measure the degree of translational displacement at the fracture site.
2
Translation ≤ 2 mm + intact posterior hinge: Consider anterior-only approach (shortest operative time ~81 min, lowest blood loss ~87.5 mL).E-III
3
Unstable fracture, severe deformity, cervicothoracic junction involvement: Use posterior-only (the "workhorse") with long-segment fixation ≥2 levels above and below.E-III
4
Translation > 3.7 mm, massive comminution, pre-existing pseudoarthrosis: Employ combined 360° approach (highest stability but greatest morbidity: ~266 min, ~912 mL blood loss, ~22.6% complication rate).E-III

2.2.1 Anterior-Only Approach

Involves standard cervical exposure, discectomy/corpectomy, structural grafting, and anterior plating. In the ankylosed spine, anterior stabilization is generally considered biomechanically inferior when used in isolation due to immense pull-out forces on osteoporotic anterior screws.E-V It should be strictly reserved for highly stable cases with minimal displacement (translation ≤ 2 mm), no significant posterior tension band disruption, and an intact posterior hinge.E-III

Advantages: Shortest operative time (mean 81.4 minutes) and lowest blood loss (mean 87.5 mL).E-III Allows the patient to remain supine, avoiding the perilous prone flip maneuver that carries severe risk of iatrogenic cord transection.E-V Primary risk: Highest rate of late re-operation due to hardware failure and screw pull-out.E-V

2.2.2 Posterior-Only Approach (Workhorse)

Utilizes lateral mass and pedicle screw constructs connected to robust rods. Provides vastly superior mechanical stability compared to anterior approaches for highly unstable distraction-extension injuries.E-V Multi-level posterior fixation acts as an effective, load-sharing buttress against massive leverage forces from fused segments.E-V

Advantages: Enables concomitant wide multi-level laminectomies for direct cord decompression and epidural hematoma evacuation.E-V Moderate operative times (mean 124 min) and acceptable blood loss (mean 714.7 mL).E-III MIS percutaneous techniques are increasingly utilized.E-V

Key precautions: Higher wound infection risk; prone positioning in fixed hyperkyphotic deformity requires customized table padding and mandatory awake fiberoptic intubation for continuous neurological monitoring.E-V

2.2.3 Combined Anteroposterior (360°) Approach

Utilizes both anterior structural plating and posterior long-segment screw-rod constructs. Yields the absolute highest initial biomechanical stability, maximizing ROM reduction across the fracture site in all planes.E-V Reserved for the most catastrophic, highly displaced fractures (translation > 3.7 mm), severe instability, or pre-existing pseudoarthrosis (Andersson lesions).E-V

Major limitation: The physiological toll is immense — longest operative time (mean 266.6 min), highest blood loss (mean 912.7 mL),E-III and a complication rate of 22.6% (vs. 7.5% posterior-only), driven predominantly by devastating cardiopulmonary events (pneumonia, ARDS, pulmonary embolism).E-III

2.4 The Absolute Consensus on Long-Segment Fixation

Standard of Care: Long-Segment Posterior Fixation Short-segment fixation is destined for catastrophic mechanical failure in the ankylosed spine, as it concentrates immense stress at the bone-metal interface. The definitive standard mandates instrumentation extending a minimum of two healthy levels above and two below the fracture site.E-V Cement-augmented pedicle screws (PMMA) significantly enhance pull-out strength in osteoporotic bone.E-V

2.5 The Mortality Paradox and Neurological Outcomes

Raw, unadjusted data reveal a seemingly contradictory finding: higher crude mortality in the posterior-only cohort (up to 24.5%) compared to combined approaches (9.2%).E-III This must be interpreted through the lens of profound selection bias — the combined 360° approach is reserved strictly for younger, biologically robust patients, while elderly, frail AS patients with severe cardiopulmonary disease are shunted toward posterior-only or MIS approaches. The elevated mortality reflects baseline patient frailty, not failure of the posterior procedure itself.E-V

Regarding neurological outcomes, comparative meta-analyses definitively demonstrate no statistically significant difference in rates of neurological improvement among anterior, posterior, or combined cohorts. The primary determinant of recovery is the rapidity of decompression and severity of the initial insult, not the surgical trajectory.E-III

3. Recent Clinical Advances in Early to Mid-Stage AS Management

The introduction of biological DMARDs (bDMARDs) and targeted synthetic DMARDs (tsDMARDs) has fundamentally altered the disease trajectory. The 2024/2025 EULAR/ASAS and BSR guidelines establish a clear, evidence-based algorithm prioritizing TNF-α inhibitors, IL-17 inhibitors, and JAK inhibitors for patients with persistently high disease activity.E-V

3.1 TNF-α Inhibitors (TNFi)

TNF-α drives lymphocyte activation, metalloproteinase induction, and the osteoproliferative response.E-V TNFi agents — including adalimumab, infliximab, golimumab, etanercept, and certolizumab pegol — remain the undisputed gold-standard first-line targeted therapy after NSAID failure.E-V

Efficacy Superiority Recent Bayesian network meta-analyses confirm TNFi agents remain statistically superior to both IL-17 and JAK inhibitors in achieving rapid, profound disease control, with the highest relative risk improvements in ASAS20 and ASAS40 response criteria.E-II

3.2 Interleukin-17 (IL-17) Inhibitors

The IL-23/IL-17 axis is the second critical pathway, particularly implicated in enthesitis and pathological osteoproliferation. FDA-approved agents include secukinumab, ixekizumab, and the recently approved bimekizumab.E-V

Bimekizumab: A Pharmacological Advance

Bimekizumab is a dual inhibitor of both IL-17A and IL-17F. Because IL-17F shares overlapping pro-inflammatory biology and drives inflammation independently, dual synergistic inhibition suppresses the cascade to a significantly greater extent than targeting IL-17A alone.E-V

BE MOBILE 1 & 2 Trials (Phase 3) Published in The Lancet and Annals of the Rheumatic Diseases, these landmark trials demonstrated:
  • At 52 weeks, ~60% of axSpA patients achieved ASAS40 response.E-I
  • Significant improvements in morning stiffness, fatigue, and axial pain.E-I
  • Long-term extension data (up to 3 years): MRI remission in >57%, sustained clinical remission in both biologic-naïve and TNFi-refractory patients.E-I

Treatment sequencing insight: Retrospective cohort studies (Korean National Health Insurance Service) suggest patients with primary failure to a first-line TNFi exhibit significantly better long-term drug retention when switched to an IL-17i rather than cycling to a second TNFi.E-III

3.3 Janus Kinase (JAK) Inhibitors

JAK inhibitors (upadacitinib, tofacitinib, filgotinib) are oral small molecules classified as tsDMARDs. They operate intracellularly to block the JAK/STAT signaling pathway, simultaneously inhibiting downstream signal transduction of over 60 inflammatory cytokines.E-V They strongly inhibit γδ T cells, type 3 innate lymphoid cells, and mucosa-associated invariant T cells — upon which the IL-23/IL-17 axis relies.E-V

SELECT-AXIS 2 Trials (Phase 3) Evaluated upadacitinib (15 mg daily) in radiographic and non-radiographic axSpA, including bDMARD-refractory populations:
  • Statistically significant improvements in disease activity, physical function, and MRI-detected inflammation within 14 weeks.E-I
  • Robustly sustained over 2-year follow-up.E-I
  • Acceptable long-term safety profile with no significant increase in MACE in this cohort.E-I

3.5 Modern Treatment Sequencing (2024/2025 Guidelines)

Key Guideline Principles
  • Treat-to-Target (T2T) caution: The BSR guideline warns against strictly treating to an index-based target, as aggressive rapid cycling can prematurely exhaust lifetime therapeutic options. Elevated indices (BASDAI) can be falsely inflated by structural damage or fibromyalgia.E-V
  • Tapering vs. Withdrawal: For sustained remission (>6 months), guidelines recommend dose tapering (extending injection intervals or reducing pill dosage). Complete withdrawal is explicitly not recommended, as it almost universally results in severe, rapid, recalcitrant flares.E-V
  • Comorbidity-driven selection: In concomitant IBD, prefer monoclonal TNFi or JAKi; IL-17i are contraindicated. In severe psoriasis, prioritize IL-17i.E-V

4. Spinal Osteotomy Techniques for Kyphosis Correction

The hallmark end-stage deformity is severe, rigid thoracolumbar kyphosis — frequently a devastating chin-on-chest deformity that severely restricts forward vision, compromises diaphragmatic excursion and cardiopulmonary capacity, and precludes supine positioning.E-V

4.2 The Two-Level Modified PSO

For exceptionally severe, round kyphosis requiring correction >60°, a single-level PSO is insufficient. EBM literature indicates that precise preoperative spinopelvic parameters predict the necessity of the two-level approach:E-III

Predictive Thresholds for Two-Level PSO
  • Chin-Brow Vertical Angle (CBVA) > 63.9° — independent predictor for two-level PSO
  • Global Kyphosis (GK) > 91.1° — independent predictor for two-level PSO

By distributing the correction across two discrete levels (e.g., one thoracic and one lumbar), surgeons safely achieve total sagittal corrections averaging 69°, reducing localized compressive stress on any single neural foramen.E-III

However, the physiological burden is immense: operative times average 550.0 ± 155.2 minutes (vs. 371.2 ± 90.8 min for single-level),E-III requiring meticulous multidisciplinary perioperative management including continuous neuro-monitoring (SSEP/MMEP), controlled intraoperative hypotension, and advanced blood resuscitation protocols.E-V

5. Total Hip Arthroplasty (THA) in the Ankylosed Hip

Between 30–50% of AS patients experience aggressive chronic hip synovitis, and up to 25% progress to complete, bilateral bony ankylosis of the hips.E-III These patients frequently present in their 30s–40s with hips fused in varying degrees of fixed flexion contracture, compounding spinal kyphosis and rendering ambulation impossible.E-V

5.1 The Spinopelvic Dilemma

In a healthy individual, the lumbar spine smoothly flexes/extends to accommodate postural transitions, tilting the pelvis to maintain safe acetabular orientation. In advanced AS, the "bamboo spine" locks the pelvis in a fixed position. Because the pelvis cannot retrovert during sitting, the anterior prosthetic femoral neck violently impinges against the acetabular cup, forcefully levering the head out of the socket.E-V

5.2 Dual Mobility (DM) Cups: A Paradigm Shift

The widespread adoption of Dual Mobility (DM) cups represents a paramount advancement over the last five years.E-V

Dual Mobility Mechanism A DM construct features a "bearing-within-a-bearing" design: a large, highly cross-linked polyethylene liner articulates freely within a fixed metal shell while simultaneously capturing a smaller inner femoral head. This exponentially increases the effective "jump distance" and allows massive, supraphysiologic range of motion before impingement.E-V

Meta-analyses and national registry data demonstrate that DM cups drive the postoperative dislocation rate down from roughly 4–7% (standard implants) to 0.0–0.3%.E-II DM implants also provide significantly faster early functional recovery with superior pain and mobility improvements within 3–6 months.E-III

5.3 Heterotopic Ossification (HO)

HO — pathological ectopic bone formation in periarticular soft tissues — occurs in approximately 15.11% of AS patients following THA, significantly higher than in osteoarthritic populations.E-III Severe HO (Brooker Class III–IV) can bridge femur to pelvis, causing complete re-anklyosis of the new joint. Aggressive perioperative prophylaxis (localized radiation or prolonged NSAIDs such as indomethacin) must be integrated into the surgical plan.E-V

5.4 Robotic-Assisted THA (RA-THA)

RA-THA utilizes preoperative 3D CT mapping and real-time haptic feedback to precisely mill the acetabulum to patient-specific parameters.E-V In AS anatomy, robotic guidance ensures the cup is implanted within the required "safe zone," compensating for fixed abnormal pelvic tilt. NIS data shows RA-THA adoption is rapidly expanding (1.2% → 6.7% of all THA cases).E-III Matched cohorts demonstrate significantly lower DVT risk, fewer mechanical complications, and shorter hospital stays compared to conventional THA.E-III

6. Conclusion

The comprehensive management of Ankylosing Spondylitis requires a deeply integrated, multidisciplinary approach spanning the entire chronological arc of the disease. In the early to mid-stages, pharmacological mastery is paramount. While TNF-α inhibitors remain the gold standard for rapid disease control, the introduction of dual IL-17A/F inhibitors (bimekizumab) and oral JAK inhibitors (upadacitinib) provides powerful alternatives capable of sustaining deep remission over multiple years. Modern guidelines prioritize individualized therapy tailored to extra-musculoskeletal comorbidities and endorse sustained dose tapering rather than rapid cycling or complete withdrawal.E-V

For acute cervical fractures, evidence-based literature unequivocally supports long-segment posterior fixation as the procedural workhorse. While combined 360° approaches offer maximum biomechanical rigidity, their severe operative morbidity relegates their use to the most catastrophic fractures in patients with sufficient physiological reserve.E-III

Finally, the integration of Two-Level Modified PSOs for extreme kyphosis and Dual Mobility cups with Robotic-Assisted 3D navigation for fused hips has dramatically shifted the risk-benefit ratio in favor of the patient. By deeply acknowledging the unique spinopelvic kinematics and brittle biomechanics inherent to AS, surgeons can safely execute these profound reconstructions, mitigating catastrophic hardware failure, dislocation, and mortality, ultimately restoring function, mobility, and dignity.E-V
