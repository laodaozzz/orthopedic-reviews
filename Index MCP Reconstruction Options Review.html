1 Introduction & Clinical Problem

Reconstruction of the index finger metacarpophalangeal (MCP) joint following septic arthritis represents one of the most formidable challenges in reconstructive hand surgery. Unlike degenerative osteoarthritis or inflammatory arthropathies, the post-septic digit presents a "hostile environment" characterized by scarring, stiffness, compromised vascularity, and varying degrees of osteomyelitis.[1]

The destructive sequelae of septic arthritis—cartilage lysis, subchondral bone collapse, and capsuloligamentous fibrosis—functionally amputate the digit's utility while leaving its physical form intact.

🔑 Biomechanical Uniqueness of the Index MCP The index finger MCP joint serves as the primary "post" against which the thumb exerts force during tripod pinch, key (lateral) pinch, and power grip.[3] This functional demand imposes significant shear and valgus stress on the joint, requiring stability far exceeding that of the ulnar digits.[5]

In the specific context of post-septic reconstruction, prosthetic arthroplasty (silicone or pyrolytic carbon implants) is frequently contraindicated due to:[6]

  • Risk of recurrent infection from biofilm persistence on foreign materials
  • Poor osseointegration in compromised bone stock
  • Insufficient soft tissue envelope to cover bulky implants

This necessitates reliance on three biological reconstruction options:

  1. Arthrodesis — stability at the expense of motion
  2. Soft Tissue Interposition Arthroplasty — motion with limited stability
  3. Vascularized Joint Transfer (VJT) — biological "like-with-like" reconstruction
⚠️ The Central Dilemma Each modality offers a distinct trade-off between stability, mobility, and surgical morbidity. For the index finger, motion without lateral stability renders the digit functionally useless for key pinch.

2 Pathophysiology & the Post-Septic Environment

2.1 Mechanisms of Joint Destruction

Joint destruction in septic arthritis is mediated by a "double hit" phenomenon: direct bacterial toxicity combined with the host's inflammatory response.[8]

  1. Enzymatic Cartilage Lysis: Synovial cells and infiltrating polymorphonuclear leukocytes (PMNs) release proteolytic enzymes including metalloproteinases and elastase.[2] Significant glycosaminoglycan loss occurs within 8 hours of infection onset, with irreversible chondrocyte death shortly thereafter.[8]
  2. Increased Intra-articular Pressure: Purulent effusion accumulates, tamponading the synovial capillary bed and inducing ischemia in cartilage and epiphyseal bone, potentially causing avascular necrosis of the metacarpal head.[2]
  3. Osteomyelitis and Bone Loss: Delayed presentations allow infection to breach the subchondral plate, establishing osteomyelitis. Surgical debridement often necessitates significant bone stock resection, creating skeletal defects.[9]

2.2 The "Septic Gap" and Soft Tissue Contracture

The aftermath is rarely a simple joint deficiency—it is a composite tissue loss. The "septic gap" refers to the void after debridement. As healing progresses, surrounding tissues undergo fibrotic contracture:[10]

  • Collateral Ligament Fibrosis: Shortened and fibrotic ligaments lock the joint in extension or facilitate subluxation.[3]
  • Extensor Mechanism Adhesion: The extensor tendon adheres to the dorsal metacarpal or ruptures from ischemic necrosis.[11]

2.3 Staged Reconstruction: The Masquelet Technique

Given the high risk of latent infection, single-stage reconstruction is often imprudent. The induced membrane technique (Masquelet) has revolutionized management of septic bone defects in the hand:[9]

🦴 Masquelet Technique — Two Stages Stage 1: Radical debridement of all infected bone/soft tissue. A PMMA cement spacer loaded with heat-stable antibiotics (vancomycin, tobramycin) is placed. The spacer elutes high local antibiotic concentrations and maintains soft tissue volume.
Induced Membrane (4–8 weeks): A foreign body reaction creates a vascularized, bioactive membrane secreting VEGF, TGF-beta, and containing mesenchymal stem cells.[13]
Stage 2: Spacer removal; reconstruction (bone graft or VJT) is performed inside this "biological chamber."

3 Surgical Anatomy & Biomechanics

3.1 Osseous and Ligamentous Anatomy

The metacarpal head is cam-shaped, wider volarly than dorsally, which tightens collateral ligaments in flexion to maximize stability during grip.[3]

  • Proper Collateral Ligaments (PCL): Resist valgus/varus stress in flexion[14]
  • Accessory Collateral Ligaments (ACL): Tighten in extension
  • Volar Plate: Thick fibrocartilaginous structure preventing hyperextension; often preserved longer than the dorsal capsule in sepsis[15]
  • Sagittal Bands: Stabilize the EDC tendon centrally; destruction causes ulnar subluxation[12]

4 Option I: Arthrodesis (The Stabilizer)

4.1 Overview and Indications

Arthrodesis creates a rigid, stable post by fusing the metacarpal head to the proximal phalanx base. It is widely considered the gold standard for the majority of post-septic index MCP reconstructions.[7,14]

Indications and Contraindications for Index MCP Arthrodesis
IndicationsContraindications
Manual laborers requiring high power grip Patients requiring fine independent flexion (e.g., pianists)
Severe bone loss (resection would leave digit unstable) Concomitant PIP stiffness — stiff MCP + stiff PIP = fixed stick[18]
Chronic infection history (eliminates joint space nidus)
Soft tissue compromise (requires less excursion)

4.2 Surgical Fixation Techniques

Dorsal Compression Plating

2.0mm or 2.4mm locking plates applied dorsally with compression.[19]

✓ Pros: Absolute stability, immediate ROM of PIP/wrist, union rates up to 100%[18]

✗ Cons: Bulky hardware under thin dorsal skin; 23% hardware removal rate in post-septic cases[19]; theoretical biofilm risk

Tension Band Wiring (TBW)

K-wires with figure-of-eight stainless steel wire.[20]

✓ Pros: Low profile, inexpensive; less soft tissue stripping; converts distraction to compression

✗ Cons: Technically demanding; K-wire back-out; pin tract infection risk

Intramedullary Screw

Headless compression screw (Herbert/Acutrak).[21]

✓ Pros: Zero profile — no dorsal hardware; ideal for subsequent tendon grafting

✗ Cons: Requires adequate medullary bone stock; canals may be obliterated by sclerosis; difficult angle adjustment[14]

4.3 Critical Position of Fusion

🎯 Fusion Position Is Permanent — Errors Are Life-Long An error of 10° can mean the difference between a useful finger and one that obstructs the entire hand.
Recommended Fusion Positions for Index MCP Joint (Source consensus)
ParameterRecommended ValueRationale
Flexion Angle 20°–25°[14] Allows pulp-to-pulp pinch while clearing objects during hand opening; excessive flexion creates a "hook"[20]
Pronation 5°–15°[14] Orients index pulp toward thumb for precision pinch; neutral/supination causes side-to-side contact[17]
Abduction Neutral to slight radial deviation[17] Ulnar deviation causes scissoring with middle finger during flexion
Source: Woodworth et al. demonstrated increased flexion angles restrict maximal pinch aperture (same principle applies to MCP)[23]

4.4 Outcomes

  • Stability: Absolute — zero valgus deflection under key pinch loading[18]
  • Pinch Strength: Preserved or improved; comparative data from thumb CMC/PIP studies show fusion groups generating 20–30% higher pinch forces than arthroplasty groups[24]
  • Nonunion Rate: Up to 16% in septic revisions (vs. <5% in osteoarthritis)[18]
  • Quadriga Effect: If index FDP is tethered or digit significantly shortened, global grip strength may weaken[20]

5 Option II: Soft Tissue Interposition Arthroplasty

5.1 Concept

Interposition arthroplasty involves resecting destroyed joint surfaces and placing a biological barrier between bone ends to create a "fibrous pseudoarthrosis" allowing motion while preventing pain.[27]

Indications: Elderly or low-demand patients; salvage of failed arthroplasty with preserved bone stock; patients prioritizing any motion over strength who refuse fusion.[28]

5.2 Techniques

Interposition Arthroplasty Techniques Compared (Source document)
TechniqueMaterialKey AdvantageKey Limitation in Sepsis
Dorsal Capsule Interposition (DCI)[27] Distally-based dorsal capsular flap Vascularized, locally available; preserves collateral ligaments Capsule may be hypertrophic/scarred
Volar Plate Arthroplasty[28] Advanced volar plate Thick, fibrocartilaginous, wear-resistant Often contracted/necrotic in sepsis; risks neurovascular injury; requires collateral ligament sectioning
Autograft Interposition[30] Palmaris longus, retinaculum, periosteum Readily available Acts as spacer; undergoes resorption/fibrosis with loss of joint space

5.3 The Index Finger Stability Problem

🚨 Fundamental Flaw for the Index Finger Without bony congruity, the joint relies entirely on soft tissue tension. Key pinch generates a lateral force that pushes the finger ulnarly, leading to radial instability and ulnar deviation.[5]

Mitigation strategies:

  1. Prolonged K-wire fixation (4–6 weeks) to allow soft tissue scarring[31]
  2. Collateral ligament reconstruction with tendon graft at time of arthroplasty

Conclusion: Interposition arthroplasty is suboptimal for the index finger unless the patient is extremely low demand.

6 Option III: Vascularized Joint Transfer (VJT)

6.1 The Biological "Gold Standard" for Motion

VJT restores a functional, gliding, hyaline-cartilage joint with biological remodeling potential. It adheres to the principle of "like-with-like" reconstruction.[33]

Indications: Young, active patients; multiple digit involvement; composite defects (skin + bone + joint).[33]

6.3 Outcomes

📊 The Extensor Lag Dilemma Systematic reviews reveal a discrepancy between biological success and functional success of VJT.[37]
  • Flap Survival: 95–100% in experienced centers[37]
  • Average Active ROM: 30°–40° (vs. 90° normal)[37]
  • Extensor Lag: 29°–40° — the most common complication[37]
    • Cause: Weaker toe extensor mechanism with less excursion
    • Impact: Perpetually bent finger, interfering with hand opening
  • Lateral Stability: Good — collateral ligaments transfer intact (superior to interposition)[34]
📌 Source Limitation: The source document provides outcome data primarily from systematic reviews and case series. No direct RCTs comparing VJT to arthrodesis for index MCP were identified.

7 Concomitant Extensor Tendon Reconstruction

⚠️ Mandatory Principle No joint reconstruction—whether fusion or VJT—will function without a reconstructed extensor mechanism. Even in fusion, the extensor tendon must be centralized to prevent soft tissue imbalance.

Centralization Techniques

  1. Direct Repair/Imbrication: Reefing the radial sagittal band if local tissue is scarred but present[6]
  2. Suture Anchor Reconstruction: Anchor in radial metacarpal head to tether EDC centrally[12]
  3. Slip Reconstruction (Kilgore/Carroll): EDC slip passed deep to intermetacarpal ligament creating a dynamic radial sling[40]
  4. Synthetic Suture Tape: Loop of suture tape acting as neo-pulley when no local tissue available[41]

Tendon Grafting vs. Transfer

Decision Algorithm

Select the patient profile matching your clinical scenario:

1
"The Working Hand" — Manual laborer or patient prioritizing strength
→ Arthrodesis (20–25° flexion, 5–15° pronation) is the only reliable option. Interposition failure risk is too high.
2
"The Social Hand" — Elderly/low-demand, values aesthetics/motion
→ Soft Tissue Interposition (Dorsal Capsule Interposition preferred) — reasonable, low-morbidity salvage
3
"The High-Demand Mobile Hand" — Young patient who refuses fusion, accepts microsurgery risks
→ Vascularized Joint Transfer — best chance of mobile, stable joint. Counsel: "normal" motion unlikely; toe donor is permanent loss.

9 Conclusion

The reconstruction of the post-septic index MCP joint is a salvage endeavor where restoration of a "normal" joint is functionally impossible. The choice is a compromise between stability and mobility.

Evidence supports Arthrodesis (20–25° flexion, 5–15° pronation) as the most predictable and functional solution for the majority of patients. It restores the index finger's primary function—stable opposition—and allows powerful key pinch without deviation.

Soft Tissue Interposition is useful for low-demand patients but biomechanically ill-suited for the index finger's stability requirements. VJT represents the pinnacle of biological reconstruction but should be reserved for select cases where 30–40° of motion outweighs complexity and donor morbidity.

✅ Mandatory Principle Regardless of Technique Concomitant extensor reconstruction—preferably via EIP transfer or sagittal band centralization—is mandatory. A stable joint with no active extension is a functional failure. Successful management addresses infection (Masquelet staging), bone stability (fusion/transfer), and dynamic function (tendon reconstruction) in unison.
