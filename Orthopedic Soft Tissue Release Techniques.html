Principles and Surgical Techniques for Soft Tissue Release in Orthopedic Surgery:
Managing Severe Flexion Contractures

Evidence-based interactive educational summary · Derived from the provided source document

Introduction: Pathophysiological and Biomechanical Basis

A flexion contracture is defined not merely by the inability to extend a joint but by a complex, multi-layered reorganization of the musculoskeletal unit that alters the biomechanical axis, increases energy expenditure, and predisposes articular cartilage to supraphysiological contact pressures. Level V [1]

Whether the etiology is post-traumatic arthrofibrosis, prolonged immobilization, thermal injury, or degenerative osteoarthritis, the resultant pathology represents a failure of the normal remodeling phase of healing, leading to a fixed deformity that resists physiological load. [2]

Key Pathobiology: Following injury, the inflammatory cascade initiates a proliferative phase where fibroblasts differentiate into myofibroblasts. These cells express alpha-smooth muscle actin and generate contractile forces analogous to smooth muscle cells, actively shortening the extracellular matrix. Over time, collagen cross-linking increases, water content decreases, and tissues lose their viscoelastic properties. Level V [2]

Surgical management requires a departure from standard anatomical dissection. The surgeon must adopt a "layered approach"—a hierarchical philosophy that prioritizes the systematic release of restraining structures from superficial to deep. This hierarchy is rooted in the pathobiology of contracture itself.

Chapter 1: The Layered Approach and Hierarchy of Release

The "layered approach" is the fundamental doctrine of contracture release. It dictates that the surgeon must address restricting structures in a specific sequence, typically moving from the anatomical known to the unknown, and from superficial to deep. This ensures that the minimum amount of tissue is destabilized to achieve the desired range of motion (ROM), thereby preserving joint stability and proprioception. Level V [1]

A contracture is rarely an isolated capsular problem; rather, it is a "summation of restraints" involving skin, fascia, muscle, and capsule.

Level I — The Dermo-Fascial Envelope

In cases of burn contractures or severe scarring, the skin and subcutaneous fascia are often the definitive restraints. The collagen deposition in hypertrophic scars is disorganized, with whorls of collagen replacing the normal basket-weave pattern, creating rigid bands that do not obey Hooke's law of elasticity. Level V [3]

  • Surgical Strategy: If the skin blanches or creates a palpable band upon extension, a Z-plasty or local flap is indicated. Releasing the skin often reveals that the underlying deep fascia is also contracted—this fascia must be incised or excised.
  • Check-Rein Effect: Failure to release the fascia lata (knee) or lacertus fibrosus (elbow) can render deep capsular releases futile. The surgeon must verify excursion at each level before proceeding deeper. [2]

Level II — The Myotendinous Units

In chronic contractures, muscles undergo myostatic contracture, where the number of sarcomeres in series decreases, physically shortening the muscle belly.

  • Fractional Lengthening: Rather than complete tenotomy, techniques at the musculotendinous junction allow controlled elongation while maintaining continuity.
  • Reciprocal Inhibition: Applying resistance to the antagonist muscle can induce relaxation in the agonist—a principle exploitable during examination under anesthesia to differentiate dynamic spasm from fixed contracture. [5]

Level III — The Capsuloligamentous Complex

In the knee and elbow, the posterior capsule thickens significantly in flexion contractures.

  • Collateral Ligament Balancing: Released in a graded fashion (e.g., pie-crusting) to restore the joint line without creating instability.
  • Posterior Capsule: Often the "final frontier" of release—involving the PCL (knee) or anterior/posterior capsule (elbow). Level V [1]

Level IV — Osseous Constraints

Osteophytes, heterotopic ossification (HO), and malunited fractures create mechanical blocks that no amount of soft tissue release can overcome. Osteophytes on the olecranon tip or coronoid process (elbow) or posterior condylar osteophytes (knee) act as distinct mechanical stops requiring resection. [7]

The Principle of "Gap Balancing"

A critical component of the layered approach, particularly in arthroplasty, is the iterative assessment of tension known as "gap balancing." This distinguishes between symmetric tightness (requiring capsular release or bony resection) and asymmetric tightness (requiring collateral ligament release). In a fixed flexion contracture, the extension gap is tighter than the flexion gap. The goal is to enlarge the extension space until it matches the flexion space, creating a rectangular gap that allows full extension without hyperextension. Level V [1]

Chapter 2: Intraoperative Neurovascular Protection

Operating in a field of extensive fibrosis fundamentally alters the surgical risk profile. Normal fat planes are obliterated, and nerves may be tethered to bone, encased in HO, or displaced by scar tissue. [8]

The "Normal to Abnormal" Dissection Principle

The cardinal rule is to identify neurovascular structures in a virgin field proximal or distal to the zone of injury before tracing them into the scar. This vector minimizes inadvertent transection.

  • Elbow: The ulnar nerve is located proximally in unscarred tissue and traced distally into the cubital tunnel. [6]
  • Hip/Knee: The sciatic nerve is identified distally between the adductor magnus and biceps femoris before approaching a scarred acetabulum. [9]

"Push and Spread" Technique

The gold standard for dissecting near neurovascular bundles in fibrotic fields. Level V [10]

  • The surgeon utilizes blunt scissors (Metzenbaum). Tips are inserted closed into the tissue plane and then opened parallel to the expected nerve course.
  • This creates tension that tears weaker scar tissue along lines of least resistance while preserving the stronger perineurium and adventitia.
  • Traction-Countertraction: Effective tension from the assistant is essential to "tent" the scar and identify the cleavage plane. [10]

Subperiosteal "Safe Zones"

Nerves and vessels rarely course beneath the periosteum. Maintaining a subperiosteal dissection plane—scraping the bone—provides a consistent safety margin. By elevating muscle and periosteum as a single composite flap, the muscle belly acts as a protective cushion between the elevator and the neurovascular bundle. [11]

Intraoperative Adjuncts

  • Vessel Loops: Once identified, nerves are isolated with vascular tape for visual cue and gentle retraction. Beware excessive traction causing neurapraxia. [12]
  • Electrical Stimulation: Invaluable when a nerve is indistinguishable from scar or encased in HO. Maps nerve course before incision. [11]
  • Doppler Ultrasound: Locates major arteries when pulses are palpable but vessel location is obscured by fibrosis. [8]

Chapter 3: The Knee — Managing Flexion Contracture in Arthroplasty

In Total Knee Arthroplasty (TKA), a fixed flexion contracture is a significant cause of postoperative disability. A contracture greater than 15° significantly increases quadriceps load, alters gait mechanics by preventing the "screw-home" mechanism, and leads to anterior knee pain. Level IV [1]

Surgical Goal: Create a rectangular extension gap equal to the flexion gap, restoring full extension without hyperextension.

Step 1: Osteophyte Resection and Bony Recuts

Osteophytes tent the posterior capsule and collateral ligaments, artificially shortening them. [1]

  • Posterior Condylar Osteophytes: Must be removed aggressively using a curved osteotome or rongeur. They tent the posterior capsule, preventing full extension.
  • Distal Femoral Resection: If soft tissue release is insufficient, increasing the distal femoral resection (raising the joint line) opens the extension gap. An additional 2 mm resection can yield significant gains, but excessive resection risks mid-flexion instability. Level IV [1]

Step 2: Posterior Capsular Release

A high-risk maneuver due to proximity of the popliteal neurovascular bundle.

  • With the knee flexed to 90°, laminar spreaders tension the posterior capsule. The capsule is stripped from the posterior femur using a curved periosteal elevator or electrocautery.
  • Popliteal Protection: Release performed in flexion allows the popliteal artery to fall posteriorly. Subperiosteal technique is safer than sharp dissection. Level IV [13]
  • iPACK Synergy: Infiltration between the Popliteal Artery and Capsule of the Knee (iPACK) provides analgesia and hydro-dissects the safety plane. Level IV [16]

Step 3: Collateral Ligament Release — "Pie-Crusting"

The pie-crusting technique has revolutionized soft tissue balancing, particularly for the MCL, allowing controlled, incremental lengthening. Level III [17]

Grid-Assisted Pie-Crusting Technique

Recent biomechanical studies have refined the technique using a "grid" approach. Multiple horizontal stab incisions are made in the tight ligament; as valgus stress is applied, the perforations expand like an expandable mesh, effectively lengthening the structure. This reduces stiffness without catastrophic rupture. Level III [18]

  • A hypothetical or physical grid with 3 mm × 3 mm spacing is visualized over the MCL.
  • An 18-gauge needle creates perforations at intersections. Studies indicate that ~12 punctures can reduce stiffness by ~6.47 N/mm and increase elongation. [18]
  • 18-gauge needles are preferred over blades for the superficial MCL to avoid inadvertent complete transection ("damage-control" method). [17]
  • For a tight medial compartment in extension, release focuses on the posterior oblique fibers of the MCL and posteromedial capsule; anterior fibers are preserved for flexion stability. [13]

Step 4: The Distal Release

In severe contractures, the PCL tethers the tibia. PCL resection increases the extension gap. Finally, release of the gastrocnemius heads from the posterior femoral condyles removes the dynamic muscular component. Level IV [1, 13]

Chapter 4: The Elbow — Surgical Release for Stiffness

The elbow is notoriously unforgiving of trauma, prone to rapid stiffening due to the high congruency of the ulnohumeral joint and the propensity of the anterior capsule to fibrose. Unlike the knee, elbow release relies heavily on capsulectomy and osteophyte debridement. The goal is a functional arc of 30°–130°. Level IV [7, 21]

The 7-Step Protocol for Elbow Release

Step 1: Ulnar Nerve Mobilization (Prerequisite)

Before any capsular release, the ulnar nerve must be identified and protected. Via a medial incision, the nerve is identified proximally and traced distally. Anterior transposition is frequently required: release of the posterior capsule and MCL increases flexion, subjecting an in-situ nerve to excessive traction and compression. Level IV [6]

Steps 2 & 3: Posterior Column Release

  • Triceps Management: Elevated from the posterior humerus using a periosteal elevator if adherent.
  • Olecranon Fossa Debridement: The olecranon tip and osteophytes filling the fossa are resected to remove the mechanical block to extension. Aggressive debridement is critical; failure to clear the fossa is a common cause of residual contracture. [6]
  • Posterior MCL Band: The posterior bundle of the MCL—primary restraint to flexion—is resected incrementally while checking the flexion arc. The surgeon must stop before compromising the anterior bundle (primary valgus stabilizer). [6]

Step 4: Anterior Column Release — Subperiosteal Peeling

The anterior capsule is often the thickest, most resistant structure. The subperiosteal peeling technique allows safe access without directly exposing the brachial artery and median nerve. Level V [11]

  1. Lateral Approach (Kocher): Interval between Anconeus/ECU and ECRL is exploited.
  2. The "Peel": Using a sharp periosteal elevator, the origins of ECRL, Brachioradialis, and Brachialis are elevated en masse off the anterior humerus.
  3. Safety Mechanism: Staying strictly subperiosteal, the brachialis muscle acts as a protective cushion between the elevator and the neurovascular bundle. The surgeon does not need to see the nerve to know it is safe—provided they stay on bone. [11]
  4. Capsulotomy: A "rectangular" capsulectomy is performed to prevent re-adherence. [11]

Steps 5–7: Final Adjustments

  • Triceps Lengthening: If passive flexion remains limited, V-Y plasty of the triceps tendon is performed. [6]
  • Ulnar Stabilization: The transposed ulnar nerve is secured with a dermofascial sling (stitches between medial epicondyle and subcutaneous tissue) to prevent posterior subluxation during flexion. [6]

Chapter 5: Postoperative Management and Rehabilitation

Surgical release is only half the battle; maintaining gains requires aggressive postoperative management to prevent recurrence of fibrosis and adhesions. The "layered approach" extends to casting and splinting. [22, 24]

Casting and Splinting Principles

  • Layered Cast: The underlayer provides cushioning; the overlayer provides rigidity. Padding applied evenly, overlapping by 50%, prevents pressure points over the olecranon or fibular head. Level V [22]
  • Positioning: The limb is splinted in the position of maximum gain (usually extension) for 24–48 hours. This prevents hematoma accumulation in the dead space, which leads to recurrent fibrosis. [24]

Mobilization Strategies

  • Continuous Passive Motion (CPM): Valuable in arthrolysis—prevents cross-links in the early collagen matrix and reduces edema. For elbow releases, CPM is initiated immediately or within 3 days. Level IV [16]
  • Static Progressive Splinting: Turnbuckle splints or static progressive orthoses apply low-load, prolonged stretch (LLPS), exploiting the viscoelastic property of "creep." By holding tissue at its yield point for extended periods, collagen fibers realign and lengthen permanently without tearing. Level IV [7]
  • HO Prophylaxis: In high-risk patients (elbow trauma, burns, head injury), prophylaxis with Indomethacin or single-dose radiation prevents rapid re-ankylosis. [7]
⚠ Limitation Note: The source document does not provide specific RCT data comparing CPM vs. static progressive splinting outcomes. Evidence for rehabilitation protocols is primarily from case series and expert opinion (Level IV–V). Higher-quality comparative studies are needed.

Conclusion

The management of severe flexion contractures demands a surgical philosophy that balances aggression with precision. The layered approach provides the structural framework, ensuring releases are performed systematically—from skin to bone—respecting the biomechanical contributions of each tissue.

Integration of advanced techniques such as grid-assisted pie-crusting for ligaments and the subperiosteal peel for capsular exposure allows surgeons to achieve releases previously deemed too risky. However, surgical release alone is insufficient. It must be coupled with a comprehensive postoperative regimen utilizing static progressive splinting and CPM to maintain gains.
