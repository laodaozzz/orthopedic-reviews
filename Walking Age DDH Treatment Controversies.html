Developmental Dysplasia of the Hip in the Walking-Age Child

Pathoanatomy & Contemporary Treatment Controversies (12–24 Months) · Interactive Evidence-Based Summary

I. The Clinical Challenge of Walking-Age DDH

The diagnosis of Developmental Dysplasia of the Hip (DDH) in the ambulatory toddler (12–24 months) represents a distinct clinical entity from its infantile counterpart. This late presentation is often the result of failed neonatal screening or unsuccessful non-operative management. Ambulation on an unstable or dislocated hip introduces weight-bearing forces that rapidly accelerate pathological changes, transforming a condition of biologic laxity into one of fixed structural deformity.Ref 1

A. Clinical Presentation and Diagnosis

In contrast to the first three months of life—where palpable instability (Ortolani "clunk of entry," Barlow "click of exit") are the hallmarksRef 3—the walking-age child presents with fixed deformity and functional deficit, not joint instability. These instability signs vanish by 3–6 months as capsular laxity is replaced by adaptive soft-tissue contractures.

Key Clinical Findings (12–24 Months)

  • Trendelenburg Gait: A lurching or waddling motion pathognomonic for hip abductor insufficiency, caused by superior/posterior migration of the femoral head shortening the abductor lever arm.Ref 3
  • Lumbar Lordosis: In bilateral dislocations, compensatory anterior pelvic tilt addresses fixed bilateral hip flexion contractures.Ref 3
  • Toe-Walking: On the affected side, compensating for functional limb shortening.Ref 3
  • Limited Hip Abduction: The most sensitive and reliable clinical test in this age group—a direct consequence of adductor contracture.Ref 3
  • Galeazzi (Allis) Sign: With hips and knees flexed to 90°, the femur on the dislocated side appears shortened due to posterior displacement of the femoral head.Ref 6
  • Asymmetric Skin Folds: Non-specific but classic indicators (gluteal, anterior thigh, groin creases).Ref 4
  • Klisic Test: An imaginary line from the greater trochanter to the ASIS normally points to the umbilicus; in dislocation, the elevated trochanter shifts this line inferiorly.Ref 3

B. Radiographic Assessment

After 4–6 months, the femoral head begins to ossify, making plain radiography the primary imaging modality. However, ossific nucleus appearance is often significantly delayed in dysplastic hips.Ref 7

Key Radiographic Parameters

  • Acetabular Index (AI): The angle between Hilgenreiner's line and the lateral acetabular edge. Normative values: ~27.5° at birth, ~23.5° at 6 months, ~20° at 24 months. An AI >30° at 24 months is definitive for acetabular dysplasia. Pre-operative AI in surgical candidates ranges from 36.6° to 44.7°.Ref 8, 9
  • Shenton's Line: Disruption of the arc (medial femoral neck to inferior pubic ramus) indicates superior/lateral migration.Ref 9
  • Hilgenreiner's & Perkin's Lines: The femoral head normally resides in the inferomedial quadrant; in dislocation, it displaces superolaterally.Ref 6

Severity Classifications

  • IHDI Classification: Grades severity based on femoral head position relative to Perkin's and Hilgenreiner's lines; does not require an ossific nucleus.Ref 6
  • Tönnis Classification: Grades dislocation based on ossific nucleus position.Ref 12
⚠ Clinical Pearl

High-grade dislocations (IHDI III/IV, Tönnis III/IV) are strongly associated with failure of closed reduction and typically necessitate open surgical intervention.Ref 14

II. Pathoanatomy of Late-Diagnosis DDH

Persistent dislocation throughout infancy induces a cascade of predictable adaptive changes in bone and soft tissue. These changes constitute the mechanical barriers that prevent concentric reduction.Ref 1 Treatment is defined by systematically identifying and releasing or excising these blocks.

A. Adaptive Bony Deformities

  • Acetabular Dysplasia: The shallow, oblique acetabulum. Normal growth requires a spherical femoral head as a mechanical stimulus; without it, the roof fails to develop (high AI).Ref 8
  • Neolimbus (False Acetabulum): Weight-bearing forces the femoral head against the iliac wing, forming a fibrocartilaginous rim superior to the true socket that stabilizes the dislocation and blocks reduction.Ref 1
  • Excessive Femoral Anteversion: Twisted femoral neck, a primary contributor to post-reduction instability, often requiring femoral derotation osteotomy.Ref 2, 13
  • Coxa Valga: Increased neck-shaft angle, further contributing to instability.Ref 3

B. Soft-Tissue Contractures and Intra-Articular Blocks

Extra-Articular Blocks

  • Iliopsoas Tendon: Functionally shortened, constricts the capsule into an "hourglass" deformity; primary target for tenotomy.Ref 3, 18
  • Adductor Musculature: Severe contracture tethering the femur medially; direct cause of limited abduction.Ref 3, 18

Intra-Articular Blocks

  • Inverted Labrum (Limbus): Hypertrophied and folded inward, acting as a flap valve blocking femoral head entry.Ref 1
  • Hypertrophied Pulvinar: Fibrofatty mass filling the empty acetabulum; must be excised during open reduction.Ref 1, 18, 26
  • Elongated Ligamentum Teres: Stretched and thickened, tethering the head from full seating.Ref 1, 18
  • Contracted Transverse Acetabular Ligament (TAL): Acts as an inferior sling preventing deep seating.Ref 3, 28, 31
🔑 Mechanical Concept

The femoral head ("key") is tethered by extra-articular contractures (psoas, adductors), and the acetabulum ("lock") is occluded by intra-articular blocks (pulvinar, labrum, ligamentum teres). Closed reduction can only succeed by compressing these blocks; open reduction succeeds by surgically removing them.

III. The Primary Treatment Dilemma: Closed vs. Open Reduction

Non-operative treatment (Pavlik harness) is ineffective and contraindicated at this age,Ref 3 and open reduction with osteotomy is the clear choice over age 2. The 12–24 month window is the critical transition zone where consensus fractures.

A. The Case for Closed Reduction (CR)

CR is a non-arthrotomy procedure: (1) adductor tenotomy, (2) gentle manipulation under GA, (3) intra-operative arthrogram confirmation, and (4) spica cast immobilization.Ref 3

A 2025 comparative study found no significant difference in radiographic outcomes (AI, Center-Edge Angle) or clinical outcomes (Severin, McKay grades) between CR and ORPO in 18–24 month-olds.Ref 34 AVN was lower (though not statistically significant, p = 0.131) in the CR group (18.9% vs 33.3%).Ref 35

Some evidence suggests that an "imperfect" reduction may be acceptable—if stable CR is obtained with medial dye pool up to 6 mm, the femoral head may "dock" and achieve concentricity over time.Ref 32

B. Predictors of CR Failure

📊 Failure Rates

Overall CR failure rate: 20% (2024 meta-analysis, 5,281 hips)Ref 16 and 31.16% (2020 retrospective study).Ref 15

  • Age ≥ 18.35 months — significant risk factor for failureRef 15
  • High-grade dislocations (IHDI III/IV, Tönnis IV) — major predictorRef 15, 16
  • Bilateral disease; Male genderRef 16

Arthrographic Predictors of Failure

  • Obstructive inverted limbusRef 16
  • Medial Dye Pool (MDP) ≥ 6 mm — femoral head not deeply seatedRef 15
  • Poor femoral head coverage — risk for residual dysplasiaRef 25

C. The Argument for Primary Open Reduction (OR)

OR allows direct visualization and removal of all pathoanatomical blocks.Ref 28

  • A 2022 study (701 patients): initial CR associated with 1.8–9.0× higher risk of re-operation vs. initial OR.Ref 37
  • A 2017 meta-analysis: OR patients had significantly lower need for further surgery (OR 0.30).Ref 38
  • Re-operation rate for residual dysplasia: 29.7% for isolated OR but only 15% for OR + pelvic osteotomy.Ref 39
⚖ Reframing the Debate

The true choice is: "CR with a 20–30% risk of requiring a future ORPO" vs. "a definitive primary ORPO." CR prioritizes avoiding initial surgical morbidity; ORPO prioritizes minimizing lifetime re-operation rate. For high-grade (IHDI III/IV) dislocations, most surgeons consider primary OR standard of care.Ref 15

IV. Open Reduction Surgical Strategies

A. Approach Controversy: Medial vs. Anterior

Medial Approach (Ludloff, Weinstein-Ponseti)

  • Advantages: Less soft-tissue dissection, reduced blood loss, shorter operative time, cosmetic scar; direct visualization of medial blocks (psoas, TAL).Ref 28
  • Disadvantages: Cannot perform capsulorrhaphy; cannot perform pelvic osteotomy.Ref 28

Anterior Approach (Smith-Petersen)

  • Advantages: Wide exposure; enables robust capsulorrhaphy; only approach for pelvic osteotomy.Ref 12, 22, 28
  • Disadvantages: More extensive dissection; longer operative time; theoretical MFCA risk.Ref 30
🔬 Modern AVN Evidence

A 2022 systematic review: no significant difference in AVN (18.7% medial vs 19.6% anterior).Ref 30 A 2023 meta-analysis: anterior approach had 2.27× higher odds of AVN vs. medial—but this likely reflects selection bias (anterior is chosen for older, more severe cases).Ref 44

The decision driver is the surgical plan, not AVN risk: If an osteotomy is needed → anterior approach is mandatory.Ref 12

B. The Osteotomy Debate: OR Alone vs. One-Stage Combined Procedure

Case for OR Alone (Remodeling Potential)

A 2021 study: 85% of hips achieved satisfactory remodeling (AI < 30°) by age 6, with remodeling significantly better when surgery was at ≤24 months.Ref 10 Another study reported 90% success, correcting AI from 41.8° to 16.1°.Ref 11

Case for Concomitant Osteotomy (ORPO)

  • Pelvic osteotomy (Salter, Dega, Pemberton): Corrects acetabular dysplasia; recommended over 18 months.Ref 2, 3
  • Femoral osteotomy (shortening, derotation, varus): Corrects femoral deformity; femoral shortening reduces joint pressure to prevent AVN.Ref 14, 17

The Trade-Off

  • OR alone: high remodeling potential10 but ~30% re-operation rate.Ref 39
  • ORPO: prophylactic dysplasia correction, re-op OR 0.34,Ref 37 but AVN more frequent when Salter is combined with OR.Ref 51
  • Triple procedure (OR + pelvic + femoral): definitive but highest AVN rates.Ref 40

C. Bilateral DDH: Simultaneous vs. Staged

Multiple retrospective analyses found no statistically significant difference in radiographic outcomes, AVN rates, or clinical scores between simultaneous and staged bilateral procedures (mean age ~18 months).Ref 52, 53 Simultaneous surgery is advantageous for reduced total anesthesia duration, shorter hospitalization, and lower costs.Ref 52

V. Avascular Necrosis: Aetiology and Prevention

AVN of the femoral head is the most feared complication of DDH treatment—an iatrogenic ischemic injury caused by compression/kinking of the fragile blood supply (primarily MFCA) to the cartilaginous epiphysis.Ref 1, 30 Reported rates: 4%–60%.Ref 55

Kalamchi-MacEwen Classification

  • Group I: Delayed appearance/mottling of ossific nucleus only (transient, good outcomes).Ref 56
  • Group II: Lateral physis damage.Ref 56
  • Group III: Central physis damage.Ref 56
  • Group IV: Entire femoral head and physis involved.Ref 56

Pre-operative Traction: An Unresolved Debate

🌍 International Disagreement

Most EPOS members utilize traction; few POSNA members do.Ref 62

Against traction: Multiple meta-analyses show no significant difference in AVN rates.Ref 60, 64

For traction (new data): A large 2023 cohort study found traction significantly decreased AVN risk (OR 0.37 overall; OR 0.28 in the 1–3 year age group), particularly for open reductions.Ref 67 This re-opens the debate for this specific high-risk population.

Intra- and Post-operative AVN Prevention Strategies

The "Safe Zone" of Reduction

The hip should be stable in high flexion (90°–100°) and moderate abduction without excessive force. If only stable in wide abduction → iatrogenic AVN risk.Ref 21, 71

The "Human Position" Spica Cast

🚨 CRITICAL THRESHOLD

Spica cast abduction > 54° is associated with a 38.44-fold increase in AVN risk.Ref 72

Safe position: High flexion (90°–110°), moderate abduction (< 60°, ideally 45°–50°), neutral rotation.Ref 3, 73, 74

VI. Emerging Horizons and Synthesis

A. Arthroscopic-Assisted Reduction

A paradigm-shifting technique that challenges the traditional CR vs. OR dichotomy. Hip arthroscopy (2 portals) allows resection of intra-articular blocks—hypertrophied ligamentum teres, pulvinar, contracted TAL—without open incision.Ref 26, 31, 76

  • Primary indication: Failed CR in walking-age children (12–24 months).Ref 31
  • Radiographic outcomes: Comparable to OR. AI corrected from ~38° to 26°Ref 27 and from 40.3° to 21.9° at 5-year follow-up.Ref 31
  • Morbidity advantage: Lower redislocation rates, shorter OR time, less blood loss.Ref 78 AVN: 21% arthroscopic vs. 44% open (p = 0.074).Ref 78
  • 5-year outcomes: Zero AVN, zero redislocation, zero re-operation for residual dysplasia.Ref 31
  • Limitation: Cannot be combined with pelvic osteotomy (same as medial OR).

B. Evidence-Based Clinical Algorithm

Step 1 — All patients (12–24 mo): Attempt Closed Reduction with adductor tenotomy under GA → Perform intra-operative arthrogram.
▼
Step 2 — Decision (Arthrogram):
If CR is stable & concentric (MDP < 6 mm, no limbus block):
→ "Human Position" Spica Cast (flexion 90°–110°, abduction 45°–50°)
→ Monitor for remodeling; counsel on ~20–30% risk of future pelvic osteotomy.

If CR is non-concentric or unstable (MDP ≥ 6 mm, obstructive limbus):
▼
Age 12–18 mo (or < 24 mo with low-grade):
→ Arthroscopic-Assisted Reduction OR Medial Open Reduction to clear blocks → "Human Position" Cast
Age 18–24 mo (especially IHDI III/IV):
→ One-Stage Anterior OR + Femoral Shortening/Derotation + Pelvic Osteotomy (Salter/Dega)
→ Definitively addresses all deformities; minimizes re-operation risk; femoral shortening prevents AVN.
