Infectious Spondylitis Following Vertebroplasty & Kyphoplasty

Evidence-Based Educational Summary · Diagnosis, Surgical Strategy & Antibiotic Management

1. Introduction & Epidemiology

1.1 The Clinical Imperative

Vertebral augmentation procedures (VAPs)—specifically percutaneous vertebroplasty (PVP) and percutaneous kyphoplasty (PKP)—have revolutionized the management of osteoporotic vertebral compression fractures (OVCFs) and neoplastic spinal lesions. These minimally invasive interventions offer rapid pain relief and mechanical stabilization for patients who are often frail, elderly, and burdened with significant comorbidities. However, as procedural volume has escalated globally, a rare but catastrophic complication has emerged: infectious spondylitis following cement augmentation.

Unlike native vertebral osteomyelitis (NVO), post-augmentation infectious spondylitis involves the presence of a large, avascular foreign body—polymethylmethacrylate (PMMA) cement—implanted within a necrotic vertebral core. This unique pathophysiological environment challenges traditional orthopedic dogmas and necessitates a nuanced, multidisciplinary approach. LoE II

Large-scale systematic reviews and retrospective cohort studies estimate the incidence of infectious spondylitis after VAPs to be between 0.20% and 0.46%.1,5 A nationwide analysis of over 23,000 patients identified surgical site infection (SSI) rates of 0.21% for PVP and 0.13% for PKP, with no statistically significant difference between the two modalities.4 LoE II

Despite the low incidence, absolute case numbers are rising due to the aging population and increasing utilization of VAPs. Affected patients are typically elderly females (mean age ≈ 71 years), reflecting the epidemiology of osteoporosis.5 Predisposing comorbidities include diabetes mellitus, obesity, malnutrition, and varying degrees of immunocompromise.7 LoE III

1.3 Pathophysiology: The "Perfect Storm"

🔥 Thermal Necrosis Zone

The exothermic polymerization of PMMA generates heat sufficient to cause thermal necrosis of surrounding cancellous bone and vascular structures. This creates an avascular, necrotic zone at the cement-bone interface—a sequestrum impervious to immune cells and antibiotics.9

🛡️ Foreign Body Effect

PMMA lowers the bacterial inoculum required for infection. Bacteria adhere to the cement surface, producing a polysaccharide biofilm that shields them from phagocytosis and antibiotic diffusion—the primary driver of conservative treatment failure.10

🎯 Modes of Inoculation

Direct: Skin flora introduced via trocar (indolent, delayed infections). Hematogenous: Transient bacteremia seeding the cement-bone interface. Reactivation: Trauma reactivating quiescent mycobacterial foci (TB-endemic regions).2,7

2. Microbiological Landscape

The microbiological spectrum carries significant implications for treatment strategy and prognosis. Three primary categories are delineated: Pyogenic, Tuberculous, and Indolent.

2.1 Pyogenic Pathogens

Staphylococcus aureus remains the predominant organism in acute post-procedural spondylitis.11 MRSA presents a formidable challenge due to limited bone-penetrating antibiotics. Gram-negative organisms (E. coli, Pseudomonas aeruginosa) are less frequent but associated with genitourinary sources or nosocomial acquisition; these tend to be more aggressive with rapid abscess formation and sepsis.2 LoE III

2.2 Tuberculous Spondylitis (M. tuberculosis)

A striking finding in recent literature—particularly from Asian case series—is the high prevalence of TB spondylitis post-VAP. In a systematic review of 102 patients, M. tuberculosis was the causative pathogen in 35.3% of cases.5 LoE II

⚠️ The Diagnostic Trap Many cases represent a diagnostic error: patients presenting with "osteoporotic" fractures may harbor undiagnosed Pott's disease. Cement injection into a tuberculous abscess can disrupt the granuloma, causing local dissemination and potential systemic spread.11 Risk factors include a history of pulmonary TB and diabetes.7

2.3 The Indolent Threat: Cutibacterium acnes

C. acnes (formerly Propionibacterium acnes) is an under-recognized pathogen in delayed-onset spinal infections. As an anaerobic, Gram-positive bacillus colonizing pilosebaceous glands, it is easily introduced despite standard skin preparation.12 Infections are characterized by indolent onset (months to years), normal/near-normal inflammatory markers, and cultures requiring extended incubation (up to 14 days).14 It is a potent biofilm producer, often necessitating Rifampin-based regimens and implant removal.15 LoE IV

3. Diagnostic Evaluation

Early and accurate diagnosis is the cornerstone of effective management, yet it is frequently delayed due to the confounding factor of initial fracture pain.

3.1 Clinical Presentation

The hallmark symptom is recurrent or recalcitrant back pain at the treated level. Patients typically report a "pain-free interval" followed by a gradual return of pain that is different in character—unremitting, nocturnal, and poorly responsive to analgesics.12 Fever is inconsistent, present in <50% of cases; its absence should not rule out infection.2 LoE IV

  • ESR and CRP: The most sensitive screening tools. CRP should normalize within 2–3 weeks post-procedure. Persistently elevated or rising CRP/ESR beyond this window is highly suspicious for infection.17 LoE I
  • WBC Count: Frequently normal and a poor discriminator, especially for indolent or TB infections.5

3.3 Imaging Modalities

  • MRI: Gold standard. Key findings include T2 hyperintensity in the vertebral body and disc space, endplate erosion, and paravertebral/epidural fluid collections. Contrast enhancement differentiates phlegmon from abscess.16 Caution: PMMA can create susceptibility artifacts at the bone-cement interface.
  • CT: Superior for bony destruction, sequestration, and gas formation. Essential for surgical planning and pedicle integrity assessment.16
  • FDG-PET/CT or Gallium Scans: Useful adjuncts when MRI is contraindicated or equivocal—distinguish infection (high metabolic activity) from aseptic loosening.18

3.4 Microbiological Diagnosis

  • Blood Cultures: Obtain in all febrile patients (variable yield).
  • CT-Guided Biopsy: Recommended before initiating antibiotics in stable patients to optimize culture yield; diagnostic yield may be lower in the presence of cement.17
  • Open Biopsy: Often the definitive step at surgical debridement. Send multiple samples for aerobic, anaerobic, mycobacterial, and fungal cultures, plus histopathology.
ℹ️ Diagnostic Timeline

Suspect infection when back pain recurs after a pain-free interval post-VAP, with rising CRP/ESR >3 weeks post-procedure. Do not rely on fever or WBC alone. Always consider TB and C. acnes in culture-negative cases.

4. Surgical Strategy

Management of infected PVP/PKP is overwhelmingly surgical. The literature indicates that 93.1% of patients require revision surgery; conservative management is reserved for 6.1% who are too frail or have very early, superficial infections.5 LoE II

🚨 Core Surgical Principles: 3 D's

Debridement (infection control) → Decompression (neurological protection) → Stabilization (mechanical restoration)

4.1 Indications for Surgery

  1. Neurological Deficit: Compression by epidural abscess, granulation tissue, or retropulsed bone/cement. Preoperative neurological status is the strongest predictor of outcome—urgent decompression is required.19 LoE II
  2. Sepsis: Failure of source control with antibiotics alone.
  3. Instability: Progressive kyphosis or vertebral collapse.
  4. Intractable Pain: Failure of conservative management.

4.3 Advanced Surgical Techniques

Masquelet (Induced Membrane) Technique

Two-stage procedure for large bone defects: PMMA spacer (often antibiotic-loaded) induces a vascularized membrane, then is replaced with bone graft. Useful when defects are too large for standard cages or active infection precludes immediate grafting.22

Vacuum-Assisted Closure (VAC)

For deep wound infections where primary closure is inadvisable due to purulence. Promotes granulation tissue and exudate drainage. Requires extreme caution near exposed dura to avoid CSF fistulas.24

Minimally Invasive / Endoscopic Debridement

Transforaminal endoscopic approaches for disc space debridement and paraspinal abscess drainage. Minimally invasive but limited ability to remove large cement masses. Option for patients who are poor surgical candidates.25

4.4 Instrumentation in the Setting of Infection

Current evidence overwhelmingly supports the use of titanium instrumentation in pyogenic vertebral osteomyelitis:21

  • Stability is key: A stable spine is a prerequisite for infection clearance. Instability perpetuates inflammation and hinders revascularization.
  • Titanium is resistant to bacterial adherence compared to stainless steel.
  • Cement-augmented screws: In osteoporotic patients, surgeons may use new PMMA cement to augment fenestrated pedicle screws in healthy levels while removing infected cement from the index level.21

5. Cement Management: The "Removal vs. Retention" Dilemma

⚖️ The Central Tension

Biofilm theory dictates that any foreign body in an infected field acts as a nidus for persistence. Yet radical cement removal carries high risks of dural tears, spinal cord injury, and massive hemorrhage. The field has evolved toward a nuanced "functional debridement" approach.

5.1 The Case for Radical Removal

Bacteria within biofilm are metabolically quiescent and resistant to systemic antibiotics. Complete removal is the ideal goal. However, PMMA interdigitates with trabecular bone; over time, the interface may integrate or develop a sclerotic rim, making aggressive removal perilous—particularly when cement is adherent to the posterior vertebral wall.

5.2 The Case for Partial Removal / Retention

A pragmatic shift toward "functional debridement" has emerged. Partial removal—excising loose pieces, sequestrum, and necrotic bone while retaining firmly integrated cement—is supported by outcome data. In studies comparing complete vs. partial cement removal, no statistically significant difference was found in postoperative ODI scores or infection resolution rates.20 This suggests that debulking + rigid stabilization + prolonged suppression may suffice for the host immune system. LoE III

5.3 Removal Techniques

  • Piezosurgery: Ultrasonic bone scalpels vibrate at frequencies that cut mineralized bone/cement but spare soft tissue (dura, nerves). Safer dissection near the thecal sac compared to high-speed drills.1
  • High-Speed Burrs: Diamond/carbide burrs are standard but require meticulous handling.
  • En Bloc Removal: Rarely possible unless the cement ball is entirely loose within a lytic cavity.

5.4 Antibiotic-Loaded Cement Spacers

When debridement creates a void, filling it with antibiotic-loaded PMMA serves dual purposes: mechanical support and local antibiotic delivery. High local concentrations of Vancomycin, Tobramycin, or Gentamicin exceed the MIC for biofilm-embedded bacteria without systemic toxicity.29,30 LoE III

6. Antibiotic Regimen & Pharmacotherapy

Antibiotic therapy acts as the "scaffold" upon which surgical success is built. The regimen must be tailored to the pathogen, the presence of retained material, and patient tolerance.

6.1 Principles of Therapy

  1. Culture-Guided Treatment: Empiric therapy should be avoided until deep tissue cultures are obtained (unless septic/neurological emergency).17 LoE I
  2. Biofilm Penetration: For retained cement/hardware, Rifampin is the prototype for staphylococcal infections, used in combination (e.g., + Ciprofloxacin) to prevent resistance.31
  3. Bone Penetration: Fluoroquinolones, Linezolid, and Clindamycin have excellent bone bioavailability.

7. Outcomes & Prognosis

Prognosis is guarded. While infection control is achievable, functional recovery is often incomplete and mortality risk substantial.

7.1 Mortality

⚠️ 12.3% Mortality Rate

A systematic review of 102 patients reported 12.3% mortality; other series report up to 17%.5,36 Deaths are rarely from sepsis itself, but from sequelae in a frail population: immobility → pneumonia, DVT/PE, cardiac/renal failure exacerbation.

7.2 Neurological Recovery

The strongest predictor of post-treatment mobility is preoperative neurological status (OR = 6.0, P = 0.001).5 Patients presenting with frank paralysis have significantly poorer outcomes than those still ambulatory. This underscores the urgency of decompression. LoE II

7.3 Functional Outcomes

  • Pain Relief: Surgical revision reduces VAS scores from a mean of 8.2 preoperatively to 4.3 post-revision.37
  • No difference in ODI improvement between radical vs. partial cement removal—supporting the "functional debridement" approach.20

7.4 Recurrence

Recurrence rates are 0–10% in surgically treated cohorts.31 Risk factors include inadequate debridement, undiagnosed fungal/mycobacterial infection, antibiotic non-compliance, and host factors (uncontrolled diabetes, malnutrition).4

8. Integrated Synthesis & Future Directions

8.1 The "Paradox of Stability"

A key insight from the literature: in traditional osteomyelitis treatment, foreign bodies are the enemy. Yet in infected PVP, the cure often involves adding more foreign material (pedicle screws, titanium cages). Stability acts as an immunomodulator—a mechanically stable spine reduces local inflammation, prevents micro-trauma, and facilitates neovascularization, allowing the immune system and antibiotics to reach the infection. Rigid fixation is thus a biological necessity, not merely a mechanical adjunct.

8.2 The Shift Toward "Functional" Clearance

The field is moving from a "radical resection" model toward a "functional clearance" model. Data supporting clinical cure despite retained cement—provided the spine is stable and antibiotics are prolonged—offers a pragmatic solution for elderly, high-risk patients.

8.3 The "Silent Epidemic" of Misdiagnosis

The high TB rate in infected PVP cases (35.3%) suggests systematic failure in preoperative screening, particularly in endemic regions. This necessitates a lower threshold for preoperative MRI and biopsy in patients with atypical fracture patterns (e.g., posterior element involvement, soft tissue mass) or constitutional symptoms before the initial vertebroplasty is performed.

8.4 Future Directions

  1. Bioactive Cements: Developing cements with intrinsic antimicrobial properties (silver nanoparticles, bioactive glass) that do not rely on antibiotic elution.
  2. Rapid Diagnostics: Next-generation sequencing (NGS) for rapid identification of slow-growing pathogens like C. acnes or TB.
  3. Preventive Strategies: Clarifying the role of pre-procedural screening (MRI, inflammatory markers) to identify "at-risk" fractures before cement injection.

9. Conclusion & Key Takeaways

✅ Five Critical Principles
  1. Prevention is paramount: Rigorous aseptic technique and preoperative screening for occult infection or atypical fracture patterns (TB, tumor).
  2. Surgery is standard: Conservative management has a high failure rate (93.1% require revision). Offer early surgical debridement/stabilization, especially with neurological deficits.
  3. Tailored debridement: Complete cement removal is ideal but should not compromise spinal cord integrity. Partial removal + stability + prolonged antibiotics is valid.
  4. Hardware is safe: Titanium instrumentation in the infected spine is safe and necessary for cure.
  5. Antibiotic precision: Culture-guided therapy. Cover MRSA and Gram-negatives empirically. Maintain high suspicion for TB and C. acnes in culture-negative cases.
