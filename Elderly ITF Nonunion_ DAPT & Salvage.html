1. Pathophysiological & Biomechanical Context

The management of an intertrochanteric femur fracture (ITF) nonunion in an elderly patient who has failed prolonged conservative management via skeletal traction represents one of the most formidable challenges in modern clinical medicine. This clinical scenario presents a devastating triad:

  • A painful, ununited proximal femur fracture with no radiographic bridging callus at ≥15 weeks post-injury
  • Severe systemic deconditioning from three months of forced recumbency
  • An extreme cardiovascular risk profile dictated by a recent coronary drug-eluting stent (DES) on dual antiplatelet therapy (DAPT)
⚠ Critical Insight: The failure of non-operative management in this demographic guarantees catastrophic functional decline — severe regional disuse osteopenia, decubitus ulcerations, high DVT incidence, and fatal hypostatic pneumonia from poor pulmonary excursion. Level III

1.1 Why Intertrochanteric Nonunion Occurs

Intertrochanteric fractures are extracapsular and occur in the highly vascularized metaphyseal region of the proximal femur, an area historically associated with high primary union rates. Nonunion in this region is relatively rare in the modern era due to abundant blood supply and advanced internal fixation devices. Level III

Primary intertrochanteric nonunion — defined as lack of radiographic progression toward healing with persistent fracture lines and no bridging callus at ≥15 weeks post-injury — is primarily a mechanical issue resulting from:[1,3]

  • Suboptimal stabilization and fracture collapse into severe varus alignment
  • Posteromedial comminution depleting the critical calcar buttress
  • Three months of skeletal traction guaranteeing mechanical collapse via continuous muscular pull of the iliopsoas and hip abductors
  • Chronic displacement creating an atrophic, sclerotic nonunion bed biologically hostile to spontaneous healing Level III
🔴 The Surgical Dilemma: Surgical intervention threatens acute stent thrombosis (mortality >40%) if DAPT is prematurely ceased, or fatal uncontrollable perioperative hemorrhage if systemic antiplatelet inhibition is maintained during massive endosteal reaming and osteotomy. Level III

2. Perioperative Antithrombotic Management & 2024 AHA/ACC Guidelines

The presence of a recently implanted coronary drug-eluting stent fundamentally alters the surgical timeline. The exposed metallic strut architecture within the coronary endothelium remains highly thrombogenic until complete endothelialization. Premature cessation of P2Y12 inhibitors incites a rebound hypercoagulable state, frequently culminating in acute STEMI with mortality rates exceeding 40%.[5,6]

2.1 Paradigm Shift: Compressed DAPT Timelines

The updated 2024 AHA/ACC/ACS/ASNC/HRS/SCA/SCCT/SCMR/SVM Guideline for Perioperative Cardiovascular Management for Noncardiac Surgery introduces a critical paradigm shift. Historically, a rigid 6–12 month delay was mandated for noncardiac surgery post-DES. The 2024 consensus has significantly compressed this timeline based on the rapid endothelialization profile of contemporary second- and third-generation DES.[6,10] Level I

📋 2024 AHA/ACC Key Thresholds:
  • DAPT should continue without interruption for minimum 3 months post-DES placement
  • DAPT should continue for minimum 30 days post-bare-metal stent (BMS)
  • When surgery cannot be deferred, P2Y12 inhibitor must be temporarily discontinued while aspirin monotherapy is rigidly maintained
Level I — 2024 AHA/ACC Guidelines

2.2 Aspirin Monotherapy: The Uninterrupted Baseline

Continuation of aspirin monotherapy throughout the perioperative window is a cornerstone of modern perioperative consensus. Aspirin irreversibly acetylates COX-1, preventing thromboxane A2 synthesis. A large-scale meta-analysis of 41 studies and 49,590 patients demonstrated that while uninterrupted perioperative aspirin increases bleeding events by ~50%, it does not significantly increase severity of bleeding, need for massive reoperations, or fatal hemorrhagic complications.[12] Level II

2.4 Intravenous Bridging Protocols

In high-risk cohorts undergoing major surgery within the vulnerable 3-month post-DES window, the mandated 3–7 day oral P2Y12 washout period creates a critical vulnerability. Intravenous bridging therapies provide potent, short-acting platelet inhibition that can be discontinued immediately pre-incision and rapidly reversed.[12,14]

Cangrelor: The Premier Bridging Agent

Cangrelor is a potent, intravenous, direct, reversible P2Y12 receptor antagonist with an ultra-short plasma half-life of 3–5 minutes and complete platelet function restoration within 60 minutes of cessation.[14] Level II

✅ BRIDGE Trial (Phase II RCT, N=210): Cangrelor maintained optimal platelet inhibition (PRU < 240) in 98.8% of patients vs. 19.0% placebo (p<0.001), without significantly increasing severe surgery-related bleeding (11.8% vs. 10.4%).[14] Level II
✅ MONET BRIDGE Study: Assessed cangrelor bridging in patients undergoing cardiac and noncardiac surgeries within 12 months of coronary stent implantation. No ischemic outcomes or stent thromboses postoperatively at 30-day follow-up. Mean hemoglobin drop restricted to <2 g/dL with no fatal hemorrhages.[17,18] Level II

Tirofiban & Eptifibatide: Secondary Alternatives

In the absence of cangrelor, small-molecule Glycoprotein IIb/IIIa (GPIIb/IIIa) receptor antagonists serve as secondary bridging alternatives. Tirofiban competitively blocks the final common pathway of platelet aggregation. However, it has a longer half-life (~2 hours), requires a 4–8 hour washout period, and must be reduced by 50% in renal impairment.[8,21] Level III

📋 Cangrelor Protocol (MD Anderson Standard):
  • Cease oral P2Y12 inhibitor per pharmacokinetic window (e.g., 5 days for clopidogrel)
  • Admit 72 hrs pre-op → continuous IV cangrelor at 0.75 mcg/kg/min
  • Halt infusion 1–6 hours pre-incision
  • If neuraxial anesthesia planned: hold ≥3 hours prior; resume 8 hours after catheter removal
  • Post-op: load oral P2Y12 (e.g., clopidogrel 300–600 mg) once hemostasis confirmed
Level II

3. Salvage Surgical Strategies

The decision between revision Cephalomedullary Nailing (CMN/PFNA) and Conversion Total Hip Arthroplasty (cTHA) or Conversion Hemiarthroplasty (cHA) is dictated by residual proximal femoral bone stock, fracture geometry, and the patient's physiological capacity.[23]

3.1 The Biomechanical Futility of Revision CMN in Atrophic Nonunion

🔴 Contraindicated Approach: Revision osteosynthesis with CMN is mechanically contraindicated in this scenario. Three months of skeletal traction has decimated biological potential and BMD of the proximal femur.

In an atrophic nonunion with profound varus collapse, the femoral head is frequently hollowed out ("eggshell" bone phenomenon), and the posteromedial calcar is non-existent. Cyclic physiological loading transfers entirely to the metallic implant, inevitably leading to:[4,25] Level III

  • Implant fatigue failure and catastrophic lag screw cut-out
  • "Z-effect" hardware migration through osteoporotic articular surface
  • TFNA breakage rate of 1.5% (7× higher than predecessor PFNA at 0.2%, p<0.001)[27]
  • Short nails: OR 2.12 for implant failure, OR 4.03 for peri-implant fractures[28]
📊 JBJS Registry Data (N=22,308): In patients with severe systemic disease (ASA ≥3), helical blades showed no protective effect against aseptic revision compared to lag screws (adjusted HR 1.03, p=NS).[26] Level II

3.2 Conversion Arthroplasty: The Definitive Mechanical Solution

Salvage hip arthroplasty completely bypasses biological fracture healing by replacing compromised osteoarticular segments, allowing immediate, unrestricted full weight-bearing.[30] Level III

Key Technical Principles

  • Calcar-replacing implants with built-up medial metallic platforms are mandatory to substitute for the missing posteromedial buttress[32,33]
  • Long diaphyseal-engaging stems must bypass old traction pin tracts and achieve secure engagement in the pristine diaphyseal isthmus[32]
  • Third-generation PMMA cementing techniques provide immediate micro-interlock with osteoporotic endosteum, filling structural voids from prior pin tracts[35]
  • Dual-mobility components or large heads (32–36+ mm) recommended due to abductor insufficiency[32]
✅ Survivorship Data: Calcar-replacement designs demonstrate 100% survival at 7 years and 87.5% at 10 years (95% CI 67.3%–100%).[34] Level III

4. Adjuvant Osteoanabolic Pharmacotherapy

When the patient is medically unfit for major surgery — due to refractory cardiogenic shock, severe uncorrectable bleeding diathesis, or inability to safely navigate DAPT bridging — teriparatide (TPTD, PTH 1-34) represents the only evidence-based non-invasive pharmacological avenue to stimulate biological union.[40,43]

4.1 Mechanism of Action

TPTD is a synthetic analogue of the 34 N-terminal amino acids of endogenous PTH. Intermittent daily subcutaneous administration yields a profound osteoanabolic effect by:[40,42] Level II

  • Stimulating osteoblast differentiation and downregulating apoptosis
  • Upregulating IGF-1 and canonical Wnt/β-catenin signaling
  • Driving accelerated cartilaginous callus formation and endochondral ossification
  • Enhancing endocortical and periosteal bone apposition

4.2 Clinical Efficacy Evidence

✅ Systematic Review (64 subjects): Off-label TPTD (20 μg/day SC) for established delayed/nonunion achieved 95.3% complete radiographic union rate. Mean therapy duration: 7.3 weeks to 9.7 months. No severe side effects reported.[43] Level II
✅ RCT — TPTD vs. Risedronate (N=224, 78 weeks): In pertrochanteric hip fractures, TPTD cohort demonstrated:
  • Significantly accelerated TUG test recovery at 6, 12, 18, 26 weeks (differences: −5.7, −4.4, −3.1, −3.1 sec; p<0.05 overall)
  • Lower VAS pain scores at 12 and 18 weeks (−10.6 and −11.9 mm; p<0.05)
Level I
✅ Meta-Analysis (2026, Keshav et al.): TPTD significantly reduces union time, diminishes varus collapse, and mitigates mechanical complication rates in osteoporotic intertrochanteric fractures. Patients demonstrated superior early functional outcomes enabling faster mobilization.[44,45] Level II
📋 Safety in Cardiac Patients: TPTD operates entirely independently of the coagulation cascade and has no known interactions with P2Y12 inhibitors, aspirin, cangrelor, or tirofiban. Adverse events are mild: transient hypercalcemia, hyperuricemia, and injection site pruritus/bruising.[43,48] Level I
⚠ Off-Label Use Notice: TPTD for fracture nonunion is strictly off-label. Clinicians must engage in extensive shared decision-making, obtain explicit informed consent regarding unapproved indication, and navigate insurance coverage barriers.[50]

5. Clinical Decision Matrix for the Geriatric DAPT Patient

The following algorithm synthesizes the EBM consensus into a rigid, sequential decision pathway:

1

Phase 1: Preoperative Cardiovascular Risk Stratification & DAPT Bridging

  • Verify exact DES implantation date
  • Maintain aspirin 81 mg daily without interruption
  • Discontinue oral P2Y12 per pharmacokinetic window (7/5/3 days for prasugrel/clopidogrel/ticagrelor)
  • Admit 72 hrs pre-op for continuous IV cangrelor 0.75 mcg/kg/min (or tirofiban 0.1 mcg/kg/min if cangrelor unavailable)
  • Halt cangrelor 1–6 hrs pre-incision
Level I
2

Phase 2: Intraoperative Salvage Modality Selection

  • Reject revision CMN — mechanically contraindicated in atrophic nonunion with eggshell bone
  • Execute Conversion Arthroplasty (cTHA preferred over cHA)
  • Use calcar-replacing, long diaphyseal-engaging, cemented stems
  • Employ dual-mobility or large-head (32–36+ mm) acetabular components
  • Anticipate massive blood loss (>1000 mL) and 44% transfusion probability
  • If hemodynamics crash or procedure >2 hrs prohibited: accept rapidly cemented cHA over cTHA
Level II–III
3

Phase 3: Postoperative Antithrombotic Resumption & Osteoanabolic Support

  • Immediately resume oral P2Y12 with full loading dose (e.g., clopidogrel 300–600 mg) once hemostasis verified
  • If NPO or intubated: restart IV cangrelor
  • Initiate off-label teriparatide 20 μg/day SC to accelerate osseointegration and reduce pain
Level I–II
