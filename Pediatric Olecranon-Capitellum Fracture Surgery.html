1. Introduction: The Challenge of a Rare Injury Pattern

Concomitant intra-articular fractures of the olecranon and capitellum in the pediatric population represent a formidable surgical challenge due to their extreme rarity and the complex biomechanics of the developing elbow joint. Evidence Level V

Epidemiologically, this injury pattern is exceptional: olecranon fractures comprise approximately 5% of all elbow fractures in children, while capitellum fractures account for less than 1%. [1] Their simultaneous occurrence strongly suggests a high-energy trauma mechanism—most commonly a fall onto an outstretched hand or a flexed elbow—generating a combination of axial loading, shear forces, and rotational moments that overwhelm both the ulnohumeral and radiocapitellar joints. [1]

This injury pattern is often associated with elbow dislocation or subluxation. During such an event, the radial head and coronoid process can impact the distal humeral articular surface, shearing off a capitellar fragment either during dislocation or subsequent reduction. [3] It should therefore be viewed not as two discrete fractures but as a complex fracture-dislocation pattern indicative of significant joint instability, drawing parallels to the treatment of pediatric variants of the "terrible triad" of the elbow. [6]

Key Concept — CRITOE Mnemonic The predictable, age-dependent sequence of ossification (Capitellum → Radial head → Internal epicondyle → Trochlea → Olecranon → External epicondyle) can obscure fracture patterns on standard radiographs and significantly influences treatment strategies. The capitellum ossifies early (~3 months) whereas the olecranon ossifies late (~11 years), meaning fracture characteristics and fixation potential vary dramatically with patient age. [4]

While children benefit from a thick, robust periosteum that enhances healing potential, the capacity for remodeling intra-articular incongruity is minimal, making anatomic reduction an absolute imperative. [6] Furthermore, the capitellum possesses a tenuous, extraosseous blood supply derived primarily from posterior vessels, rendering it highly susceptible to avascular necrosis (AVN)—a risk compounded by initial trauma and potentially exacerbated by surgical dissection. [14]

Core Goals of Surgical Treatment

  • Precise anatomic reduction of both intra-articular surfaces to restore joint congruity, minimizing long-term post-traumatic arthritis risk. [14]
  • Stable internal fixation permitting early, controlled range of motion—the most effective measure against debilitating stiffness. [13]
  • Preservation of growth plates (physes) to prevent angular deformity or limb length discrepancy. [14]
  • Restoration of a stable, painless elbow with functional arc of motion and normal cosmetic appearance. [16]

2. Preoperative Assessment and Strategic Planning

Clinical Evaluation

The initial priority is a thorough neurovascular examination—function of the median, ulnar, and radial nerves must be assessed and documented, along with distal perfusion (capillary refill, radial pulse). [4] The integument must be inspected for open fracture (puncture wounds, deep abrasions, skin tenting), which constitutes a surgical emergency requiring prompt irrigation, debridement, and antibiotics. [16] Significant soft tissue swelling may necessitate a brief period of immobilization and elevation before definitive surgery. [10]

Radiographic Analysis

High-quality AP and true lateral radiographs are essential, supplemented by internal and external oblique views to unmask subtle fracture lines, particularly for the capitellum and lateral condyle. [15]

A methodical review must include: [23]

  1. Joint Effusion: Anterior or posterior fat pad sign on lateral view indicates intra-articular hemarthrosis and occult fracture. [12]
  2. Alignment: The anterior humeral line should intersect the middle third of the capitellum; the radiocapitellar line must intersect the capitellum on all projections. [4]
  3. Ossification Centers: Understanding CRITOE is essential to differentiate developing apophyses from fracture fragments—a common diagnostic pitfall. [10]
  4. Specific Signs: A "double contour sign" on the lateral view may indicate a displaced osteochondral fragment. [14] The osseous portion visible on radiograph may represent only a small fraction of the total osteochondral piece, which is predominantly cartilaginous. [2]

Advanced Imaging

CT Scan — Standard of Care for Planning A CT scan with 3D reconstructions is strongly advocated as the standard of care for preoperative planning. [2] It provides unparalleled detail regarding fracture morphology, comminution, articular involvement, posterior condylar comminution, and fragment orientation. Proceeding to surgery without a CT scan is to operate with incomplete information.

MRI is a useful adjunct for visualizing purely cartilaginous fragments, assessing capitellar vascularity/AVN risk, and evaluating collateral ligament injuries. It is particularly valuable when radiographs are inconclusive but clinical suspicion for osteochondral injury persists. [2,16]

3. Surgical Approaches: A Comparative Analysis

The selection of the surgical approach balances the need for adequate exposure against the risk of iatrogenic injury. The complexity of the capitellum fracture typically drives approach selection. [14]

Lateral Approach (Kocher / Kaplan)

The lateral approach is the workhorse for isolated or simple capitellar fractures. [14]

  • Technique: The Kocher approach uses the interval between anconeus and ECU; the Kaplan approach develops the plane between ECRL/B and EDC. Maintaining the forearm in pronation relaxes the PIN, moving it anteriorly. [14]
  • Advantages: Excellent direct visualization of the anterior and lateral capitellar surfaces; generally less soft tissue dissection. [19]
  • Limitations: Poor visualization of the posterior distal humerus and very limited trochlear access; requires a separate posterior incision for olecranon fixation. [14]
  • Primary Risk: Posterior Interosseous Nerve (PIN) injury. [25]

Posterior Approach (Paratricipital)

  • Technique: Midline posterior incision with subcutaneous flaps; deep dissection via paratricipital (triceps-sparing), triceps-splitting, or triceps-reflecting methods. [25]
  • Advantages: Single skin incision for both fractures. [25]
  • Limitations: Indirect and restricted visualization of the anterior capitellum, potentially compromising articular reduction. [25]
  • Primary Risk: Ulnar nerve—must be identified and protected; formal mobilization often required for medial exposure. [28]

Trans-Olecranon Approach

This extensile approach provides unparalleled, panoramic visualization of the entire distal humeral articular surface. [29]

  • Technique: A chevron-type osteotomy of the olecranon at the "bare area" of the greater sigmoid notch; the proximal fragment with triceps is reflected proximally, opening the joint like a book. [18,29]
  • Indications: Complex capitellum fractures with significant medial extension into the trochlea (Dubberley type 2 or 3) or substantial posterior condylar comminution (type B). [14]
  • Advantages: Unmatched exposure allows precise reduction of complex comminuted fragments impossible through limited approaches. [25]
  • Limitations: Most invasive approach; intentionally creates a new intra-articular fracture (osteotomy) with its own complication risks (delayed union, nonunion, symptomatic hardware). [25]
  • Primary Risk: Ulnar nerve—mobilization and protection mandatory. [29]

4. Operative Sequence and Fixation Techniques

Fundamental Principle

The distal humeral articular block (the "column") should be reconstructed first, creating a stable anatomical template against which the proximal ulna and radius (the "hinge") can be accurately reduced and stabilized. In this injury, the capitellum fracture should be addressed before or concurrently with final olecranon fixation.

Anesthesia and Positioning

General anesthesia with a sterile pneumatic tourniquet. [2,18] Supine position for lateral/sequential approaches; lateral decubitus or prone for posterior/trans-olecranon approaches. [18]

Workflow 1: Sequential Fixation (Simpler Patterns)

Step 1 — Capitellum ORIF:

  • Lateral approach → joint opened, hematoma irrigated, fragments identified and meticulously reduced under direct vision. [14]
  • Provisional fixation with small-diameter K-wires. [3]
  • Definitive fixation options:
    • Headless Compression Screws (preferred): Excellent interfragmentary compression, buried beneath articular cartilage, minimize impingement and need for removal. Inserted anterior-to-posterior, perpendicular to fracture plane. [8,14] Level IV
    • Bioabsorbable Pins/Screws: Increasingly popular in pediatrics; obviate hardware removal. [2] Level V
    • K-Wires: May lack adequate compression/rotational stability; prolonged immobilization often required. Useful for very small fragments. [16]
    • Lag Screws: Excellent compression but screw head may be prominent unless inserted posterior-to-anterior. [19]

Step 2 — Olecranon Fixation:

  • Separate posterior incision; open reduction to ensure anatomic trochlear notch alignment. [1]
  • Tension Band Construct (gold standard for simple transverse fractures): Two parallel K-wires + figure-of-eight wire or heavy non-absorbable suture through a transverse drill hole. Converts triceps distraction into compression across the articular surface. [1]
  • Tension band sutures (TBS) preferred in younger children; caution in older/heavier adolescents due to higher failure risk. [1] Level IV
  • Plate and Screw Fixation: Preferred for comminuted fractures, fractures extending into the shaft, or oblique patterns not amenable to tension banding. [1]

Workflow 2: Integrated Fixation (Trans-Olecranon Approach for Complex Patterns)

  • Step 1: Chevron olecranon osteotomy; proximal fragment with triceps reflected proximally. [29]
  • Step 2: With panoramic exposure, anatomic reconstruction of all capitellar and trochlear fragments using multiple headless compression screws. A small lateral column plate may be added for metaphyseal/posterior condylar comminution. [18]
  • Step 3: Anatomic reduction and stable fixation of the olecranon osteotomy using tension band or plate fixation. [18]
Intraoperative Assessment — Critical Final Steps Multi-planar fluoroscopy (AP, lateral, oblique) must confirm anatomic reduction and correct hardware placement. The elbow is taken through full passive ROM under direct vision and fluoroscopy to verify stability, absence of crepitus or hardware impingement, and congruent reduction throughout the entire arc of motion. [8,14]

5. Postoperative Management and Rehabilitation

The stability of fixation achieved intraoperatively is the primary factor dictating the timing and aggressiveness of rehabilitation. A robust construct allows earlier mobilization—the most effective way to prevent post-traumatic arthrofibrosis. [2,6]

Immobilization

The elbow is typically immobilized in a posterior splint or long-arm cast at approximately 90° of flexion with the forearm in neutral position. [6] Duration varies from 10 days to 6 weeks depending on fixation stability, with a typical period of 3–4 weeks for stable constructs. [2] Active finger, wrist, and shoulder motion is encouraged during immobilization.

Pain Control

A multimodal approach is recommended: scheduled NSAIDs, oral opioid/acetaminophen combinations for breakthrough pain, and intra-articular injection of long-acting local anesthetic (bupivacaine or ropivacaine) at case conclusion, which can significantly reduce opioid requirements in the first 72 hours. [17,35]

Rehabilitation Protocol

I

Phase 1 — Immobilization (0–4 weeks)

Protect repair, control edema, maintain motion in adjacent joints (fingers, wrist, shoulder).

II

Phase 2 — Early Mobilization (4–8 weeks)

Gentle active and active-assisted ROM exercises. Aggressive passive stretching is explicitly avoided to prevent heterotopic ossification. [17] Hydrotherapy/swimming excellent for gentle, low-impact motion. A hinged elbow brace may provide protection with controlled arc of motion. [8]

III

Phase 3 — Strengthening (8–12+ weeks)

Gradual strengthening once functional, pain-free arc achieved with radiographic union. Non-contact sports at ~3 months; contact sports delayed further based on individual progress. [9]

Hardware Removal

  • Percutaneous K-wires: Removed in clinic at 3–6 weeks. [8]
  • Headless compression screws / buried implants: Do not routinely require removal unless symptomatic. [14]
  • Bioabsorbable implants: Designed to resorb; no removal needed. [2]
  • Olecranon plates / prominent TBW: Common reason for elective removal after healing. [14]

7. Recommended Management Algorithm

1

Diagnosis

Maintain high index of suspicion in high-energy elbow trauma. Obtain multi-planar radiographs (AP, lateral, obliques). Proceed to CT with 3D reconstructions in all confirmed or highly suspected cases. [2,4]

2

Surgical Planning

Classify both fractures (Dubberley for capitellum, Mayo for olecranon). The capitellum fracture's complexity dictates the approach:

  • Simple Capitellum (Dubberley 1A): Sequential fixation — lateral approach for capitellum + posterior approach for olecranon.
  • Complex Capitellum (Dubberley 2/3, comminuted): Integrated fixation via trans-olecranon osteotomy. [14]
3

Fixation

Capitellum: headless compression screws or bioabsorbable implants for interfragmentary compression and to avoid hardware prominence. Olecranon: tension band for simple transverse patterns; plate fixation for comminuted or extensive fractures. [1,8,14]

4

Rehabilitation

Dictated by fixation stability. Initiate motion as early as safely possible to mitigate the significant risk of post-traumatic stiffness. [6,17]

8. Evidence Gaps and Future Directions

⚠ Source Limitation: Due to the rarity of this specific injury combination, the existing literature consists primarily of case reports and small series focused on the individual fracture components. No landmark RCTs identified in source There is a clear need for future prospective, multi-center studies to compare long-term outcomes of different surgical approaches and fixation strategies, ultimately leading to a more definitive, evidence-based treatment protocol.
