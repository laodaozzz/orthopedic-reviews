Introduction: The Critical Final Step in Orthopedic Surgery

The Significance of Closure

Surgical wound closure in orthopedics represents far more than the perfunctory final step of a procedure; it is a critical phase that profoundly influences postoperative recovery, patient morbidity, and the overall economic burden on the healthcare system. Complications arising from inadequate closure—persistent wound drainage, dehiscence, and surgical site infection (SSI)—can precipitate a cascade of adverse events. An SSI, even when superficial, can extend hospital stay, double readmission rates, and increase annual healthcare costs by as much as 300%. Level I

In the context of arthroplasty, a seemingly minor superficial infection can progress to a deep periprosthetic joint infection (PJI)—a catastrophic complication associated with multiple revision surgeries, prolonged antibiotic therapy, and a significant negative impact on patient quality of life. The link between superficial wound issues and deep infection is well-established: prolonged wound drainage is associated with up to a 20% increase in infection rates, underscoring the paramount importance of achieving a watertight and durable closure.

Key Statistic: PJI arising from superficial wound complications represents one of the most devastating and costly complications in all of orthopedic surgery, often requiring multiple staged revision surgeries.

Unique Challenges in Orthopedics

Wound healing in orthopedic surgery is complicated by a unique confluence of factors:

  • Foreign-body implantation: Large devices (plates, rods, joint prostheses) create a permanent nidus for bacterial colonization and biofilm formation.
  • Compromised tissues: High-energy trauma causes severe contusion and devitalization, impairing local blood supply and innate immune response.
  • High-tension closures: Procedures over mobile joints subject the incision to significant tensile and shear forces.
  • Patient comorbidities: Older adults with fragile, atrophic skin; diabetes mellitus; immunosuppression; and smoking—all impair wound healing.

The Problem of Clinical Variability

Despite the clear significance of wound closure, there is a striking lack of international consensus on the optimal method. Surveys reveal that while 85–87% of surgeons believe closure directly affects outcomes after joint replacement, practices diverge considerably. A national survey in Spain found >90% of surgeons use metal staples for skin closure in joint arthroplasty—a practice contested by substantial evidence.

Evidence–Practice Gap: In some settings, residents close the wound in approximately half of all cases, and only 57% of attending surgeons perform the closure themselves. This delegation may suggest a perception of closure as a less critical part of the operation.

This report aims to address this gap by providing a rigorous, systematic synthesis of the available clinical data on closure techniques, materials, and adjunctive therapies.

Section 1: Staples versus Sutures — A Critical Appraisal

The debate over metal staples versus traditional sutures is one of the most enduring and contentious topics in orthopedic surgery. The body of high-level evidence is fraught with conflicting conclusions, creating a confusing landscape for clinicians.

1.1 Evidence Favoring Sutures: Higher SSI Risk with Staples

A landmark 2010 meta-analysis published in the BMJ (Smith et al.), including 683 wounds from six trials, found that the risk of superficial wound infection was over three times greater when the skin was closed with staples compared to sutures (RR 3.83, 95% CI 1.38–10.68; P=0.01). In the hip surgery subgroup, the risk was nearly five times higher (RR 4.79, 95% CI 1.24–18.47; P=0.02). Level I

This finding was corroborated by a 2022 systematic review (van de Kuit et al.) focusing on elective knee and hip arthroplasty: when restricted to high-quality studies with low risk of bias, patients with staples had a significantly higher SSI risk (RR 2.56, 95% CI 1.20–5.44), driven primarily by hip arthroplasty (RR 2.51, 95% CI 1.15–5.50). Level I

A 2019 meta-analysis (Krishnan et al.) incorporating 17 RCTs (2,446 patients) also found staples associated with higher SSI risk in primary analysis (RR 2.05, 95% CI 1.38–3.06). Level I

1.2 Countervailing Evidence: Equivalence or Staple Superiority

A notable 2017 meta-analysis (Smith & Nace) focusing exclusively on TKA arrived at an opposite conclusion: sutures were associated with a higher likelihood of superficial and deep infections, abscess formation, and wound dehiscence. The primary drawback of staples was higher tendency for prolonged wound discharge. Level I

A 2019 meta-analysis, after removing a single influential trial with high risk of bias, reported results became "highly fragile." When restricted to only the highest-quality RCTs, no statistically significant difference in SSI risk was detected. Another meta-analysis (Rui et al., 2016) found no significant difference (cumulative RR 1.06, 95% CI 0.46–2.44). Level I

The primary, undisputed advantage of staples is speed and ease of application, with consistent reports of significantly faster closure time. While the stapling device and remover are more expensive, savings in OR time could make staples more cost-effective overall.

1.3 Deeper Insight: Deconstructing the "Evidence Paradox"

Core Insight: The existence of high-level meta-analyses with diametrically opposed conclusions points not to a true biological paradox, but to profound methodological weaknesses in the underlying primary research—small, underpowered cohorts; poor randomization; no blinding of outcome assessors.

This "evidence paradox" directly contributes to the disconnect between published evidence and routine clinical practice. Surgeons may selectively cite evidence supporting their practice, and the tangible benefit of faster closure may be perceived as more valuable than the abstract risk of SSI. Other factors include:

  • Pain: Staple removal is consistently more painful than suture removal.
  • Cosmesis: Sutures are generally associated with superior cosmetic outcomes.
  • Prolonged drainage: Data is inconsistent, with studies favoring both methods.

Section 2: Suture Techniques — An Evidence-Based Comparison

2.1 Subcuticular (Intradermal) Closure

The subcuticular technique—passing the suture through the dermal layer parallel to the skin plane—is widely regarded as the gold standard for cosmetic results. By burying the suture beneath the epidermis, it avoids the "train track" marks of transdermal sutures. Level II

A comprehensive 2020 Cochrane Review found moderate-certainty evidence that subcuticular sutures probably improve patient satisfaction compared to transdermal sutures. A literature review in the Journal of Drugs in Dermatology (15 studies) concluded subcuticular closure is superior for aesthetic outcomes on trunk and extremities.

Crucially, this cosmetic benefit does not come at the cost of increased complications. The same Cochrane review found:

  • Subcuticular sutures vs. staples: little to no effect on SSI rate (RR 0.81, 95% CI 0.64–1.01) Level II
  • Subcuticular sutures probably decrease overall wound complications including minor dehiscence and skin necrosis (RR 0.79, 95% CI 0.64–0.98) Level II
Key Finding: Subcuticular closure provides both superior cosmetic outcomes AND a more favorable wound healing profile compared to interrupted transdermal sutures or staples.

Limitations: More technically demanding, requires greater precision for wound edge eversion, and is more time-consuming than interrupted sutures or staples.

2.2 Continuous vs. Interrupted Suturing

The choice involves a fundamental trade-off between efficiency and security:

Continuous (Running) Suture

  • Single strand along entire incision; knots only at beginning and end
  • Speed of placement and more even tension distribution
  • Less foreign material left in wound (only two knots)
  • The 2024 EFORT consensus gives a strong recommendation for continuous barbed sutures for deep fascial layers in arthroplasty Level II

Interrupted Suture

  • Each stitch placed and tied individually → superior security
  • If one suture fails, remaining closure remains intact
  • Preferred for contaminated wounds or high-risk patients (e.g., diabetes)
  • Most common method for skin closure after ankle fracture ORIF
Evidence-Based Layered Approach: An optimal strategy may combine both—continuous barbed sutures for deep layers (speed, watertight seal, minimal foreign body) and subcuticular closure with smooth monofilament for skin (cosmesis, edge security).

Section 3: Suture Material Selection

3.1 Absorbable vs. Non-Absorbable Sutures

A long-held tenet has been that non-absorbable sutures are required for skin closure. However, a 2023 systematic review analyzing 27 RCTs (5,096 patients) found no significant difference between absorbable and non-absorbable sutures for wound infection, scar formation, and wound dehiscence. Level I

An earlier meta-analysis of 10 RCTs found that absorbable sutures were associated with a significantly lower risk of wound dehiscence (OR 0.12; 95% CI: 0.04–0.39). Level I

Practice-Changing Insight: Given equivalence (or superiority) of absorbable sutures for major wound complications, the decision is guided by secondary factors that overwhelmingly favor absorbable sutures: no need for removal visit, patient convenience, comfort, and cost savings.

3.2 Monofilament vs. Multifilament (Braided) Sutures

The structural distinction has profound implications:

  • Multifilament: Braided structure creates microscopic interstices that harbor bacteria (capillarity), potentially increasing infection risk. However, they offer superior handling and knot security.
  • Monofilament: Smooth, non-porous surface resists bacterial adherence and generates less friction. The trade-off is more "memory" and less knot security, requiring more throws.

For superficial layers in orthopedic closure, monofilament sutures offer a theoretical and clinical advantage in reducing bacterial bioburden near implants.

3.3 Innovations in Suture Technology

Barbed Sutures (e.g., STRATAFIX)

Monofilament sutures with circumferential barbs that anchor into tissue, eliminating the need for knots. The 2024 EFORT consensus gives a strong recommendation with high-level evidence for use in deep layer closure. Advantages: shorter surgical duration, tighter watertight closure, uniform tension distribution, and added security if nicked. Level II

Antimicrobial-Coated Sutures (e.g., triclosan)

Coated with broad-spectrum antimicrobial agents that elute into surrounding tissue. The EFORT consensus gives a strong recommendation based on multiple meta-analyses demonstrating decreased overall SSI risk. Level II

Critical Caveat: There is currently no specific evidence demonstrating that antimicrobial-coated sutures reduce the risk of deep prosthetic joint infection in arthroplasty. They are effective at reducing superficial wound infections only.

Section 4: Adjunctive and Complementary Therapies

4.1 Topical Skin Adhesives (e.g., 2-octyl cyanoacrylate)

Skin adhesives polymerize upon contact with tissue to form a strong, flexible film. In orthopedic surgery, they are used as adjunctive therapy over a well-approximated subcuticular closure, serving two functions: additional wound security and an impermeable microbial barrier. Level III

A study comparing 2-octyl cyanoacrylate over subcuticular closure vs. staples for geriatric hip fractures found the suture/glue combination associated with shorter hospital stay and fewer interventions for wound drainage. The EFORT consensus notes that 2-octyl cyanoacrylate has been associated with lower wound complications than standard silver dressings in TKA. Level II

Primary risk: Potential localized contact dermatitis, occasionally severe. The EFORT consensus suggests a preoperative sensitivity test may be prudent. Level II

4.2 Incisional Negative Pressure Wound Therapy (iNPWT)

iNPWT involves a sterile foam dressing over the closed incision, sealed with an occlusive film and connected to a vacuum pump delivering sub-atmospheric pressure. Mechanisms include: active exudate removal, reduced hematoma/seroma formation, mechanical reduction of lateral tension, and sealed contamination barrier.

The AAOS clinical practice guideline recommends iNPWT for high-risk surgical incisions after major extremity trauma. The EFORT consensus concludes iNPWT effectively reduces complications in high-risk scenarios (periprosthetic fractures, revision arthroplasty), making it cost-effective in this population. Level II

Important Distinction: For low-risk patients undergoing primary joint arthroplasty, iNPWT offers no additional benefit compared to modern active dressings and substantially increases costs. Use should be reserved for high-risk patients only.

4.3 Hemostasis and Dressings

Tranexamic Acid (TXA)

The EFORT consensus gives a strong recommendation for intravenous or topical TXA in hip and knee arthroplasty. TXA reduces perioperative blood loss, minimizes hematoma risk, and has a protective effect against PJI. Level II

Dressing Management

  • Active/interactive dressings are superior to simple passive dressings
  • The initial dressing should be left undisturbed for a minimum of 48 hours
  • Change only when saturated or at manufacturer-recommended duration
  • Minimize the number of times the fresh incision is exposed to contaminants

Conclusion: Evidence-Based, Risk-Stratified Framework

Synthesis of Findings

The staple vs. suture debate remains technically unresolved due to poor methodological quality of primary research. However, the weight of higher-quality evidence suggests caution with staples, particularly in high-tension closures (hip arthroplasty). For suture technique, subcuticular closure offers superior cosmesis and patient satisfaction without increasing SSI risk. Modern absorbable sutures provide equivalent outcomes to non-absorbable with significant convenience advantages. Monofilament sutures are preferred for skin closure due to reduced bacterial adherence. Knotless barbed sutures demonstrate clear superiority for deep fascial layer closure.

The Guideline Gap

Notably, major professional bodies including the AAOS have intentionally refrained from issuing prescriptive guidelines dictating a specific choice of skin closure material or technique. This "guideline gap" directly reflects the contested and often low-quality evidence, placing the onus on individual surgeons to critically appraise the literature.

Proposed Decision Algorithm

🟢 Standard Protocol (Low-Risk Patient / Primary Arthroplasty)

  1. Hemostasis: Meticulous intraoperative hemostasis + routine IV or topical TXA Strong
  2. Deep Layer: Continuous, absorbable, antimicrobial-coated barbed suture (e.g., STRATAFIX PDS Plus) Strong
  3. Skin: Continuous, absorbable monofilament suture (e.g., Monocryl) via subcuticular technique
  4. Adjunctive Barrier: Topical skin adhesive (2-octyl cyanoacrylate) over closed incision Strong
  5. Dressing: Modern, active, absorbent dressing; leave undisturbed ≥48–72h unless saturated Strong

🟡 Enhanced Protocol (High-Risk: Major Trauma, Revision Arthroplasty, Uncontrolled DM, Immunosuppression)

  1. Follow all steps of the Standard Protocol above
  2. Substitute Dressing with iNPWT: Apply incisional Negative Pressure Wound Therapy over the closed incision to proactively manage exudate and reduce tension Strong

Future Directions

The path forward requires large-scale, multicenter, methodologically rigorous RCTs with adequate statistical power and long-term follow-up to definitively resolve the staple vs. suture question. Future research should evaluate bundled "closure systems" rather than isolated components, reflecting the multi-step reality of modern wound closure.
