1. Introduction & Historical Context

The surgical management of posterolateral tibial plateau fractures (PTF) represents one of the most technically demanding challenges in orthopedic trauma surgery. The posterolateral quadrant of the proximal tibia is shielded by a dense network of neurovascular and ligamentous structures, making direct surgical exposure inherently perilous. Historically, the need for anatomical articular reduction and rigid internal fixation led surgeons to employ highly morbid exposures, most notably the transfibular approach involving a fibular head or neck osteotomy. While these techniques afforded adequate visualization, they routinely compromised the static and dynamic stabilizers of the posterolateral corner (PLC), leading to profound iatrogenic complications including posterolateral rotatory instability (PLRI), fibular non-union, and common peroneal nerve (CPN) injury.

In 2010, Karl-Heinz Frosch revolutionized the operative paradigm by introducing a novel posterolateral approach without fibular osteotomy.[1] By utilizing a single strategically placed skin incision to develop two distinct deep soft-tissue windows, the Frosch approach allows for both direct articular visualization and application of a rigid posterior anti-glide buttress plate, all while preserving the integrity of the fibula and the lateral collateral ligament (LCL) complex.[1]

2. Anatomical Danger Zones & Dissection Pearls

The Frosch approach is predicated on absolute mastery of the posterolateral anatomy of the knee. The procedure capitalizes on distinct internervous and intermuscular planes to navigate safely around primary neurovascular bundles, exposing the posterolateral tibial cortex without detaching major ligamentous structures.[1]

2.1 Patient Positioning & Incision Dynamics

  • Optimal Positioning: The patient is typically placed in the lateral decubitus position on the contralateral side, with the injured knee supported by a thick rolled pillow. The intrinsic weight of the lower leg applies a sustained, natural varus stress to the knee, opening the joint gap and facilitating partial reduction of lateral and posterolateral articular fragments via ligamentotaxis.[2,3] Level IV Alternatively, the prone position may be utilized for exceptional access to the direct posterior metaphysis, though it complicates simultaneous anterior joint access.[5]
  • Incision Geometry: A precise skin incision is centered primarily over the fibular head, starting 2–3 cm proximal to the joint line and extending distally along the posterior border of the fibula for an average total length of 12–15 cm.[2]
  • The "Lazy-S" Pearl: If the incision extends proximally, it must cross the popliteal crease parallel to natural skin folds rather than perpendicularly, to prevent hypertrophic scarring, keloid formation, and severe flexion contractures.[7,8]
  • Superficial Danger Zones: During elevation of full-thickness fasciocutaneous flaps, the sural nerve and short saphenous vein must be identified and protected.[5]

2.2 Safe Isolation of the Common Peroneal Nerve (CPN)

⚠ Critical Safety Structure The CPN is the most vulnerable structure during the Frosch approach. Iatrogenic injury results in devastating motor loss (foot drop) and sensory deficits along the lateral leg and dorsum of the foot.[10]
  • Identification Landmark: The CPN is consistently found directly at the distal insertion of the biceps femoris tendon as it attaches to the fibular head.[8,12]
  • Surgical Neurolysis: A methodical neurolysis is performed for 6–8 cm along the nerve course, tracking it as it wraps anteriorly around the fibular neck and enters the peroneal tunnel.[14,15]
  • Fascial Release: The posterior and anterior crural intermuscular septa must be released to permit free mobilization. The small articular branch to the proximal tibiofibular joint may be sharply transected.[11,16]
  • Retraction Protocol: A vessel loop or soft rubber tape is passed circumferentially around the CPN, which is gently retracted anteriorly.[16,17]
  • Traction Neuropraxia Warning: A dedicated surgical assistant must monitor CPN tension throughout the procedure. The nerve must be kept completely tension-free whenever drilling is not actively occurring in its immediate vicinity.[13]

2.3 The Dual-Window Concept

The architectural genius of the Frosch approach lies in creating two distinct surgical windows through a single skin incision, using the isolated CPN as the anatomical equator.[8]

  • Lateral Arthrotomy Window (Anterior to CPN): The iliotibial tract is incised, detaching its dorsal fibers from Gerdy's tubercle. A lateral arthrotomy is performed and the lateral meniscotibial ligament is sharply detached just posterior to the LCL and PCL. A heavy non-absorbable suture through the lateral meniscus allows superior subluxation, providing panoramic visualization of the articular surface for direct fragment elevation.[8,16,18]
  • Posterolateral Window (Posterior to CPN): Working posterior and medial to the isolated CPN, the surgeon accesses deep intermuscular intervals to expose the posterior cortical wall of the tibia for buttress plate application.[16]

2.4 Deep Intermuscular Intervals

  • Lateral Gastrocnemius Retraction: The lateral head of the gastrocnemius is mobilized and retracted forcefully medially. Its bulk serves as a physical barrier that sweeps the popliteal artery, vein, and tibial nerve safely out of the operative field.[12,19]
  • Inferior Lateral Genicular Artery: This vessel is reliably encountered crossing the field horizontally and must be ligated or cauterized to prevent hematoma.[12,13]
  • Soleus Muscle Detachment: The proximal origin of the soleus is sharply detached from the fibula and retracted distally and medially, opening the corridor to the deepest muscular layer.[18]

2.5 Management of the Popliteus Complex

  • The distal continuation of the popliteal neurovascular bundle lies directly superficial (posterior) to the popliteus muscle belly. Dissection must remain strictly intimate to the bone.[8]
  • The popliteus muscle is subperiosteally elevated off its posterolateral tibial insertion, working from medial to lateral with a Cobb elevator.[5]
  • In highly comminuted fractures, a planned tenotomy of the popliteal tendon may be necessary; both ends must be tagged with heavy sutures for precise anatomical repair to prevent PLRI.[12]
  • Cadaveric studies confirm the approach reliably exposes a mean bony surface area of 3.59 ± 0.65 cm² on the posterolateral plateau.[18] Level IV

2.6 The Distal Danger Zone: Anterior Tibial Vessels

⚠ "5 cm Rule" — Absolute Distal Boundary The anterior tibial artery (ATA) pierces the interosseous membrane at a mean distance of 46.3 ± 9.0 mm distal to the joint line and 35.7 ± 9.0 mm distal to the fibular head tip.[26] It is rigidly anchored with zero tolerance for stretch. All dissection and hardware placement must be confined to ≤ 4–5 cm below the joint line.[8,26]

Standard long plates cannot be utilized. Pre-contoured posterolateral plates or reshaped 2.4/2.7 mm distal radius locking plates must be limited to a maximum length of 3–4 cm to remain entirely proximal to the IOM hiatus.[2]

3. Indications & Biomechanics

3.1 Fracture Classification Evolution: Schatzker → Luo

The traditional Schatzker classification, developed from 2D AP radiographs, is inherently deficient at mapping coronal-plane fractures. Complex Schatzker Type II (split-depression) fractures frequently exhibit profound, unrecognized posterior depression. Type V (bicondylar) and Type VI (metaphyseal-diaphyseal dissociation) fractures almost universally involve posterior column comminution.[13,20,29]

Luo's three-column classification (medial, lateral, posterior) emerged from routine CT with 3D reconstruction, catalyzing a paradigm shift from "condyle" to "column" concept.[28] Epidemiological CT mapping reveals that isolated lateral column fractures are rare (12.54%), while fractures involving the posterolateral column (PLCF) represent 62.69% of all lateral-sided injuries. PLCF involvement is observed in up to 44.2% of all tibial plateau fractures and over 50% of fractures traditionally designated as Schatzker Type II.[18,31] Level III

3.2 Pathomechanics of Posterolateral Shear Fracture

Posterolateral column fractures are generated by a combination of profound axial loading and a valgus bending moment applied to a flexed or semi-flexed knee. In flexion, the posterior half of the lateral femoral condyle rolls into direct contact with the posterior slope of the lateral tibial plateau. Upon traumatic impact, the spherical lateral femoral condyle acts as a chisel, driving downwards and backwards to shear off the posterolateral corner—resulting in articular depression, containment loss, and cortical wall disruption.[13]

3.3 Indications for the Frosch Approach

Primary Indication A posterolateral tibial plateau fracture (isolated, Schatzker Type II variant, or component of Type V/VI bicondylar injury) exhibiting cortical disruption of the posterolateral wall (Type II morphology).[13]
  • If the posterolateral fracture represents a pure central articular depression with an entirely intact posterior cortical wall (Type I morphology), less invasive techniques (lateral cortical window or supra-fibular head approach) may be safely employed.[13] Level III
  • When the posterior cortex is ruptured, comminuted, or displaced (Type II morphology), indirect reduction is impossible and graft containment is lost. The Frosch approach provides indispensable direct visualization.[13]

3.4 Biomechanical Imperative for Direct Buttress Plating

  • Failure of Anterior Rafting Constructs: The most posterior screws achievable through an anterolateral plate typically sit at least 16 mm anterior to the posterolateral rim, leaving ~40% of the posterior articular surface unsupported.[20] Level III
  • Joint Reaction Forces: During standard flexion/extension, joint reaction force reaches 4–5× body weight. Articular step-offs > 3 mm increase local cartilage contact stress by up to 75%, accelerating post-traumatic osteoarthritis.[20]
  • Anti-Glide Buttress Principle: The Frosch approach allows a pre-contoured plate to be applied directly against the posterior fracture apex, functioning as a true anti-glide buttress that neutralizes posterior shear forces. Biomechanical analysis confirms vastly superior structural stiffness compared to isolated lateral locking plates.[20] Level III

7. Conclusion

The Frosch approach represents a monumental advancement in surgical management of complex posterolateral tibial plateau fractures. By discarding the highly morbid fibular osteotomy in favor of a single-incision, dual-window anatomical corridor, the surgeon achieves the dual imperatives of direct intra-articular visualization and rigid anti-glide buttress plating of the posterior column.

Success relies on absolute anatomical discipline: meticulous CPN isolation with tension-free retraction, sequential mobilization of the gastrocnemius and soleus, subperiosteal popliteus elevation, and strict adherence to the "5 cm rule" to protect the anterior tibial artery.

Extensive EBM metrics—spanning Level III meta-analyses and retrospective cohorts—definitively establish that while the Frosch approach is technically demanding and marginally increases operative duration, it yields anatomically superior reductions, significantly enhances long-term functional knee scores, ensures near-100% union rates, and eradicates the debilitating complications associated with traditional fibular osteotomies.
