1. The Paradigm Shift in PGY-1 Education

The transition from medical student to PGY-1 orthopaedic resident represents one of the most profound shifts in medical education. Historically, the orthopaedic internship was often fragmented, frequently indistinguishable from a preliminary general surgery year, with residents spending minimal time on orthopaedic services. This model was predicated on the assumption that surgical principles were universal and that orthopaedic-specific skills could be delayed until the PGY-2 year. However, the increasing complexity of musculoskeletal care, the technological demands of modern instrumentation, and the emphasis on patient safety have necessitated a fundamental restructuring of the intern curriculum.[1]

1.1 ACGME and ABOS Regulatory Framework

In response to these evolving demands, the Accreditation Council for Graduate Medical Education (ACGME) and the American Board of Orthopaedic Surgery (ABOS) implemented significant changes to the PGY-1 curriculum. The current mandates require that the PGY-1 year must include six months of orthopaedic surgery rotations.[1] Level III This regulatory shift ensures that residents are immersed in their specialty early, fostering proficiency in basic surgical skills, the general care of orthopaedic patients (both inpatient and outpatient), and the management of orthopaedic emergencies.

The remaining six months are dedicated to a broad-based surgical education. This multidisciplinary exposure is critical for developing a holistic understanding of the surgical patient. The curriculum typically includes rotations in general surgery, trauma, plastic surgery (often focusing on soft tissue coverage and wound management), vascular surgery, and critical care.[1] This structure is designed to equip the orthopaedic intern with the hemodynamic and physiologic knowledge necessary to manage polytrauma patients and complex postoperative courses.

Key Regulatory Change
The ACGME mandate ensures that PGY-1 residents now spend a minimum of 6 of 12 months on orthopaedic rotations, fundamentally shifting the paradigm from general surgical internship to specialty-focused training from day one.

1.2 The Competency-Based Milestone Model

The evaluation of resident performance has moved away from purely time-based metrics to a competency-based framework known as the Orthopaedic Surgery Milestones. These milestones provide a descriptive roadmap of resident development, organized into levels ranging from Level 1 (Novice) to Level 5 (Expert).[3] Level III

For the PGY-1 resident, the primary goal is to establish a foundation at Level 1 and progress toward Level 2 in core competencies. It is crucial to understand that these levels do not strictly correspond to postgraduate years; a junior resident may achieve higher levels in specific domains based on prior experience or aptitude, while regression is also possible if skills are not maintained.

Table 1. PGY-1 Target Objectives by Competency Domain (Levels 1–2) — Derived from ACGME Orthopaedic Surgery Milestones[3]
Competency Domain PGY-1 Target Objectives (Level 1–2)
Patient Care Proficiency in musculoskeletal history taking; focused physical examination of the spine and extremities; formulation of differential diagnoses; management of postoperative pain and anticoagulation.
Medical Knowledge Understanding the biology of fracture healing; anatomy of surgical approaches; basic biomechanics of fixation; pathophysiology of compartment syndrome and infection.
Systems-Based Practice Navigating the hospital system; coordinating care with physical therapy and social work; ensuring safe discharge planning; understanding cost-containment in implant selection.
Procedural Skills Mastery of basic psychomotor skills: suturing, knot tying, splinting, casting, and basic instrumentation (drilling/tapping).
Professionalism Demonstrating responsibility for patient care; effective communication with the healthcare team; adherence to ethical principles.

2. The Essential Library: Categorizing the Orthopaedic Canon

A common pitfall for the PGY-1 resident is "resource overload." With hundreds of textbooks available, the intern must distinguish between books that should be read cover-to-cover ("Classics") and encyclopedic volumes used for deep dives into specific topics ("Reference Texts"). The following categorization is derived from resident recommendations, program director surveys, and authoritative reviews.[7] Level IV

2.1 Must-Read Classics: The Foundational Layer

These texts are the "daily drivers" of residency. They are generally compact enough to be carried or read in their entirety over the course of the first two years. They prioritize high-yield anatomy, physical exam maneuvers, and fracture classification.

Handbook of Fractures
Egol, et al.
White Coat Essential Trauma

Provides concise, bulleted information on fracture classification, radiographic evaluation, and initial management (splinting/casting) for adults and pediatrics. When a consult comes for a "distal radius fracture," the resident should consult this text to determine the eponym (Colles vs. Smith vs. Barton), the appropriate splint, and the reduction maneuver.[7]

Netter's Concise Orthopaedic Anatomy
Thompson
Anatomy Clinical Correlation

Unlike a standard anatomical atlas, this book integrates anatomy with clinical relevance. It presents "need-to-know" anatomy including cross-sectional anatomy (vital for MRI interpretation), surgical approaches, and specific physical exam tests. Consistently rated as a top resource for "quick hits" before clinic or the OR.[7]

Surgical Exposures in Orthopaedics
Hoppenfeld
Gold Standard Surgical Approach

The gold standard for understanding dissection planes, "internervous" intervals (safe zones between nerve distributions), and the dangers associated with each approach. Reviewing the specific approach the night before a case is a non-negotiable habit for the PGY-1.[10]

Physical Examination of the Spine and Extremities
Hoppenfeld
Physical Exam

While older, this text remains the definitive guide on surface anatomy and provocative testing. It teaches exactly where to palpate bony landmarks and how to perform physical exam maneuvers correctly. Essential for developing clinical acumen in the outpatient setting.[8]

Pocket Pimped: Orthopedic Surgery
—
Survival Guide Socratic Method

Designed specifically for the Socratic method ("pimping") used in surgical education. Covers the most common questions attendings ask during cases, focusing on high-yield facts, classifications, and clinical pearls.[10]

2.2 Reference Texts: The Subspecialty "Bibles"

Reference texts are encyclopedic in nature. Residents are not expected to read these cover-to-cover during their intern year. Instead, these should be consulted when preparing for a specific case, writing a presentation, or conducting a deep dive into a pathology encountered on service.

Table 2. Reference Texts by Subspecialty — Derived from resident recommendations and program director surveys[7][15]
Subspecialty The "Bible" (Reference Text) Significance & Scope
General / Operative Campbell's Operative Orthopaedics (4-Volume Set) The definitive text of the specialty. Covers surgical techniques for virtually every procedure, detailing indications, steps, and complications. The ultimate authority for "how to do it."[9]
Trauma Rockwood and Green's Fractures in Adults The source of truth for trauma. Exhaustive detail on mechanisms of injury, biomechanics of fixation, and evidence-based treatment algorithms. Critical for fracture conference preparation.[7]
Trauma Principles AO Principles of Fracture Management The philosophical backbone of internal fixation. Explains the biology of bone healing (absolute vs. relative stability) and the mechanics of implants (lag screws, compression plating). Essential for understanding why a construct works.[13][14]
Pediatrics Lovell and Winter's Pediatric Orthopaedics The gold standard for pediatric musculoskeletal conditions. Comprehensive and evidence-based, covering congenital deformities, neuromuscular disorders, and trauma.[15][16]
Hand Green's Operative Hand Surgery The undisputed authority on hand surgery. Covers everything from microvascular repair to congenital differences.[8]
Spine Rothman-Simeone and Herkowitz's The Spine The standard reference for spinal surgery, covering degenerative, traumatic, and oncologic conditions. Emphasizes natural history and decision-making.[15][17]
Sports Medicine Master Techniques in Orthopaedic Surgery: Sports Medicine Focuses on the technical steps of arthroscopic and open sports procedures. Valuable for understanding the flow of surgeries like ACL reconstruction.[15]
Oncology Musculoskeletal Tumors (Enneking/Peabody) Enneking's original works established the staging systems (GTM). Modern texts like Peabody's Orthopaedic Oncology serve as the current reference. Understanding the Enneking Staging System is a PGY-1 milestone.[18][19][20]

3. The Digital Curriculum: AAOS ROCK and Standardized Learning

In addition to physical textbooks, the modern orthopaedic residency is anchored by digital curricula. The AAOS Resident Orthopaedic Core Knowledge (ROCK) program represents a standardized, comprehensive online curriculum designed to unify resident education across the country.[21] Level III

3.1 The ROCK Platform Architecture

The ROCK curriculum is not merely a question bank; it is a structured learning environment that establishes the core knowledge baseline required for orthopaedic practice.

  • Scope: It covers all 11 orthopaedic subspecialties (General Principles, Trauma, Spine, Foot & Ankle, Hand, Pediatrics, Sports, Shoulder & Elbow, Hip & Knee, Oncology, and Basic Science).[21]
  • Content: The platform houses over 545 chapters written and reviewed by subject matter experts. This content is integrated with multimedia resources, including surgical videos and techniques.[22]
  • Adaptive Learning: ROCK allows for PGY-specific tracking. Program directors can assign specific modules to PGY-1s (e.g., Basic Science, Principles of Trauma) to ensure a foundational understanding before advancing to complex subspecialty topics.
Strategic Tip
The PGY-1 should focus on the "General Principles" and "Basic Science" sections of ROCK and ResStudy, as these are heavily weighted for interns. The goal is to build a base, not just memorize answers.[10]

3.2 Integration with OITE Preparation

The Orthopaedic In-Training Examination (OITE) is an annual exam taken by all residents. Success on the OITE is predictive of success on the ABOS Part I certification exam.[24] Level IV

  • ResStudy: This is the interactive question bank associated with AAOS. It allows residents to identify strengths and weaknesses.
  • Strategy: PGY-1s should concentrate on building foundational knowledge through structured ROCK modules rather than advanced subspecialty question sets.

4. Developing Evidence-Based Practice Habits

Evidence-Based Medicine (EBM) in orthopaedics is the habit of questioning current practices and seeking validated data to support clinical decisions. The PGY-1 year is the critical period for establishing this habit loop, moving beyond "eminence-based medicine" to true evidence-based practice.

4.1 The Hierarchy of Orthopaedic Evidence

Residents should prioritize high-quality secondary syntheses before diving into primary literature, unless investigating a specific niche topic.

Table 3. Hierarchy of Orthopaedic Evidence for PGY-1 Residents — Derived from source document recommendations[23][24]
Priority Evidence Source Utility & Example
1 (Highest) AAOS Clinical Practice Guidelines (CPGs) Rigorously developed using systematic reviews. Provide "Strong," "Moderate," or "Limited" recommendations. Example: CPG for Hip Fractures in the Elderly provides strong evidence for surgery within 48 hours and regional anesthesia.[23] Level I
2 JAAOS (The "Yellow Journal") Publishes review articles synthesizing current knowledge. Perfect for rotation preparation. Reading a JAAOS review on "Supracondylar Humerus Fractures" provides a high-yield summary suitable for clinical application and exam prep.[24] Level II
3 OTA Evidence-Based Resource Lists The Orthopaedic Trauma Association (OTA) maintains curated lists of "Classic" and "Current" evidence. Invaluable for distinguishing between historical foundations and modern practice.[26]

4.3 Journal Club and Critical Appraisal

Participating in journal clubs is a key EBM skill. Residents must learn to critically appraise literature rather than passively accepting conclusions.[27]

  • The Habit: When reading a paper, use a structured checklist (e.g., CONSORT for RCTs). Ask: Was follow-up sufficient (typically >2 years for ortho outcomes)? Were the outcome measures validated (e.g., PROMIS, Oswestry Disability Index)?
  • Application: Determine if the study changes practice. Does a new study refute a "Classic" paper? (e.g., recent studies challenging the utility of BMP-2 in open tibial fractures vs. the classic BESTT study).[27]

5. Mastering Surgical Skills: The PGY-1 Simulation Mandate

The PGY-1 year is no longer purely observational. The ABOS and ACGME now require a dedicated Surgical Skills Curriculum to accelerate the acquisition of technical skills. This mandate recognizes that the operating room is an inefficient and expensive classroom for basic psychomotor acquisition.[29] Level II

5.1 The ABOS Surgical Skills Modules

The ABOS has defined specific modules that PGY-1 residents must complete. These are often integrated into a "Skills Month" or distributed throughout the year. The curriculum typically includes 17 modules focusing on basic principles.[30] Level III

Table 5. Core ABOS Surgical Skills Modules for PGY-1 Residents[30][31]
Module Objective & Key Concepts Simulation Method
Soft Tissue Handling & Suturing Mastery of simple interrupted, vertical/horizontal mattress, running subcuticular, and deep dermal sutures. Knot tying (one-handed and two-handed) until automaticity without visual fixation.[31] Foam suture pads and pig feet for practicing depth control and needle angles.
Splinting and Casting Proficiency in short arm, short leg, ulnar gutter, and thumb spica splints. Understanding the "safe position" (intrinsic plus) for hand immobilization and the three-point mold technique.[31] Plaster workshops on mannequins, followed by removal with cast saw to learn safety.
Basic Internal Fixation (AO Principles) Understanding the sequence "Drill, Measure, Tap, Screw." Differentiating cortical vs. cancellous screws. Mastering the Lag Screw technique (overdrilling the near cortex) and Neutralization Plating.[33] Sawbones laboratories with real instrumentation on plastic bone models.
Arthroscopy Basics (FAST Program) Developing triangulation skills — the ability to move arthroscope and instrument independently in 3D space while viewing a 2D monitor.[35] FAST workstation: Timed tasks (ring transfer, maze navigation) for objective feedback on skill progression.

5.2 Translating Simulation to the Operating Room

The ultimate goal of simulation is transfer validity—applying the skill to a live patient.

  • Pre-Op Rehearsal: The resident should "mentally rehearse" the case, writing down the steps from positioning to closure.[10]
  • Instrument Knowledge: The intern must know the names of instruments (Retractors: Army-Navy, Hohmann, Richardson).
  • Active Observation: Instead of passively watching, the resident should be predicting the surgeon's next move. "Anticipation is the highest form of assistance."
Key Principle
"The operating room is an inefficient and expensive classroom for basic psychomotor acquisition." Simulation must precede clinical application.[29]

6. Conclusion: The Trajectory of the Orthopaedic Surgeon

The PGY-1 year is a transformative period where the identity of a surgeon is forged. It is a time of intense growth, bridging the gap between the theoretical knowledge of medical school and the practical realities of surgical care. Success is not defined by reading the most pages, but by the consistent application of knowledge to patient care and the deliberate practice of surgical skills.

By adhering to this roadmap—mastering the "Classics" like Handbook of Fractures and Netter's, utilizing EBM resources like the OTA guidelines and AAOS CPGs, and rigorously engaging in the Intern Skills curriculum—the resident will build a foundation robust enough to support the lifelong learning required in orthopaedic surgery. The objective is clear: to move from the conscious incompetence of the novice to the conscious competence of the advanced beginner, ready to tackle the clinical and surgical challenges of the PGY-2 year and beyond.
