1. Foundational Principles & Indications

The Paradigm Shift: From Fixation to Replacement

The management of complex periarticular and intra-articular fractures has evolved significantly, driven by an aging population with increasingly prevalent osteoporotic bone. While open reduction and internal fixation (ORIF) remains the cornerstone of treatment for many fractures, its limitations in certain demographics have catalyzed a paradigm shift towards primary arthroplasty.[1]

Historically, the primary goal was anatomical restoration and stable internal fixation to facilitate biological union. However, in the geriatric population, this approach has demonstrated disappointingly high failure rates. The inherent challenges of achieving durable fixation in osteoporotic bone, coupled with compromised healing potential, often lead to fixation failure, screw cutout, varus collapse, nonunion, and post-traumatic avascular necrosis (AVN).[2] These failures frequently necessitate complex revision surgeries and prolonged immobility.

Core Philosophical Shift: Primary arthroplasty redefines success — shifting the focus from achieving radiographic union to achieving immediate joint stability, durable pain relief, and facilitating early mobilization. This "function preservation" approach acknowledges that for a specific subset of patients, the most effective way to preserve quality of life is to replace the unsalvageable joint.[1]

The key advantage of arthroplasty is immediate mechanical stability, which allows patients to begin rehabilitation almost immediately, thereby mitigating severe systemic complications associated with prolonged recumbency: venous thromboembolism (VTE), pneumonia, decubitus ulcers, and cognitive decline.[1]

Patient-Centric Decision Making

The decision between ORIF and arthroplasty is not based on fracture pattern alone but on a comprehensive assessment of the patient's unique profile.

Patient Age & Physiological Status

Arthroplasty is generally favored in older, lower-demand individuals for whom a predictable, rapid recovery is paramount.[2] In younger, active patients, every effort should be made to preserve the native joint through ORIF, as prosthetic longevity is a major concern and revision arthroplasty is more complex than a primary procedure.[5] Importantly, a physiologically young 75-year-old may be a better ORIF candidate than a frail, comorbid 60-year-old.

Bone Quality

Osteoporosis is a primary driver of the shift towards arthroplasty. Poor bone quality severely compromises screw purchase and ORIF construct stability, particularly in the cancellous bone of the humeral head, femoral head, and tibial plateau.[1] Arthroplasty circumvents this by anchoring the prosthesis in more robust cortical bone of the diaphysis, either through press-fit or cement fixation.[10]

Pre-existing Arthropathy

Pre-existing symptomatic joint disease is one of the most compelling indications for primary arthroplasty. Performing complex ORIF on a joint with end-stage osteoarthritis or inflammatory arthritis is fundamentally flawed — even if the fracture heals, the patient will likely have a painful, stiff joint.[1,13] Addressing both problems in a single, definitive procedure is more efficient, cost-effective, and provides superior outcomes.[1]

Functional Demands & Comorbidities

High-functioning individuals may accept longer rehabilitation to preserve their native joint.[15] For frail patients with multiple comorbidities or low functional demands, the reliability, reduced surgical time, and rapid rehabilitation of arthroplasty are highly advantageous.[6]

Fracture-Specific Indications

  • Articular Surface Integrity: Severe articular comminution, head-splitting patterns, or significant osteochondral impaction/bone loss preclude anatomical reconstruction.[1]
  • Metaphyseal Comminution & Bone Loss: Loss of the medial calcar in osteoporotic patients almost invariably leads to varus collapse of fixation constructs.[1,14]
  • Vascularity & AVN Risk: Displaced intracapsular femoral neck fractures (Garden III/IV) have near-universal disruption of the retinacular blood supply.[4] In the proximal humerus, Hertel criteria (calcar length <8 mm, disrupted medial hinge, complex fracture pattern) predict humeral head ischemia.[16]

Preoperative Assessment & Planning

High-quality orthogonal radiographs are the diagnostic foundation, but CT with 3D reconstructions is indispensable for complex periarticular fractures — providing detail on comminution, displacement, head-splitting components, and bone stock.[8,22]

Preoperative templating using the contralateral limb estimates implant size, neck length, offset, and component position to restore native anatomy.[23] A wide range of implant options must be available, including revision stems, modular augments, and potentially constrained components.[23]

2. Arthroplasty for Upper Extremity Fractures

Proximal Humerus: Hemiarthroplasty vs. Reverse Shoulder Arthroplasty

Complex proximal humerus fractures — particularly displaced 3- and 4-part patterns and fracture-dislocations — present formidable surgical challenges. When ORIF is inappropriate, the choice lies between hemiarthroplasty (HHR) and reverse shoulder arthroplasty (RSA).Level II

Hemiarthroplasty (HHR)

The traditional arthroplasty solution involving replacement of the humeral head alone. Best indicated for unreconstructable head-splitting fractures or fracture-dislocations in younger, active patients (generally <65–70 years) with a healthy, functional rotator cuff and robust tuberosity fragments suitable for repair.[5] Success is critically dependent on anatomical reduction and stable healing of the tuberosities.[2] Tuberosity failure is the most common cause of poor outcome after HHR.

Reverse Shoulder Arthroplasty (RSA)

RSA has become the dominant treatment for complex proximal humerus fractures in the elderly (generally >65–70 years).[15] Its primary indication is in patients with an irreparable or dysfunctional rotator cuff or where tuberosities are severely comminuted/osteoporotic, making reliable healing unlikely.[2]

RSA Biomechanical Advantage: The reversed design (sphere on glenoid, cup on humerus) medializes and lowers the center of rotation, increasing the deltoid lever arm and allowing it to become the primary arm elevator — bypassing the need for a functional rotator cuff.[29]

Evidence Summary: ORIF vs. HHR vs. RSA

Systematic reviews and meta-analyses have shown that for displaced proximal humerus fractures, ORIF provides superior clinical outcomes and range of motion compared to HHR.Level I[30] However, for the elderly population, RSA demonstrates superior outcomes to HHR and outcomes comparable or superior to ORIF, often resulting in better patient-reported outcome scores and greater active forward elevation.[15]

Surgical Technique Essentials

Positioning & Approach: Beach chair position with a deltopectoral approach (internervous plane between deltoid and pectoralis major) provides safe access without violating the deltoid — critical for RSA.[27,29]

Fragment Management: The long head of biceps tendon delineates the greater and lesser tuberosity interval. Heavy, non-absorbable stay sutures through the bone-tendon junction provide crucial control.[29,32] The devascularized humeral head can serve as autologous bone graft.[27]

Humeral Component Sizing: The superior border of the pectoralis major tendon insertion serves as a landmark — the top of the prosthetic head should be approximately 5.6 cm proximal to this point. Version should be set at 20–30 degrees of retroversion.[16,27]

Fracture-specific stems with fins or porous coatings to enhance tuberosity fixation have shown improved functional outcomes and healing rates.[28]

The Critical Role of Tuberosity Management

Anatomic tuberosity reconstruction is the single most important determinant of outcome following HHR and significantly enhances external rotation after RSA.[2]

  • 3–4 heavy non-absorbable sutures passed horizontally through cuff tendons and the prosthesis
  • Additional vertical sutures through drill holes in the humeral shaft distal to the fracture
  • Greater tuberosity should be repaired ~8 mm inferior to the prosthetic head to avoid subacromial impingement[16]

Distal Humerus: Total Elbow Arthroplasty (TEA)

While ORIF is treatment of choice for most distal humerus fractures, primary TEA is a viable salvage for a specific subset.

Indications

Primary TEA is indicated for severely comminuted, intra-articular distal humerus fractures (AO/OTA 13-C3) in elderly, low-demand patients with poor bone quality.[6] It is also considered in patients with pre-existing inflammatory conditions (e.g., rheumatoid arthritis).[12]

Contraindication: TEA for fracture is contraindicated in younger, active patients or those unable to comply with lifelong activity restrictions (permanent lifting restriction of 5–10 lbs), due to concerns about long-term implant survival and aseptic loosening.[6]

Meta-analyses comparing TEA to ORIF in patients over 65 show TEA results in significantly lower reoperation rates and a better arc of motion, with comparable functional scores.Level I[39,41]

A linked, semi-constrained prosthesis is almost always used due to inherent instability and potential collateral ligament compromise.[37] Meticulous ulnar nerve identification and protection is mandatory.

3. Arthroplasty for Lower Extremity Fractures

In the lower extremity, the primary goal is immediate, stable weight-bearing. Prolonged immobilization in elderly patients can be catastrophic, leading to rapid functional decline, loss of independence, and cascading systemic complications.

Femoral Neck: Hemiarthroplasty vs. Total Hip Arthroplasty

Displaced intracapsular femoral neck fractures (Garden III/IV) in the elderly represent the archetypal indication for fracture arthroplasty. The near-universal disruption of the retinacular blood supply leads to unacceptably high AVN and nonunion rates with internal fixation.[4]

Surgical Technique: THA for Femoral Neck Fracture

  • Approach: Choice based on surgeon experience. Posterolateral approach widely used but associated with higher posterior dislocation risk if posterior soft-tissue repair is not meticulous.[10,23]
  • Acetabular Component: ~45° abduction (inclination) and 15–20° anteversion.[23]
  • Femoral Stem: ~15° anteversion relative to femoral condyles. Cemented fixation often preferred in osteoporotic bone — provides immediate robust fixation and reduces periprosthetic fracture risk vs. uncemented stems.[10]
  • Stability Assessment: Full intraoperative range-of-motion testing with trial components to check for impingement or subluxation. Adjustments to head length/offset optimize stability.[23]

Knee: Primary TKA for Distal Femur & Tibial Plateau Fractures

Primary total knee arthroplasty (TKA) is an increasingly recognized option for select complex fractures. The ideal candidate is an elderly, osteoporotic patient with a severely comminuted intra-articular distal femur fracture (AO/OTA 33-C) or proximal tibia fracture (AO/OTA 41-C) who also has pre-existing, end-stage osteoarthritis.[1]

Advantage of Primary TKA: Addresses both the acute fracture and chronic arthritis in a single procedure — avoiding the pitfalls of ORIF (high fixation failure risk, malunion, persistent pain from underlying arthritis) and the need for difficult secondary TKA later.[1]

Technical Considerations

  • Implant Constraint: Intra-articular fractures often disrupt collateral ligament origins, necessitating constrained condylar knee (CCK) or rotating-hinge implants.[1]
  • Stems & Augments: Stemmed components on both sides are almost always required to bypass comminuted metaphyseal bone. Modular metallic augments fill bone defects and restore joint line height.[9]
  • Supplemental Fixation: Cerclage cables, plates, and screws may be needed to secure fragments around the prosthesis before cementing.[44]

4. Management of Periprosthetic Fractures

Periprosthetic fractures (PPFs) represent one of the most complex challenges in orthopaedic surgery, occurring at the intersection of trauma and adult reconstruction. Their incidence is rising with the increasing number of primary arthroplasties.[47]

Two Critical Questions

Question 1: Is the prosthesis stable or loose?
A stable, well-fixed implant can be retained → focus on ORIF. A loose implant must be revised.[22,25]
↓
Question 2: What is the quality and quantity of remaining bone stock?
Dictates feasibility of fixation vs. need for complex reconstruction (long stems, augments, allografts, megaprostheses).[25]

Radiographic signs of loosening include: progressive radiolucent lines at the bone-implant interface (>2 mm), implant subsidence or migration, broken cement mantle, or pre-fracture start-up pain.[22]

Periprosthetic Shoulder Fractures: Wright & Cofield Classification

  • Type A: Fracture centered near stem tip, extending proximally
  • Type B: Fracture centered near stem tip, extending distally
  • Type C: Fracture entirely distal to stem tip

Treatment follows core principles: stable stem → ORIF or nonoperative depending on pattern; loose stem → revision to long-stemmed component (bypass by 2–3 cortical diameters).[22]

Surgical Technique: Hybrid ORIF + Revision

  • Exposure: Extensile approaches for visualization of fracture, implant, and healthy bone. Careful implant removal to preserve bone stock.[22]
  • Fixation Around Stable Stems (B1): Locking plates, cerclage cables, unicortical screws, or specialized plates.[25]
  • Long Stem Revision: Bypass fracture by ≥2 cortical diameters for stable diaphyseal fixation.[22]
  • Bone Loss Management: Impaction grafting, modular metal augments/cones/sleeves, structural allografts, or megaprostheses.[47]

5. Complication Mitigation & Management

The risk profile for fracture arthroplasty patients is inherently higher than elective cases — older, frailer patients, traumatized soft tissues, poor bone quality, and more complex/lengthy procedures.[64]

Prosthetic Joint Infection (PJI)

PJI is one of the most devastating complications (~1% after primary THA; 0.4–2.0% after primary TKA; elevated in trauma).[65]

Prevention — Multimodal Approach

  • Preoperative: S. aureus screening/decolonization, aggressive glycemic control (glucose <11.1 mmol/L), smoking cessation, nutritional optimization[67]
  • Perioperative Antibiotics: Within 1 hour pre-incision; limited to 24 hours postoperatively[67]
  • Intraoperative: Laminar flow, minimized theatre traffic, chlorhexidine-alcohol prep, double-gloving, normothermia[67]

Management of Established PJI

Typically requires two-stage revision: (1) complete implant removal, radical debridement, antibiotic-eluting cement spacer + 6 weeks IV antibiotics; (2) reimplantation once infection eradicated.[65,68]

Instability & Dislocation

Leading cause of early failure, especially after THA for femoral neck fracture (dislocation rate up to 10%) and RSA.[10]

  • THA Prevention: Accurate component positioning, soft tissue tension, posterior repair, larger femoral heads (≥36 mm) or dual mobility constructs[23]
  • RSA Prevention: Correct glenoid baseplate placement (slight inferior tilt), appropriate humeral length/version, deltoid tension, lateralized center of rotation[69]
  • Recurrent Instability: Almost always requires revision surgery[69]

Aseptic Loosening, Osteolysis & Wear

Most common reason for late failure. Prevention: meticulous cement technique in osteoporotic bone, modern highly cross-linked polyethylene (HXLPE) bearing surfaces to reduce wear particle generation and osteolysis.[10,68] Stress shielding can lead to bone loss and increased loosening/fracture risk.[47]

Nonunion & Malunion

While arthroplasty often bypasses fracture healing, tuberosity union remains critical after shoulder arthroplasty. Nonunion/malunion of the greater tuberosity results in dysfunctional rotator cuff, profound weakness, and functional failure.[16]

Systemic Complications

  • VTE: Standardized mechanical and chemical prophylaxis protocols are mandatory[4]
  • Medical Co-management: Collaboration with geriatricians/internal medicine reduces overall morbidity and mortality[10]

6. The Future of Fracture Arthroplasty

The trajectory is toward a more personalized and biologically integrated approach, moving beyond purely mechanical solutions.

Robotics, Navigation & AI

Robotic-assisted systems (Mako, ROSA, VELYS) and computer navigation use preoperative CT or intraoperative mapping for precise, pre-planned bone cuts and positioning.[73,78] Their application in fracture cases — where landmarks are obscured — could be particularly beneficial, though cost-effectiveness requires evaluation.

AI and machine learning are poised to revolutionize preoperative planning: predicting optimal implant choice/size/position from large datasets, and developing patient-specific risk models for complications.[73]

Advanced Biomaterials & Patient-Specific Implants

  • Bioactive coatings: Hydroxyapatite and titanium plasma spray for osseointegration[73]
  • Antimicrobial coatings: Silver, iodine, or antibiotic-releasing surfaces against PJI[73]
  • 3D Printing: Patient-specific implants with porous, trabecular-like structures mimicking natural bone for enhanced tissue ingrowth[83]
  • "Smart" Implants: Embedded sensors for real-time data on loading, micromotion, and temperature — enabling remote monitoring[82]

Biologics for Augmenting Healing

The "diamond concept" emphasizes that successful union requires mechanical stability, an osteoconductive scaffold, osteogenic cells, and osteoinductive growth factors.[85]

  • Autologous bone graft: Gold standard — provides all three elements[85]
  • rhBMP-2/7: Powerful osteoinductive agents for recalcitrant nonunions/segmental defects[85]
  • BMAC & PRP: Autologous cell-based therapies with minimal donor site morbidity[85]
Future Vision: The modern fracture surgeon must be not only a master technician but a discerning adopter of new technologies — critically evaluating evidence and applying these tools judiciously to create personalized, bio-integrative solutions that precisely restore anatomy, promote robust healing, and provide durable function.
