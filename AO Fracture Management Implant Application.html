Biomechanical & Mechanobiological Foundations in AO Principles of Fracture Management

Interactive Educational Summary · Based on AO Principles of Fracture Management, 3rd Edition (2018) & Supporting Literature

1 Introduction: Evolution of Fracture Osteosynthesis

The operative management of skeletal trauma has evolved from an era of strict absolute mechanical rigidity to a contemporary paradigm prioritizing the equilibrium between biomechanical stability and biological preservation.

The fundamental tenets of fracture surgery, originally established in 1958 by the Arbeitsgemeinschaft für Osteosynthesefragen (AO), have guided orthopedic trauma surgeons for over six decades. Lvl I

The four traditional AO principles of fracture fixation remain the bedrock of care:

  1. Anatomical reduction of fracture fragments, particularly in intra-articular injuries
  2. Stable internal fixation ensuring proper healing while allowing surrounding tissue movement
  3. Atraumatic surgical techniques preserving blood supply to bone fragments and soft tissue
  4. Early, active, pain-free mobilization to return the patient to function as quickly as possible

From Absolute Rigidity to Biological Osteosynthesis

Historically, the paramount objective of osteosynthesis was complete eradication of interfragmentary motion to facilitate primary bone healing. This approach—reliant on direct open reduction and rigid compression plating—often exacted a severe biological toll: extensive surgical dissection devascularized bone, obliterated the osteogenic fracture hematoma, and increased susceptibility to delayed union, aseptic nonunion, and iatrogenic infection. Lvl II

The modern AO doctrine champions Biological Osteosynthesis (BO), conceptualized in the late 1980s by Mast, Krettek, and others. BO minimizes surgical trauma using indirect reduction techniques and minimally invasive implant application, acknowledging that an intact biological environment—preserved extraosseous/endosteal perfusion and an undisturbed hematoma—is often more critical than absolute mechanical rigidity. Lvl II

Key Paradigm Shift

Contemporary orthopedic trauma surgery transcends mere carpentry; it demands mastery of implant materials, construct biomechanics, and host mechanobiology to deliberately tailor the mechanical environment to the specific "personality" of the fracture, the injury, and the patient.

2 Mechanobiology & Perren's Strain Theory

The skeletal system is a dynamic, highly responsive tissue composed of inorganic minerals (~36% by volume), organic collagenous matrix (~36%), and water (~28%). Unlike other tissues that repair via fibrous scar, bone is uniquely capable of complete regeneration, seamlessly restoring its original lamellar microstructure. Lvl II

Perren's Strain Theory

The relationship between mechanical stability and the biological healing response is elucidated by Perren's strain theory. Strain (ε) is defined as the relative deformation of a material when subjected to physiological load:

ε = ΔL / L₀

Where ΔL = change in gap length, L₀ = original gap length. Strain is dimensionless, expressed as a percentage.

Perren's theory postulates that the type of reparative tissue capable of forming and surviving within a fracture gap is strictly determined by the local strain environment. Lvl I

Absolute vs. Relative Stability

Absolute Stability suppresses interfragmentary strain below 2%. Under these rigid conditions, primary (direct) bone healing occurs via intramembranous ossification and Haversian remodeling—osteoclastic "cuting cones" traverse the compressed fracture site, immediately followed by osteoblasts laying down new osteons. This is the requisite environment for articular fractures. Lvl I

Relative Stability permits controlled interfragmentary motion (2–10% strain), promoting secondary (indirect) bone healing: hematoma → microvascular ingrowth → soft callus → hard callus → remodeling. This remodeling is governed by Wolff's Law and piezoelectric effects. Lvl I

⚠ Critical Threshold

If interfragmentary strain exceeds 10%, both bone and cartilage formation are physically inhibited. The tissue is continually disrupted, leading to fibrous scar tissue, delayed union, or hypertrophic nonunion. Advanced computational modeling of ovine osteotomies reveals gap-closing strains averaging 51% at the far cortex in unstable constructs. Lvl II

3 Implant Material Properties: Stainless Steel vs. Titanium

The selection of implant material is a foundational biomechanical decision influencing construct stiffness, fatigue life, and host biological response. The two predominant alloys are electropolished stainless steel (EPSS, ISO 5832-1) and titanium alloys (cpTi, ISO 5832-2 / TAN, ISO 5832-11). Lvl II

Clinical Bottom Line

Titanium's lower modulus of elasticity allows a more favorable interfragmentary strain environment in bridge plating, optimizing secondary osteogenesis. In distal femur fractures treated with bridge plates, titanium was associated with 6.3× lower odds of nonunion compared to stainless steel. Lvl II

Compression Plating: Technical Nuances

Compression is achieved using Dynamic Compression Plate (DCP) or Limited Contact-DCP (LC-DCP) hole geometry. As a cortical screw is driven eccentrically, the spherical screw head glides down the ramped hole, translating the plate longitudinally to forcibly impact fracture surfaces. Lvl I

Critical technical point: When applying a compression plate to a straight bone segment, the plate must be slightly over-contoured (pre-bent) at its center. Without pre-bending, tightening eccentric screws compresses the cis cortex but causes the trans cortex to gap open. Pre-bending ensures uniform, symmetrical compressive forces across the entire cortical diameter. Lvl I

Bridge Plating: The Embodiment of Biological Osteosynthesis

Bridge plating is indicated for multifragmentary, segmental, or highly comminuted diaphyseal fractures. The plate functions as an extramedullary splint, anchored to proximal and distal main fragments while deliberately bypassing the comminuted zone. The goal is functional reduction: restoring length, alignment, and rotation. Lvl I

Minimally Invasive Plate Osteosynthesis (MIPO) techniques are almost exclusively used: the plate is slid submuscularly through small proximal and distal incisions under fluoroscopic guidance, drastically reducing the surgical footprint. Lvl I

5 Evolution of Plate-Bone Interfaces: Locking Constructs

Conventional plates (DCP) relied on immense friction generated by compressing the plate undersurface against cortical bone, inducing severe localized cortical ischemia, periosteal capillary obliteration, cortical necrosis, and delayed healing. Lvl II

Locking Compression Plates (LCP) fundamentally changed this via the internal fixator principle. Screw heads lock rigidly into reciprocal plate hole threads, converting the plate-screws into a single unified construct. Because stability relies on angular stability at the plate-screw interface (not friction), the plate does not need to be compressed against bone—it can sit slightly proud, preserving the periosteal blood supply. Lvl II

Advantages in Osteoporotic Bone

Locking screws cannot toggle or angulate, offering vastly superior pull-out resistance. In osteoporotic bone, a locking construct can only fail if screws are pulled out "en bloc" with a massive segment of destroyed bone—requiring significantly higher catastrophic forces. Lvl II

6 Optimization of Construct Stiffness

Despite the biological advantages of locking plates, their immense rigidity introduces a paradox: excessive stiffness can suppress interfragmentary motion below the 2% threshold needed for callus formation, leading to plate fatigue failure. Lvl II

Working Length

The working length is the unsupported span between the innermost screws proximal and distal to the fracture zone. Bending stiffness is inversely proportional to working length. Deliberately leaving screw holes empty over the fracture gap increases working length, decreases bending stiffness, and allows controlled interfragmentary motion for secondary healing. Lvl II

Plate Span Ratio

The plate span ratio = plate length / fracture zone length. AO guidelines mandate:

  • Comminuted fractures (bridge plating): Plate span ratio > 3.0 Lvl II
  • Simpler patterns (bridging): Plate span ratio > 2.0

Plate-Screw Density

The ratio of inserted screws to total available plate holes must be carefully restricted. In bridge plating, an overall plate-screw density < 0.5 (fewer than half the available holes) is recommended. Optimal configuration: 3–4 well-spaced bicortical screws in each main fragment. Lvl II

Advanced Modulations: FCL and DLS

Far Cortical Locking (FCL) intentionally over-drills the near cortex, so the screw engages only the far cortex. The unsupported shaft acts as an elastic cantilever, reducing axial stiffness by up to 84% while preserving torsional and bending strength, enabling symmetric callus formation. Lvl II

Dynamic Locking Screws (DLS) use a pin-in-sleeve design: the outer sleeve locks into the plate, the inner core anchors into bone, allowing limited micro-motion. DLS reduces initial axial stiffness by 74.4% and increases near-cortex interfragmentary motion from 0.033 mm to 0.210 mm under 200N load. Lvl II

Construct Stiffness Paradox

Short working length + high screw density + stainless steel plate = hyper-rigid construct → stress shielding → deficient callus → plate fatigue failure. The surgeon must deliberately engineer flexibility in comminuted fractures.

7 Intramedullary Nailing: Internal Splinting

Intramedullary nailing is the gold standard for long bone diaphyseal fractures (femur, tibia, humerus). The nail sits within the central mechanical axis, providing biomechanical superiority in resisting bending moments compared to eccentric side-plates. IM nails inherently provide relative stability, priming for robust callus formation. Lvl I

Reamed vs. Unreamed Nailing

Reaming creates a uniform cylindrical pathway for a larger-diameter, stiffer nail. Stiffness is proportional to the fourth power of the radius—even a 1mm increase in nail diameter drastically increases stability. Reaming also generates autologous bone graft (medullary reamings) with profound osteogenic properties. However, aggressive reaming compromises endosteal blood supply (inner 50–80% of cortical perfusion). Lvl II

Unreamed nails preserve endosteal perfusion and are preferred in severe open fractures where periosteal supply is already obliterated, though they require smaller, mechanically weaker implants. Lvl II

Working Length in IM Constructs

Working length = unsupported nail length between proximal and distal fixation points. Bending stiffness ∝ 1/L²; torsional stiffness ∝ 1/L. Even minor reductions in working length yield exponential increases in rigidity. Lvl II

Interlocking Configurations

  • Static interlocking (screws proximal + distal): Mandatory for multifragmentary fractures; minimum 2 screws per side. For unstable distal tibia: minimum 2, preferably 3 distal screws. Lvl I
  • Dynamic interlocking (through elliptical slot): Permits controlled axial translation upon weight-bearing, obliterating the fracture gap and stimulating calcified callus. For simple, length-stable fractures.
  • Poller (Blocking) screws: Placed extramedullarily to artificially narrow the canal in wide metaphyseal regions, preventing angular malalignment and dramatically increasing construct stiffness. Lvl II

8 Special Considerations

Osteoporotic & Geriatric Fractures

Osteoporotic bone presents a uniquely hostile environment: diminished trabecular density, increased brittleness, and paper-thin cortices cannot generate sufficient friction for conventional fixation. The paradigm must shift from load-sharing to load-bearing osteosynthesis. Lvl II

  • Long locking plates (LCP) are mandatory
  • Maximize plate span ratio to distribute forces over the widest area
  • Hybrid configurations: locking screws for pull-out resistance + non-locking for fragment approximation
  • Locking screws exclusively in the metaphysis

Metaphyseal-Diaphyseal Junction Fractures

These complex injuries require a hybrid mechanical approach:

  • Articular block: Absolute stability via lag screws + pre-contoured LCP (primary healing)
  • Diaphyseal extension: Bridge plating with MIPO, low screw density, long working length (secondary healing)
  • If construct proves too rigid: integrate FCL or DLS technology to maintain strain in the 2–10% window

9 Principles of Biological Osteosynthesis (BO) & MIPO

BO strictly dictates that fracture treatment must balance stability and soft-tissue integrity. Core tenets:

  • The fracture hematoma—a rich reservoir of mesenchymal stem cells, growth factors, and cytokines—must remain completely unevacuated. Lvl II
  • The periosteal blood supply must not be stripped by surgical instruments.
  • Indirect reduction techniques via ligamentotaxis (fracture tables, distractors, external fixators, percutaneous Schanz pins).
  • Goal: Functional reduction (length, alignment, rotation) — not anatomical reconstruction of every fragment.

MIPO is the specialized surgical technique: small incisions at metaphyses, submuscular plate tunneling, blind application under fluoroscopy. The fracture site is never directly visualized. Lvl I

Clinical Value of MIPO

The 3rd edition of the AO manual notes MIPO's capacity to boost fracture healing rates, improve functional outcomes, and remarkably reduce deep-space infections and nonunions. Lvl I
