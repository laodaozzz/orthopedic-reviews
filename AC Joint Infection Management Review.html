Management of Deep Surgical Site Infection Following Acromioclavicular Joint Fixation

Comprehensive Clinical Guidelines, Implant Retention Strategies, and Salvage Techniques Based on Current Evidence

Executive Summary

The management of deep surgical site infection (SSI) following acromioclavicular (AC) joint fixation represents one of the most precarious clinical scenarios in shoulder trauma surgery. The surgeon must balance two competing imperatives: aggressive eradication of infection, which often mandates removal of foreign material, and maintenance of joint reduction, which relies on that very material during the biological healing phase.

⚠️ Key Clinical Dilemma

This analysis indicates that while Debridement, Antibiotics, and Implant Retention (DAIR) protocols can be successful in select cases involving solid metal implants (e.g., hook plates), they face high failure rates with modern braided synthetic constructs due to biofilm sequestration. Consequently, early removal is frequently necessary, resulting in near-universal recurrence of deformity unless specific salvage stabilization—such as external fixation—is employed.

1. Anatomy, Biomechanics, and Pathophysiology

1.1 Anatomical Considerations and Infection Risk

The acromioclavicular joint is a diarthrodial joint linking the axial skeleton (via the clavicle) to the appendicular skeleton (scapula). Its stability is conferred by static stabilizers—the acromioclavicular (AC) ligaments for horizontal stability and the coracoclavicular (CC) ligaments (conoid and trapezoid) for vertical stability. Level II Evidence

  • Subcutaneous Nature: The distinct lack of soft tissue coverage over the distal clavicle and acromion makes this region uniquely susceptible to wound breakdown and bacterial seeding. In high-energy trauma, the soft tissue envelope is often compromised, creating a precarious environment for implant coverage. Level IV Evidence
  • Hardware Prominence: Whether using a hook plate that protrudes under the acromion or a knot stack from a suture-button construct, AC implants are frequently palpable. This prominence can lead to skin tenting, pressure necrosis, and subsequent sinus tract formation. Level IV Evidence

1.2 The Biofilm Interface: Metal vs. Synthetic

The pathophysiology of deep infection in AC joint fixation is dominated by the interaction between bacteria and the implant surface.

🔬 Biofilm Formation Differential

Metallic Implants (Hook Plates): Present a relatively smooth surface area amenable to mechanical debridement. However, hook plates have no osseointegration; they are load-sharing devices that rely on friction and mechanical interlock, leaving a potential dead space under the plate for fluid accumulation and bacterial growth. Level IV Evidence

Synthetic Constructs (TightRope, LARS, Grafts): These materials possess a massive surface area due to their braided nature. The interstices between fibers are typically 10–50 microns—large enough for bacteria (1–2 microns) to enter and establish protected colonies, but too small for host leukocytes (>10 microns) to penetrate. This "micro-sanctuary" effect renders DAIR protocols significantly less effective. Level III Evidence

1.3 Consequence of Infection on Ligament Healing

Biological healing of the CC ligaments (or their reconstructed grafts) takes approximately 8 to 12 weeks to achieve sufficient tensile strength. Deep infection disrupts this timeline through: Level IV Evidence

  1. Proteolysis: Bacterial enzymes and the host inflammatory response degrade the developing collagen scaffold, weakening the graft or repair site.
  2. Mechanical Failure: Osteolysis around screws or buttons—a hallmark of septic response—leads to fixation failure (pull-out) even before the implant is removed.

Therefore, a deep infection in the early postoperative period (<3 months) is not merely a soft tissue concern; it is a catastrophic structural failure of the reconstruction.

2. Epidemiology and Microbiology

2.1 Incidence

The reported rate of infection following AC joint surgery varies by technique but generally ranges from 0% to 9%. Level III Evidence

Infection Type Incidence Characteristics Evidence
Superficial Infection ~3.8% Cellulitis or suture abscesses that typically resolve with oral antibiotics Level II
Deep Infection 1–2% Infection breaching the deep fascia, involving the joint space, implant, or bone Level III

2.2 Microbiological Profile

The shoulder girdle has a unique microbiome compared to the hip or knee, necessitating specific antibiotic strategies. Level III Evidence

3. Management Strategy I: DAIR (Debridement, Antibiotics, and Implant Retention)

3.1 Principles and Indications

The primary goal of DAIR is to "reset the clock" by mechanically reducing the bacterial load to a level that the host immune system and antibiotics can manage, without destabilizing the joint.

✅ DAIR Indications for AC Joint Infection
  1. Acute Infection: Symptoms present for <3 weeks or surgery performed within <4 weeks. Level III Evidence
  2. Stable Implant: Hardware must be well-fixed. If buttons have toggled, screws have loosened, or hook has migrated, retention is futile.
  3. Viable Soft Tissue: Skin edges must be approximable without tension. Significant necrosis or large sinus tract favors removal.
  4. Susceptible Organism: Pathogen should be susceptible to biofilm-active agents (e.g., rifampin for Staphylococci).

3.2 Surgical Technique for DAIR

Successful DAIR requires aggressive surgical intervention, not merely "washing out" through a small incision. Level IV Evidence

  • Approach: The previous incision is opened. For arthroscopic-assisted reconstruction (e.g., TightRope), this may require extending portals or converting to a mini-open approach.
  • Modular Exchange: In arthroplasty, exchanging modular components is key to DAIR success. In AC surgery, "modular exchange" is often impossible (e.g., one cannot replace the suture tape without removing the buttons). This is a critical limitation.
  • Debridement: Radical excision of all necrotic tissue, inflammatory synovium, and fibrin membranes is mandatory. The implant surface must be mechanically scrubbed.
  • Irrigation: Copious lavage (6–9 liters) with saline. The use of adjuvants like hypochlorous acid or dilute Betadine has shown efficacy.

3.3 Outcomes of DAIR

The success of DAIR in the AC joint is variable and highly dependent on the implant type.

Table 1: DAIR Success Rates and Risk Factors by Implant Type
Implant Type Estimated Success Rate Rationale Evidence Level
Hook Plate Moderate to High (~70%) Solid metal surface; easier to debride; no braided dead space Level IV
Suture Button (TightRope) Low (<40%) Braided material harbors biofilm; impossible to mechanically clean internal fibers Level IV
Allograft/Autograft Very Low (<20%) Biological tissue becomes necrotic and avascular; acts as a sequestrum Level V
⚠️ Key Finding

Literature explicitly highlights that deep infections involving nonabsorbable tape or synthetic grafts rarely resolve with retention. Case reports document that patients with deep infections after suture-tape fixation required removal of the construct to clear the infection. Level IV Evidence

3.4 Limitations and Failure

If a single DAIR procedure fails, the prognosis for a second DAIR is poor. Unplanned repeat debridement is associated with a significantly higher risk of ultimate failure (implant removal or chronic suppression). Therefore, if an infection persists after one high-quality I&D, the surgeon should move decisively to implant removal. Level III Evidence

4. Management Strategy II: Implant Removal and The Cost of Cure

4.1 Indications for Early Removal

  1. Chronic Infection: Symptoms persisting >3–4 weeks
  2. Implant Loosening/Failure: Osteolysis around screws or button migration
  3. Fungal or Resistant Organisms: MRSA or fungal infections
  4. Sepsis: Systemic instability necessitates immediate source control

4.2 The Consequence: Recurrent Instability

The biological healing of the AC joint (ligament scarring and graft incorporation) is a time-dependent process.

Table 2: Comparative Outcomes of Implant Removal Timelines
Time of Removal Biological Status Risk of Recurrent Dislocation Functional Consequence
< 4 Weeks No healing; soft tissue inflammatory phase ~100% Return to Type III/V deformity; pain; scapular dyskinesis
4 – 8 Weeks Immature scar; weak graft integration High (60–80%) Partial recurrence; potential for "sag"
> 3 Months Mature scar; graft incorporation Low to Moderate Stable AC joint (maintenance of reduction)
⚠️ Critical Finding

Removal of fixation at <8 weeks for infection results in a near 100% rate of recurrent vertical and horizontal instability in high-grade injuries (Type IV/V). Level III Evidence

4.3 "Iatrogenic" Instability

Removal of infected hardware does not simply return the patient to their pre-injury state. It often leaves a worse condition: Level IV Evidence

  • Bone Loss: Removal of screws or buttons leaves large tunnels (4–6mm) in the clavicle and coracoid, which serve as stress risers.
  • Soft Tissue Deficit: Debridement removes the remnants of the native capsule and ligaments, potentially converting a Type III injury into a Type V (grossly unstable) injury.

5. Salvage Techniques: Maintaining Reduction During Infection Treatment

5.1 External Fixation

External fixation is the most robust salvage option for the septic AC joint. It applies the principles of damage control orthopedics to the shoulder girdle. Level IV Evidence

5.1.1 Rationale

An external fixator allows the surgeon to apply reduction forces (depressing the clavicle, elevating the scapula) via pins placed away from the zone of infection. This "bridging" allows for aggressive wound care, frequent dressing changes, or negative pressure wound therapy (NPWT) directly over the AC joint without losing reduction.

5.1.2 Surgical Technique

The construct typically involves:

  1. Scapular Fixation: Threaded Schanz pins (3.0mm or 4.0mm) inserted into the scapular spine or the acromion.
  2. Clavicular Fixation: Threaded pins inserted into the medial/mid-shaft clavicle, well medial to the infected distal end.
  3. Reduction and Connection: The frame (bars and clamps) bridges the AC joint. The clavicle is manually depressed and the scapula elevated while the clamps are tightened.
  4. Pin Care: Standard pin site care is crucial, as the frame will remain in place for 6–10 weeks.

5.1.3 Outcomes and Evidence

  • Septic Clavicle Non-union: Studies on infected clavicle non-unions treated with external fixation show a 100% union rate and infection eradication in small series. Level IV Evidence
  • AC Joint Specifics: Lollino et al. described a technique using external fixation combined with minimally invasive reconstruction. While their primary series excluded active infection, the biomechanical principle is validated. Level V Evidence
  • Advantages: Avoids internal hardware in a septic bed; adjustable reduction; facilitates wound management.
  • Disadvantages: Pin tract infections (up to 20%); patient discomfort; cumbersome construct near the neck/head.

5.2 Temporary Trans-Articular K-Wire Fixation

Historically used for primary fixation (Phemister technique), K-wires are largely abandoned due to migration risks. However, in a salvage scenario, they offer a "last resort" internal splint.

⚠️ Caution: K-Wire Use in Septic Joints

In a septic joint, K-wires are less ideal than external fixators because they pass directly through the infected joint space, potentially tracking bacteria into the medullary canal of the clavicle. The risk of wire migration into the mediastinum or spinal cord is significant. Level V Evidence

Recommendation: Use only if external fixation is not feasible, and with extreme caution.

5.3 Antibiotic Spacers and Bead Pouches

While not a stabilization technique per se, the management of dead space is critical to preventing recurrence.

  • Technique: Following removal of an infected hook plate or graft, the resulting cavity can be packed with PMMA cement beads loaded with heat-stable antibiotics (Vancomycin, Tobramycin).
  • Benefits: Delivers high local concentrations of antibiotics (100x systemic levels); maintains soft tissue volume; prepares the bed for future staged reconstruction.
  • Protocol: Beads are typically removed after 2–6 weeks, often in conjunction with staged reconstruction.

6. Staged Reconstruction: The Pathway to Function

Stage 1: Eradication

  • Hardware removal
  • Debridement of necrotic bone/soft tissue
  • Placement of antibiotic beads (optional)
  • 6 weeks of culture-directed IV/oral antibiotics Level III Evidence
  • Goal: Dry wound, normalized CRP/ESR

Stage 2: Reconstruction

Performed 3–6 months after infection clearance.

Graft Options for Staged Reconstruction
Graft Type Advantages Disadvantages Evidence Level
Autograft (Semitendinosus/Gracilis) Strongly preferred in previously infected bed; lower risk of late infection recurrence Donor site morbidity Level III
Synthetic (LARS) Readily available; no donor site morbidity Higher risk of late infection recurrence Level IV
Allograft No donor site morbidity Lower tensile strength; disease transmission risk Level IV

Reconstruction Techniques

  • Anatomic Coracoclavicular Ligament Reconstruction (ACCR): Provides superior stability (horizontal and vertical). Outcomes in revision surgery show significant improvement in ASES scores (mean improvement from 43 to 80). Level III Evidence
  • Modified Weaver-Dunn: Transfer of the CA ligament. Less biomechanically robust but uses local vascularized tissue, which is resistant to infection. A good option for lower-demand patients or those with compromised bone stock. Level IV Evidence

7. Clinical Guidelines and Algorithm

Step 1: Diagnosis and Characterization
Ultrasound-guided aspiration for culture (hold 14 days for C. acnes) • X-ray and MRI to assess hardware stability • Labs: CRP, ESR, WBC
Step 2: Stratification and Treatment
Scenario A: Acute Infection (<3 weeks), Stable Metal Hook Plate → DAIR
Scenario B: Acute Infection (<3 weeks), Suture Button/Graft → Early Removal + External Fixation (high-demand) or Sling (low-demand)
Scenario C: Chronic Infection (>4 weeks) or Loose Hardware → Removal + Staged Reconstruction
Step 3: Rehabilitation
Early passive range of motion to prevent frozen shoulder • Strengthening delayed until infection clearance (typically 3 months)

8. Conclusion

The management of deep SSI after AC joint fixation requires a decisive, tailored approach. While DAIR is a viable option for metal hook plates, deep infections involving synthetic grafts or suture buttons generally mandate implant removal due to the impossibility of eradicating biofilm from braided materials.

The inevitable consequence of early removal—recurrent instability—must be anticipated. For high-demand patients, external fixation offers a proven salvage technique to maintain reduction while the infection is treated, bridging the gap between septic failure and biological healing. In cases where deformity is accepted or recurrence occurs, staged anatomic reconstruction using autograft remains a reliable salvage option with favorable long-term functional outcomes.
