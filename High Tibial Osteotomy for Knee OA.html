🦴 High Tibial Osteotomy for Varus Medial Compartment OA

I. Foundational Principles of HTO in Joint Preservation

1.1 Biomechanical Rationale: Unloading the Medial Compartment

High tibial osteotomy (HTO) is a well-established surgical procedure predicated on a fundamental biomechanical principle: the realignment of the lower extremity to correct malalignment and redistribute pathological forces across the knee joint. 1 In the context of varus deformity, the mechanical axis of the lower limb—a line from the center of the femoral head to the center of the ankle joint—passes medial to the center of the knee, concentrating compressive and shear forces onto the medial tibiofemoral compartment. This leads to accelerated articular cartilage degeneration, subchondral bone changes, and clinical pain and dysfunction. 1

The primary goal of a valgus-producing HTO is to surgically shift this weight-bearing axis laterally, transferring the load from the diseased medial compartment to the healthier, preserved lateral compartment. 2 The ideal postoperative mechanical axis should pass through approximately 62.5% of the tibial plateau's width from the medial edge, a landmark known as the "Fujisawa point", corresponding to 3°–5° of mechanical valgus or 8°–10° of anatomical valgus. 8,9

🔑 Key Principle

HTO is a biological, joint-preserving procedure. Unlike UKA or TKA, it does not involve resection of bone and cartilage for prosthetic replacement—a profound advantage for young, active patients concerned about long-term durability and revision rates of arthroplasty. 13

1.2 Pathophysiological Impact on Articular Cartilage

HTO is not merely a palliative or pain-relieving procedure but a disease-modifying intervention capable of altering the natural history of osteoarthritis. By creating a more favorable mechanical environment, HTO can halt cartilage degradation and may facilitate a regenerative response. 16

📊 Evidence Summary Level I

A randomized trial comparing HTO with nonsurgical management found that HTO patients lost significantly less medial tibiofemoral articular cartilage thickness over 2 years on MRI: mean loss of only 0.07 mm (2% from baseline) vs. continued cartilage loss in the non-operative group. 17

Long-term second-look arthroscopy studies have documented restoration of cartilage defects with fibrocartilage in the previously overloaded medial compartment following successful realignment. 13

1.3 The Role of HTO in the Modern Treatment Algorithm

The primary indication for HTO remains symptomatic isolated medial compartment OA with varus deformity. 5 It is firmly established as a primary surgical option for young, active adults—often superior to arthroplasty given the known inferior long-term outcomes of TKA in patients under 55. 13

Expanded indications reflect a paradigm shift in understanding knee pathomechanics: 5

  • Meniscal Transplantation: HTO performed concurrently to unload transplanted tissue and improve survival. 2
  • Cartilage Restoration: HTO offloads areas of cartilage repair (e.g., ACI, osteochondral allografting) to protect the maturing graft. 2
  • Ligamentous Instability: In ACL/PCL deficiency with varus malalignment, HTO corrects the coronal plane deformity, restoring stability or creating a favorable environment for ligament reconstruction. 4,5,16
💡 Clinical Pearl

HTO elevates from a procedure solely for arthritis to a foundational, enabling surgery for a wide range of complex knee reconstructions. Long-leg radiographs are mandatory for the diagnostic workup of many knee conditions.

II. Patient Selection: Indications and Contraindications

2.1 The Ideal Candidate Profile

Appropriate patient selection is the single most important determinant of a successful outcome. 5 Decades of clinical research have defined a clear, evidence-based profile: 13

  • Age: Ideally 40–60 years; most robust data supports age <55 as a key predictor of success, with significantly higher 20-year survival rates. 12,13
  • Activity Level: Designed for moderately active, high-demand individuals—not necessarily elite athletes in repetitive, high-impact activities. 1,4
  • BMI: BMI <30 kg/m² is strongly associated with better outcomes, lower complications, and improved survivorship. Obesity (BMI >30) is a significant negative prognostic factor. 12
  • Clinical Presentation: Symptoms localized to the medial compartment, with isolated medial joint line tenderness exacerbated by weight-bearing. 4
📊 20-Year Survivorship Data Level II

The "favorable candidate" (age <55, BMI <30, less severe symptoms) demonstrates a 20-year survivorship of 62% vs. 44% for the overall cohort. This stratified approach is essential for accurate patient counseling. 13

2.2 Radiographic and Physical Examination Findings

  • Deformity Assessment: Varus malalignment confirmed on weight-bearing hip-to-ankle radiographs; ideally <15°–20°. A preoperative TBVA >5° (tibial origin) is a good prognostic factor. 19
  • Arthritis Severity: Most effective for mild to moderate medial OA (Ahlback grade 0–II). Severe joint destruction (Ahlback ≥III) is a strong contraindication. 5,16
  • Compartmental Integrity: Lateral and patellofemoral compartments must be near-normal. Significant degenerative changes represent a contraindication. 2,5
  • Range of Motion: Flexion ≥90°–120° and flexion contracture <5°–15°. Lateral tibial thrust >1 cm is a poor prognostic sign. 2,18

III. Surgical Techniques: A Comparative Analysis

The two dominant surgical techniques for valgus-producing HTO are medial opening wedge (OWHTO) and lateral closing wedge (CWHTO). Each has a distinct profile of advantages, disadvantages, and biomechanical consequences. 2

3.1 Medial Opening Wedge HTO (OWHTO)

OWHTO has gained significant popularity due to technical advancements in fixation and a perceived simpler approach. 3

  • Advantages: Avoids fibular osteotomy and PTFJ disruption, significantly reducing peroneal nerve risk. Allows precise intraoperative correction in coronal and sagittal planes. 2
  • Disadvantages: Creates a relatively unstable construct requiring robust fixation. Higher risk of delayed union, nonunion, and loss of correction. Most common complication is lateral hinge fracture (~9.1%). Leg lengthening (~6 mm), patella baja, and increased posterior tibial slope. 2,29

3.2 Lateral Closing Wedge HTO (CWHTO)

The traditional technique, still valuable for larger corrections. 2

  • Advantages: Inherent stability with bone-on-bone contact promotes rapid healing and earlier weight-bearing. Higher union rates. Shortens the leg (beneficial for contralateral shorter limb). Preserves medial proximal tibial bone stock. 2
  • Disadvantages: Technically more demanding. Requires fibular osteotomy/PTFJ disruption, placing common peroneal nerve at significant risk (~3.2%). Loss of lateral bone stock complicates future TKA. 2,41

⚠️ Biomechanical Decision-Making

The surgeon must think beyond correcting varus alignment. The choice between OWHTO and CWHTO should be a multi-variable optimization, selecting the technique whose biomechanical side-effect profile best complements the patient's limb anatomy, ligamentous status, and potential need for future surgery. 26

IV. Preoperative Planning and Technical Pearls

4.1 Preoperative Templating

Full-length, weight-bearing, hip-to-ankle anteroposterior radiographs are mandatory for assessing the mechanical axis and quantifying malalignment. 19

  • Calculation Methods: Dugdale/Noyes and Miniaci methods are the most widely used. The goal is to shift the mechanical axis to the Fujisawa point (62.5% of tibial plateau width from the medial edge). 8,9,10
  • Joint Line Convergence Angle (JLCA): A critical factor—this angle between the distal femoral and proximal tibial articular surfaces reflects soft-tissue laxity. A large JLCA (>3°–6°) can lead to iatrogenic over-correction if not accounted for. Planning should be performed on non-weight-bearing supine radiographs or the JLCA should be subtracted from the planned correction. 8,44
  • Digital Planning: PACS and specialized software (SolidWorks, mediCAD, OrthoView) offer superior accuracy, reliability, and efficiency over manual templating. 28

4.2 OWHTO Technical Pearls: Protecting the Hinge

The stability of an OWHTO construct is entirely dependent on the integrity of the lateral cortical hinge. 34

  • Safe Zone: The biomechanically most robust location for the hinge is just superior to the proximal tibiofibular joint. Aim the guide pin for the tip of the fibular head. 34,37
  • Protective K-wires: A subchondral K-wire parallel to the joint surface acts as a barrier to an errant saw blade. Crossing K-wires across the hinge reduce iatrogenic fracture rates. 37,47
  • Gradual Opening: Open the osteotomy slowly using sequential thin osteotomes or a laminar spreader—rapid opening is a common cause of hinge fracture. 34
  • Neurovascular Protection: A blunt, radiolucent Hohmann retractor placed subperiosteally posteriorly protects the popliteal artery and tibial nerve. 35

4.3 CWHTO Technical Pearls: Peroneal Nerve Protection

  • Prophylactic Nerve Identification: Meticulous identification, neurolysis, and placement of a vessel loop/snare around the common peroneal nerve is mandatory. 41
  • Fibular Management Options: Fibular shaft osteotomy (12–15 cm distal to head), PTFJ detachment, fibular head resection, or tibial-sided osteotomy (TSO)—each with distinct risk-benefit profiles. 42

4.4 Intraoperative Alignment Verification

Regardless of technique, intraoperative verification of final alignment is crucial. Use a long, radiopaque alignment rod or electrocautery cord from femoral head center to ankle center under fluoroscopy, confirming the mechanical axis passes through the intended target point. 10

VI. Clinical Outcomes and Long-Term Survivorship

6.1 Short- and Mid-Term Outcomes

In the short to medium term (1–5 years), HTO yields reliably positive results. Validated PROMs (KOOS, KSS, Lysholm, WOMAC) show marked improvement. Many young, active patients return to recreational activities and physically demanding occupations. 16,17,23

6.2 Long-Term Survivorship

  • 10-Year Survival: Varies from 56% (heterogeneous cohorts) to 92% (carefully selected). Systematic review: 75% overall. 2
  • 20-Year Survival: Landmark prospective study: 44% overall, but 62% in "favorable candidates" (age <55, BMI <30, less severe symptoms). 13

VIII. HTO vs. Unicompartmental Knee Arthroplasty (UKA)

For patients aged 50–60 with isolated medial compartment OA, the choice between HTO and UKA requires understanding their fundamentally different approaches: 59

  • HTO: Biomechanical procedure that unloads native tissue. Favored for younger (<60–65), more active patients, especially in heavy labor or high-demand recreation. 60
  • UKA: Resurfacing procedure that replaces damaged tissue. Traditionally for older, more sedentary patients, but modern designs have expanded indications. 60,62

IX. Future of HTO: Technological Advancements

9.1 Computer-Assisted Navigation (CAN)

Imageless systems using infrared trackers provide real-time 3D feedback on limb alignment. CAN significantly reduces outliers and improves coronal plane correction accuracy. 2,45

9.2 Patient-Specific Instrumentation (PSI)

Preoperative CT scans create 3D models for virtual osteotomy planning. Custom 3D-printed cutting guides replicate the plan intraoperatively with high fidelity. PSI simplifies the workflow compared to CAN and may reduce operative time and fluoroscopy exposure. 68,70

9.3 Innovations in Fixation

  • Locking Plates: Anatomically contoured, angle-stable plates (e.g., TomoFix) provide significantly greater stability, enabling earlier weight-bearing rehabilitation. 38,55
  • PEEK Implants: Radiolucent polymer with modulus of elasticity closer to bone. Less soft tissue irritation, potentially lower hardware removal rates. Comparable clinical outcomes to metal plates at 2+ years. 71
  • Bone Grafts & Substitutes: For OWHTO gaps >10–15 mm: autograft (gold standard), allograft, or synthetic substitutes (tricalcium phosphate, hydroxyapatite). 34

X. Conclusion and Recommendations

10.1 Key Clinical Recommendations

  1. Prioritize Rigorous Patient Selection: Strict adherence to evidence-based criteria (age <55, BMI <30, well-localized symptoms) is the most critical determinant of long-term success. 13
  2. Master Preoperative Planning: Use full-length weight-bearing radiographs and digital templating. Critically assess JLCA to avoid over-correction. 19,28
  3. Strategic Technique Choice: Select OWHTO vs. CWHTO based on patient-specific biomechanical considerations (limb length, patellar height, ligamentous status). 2,26
  4. Embrace Modern Technology: Utilize locking plates for rigid stability. Consider PSI or CAN for improved accuracy. 38,45,68
  5. Stratified Patient Counseling: Provide realistic prognosis based on individual profile. Favorable candidates may expect excellent long-term durability; others should understand HTO may serve as a mid-term solution. 13
  6. Evidence-Based Rehabilitation: Structured, criterion-based protocols are essential for a successful outcome. 51–57
