Introduction: The Clinical and Societal Burden of Hand Arthritis

The human hand is a marvel of biomechanical engineering—an intricate assembly of bones, joints, ligaments, and tendons enabling the full spectrum of human interaction. When this complex machinery is compromised by disease, the impact on quality of life is profound. Arthritis of the hand represents a leading cause of pain and disability worldwide, affecting a person's capacity for self-care, work, and recreation.1

By the age of 85, it is estimated that approximately half of all women and a quarter of all men will experience symptoms of hand osteoarthritis (OA).1 Inflammatory conditions like rheumatoid arthritis (RA) and the sequelae of joint injuries further contribute to this burden.3

Key Statistic
The resulting pain, stiffness, weakness, and deformity can render simple activities of daily living—opening a jar, turning a key—excruciatingly difficult or impossible.

The Evolution of Surgical Philosophy

The surgical approach has undergone a remarkable transformation. Early interventions were often ablative, prioritizing pain relief at the expense of function (amputation, arthrodesis).7 The modern era was forged during World War II, where visionary surgeons—most notably Dr. Sterling Bunnell—recognized that restoring the hand required a multidisciplinary understanding of orthopedics, plastic surgery, and neurovascular repair.9 This paradigm shift spurred the development of motion-preserving techniques, from simple resection arthroplasty to complex prosthetic replacements central to contemporary practice.7

Paradigm Shift
The fundamental change in philosophy: from merely managing pain to restoring the hand's intricate function.

Anatomic and Pathophysiologic Foundations

The Intricate Anatomy of the Hand and Wrist

The hand and wrist comprise 27 bones: 8 carpals, 5 metacarpals, and 14 phalanges.13 These form a series of complex, interconnected synovial joints that must balance mobility and stability.

The Wrist (Radiocarpal) Joint

A condyloid synovial joint allowing flexion-extension and radial-ulnar deviation.15 The ulna does not directly articulate with the carpus; it is separated by the triangular fibrocartilage complex (TFCC), crucial for DRUJ stabilization and load transmission.15 Stability relies on a complex ligamentous network—the palmar radiocarpal ligaments prevent dorsal displacement, while dorsal radiocarpal ligaments are essential for pronation.15

The Carpometacarpal (CMC) Joints

The first CMC joint is a unique sellar (saddle) joint between the trapezium and first metacarpal, providing remarkable range of motion including opposition—the cornerstone of human prehension.19 Stability depends almost entirely on the surrounding capsule and ligaments, particularly the anterior oblique ("beak") ligament, the primary restraint against dorsal subluxation.19 Attenuation of this ligament is the initiating event in thumb CMC arthritis.19

The MCP and IP Joints

MCP joints are condyloid (flexion-extension, abduction-adduction), while PIP and DIP joints are true hinge joints.18 Stability is provided by collateral ligaments and the volar plate.18

Reconstruction of the Thumb CMC Joint

The thumb CMC joint is the second most common site for hand OA and the most common site requiring surgery.1 Treatment of advanced, symptomatic CMC arthritis that has failed conservative management has evolved into a robust debate between the traditional gold standard and modern implant-based alternatives.

Trapeziectomy with LRTI: The Enduring Gold Standard

The foundational procedure was described by Eaton and Littler in 1973—one of the most frequently cited articles in hand surgery history.17,34 Seminal Paper — Impact Level I

Rationale

  1. Trapeziectomy: Complete excision of the trapezium removes the arthritic articular surfaces of both trapeziometacarpal and scaphotrapezial joints (pain relief).21
  2. Ligament Reconstruction: A slip of the flexor carpi radialis (FCR) tendon is passed through a drill hole in the first metacarpal base and secured to reconstruct ligamentous support, preventing dorsal subluxation.19
  3. Tendon Interposition: Remaining tendon is rolled into an "anchovy" and placed in the trapezial void. However, the true necessity of interposition has been debated—some studies suggest simple trapeziectomy or hematoma distraction arthroplasty can yield similar results.39

Outcomes

LRTI has an extensive track record of excellent, durable pain relief with success rates as high as 96% and results lasting 15–20 years.21 Long-term Cohort — Level II-III

Limitations
  • Potential decrease in pinch strength21
  • Shortening of thumb column
  • Lengthy, painful recovery (4–6 weeks immobilization)37
  • Secondary MCP hyperextension deformity from compensation37
  • Limited salvage options (trapezium permanently removed)37

Total Joint Replacement (TJR): The Motion-Preserving Challenger

Modern CMC implants are typically two-component prostheses (metacarpal stem + trapezial cup) made from cobalt-chromium, titanium, and polyethylene.42 The rationale is to provide pain relief while preserving anatomy and biomechanics—retaining trapezial height, offering better strength preservation, and faster recovery.44

Comparative Evidence: TJR vs. LRTI

Recent systematic reviews and meta-analyses of RCTs have converged on a nuanced conclusion:33,47 Systematic Reviews/Meta-analyses — Level I

  • Early benefit (0–6 months): TJR provides significantly faster pain relief and functional improvement.47
  • After 1 year: Differences diminish significantly; no clear evidence of TJR superiority for long-term pain relief or function.47
  • Implant survival: Modern designs show 91–95% survival at 5–10 years.49
  • Complications: Higher overall rate of complications and reoperations vs. trapeziectomy procedures (aseptic loosening, dislocation).46,49
Key Insight — Paradigm Shift
The choice is not about which procedure is definitively "better," but rather which procedure's profile of benefits and risks best aligns with the individual patient's needs. A failed TJR can be reliably salvaged by conversion to trapeziectomy/LRTI—the very procedure it was chosen over.42

Reconstruction of MCP and PIP Joints

The Arthroplasty vs. Arthrodesis Dilemma

The fundamental choice: sacrifice motion for guaranteed stability and pain relief (arthrodesis), or attempt to preserve motion with an implant (arthroplasty) at the risk of complications.51

  • MCP Joints: Indispensable for grip and span → Arthroplasty almost always preferred.51
  • PIP Joints: Nuanced. For ulnar digits (ring, small) — arthroplasty favored for power grip. For index finger — many surgeons favor arthrodesis for absolute stability in fine pinch.51,53
  • DIP Joints: Arthrodesis is the undisputed standard of care—minimal functional deficit.51

Silicone Implant Arthroplasty (Swanson)

For over half a century, the one-piece flexible silicone implant has been the workhorse of finger joint arthroplasty, especially for the rheumatoid hand.7 Long-term Clinical Series — Level II-III

The Swanson implant functions as a flexible hinged spacer—not a true joint replacement. Stability comes from biological "encapsulation" and soft-tissue balancing.7

Silicone Outcomes
Reliable pain relief and effective deformity correction. Long-term studies in OA report excellent outcomes and high patient satisfaction.57 Norwegian Arthroplasty Registry: 89% survival at 10 years, 84% at 20 years for MCP arthroplasty.73

Complications:

  • Implant fracture (most common failure mode): rates ≥12.5% in long-term series57
  • Silicone particulate synovitis: microscopic wear particles provoke chronic inflammation, bone resorption, and intraosseous cysts39

Pyrocarbon Implant Arthroplasty

Pyrocarbon is an exceptionally hard, smooth, wear-resistant material with an elastic modulus similar to cortical bone—reducing stress shielding.61,62 These are unconstrained, two-component, anatomic surface replacement designs.56 Cohort Studies — Level II-III

Outcomes: Excellent pain relief, potential for better ROM restoration, and improvements in grip/pinch strength.30

Complications:

  • Postoperative stiffness and extension lag (common and challenging)60
  • Audible "squeaking"
  • Instability and dislocation (unconstrained design requires intact ligaments)60
  • Systematic reviews: higher reoperation rates for stiffness/instability vs. silicone66
The Clinical Paradox
The material with superior mechanical properties (pyrocarbon) does not consistently yield superior clinical outcomes. This reveals a crucial lesson: material science alone does not determine success—the biological host response and soft-tissue integrity are equally critical.66

Biomaterials, Implant Durability, and Host Response

Orthopedic Biomaterials Overview

  • Metals: Cobalt-chromium (hardness, wear resistance) and titanium alloys (biocompatibility, corrosion resistance, bone-like modulus).74
  • Polymers: UHMWPE (gold-standard bearing surface; modern HXLPE dramatically reduces wear)69; silicone elastomer (dynamic spacer).57
  • Ceramics: Pyrolytic carbon (extreme hardness, chemical inertness, bone-like elastic modulus).62

The Host-Implant Interface

Any foreign material incites a Foreign Body Response (FBR)—the immune system attempts to isolate the implant with fibrous scar tissue.77,78 The critical long-term failure mechanism is the biological reaction to particulate wear debris:

  • Polyethylene: Microscopic particles phagocytosed by macrophages → release of pro-inflammatory cytokines → osteoclast activation → periprosthetic osteolysis → aseptic loosening.70,71
  • Silicone: Wear particles provoke silicone synovitis—proliferative synovium, bone erosion, lytic cysts.39
  • Metals: Nano-particles and ions (cobalt, chromium) → direct cytotoxicity, T-cell hypersensitivity, pseudotumors, metallosis, potential systemic toxicity.72,80,81
  • Pyrocarbon: Exceptional wear resistance; simulator studies show significantly less bone wear vs. CoCr.64
The "Weakest Link" Concept
Long-term success is determined by the weakest link in the implant-fixation-host biology system:
  • MoP: Weakest link = polyethylene liner → osteolysis71
  • Silicone: Weakest link = poor fatigue strength → implant fracture57
  • Pyrocarbon: Weakest link = implant-bone interface and soft tissues → instability/stiffness60

Implant Survival and Revision Strategies

Norwegian Arthroplasty Registry data: MCP implant survival 89% at 10 years, 84% at 20 years.73 Registry Study — Level II Meta-analysis of PIP arthroplasty: catastrophic revision 2–4% across types, but reoperation for complications substantially higher for pyrocarbon (7%) and MoP (10%) vs. silicone (1%).59 Meta-analysis — Level I

Revision options:

  • Revision arthroplasty (if bone stock adequate—often requires longer stems, bone graft, soft-tissue reconstruction)60
  • Arthrodesis (fusion) — the ultimate salvage for most failed finger/wrist arthroplasties8
  • Trapeziectomy — reliable salvage for failed CMC TJR42

The Future: Emerging Technologies

3D Printing: Personalized Mechanical Replacement

Additive manufacturing is transforming orthopedics from fitting the patient to the implant to fitting the implant perfectly to the patient.85

  • Current: Patient-specific anatomical models for preoperative planning; patient-specific instruments (PSIs) for precise bone cuts.86
  • Next frontier: Custom implants fabricated via selective laser sintering (SLS) or electron beam melting (EBM)—perfect mirror of patient anatomy, porous lattice structures for bone ingrowth, tailored stiffness to reduce stress shielding.74,87,88

Tissue Engineering for Cartilage Regeneration

Based on the synergistic triad:91

  1. Cells: Mesenchymal stem cells (MSCs) from bone marrow or adipose tissue → chondrocyte differentiation92
  2. Scaffolds: Biodegradable 3D frameworks (collagen, hydrogels, polycaprolactone)94
  3. Signaling Molecules: Growth factors incorporated to stimulate proliferation and matrix production94

3D Bioprinting represents the convergence—precise layer-by-layer deposition of "bioinks" (hydrogels with living cells and growth factors) to construct complex, multi-layered tissue constructs.90

Biologic Joint Resurfacing

  • Meniscus Allograft: Cadaveric meniscus shaped and implanted as a biological spacer; patient's stem cells infiltrate and revascularize the graft.97
  • OATS (Osteochondral Allograft): Transplantation of cartilage-bone plugs for focal defects—being explored for smaller hand joints.98
The Convergence Vision
The ultimate goal: a 3D-bioprinted, patient-specific, cell-laden construct that provides both the mechanical integrity of a custom-engineered implant and the regenerative, lifelong potential of living tissue. The ambition is to transcend artificial materials and harness the body's own regenerative capacity.90

2. Systematic Review & Meta-analysis: TJR vs. Trapeziectomy for CMC OA (Acta Orthopaedica, 2024)
Meta-analysis of randomized controlled trials comparing total joint arthroplasty with trapeziectomy for thumb CMC OA. Found TJR offers faster early recovery but no long-term advantage at 1+ year.47,48

3. Norwegian Arthroplasty Registry — Long-term MCP Survival
Analysis of 2,997 finger MCP joint arthroplasties. Overall survival 89% at 10 years, 84% at 20 years. Implant fracture was the most common reason for revision.73
Level II — Large Registry Study
4. Systematic Review: PIP Arthroplasty Complications and Reoperations
Meta-analysis comparing implant types for PIP joints. Catastrophic revision rates 2–4% across types. Reoperation for non-catastrophic complications: pyrocarbon 7%, MoP 10%, silicone 1%.59
Level I — Meta-analysis

Comprehensive assessment of pyrocarbon implants in rheumatoid arthritis. Demonstrated excellent pain relief but highlighted significant rates of stiffness and instability complications.30
Level II-III — Cohort Study
Evidence Gap Note
While the source document references over 100 citations, many are narrative reviews, case series, or expert opinion. The highest-level evidence (Level I systematic reviews and RCTs) is concentrated primarily in the thumb CMC TJR vs. LRTI debate. For MCP/PIP arthroplasty material comparisons, the evidence base consists largely of Level II–III cohort studies and registry analyses.
