1. Introduction: The Paradigm Shift in Wound Bed Preparation

Complex, hard-to-heal wounds remain among the most significant clinical, surgical, and economic challenges in modern orthopedic and plastic surgery. The TIME framework, formalized in 2003, provided a structured approach focusing on four physiological barriers: Tissue management, Infection and inflammation control, Moisture balance, and Epithelial edge advancement.Consensus

In 2018–2019, an international consensus panel expanded the framework to TIMERS, adding Regeneration/Repair and Social/Susceptibility factors.Consensus This marked a paradigm shift from treating the "hole" to treating the "whole" patient, incorporating advanced regenerative therapies and acknowledging the profound impact of social determinants of health.Consensus

Key Principle
The TIMERS framework serves as a critical clinical decision support tool (CDST) for orthopedic trauma surgeons, plastic surgeons, and specialized wound care clinicians — standardizing assessment, eliminating practice variation, and ensuring timely specialist referrals.

2. Pathophysiology of Hard-to-Heal Wounds in Orthopedics

Acute wound healing follows a highly regulated four-phase cascade: hemostasis, inflammation, proliferation, and remodeling.Review Chronic wounds, in contrast, become pathologically arrested — typically "stuck" in the inflammatory and early proliferative stages.

2.1 Biochemical Microenvironment

Chronic wound fluid is characterized by persistently elevated matrix metalloproteinases (MMPs) and decreased tissue inhibitors of metalloproteinases (TIMPs).Review This hyper-proteolytic environment degrades the extracellular matrix and destroys endogenous growth factors, blocking keratinocyte, fibroblast, and endothelial cell proliferation.

2.2 Biofilm: The Silent Perpetuator

Approximately 70% of chronic wounds harbor an established biofilm.Level V Biofilms are structured microbial communities encased in self-produced exopolysaccharide glycocalyx, adhering to wound beds, devitalized tissue, and abiotic surfaces such as orthopedic implants. This glycocalyx confers massive protection against host immunity and systemic antimicrobials. Bacteria within biofilms enter a metabolically dormant state rendering traditional antibiotics largely ineffective without concurrent physical disruption.

Critical Pathogenic Feedback Loop
Biofilm → Chronic inflammation → Neutrophil infiltration → Massive ROS release → Protease overactivity → ECM destruction → Failed healing → Biofilm persistence.

3. The TIMERS Framework — In-Depth Component Analysis

3.1 T — Tissue Management

The foremost priority is the meticulous assessment and aggressive management of tissue viability. Non-viable, necrotic, or devitalized tissue acts as a physical and biological barrier to granulation tissue formation and provides an ideal harbor for microbial colonization and biofilm formation.Consensus

Debridement modalities:

  • Autolytic debridement — Moisture-donating dressings (e.g., hydrogels) to facilitate endogenous enzyme activity.Consensus
  • Sharp/surgical debridement — Scalpels, curettes, or scissors for rapid selective removal; requires anatomical expertise.Consensus
  • Mechanical debridement — VERSAJET II Hydrosurgery System uses high-velocity saline jet to precisely cut and evacuate devitalized tissue while sparing healthy granulation.Consensus
  • Biological debridement — Sterile larval (maggot) therapy digests necrotic tissue and secretes antimicrobial peptides.Consensus
⚠ Critical Mandate — Lower Extremity Wounds
Lower extremity wounds (especially foot/heel) MUST NOT undergo sharp debridement without prior vascular assessment (Doppler ultrasound or ABI). Debriding an ischemic wound will precipitate rapid expansion of necrosis and potential limb loss.Consensus

In cases of complex traumatic osteomyelitis or fracture-related infections, tissue management extends to the bone: segmental resection must proceed until healthy, bleeding cortical and cancellous bone is encountered — the "paprika sign" — ensuring complete removal of intramedullary and endosteal biofilm networks.Level V

3.2 I — Infection and Inflammation Control

Accurate differentiation along the bacterial continuum is essential: contamination → colonization → local infection/biofilm → spreading infection (cellulitis) → systemic infection (sepsis).Consensus

Table 1: Clinical Differentiation of Infection States in Wound Management
State Clinical Signs Management Approach Evidence
Local infection / Biofilm Increased exudate, malodor, friable granulation, pain, stalled healing; periwound <2 cm Wound hygiene, physical debridement, topical antimicrobials (silver, cadexomer iodine, PHMB); NO systemic antibiotics indicated Consensus
Spreading infection Erythema extending >2 cm, heat, induration, swelling Immediate systemic antibiotic therapy, medical stabilization Consensus
Systemic infection Pyrexia, tachycardia, elevated inflammatory markers IV broad-spectrum antibiotics, intensive care, surgical source control Consensus
Suspected FRI Purulent drainage, sinus tract, exposed hardware, progressive loosening 5 deep tissue samples with sterile instruments before antibiotics; empiric therapy → narrowed by antibiogram Level V

Routine superficial swabbing of chronic wounds is strongly discouraged — it captures surface colonizers, not true pathogenic organisms.Consensus In orthopedic FRI, five separate deep tissue samples must be obtained before antibiotic administration to maximize diagnostic yield.Level V

3.3 M — Moisture Imbalance

Optimal moisture balance is a fundamental prerequisite for autolytic debridement, angiogenesis, and epidermal cell migration.Review The clinical objective is to avoid extremes of desiccation and maceration.

  • Dry wound bed → cell death, scab/eschar formation, blocked keratinocyte migration → Use moisture-donating dressings (amorphous hydrogels, hydrocolloids) after confirming vascular supply.Consensus
  • High-exuding wounds → saturated wound bed, periwound maceration, expanded wound footprint → Use superabsorbent polymer (SAP) dressings to sequester fluid and MMPs.Consensus

Advanced moisture management in complex surgical wounds is achieved through Negative Pressure Wound Therapy (NPWT), which promotes macro-strain (wound edge approximation) and micro-strain (cellular proliferation via mechanical stretching).Consensus For lower limb wounds with venous hypertension, compression therapy remains the gold standard when arterial supply is adequate.Consensus

3.4 E — Edge of Wound Advancement

The wound edge is the primary visual indicator of healing trajectory. In hard-to-heal wounds, edges become phenotypically abnormal — rolled (epibole), undermined, thickened, callused, or hyperkeratotic — due to failed epidermal cell migration across a protease-rich matrix.Review

Interventions:

  • Revisit T, I, and M components to ensure optimal wound bed preparation
  • Protect periwound skin with liquid barrier films or medical emollients
  • Sharp debridement of dense callus and surgical trimming of rolled edges — micro-trauma reactivates cellular signaling and converts chronic edges to an acute, proliferative stateConsensus

3.5 R — Regeneration and Repair

When wounds remain refractory to standard care despite optimal T, I, M, and E management, clinicians must pivot to advanced adjunctive therapies engineered to stimulate endogenous repair pathways.Consensus

Cellular, Acellular, and Matrix-like Products (CAMPs):

  • Sophisticated biomaterials from allogeneic, autologous, xenogeneic, and synthetic sourcesConsensus
  • Provide 3D scaffold for cellular migration + deliver exogenous growth factors and cytokines
  • Indications: Full-thickness pressure injuries, complex trauma wounds, diabetic foot ulcers, deep surgical defects with exposed tendon/bone/jointConsensus

Other regenerative modalities: continuous topical oxygen therapy, nitric oxide modulation, autologous platelet-rich plasma (PRP), and the sophisticated 5-combination technique for chronic traumatic osteomyelitis (en-bloc excision + Masquelet technique + microsurgical transfer + mixed bone grafting + PRP).Level V

FDA Regenerative Medicine Advanced Therapy (RMAT) Designation
Notable RMAT-approved therapies include StrataGraft (allogeneic keratinocyte on collagen for deep burns) and Vyjuvek (topical gene therapy for dystrophic epidermolysis bullosa).Regulatory

3.6 S — Social Factors and Susceptibility

Perfect local wound interventions can be completely undermined by unaddressed systemic, psychosocial, and socioeconomic drivers of health.Consensus

  • Medical risk factors: Polypharmacy, nutritional deficits, uncontrolled hyperglycemia, cardiovascular disease, immunological impairmentConsensus
  • Prognostic tools: Charlson Comorbidity Index (CCI) to quantify comorbidity burdenLevel V
  • Social determinants: Living environment, caregiver support, financial constraints, transportation barriers, health literacyConsensus
  • Socioeconomic disparity: Trauma patients on Medicaid or uninsured are significantly less likely to attend wound clinic follow-up, correlating with higher complication and amputation ratesLevel V

Treatment pathways must incorporate shared decision-making, realistic short-term goals (odor control, pain reduction, exudate management), accessible language, visual aids, and rapid referrals to dietitians, social workers, occupational therapists, and clinical psychologists.Consensus

4. Orthoplastic Management Protocols

4.1 Open Fractures & High-Energy Trauma

Open fractures (Gustilo-Anderson classification) result from massive kinetic energy transfer causing soft-tissue necrosis, vascular stripping, and deep bacterial inoculation.Level V

  • Immediate antibiotics: Weight-based, broad-spectrum IV antibiotics in the ED — absolute standard of care.Level V
  • Debridement timing: While 6-hour dogma prevailed historically, modern evidence (e.g., GOLIATH study) supports debridement within 24 hours for most Type III fractures with immediate antibiotics. Highly contaminated Type IIIB tibial fractures still demand urgent intervention ideally within 12 hours.Level V
  • Irrigation: Current guidelines advocate clinical endpoint of macroscopic cleanliness rather than strict arbitrary volumes (e.g., 9L). Excessive pressure can cause iatrogenic soft-tissue damage.Level V
  • Definitive flap coverage: Should be achieved ideally within 7 days. Delays significantly increase risk of nosocomial colonization, deep infection, and nonunion.Level V

4.2 Fracture-Related Infection (FRI)

FRI is a catastrophic complication involving mature biofilm on orthopedic hardware and sequestered dead bone.Level V The diagnostic workup follows standardized international criteria: confirmatory signs (purulent drainage, sinus tract, exposed hardware) versus suggestive signs (erythema, stiffness, progressive loosening).Level V

Table 2: FRI Surgical Strategy Based on Chronicity and Implant Status
Strategy Indications Key Steps Evidence
DAIR Early, acute infection; stable implants; progressive fracture healing Aggressive debridement, biofilm disruption via scrubbing + pulsatile lavage, culture-specific antibiotics from 5 deep samples Level V
Implant Exchange Loosened hardware with retained mechanical function need Remove loose implant, aggressive medullary reaming, local antibiotic carriers (e.g., Cerament G: calcium sulfate + hydroxyapatite + gentamicin), possible single-stage reconstruction Level V
Complete Removal Sequestered hardware; nonunion; mature biofilm Total explantation, medullary canal reaming, dead space management with osteoconductive/antibiotic carriers Level V

4.3 Exposed Orthopedic Hardware

Modern orthoplastic pathways emphasize selective hardware salvage when: implants remain mechanically stable, environmental exposure <2 weeks, and no evidence of deep purulent infection.Level V

NPWTi-d (Negative Pressure Wound Therapy with Instillation and Dwell Time) is highly effective: cycles antimicrobial solution (e.g., hypochlorous acid or PHMB) into the wound bed for biochemical biofilm disruption, followed by continuous negative pressure for evacuation. This rapidly decontaminates and granulates the wound bed, bridging to definitive flap closure.Level V

Palliative Wound Care — Social Factors Application
In severely compromised patients (end-stage PAD, not candidates for revascularization, physiological frailty precluding major surgery), the focus shifts to palliation: meticulous conservative local wound care, moisture control, infection prevention. Such approaches have prevented systemic infection and avoided major amputation for over a decade in selected patients.Level V

6. Global Clinical Guidelines Synthesis (2024–2026)

6.1 EWMA & SIS-E 2025: Surgical Site Infections

The 2025 guidelines address a massive historical deficit in post-operative closed incision care, particularly during the transition to community-based settings.Guideline

  • Critical directive: Distinguish Surgical Wound Dehiscence (SWD) from true Surgical Site Infection (SSI). Dehiscence (3–10 days post-op, from mechanical tension or sterile suture abscess) does not automatically require systemic antibiotics.Guideline
  • CDC grading: Superficial Incisional → Deep Incisional → Organ/SpaceGuideline
  • Southampton Wound Score recommended for objective trajectory trackingGuideline
  • Patient education: Concise, visual-aid-heavy, jargon-free materials for cross-cultural comprehension at dischargeGuideline

6.2 WUWHS Consensus: Wound Balance & Resource Optimization

The "Wound Balance" concept emphasizes continuous clinical calibration of the wound microenvironment — maintaining equilibrium between moisture, bacterial load, and protease activity.Consensus SAP dressings demonstrate prophylactic and therapeutic superiority by sequestering MMPs and inflammatory cytokines.Consensus

The 2024 Delphi consensus on resource-limited settings reinforces that strict adherence to core TIMERS logic — meticulous sharps debridement, basic moisture control, and intensive patient education — remains highly effective for achieving wound closure regardless of severe macroeconomic constraints.Delphi Consensus
