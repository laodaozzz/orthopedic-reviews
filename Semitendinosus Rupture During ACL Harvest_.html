Iatrogenic Semitendinosus Tendon Rupture in ACL Reconstruction

Comprehensive Evidence-Based Review: Etiology · Prevention · Management

The Paradox of the Hamstring Autograft

The autologous hamstring tendon (HT) graft—typically the semitendinosus (ST), often with the gracilis (G)—is among the most popular choices for ACL reconstruction, offering reduced anterior knee pain, preserved quadriceps function, and lower donor site morbidity compared to BPTB autografts. However, the harvest procedure carries a critical risk: iatrogenic semitendinosus tendon rupture ("premature graft amputation"), a complication that can force unplanned intraoperative changes and less ideal salvage options.

This review synthesizes the evidence on etiology, evidence-based prevention, and structured intraoperative management of this complication.

🔬 1. Etiology: A Multifactorial Analysis

Iatrogenic ST rupture is rarely the result of a single error. It typically represents a cascade involving complex anatomy, technical execution, and occasionally underlying patient pathology. The evidence points to an inherent conflict between traditional "blind" harvesting technique and the highly variable anatomy of the posteromedial knee.

1.1 Anatomical Risk Factors: The Unseen Impediments

The anatomy of the pes anserinus superficialis is far from uniform. Cadaveric studies reveal significant anatomical variations in nearly half of all knees. Cadaveric Studies – Level IV

Accessory Bands (Vincula): The Primary Culprit

The ST tendon is frequently tethered to adjacent structures by fibrous connections known as vincula or accessory bands. Failure to recognize and release these is arguably the single most common cause of premature graft amputation. Level not specified in source

  • Gastrocnemius Connection: The most consistently demonstrated and clinically significant accessory band connects the ST tendon to the fascia of the medial head of the gastrocnemius muscle. This strong fascial slip acts as a checkrein. If not released, it causes the tendon stripper to deviate and transect the tendon prematurely. Cadaveric Studies – Level IV
  • Other Fascial Connections: Fascial loops connecting the pes tendons to the superficial MCL and separate tendinous slips that diverge to insert independently into the crural fascia. One cadaveric study documented an accessory insertion of the ST in 77% of knees. Another found an accessory ST bundle directed toward the medial gastrocnemius in 90% of cases. Cadaveric Studies – Level IV
⚠ Clinical Pearl When a surgeon advances a tendon stripper blindly from an anterior incision, unseen posterior bands act as obstacles. The resistance is often misinterpreted as the musculotendinous junction—but it is actually an un-released vinculum. Forcing the stripper against this resistance causes its cutting edge to engage and transect the main tendon.

1.2 Technical Errors in Surgical Execution

While anatomical variations set the stage for failure, technical errors are the catalysts.

  • Inadequate Dissection and Mobilization: The most critical error. A thorough mix of blunt finger dissection and cautious sharp dissection is required to completely free the ST and gracilis tendons from the sartorial fascia and all accessory attachments before introducing the stripper. Applying tension makes bands taut and easier to identify under direct vision. Level not specified in source
  • Improper Tendon Stripper Usage:
    • Vector of Force: Must be advanced along the true anatomical vector, directed proximally and posteriorly toward the ischial tuberosity.
    • Excessive Force: The stripper should advance smoothly with minimal resistance. Resistance signals an un-released fascial band—not the musculotendinous junction.
    • Instrument Sharpness: Paradoxically, an overly sharp stripper can be a liability—it is less forgiving of minor deviations and more likely to transect the tendon itself.
    Level not specified in source
  • Poor Tendon Identification: Difficulty distinguishing ST and gracilis tendons from the overlying sartorius fascia can lead to dissection in the wrong plane. Maintaining constant, appropriate tension while advancing the stripper is crucial. Level not specified in source

1.3 Patient-Specific Pathological Considerations

In rare cases, pre-existing pathology can compromise tendon strength:

  • Osteochondroma: Documented cases where a benign bone tumor on the posteromedial proximal tibia physically obstructs the stripper path and causes chronic friction leading to tendinosis and a weakened tendon. Case Reports – Level V
  • Pre-existing Tendinopathy: Patients with chronic hamstring tendinopathy or prior significant hamstring injuries may have compromised tendon quality, lowering the threshold for rupture during harvesting. Logical inference – not explicitly cited in source

🛡️ 2. Evidence-Based Strategies for Prevention

Prevention relies on the systematic application of a multi-layered safety protocol built upon a foundational principle: maximizing visualization to mitigate the risks inherent in a "blind" procedure. The surgical philosophy should shift from one of force and feel to one of precision and sight.

2.1 Pre-Operative Assessment and Planning

Standard pre-operative MRI can provide valuable information about hamstring tendon quality and screen for rare anomalies such as a large osteochondroma. Expert recommendation – Level V

Pre-operative ultrasonography can identify the specific arrangement of pes anserinus tendons and map their relationship to saphenous nerve branches. However, neither MRI nor ultrasound can reliably predict final graft length and diameter, which are influenced by patient height and sex. Observational – Level III

2.2 Surgical Approaches and Incision Planning

Incision Choice

  • Vertical Incisions: Associated with the highest rates of injury to the infrapatellar branch of the saphenous nerve (IPBSN). Comparative study – Level III
  • Horizontal and Oblique Incisions: Run more parallel to IPBSN branches, significantly reducing nerve injury risk. One study found a horizontal incision reduced IPBSN lesions from 39.7% to 14.9%. Comparative study – Level III

Harvesting Approach

  • Traditional Anteromedial Approach: Single incision over the pes anserinus insertion. Limited visualization of posteromedial structures. Associated with higher rates of saphenous nerve injury and longer operative times. Level not specified in source
  • Posteromedial (PM) / Popliteal Fossa Approach: A small secondary incision provides direct access to and visualization of the ST and gracilis tendons where they are distinct and superficial. Allows direct identification and sharp transection of accessory tendinous connections. Associated with significantly lower rates of saphenous nerve injury and virtual elimination of premature amputation risk. Comparative data – Level III

2.3 The Core Principle: Meticulous, Visualized Dissection

✅ Non-Negotiable Safety Sequence Regardless of approach, the core principle is meticulous, step-wise dissection to completely free the tendon before stripping.
  1. Exposure: Incise the sartorial fascia longitudinally to expose the underlying ST and gracilis tendons.
  2. Mobilization: Using blunt finger dissection and a blunt instrument, develop the plane around the tendons, separating them from the deep surface of the sartorius and from each other.
  3. Tension and Identification: Place the isolated tendon under tension using a clamp or hook. This makes accessory bands taut, transforming them into palpable, cord-like structures.
  4. Systematic Release: Under direct visual control, systematically identify and sharply transect each accessory band with scissors. Continue until the tendon demonstrates "perfect elastic freedom" when pulled—indicating no significant tethers remain. Only then is it safe to proceed with the tendon stripper.

2.4 Instrumentation and Advanced Techniques

Open- vs. Closed-Loop Strippers: A common strategy involves using an open-loop stripper to release the proximal musculotendinous junction, followed by a closed-loop stripper for the distal tibial insertion. The open-loop design offers a safety advantage: if caught on an unrecognized band, it can be disengaged without causing transection. Technique description – Level V

Endoscopic-Assisted Harvest: Inserting an arthroscope through the harvest incision provides magnified, direct visualization of the entire tendon length. All fascial bands can be identified with certainty and transected under video guidance, virtually eliminating the risk of amputation. Technique study – Level IV

Ultrasound-Guided Harvest: Intraoperative ultrasound confirms precise ST location beneath the fascia, particularly useful in obese patients or for less experienced surgeons. Level not specified in source

🔧 3. Intraoperative Management of Premature Graft Rupture

Despite diligent prevention, premature graft amputation remains possible. A calm, systematic approach is essential. The preparedness of the surgeon—including having a pre-discussed contingency plan with the patient—is as critical as the technical skill used to prevent it.

3.1 Immediate Assessment and Decision-Making

  1. Retrieve and Measure: The amputated tendon segment should be retrieved. Both the harvested ST portion and remaining gracilis (if not yet harvested) must be measured for length and diameter.
  2. Assess Viability: A quadrupled (4-strand) graft with a final diameter of at least 8 mm is the target to minimize re-rupture risk. Sufficient total tendon length (e.g., >21 cm) is required for secure fixation. Commonly accepted thresholds – partially referenced in source

3.2 Graft Salvage and Augmentation Strategies

  • Harvesting the Gracilis: If the ST ruptured, the intact gracilis should be harvested. Alone it is almost always too thin for a robust 4-strand graft, but it serves as the foundation for an augmented construct. Standard practice
  • The Hybrid Graft (Auto + Allo): Supplementing available autograft tissue with a soft-tissue allograft (e.g., tibialis posterior, tibialis anterior, peroneus longus) to achieve the ≥8 mm diameter. A comprehensive systematic review and meta-analysis concluded no significant differences in failure rates, knee stability, or patient-reported outcomes between standard HT autografts and hybrid grafts. Systematic Review & Meta-Analysis – Level I

3.3 Alternative Autograft Options

  • Contralateral Hamstrings: Provides high-quality autograft of the same tissue type but introduces morbidity to the healthy limb and significantly increases surgical time. Expert recommendation – Level V
  • Ipsilateral BPTB: Gold standard biomechanical properties and bone-to-bone healing. Primary disadvantage is donor site morbidity (anterior knee pain, kneeling pain, fracture risk). Well-established standard – Level I–II for general BPTB outcomes
  • Ipsilateral Quadriceps Tendon (QT): Increasingly popular. Provides a robust graft with versatile preparation options. Associated with less anterior knee pain compared to BPTB. Systematic review data – Level II–III

3.4 Novel Graft Preparation Techniques for Short or Thin Grafts

  • Multi-Strand Grafts: A single tendon too short for doubling can be prepared as a 3-strand graft. Two thin tendons can be combined into a 5- or 6-strand graft, significantly increasing diameter and strength. Biomechanical studies – Level III–IV
  • Anastomosis and Suture Techniques: "Anastomosis" grafts (suturing short tendon ends together to create a loop) and "loop and tack" grafts have demonstrated ultimate failure loads and cyclic loading properties comparable to standard 4-strand grafts. Biomechanical studies – Level III–IV

🎯 5. Conclusion & Key Recommendations

Summary of Key Recommendations

  1. Prioritize Visualization over Blind Force: The fundamental cause of graft rupture is the mismatch between a blind technique and variable anatomy. Posteromedial or endoscopic-assisted approaches allow direct identification and release of accessory bands—the primary culprits in stripper deviation.
  2. Adopt a Multi-Layered Safety Protocol: Prevention is achieved by a sequence of risk-reducing steps: careful pre-operative imaging review, nerve-sparing oblique incision, meticulous manual and sharp dissection to completely free the tendon before stripping, and judicious stripper use without excessive force.
  3. Be Prepared with a Bailout Algorithm: Every surgeon performing hamstring ACLR should have a clear, pre-determined algorithm for a compromised graft, including knowledge of hybrid grafts, alternative autografts (BPTB, QT, contralateral hamstrings), and novel graft preparation methods.
  4. Ensure Comprehensive Pre-Operative Consent: The possibility of graft harvest failure and the need for alternative graft sources must be discussed with the patient, transforming a potential crisis into execution of a pre-approved contingency plan.

The Shift in Surgical Philosophy

Minimizing this complication requires a philosophical shift. The traditional method relying on tactile feel and mechanical power to navigate unseen anatomy is inherently fraught with risk. The modern, evidence-based approach emphasizes anatomical respect, direct visualization, and meticulous dissection—understanding that the stripper's purpose is to transect the tendon at the musculotendinous junction after it has been fully liberated from all fascial tethers, not to power through them.

Future Research Directions

  • Prospective RCTs comparing outcomes of traditional anteromedial, posteromedial, and endoscopic-assisted harvesting techniques.
  • Long-term follow-up studies on patients who underwent reconstruction with salvage/bailout grafts (hybrid grafts, improvised constructs).
  • Standardized reporting of graft rupture incidence to better quantify the true prevalence of this complication.
