Section 1: Foundational Principles and Patient Evaluation

1.1 Anatomy, Biomechanics, and Pathophysiology

The femur is the body's longest and strongest bone, with its diaphysis (shaft) possessing a distinct anterior bow (antecurvature). This anatomical feature is critical for intramedullary nail selection—a mismatch between the radius of curvature of the implant and the native femur can lead to anterior cortical perforation or iatrogenic fracture propagation. LOA: Not specified in source

Posteriorly, the linea aspera functions as a compressive strut, accommodating the anterior bow's tensile forces and serving as a major attachment site for muscles and fascia.

Bimodal Distribution

  • Younger populations: High-energy trauma (motor vehicle collisions—leading cause). LOA: Not specified in source
  • Elderly: Low-energy mechanisms (simple fall from standing), reflecting underlying bone fragility.

Pathophysiology: Hemorrhage and Muscle Spasm

Critical: Massive Blood Loss A closed femoral shaft fracture can result in internal blood loss of 1000–1500 mL—sufficient to induce hypovolemic shock. In open fractures, blood loss can be double that of closed fractures.

The powerful quadriceps, hamstrings, and adductor muscles contract in response to injury, causing significant pain, shortening, and deformity. This muscle pull must be overcome to achieve and maintain fracture reduction.

1.3 Initial Assessment and Resuscitation

Management begins with systematic trauma evaluation following Advanced Trauma Life Support (ATLS) principles: Airway → Breathing → Circulation. The femoral fracture is addressed during the secondary survey. LOA: Not specified

Compensated Shock: The Insidious Threat Normotension with tachycardia, cool extremities, weak peripheral pulses, and narrowing pulse pressure. Failure to recognize and resuscitate before nailing can lead to catastrophic decompensation.

Resuscitation Endpoints

Vital Signs: Normalized
Urine Output: >0.5 mL/kg/hr
Lactate: <2.5 mmol/L
Base Deficit: Cleared

Timing of Definitive Fixation

Early definitive fixation within 24–48 hours significantly reduces systemic complications, particularly pulmonary issues (fat embolism syndrome, ARDS). In polytrauma patients, delayed fixation (>48 hours) can increase pulmonary complications from 16% to 56% and increase mortality fivefold. LOA: II–III

Host Status Assessment

Factors negatively affecting healing and increasing complication risk: advanced age, diabetes, tobacco use, malnutrition, and poor social support systems. LOA: Not specified in source

1.4 Imaging: Ruling Out the Ipsilateral Femoral Neck Fracture

Critical Diagnostic Trap Ipsilateral femoral neck fracture occurs in 2–9% of all femoral shaft fractures but is missed in up to one-third of cases. Displacement during reaming/nailing converts a salvageable injury into one with high risk of nonunion, malunion, or AVN of the femoral head.

Mandatory Imaging Protocol

  1. AP radiograph of the entire pelvis — comparison with contralateral hip. LOA: Not specified
  2. Dedicated hip radiographs — AP view with 10–15° internal rotation to profile the femoral neck.
  3. Fine-cut (2 mm) CT scan of the hip — gold standard for definitively ruling out occult femoral neck fracture.

Clinical imperative: Treat every high-energy femoral shaft fracture as if it has an associated neck fracture until proven otherwise.

Section 2: Indications, Contraindications, and Preoperative Planning

2.1 Intramedullary Nailing: The Gold Standard

Locked intramedullary nailing is the undisputed gold standard for the vast majority of femoral shaft fractures in adults. LOA: II

Biomechanical: Load-sharing device on central axis; permits micromotion → robust secondary bone healing via external callus. Often allows early/immediate weight-bearing.
Biological: Closed IMN is minimally invasive; preserves fracture hematoma and periosteal blood supply. ORIF with plates requires soft-tissue stripping → devascularization.

2.2 Indications and Contraindications

Table 3: Indications and Contraindications for IMN

General IndicationsDiaphyseal fractures (transverse, short oblique, spiral, comminuted); segmental fractures; pathologic fractures; open fractures (Gustilo I–II after I&D); nonunion/malunion revision
Relative ContraindicationsNarrow medullary canal; pre-existing deformity/bowing; fractures with significant metaphyseal or intra-articular extension
Absolute ContraindicationsActive infection / gross contamination (Gustilo III) — staged approach required; skeletal immaturity (open physes) — specialized pediatric techniques needed

2.3 Preoperative Templating

  • Nail Length: Templated from contralateral uninjured femur with a radiolucent ruler. Too long → pain at entry site; too short → failure to control fracture.
  • Nail Diameter: Measured at the narrowest point (isthmus). Goal: largest, stiffest nail possible — bending resistance is proportional to diameter⁴.
  • Nail Curvature: Assess the lateral radiograph of the contralateral femur for anterior bow. Excessive bow → risk of anterior cortical perforation; may require a nail with greater curvature or smaller diameter.

2.4 Anesthetic Considerations

Complete neuromuscular blockade is the primary requirement to overcome powerful thigh muscles. LOA: Not specified

  • General Anesthesia: Preferred in polytrauma, head/spinal injuries, prolonged procedures.
  • Regional (Spinal/Epidural): Excellent for isolated fractures; provides muscle relaxation and postoperative analgesia.
  • Femoral Nerve Block: Invaluable adjunct — provides preoperative analgesia in the ED, reduces opioid requirements, improves positioning comfort. LOA: II

Section 3: Antegrade Femoral Nailing: Surgical Technique

3.1 Entry Point Debate: Piriformis Fossa vs. Greater Trochanter

Table 5: Piriformis vs. Trochanteric Entry Point
FeaturePiriformis EntryTrochanteric Entry
LocationJunction of femoral neck & greater trochanter; colinear with medullary canalTip of greater trochanter
AlignmentDirect alignment → straight nail insertion without varus/valgus forcesNot colinear → requires nail with proximal lateral bend (valgus angle)
AccessDifficult in obese/muscular patients; requires more dissectionSuperficial, easily accessible; reduced operative time
Key RiskEndangers MFCA (blood supply to femoral head); abductor muscle/tendon injury → Trendelenburg gait, chronic hip painUsing a straight nail from this point → varus malunion or iatrogenic greater trochanter fracture
Current TrendLess popularMore popular due to technical ease and lower abductor injury risk; equivalent outcomes with modern implants

3.2 Step-by-Step Technique

  1. Incision: 3–6 cm longitudinal or oblique, proximal to greater trochanter tip.
  2. Approach: Split gluteus maximus fascia; bluntly dissect gluteus medius.
  3. Entry Point Localization: Under fluoroscopy (AP + lateral), insert guide pin; confirm centered position in medullary canal on both views.
  4. Opening the Canal: Cannulated awl/entry reamer over guide pin with soft-tissue protection sleeve to prevent eccentric reaming.

3.3 The Art of Reduction

Anatomic reduction before nail passage is the most important step for preventing malunion. The nail maintains the reduction it is passed through; it will not reliably correct significant deformity. LOA: Not specified

  • Closed Techniques: Longitudinal traction (mechanical or manual); radiolucent bump under thigh for apex anterior angulation; direct varus/valgus pressure.
  • Percutaneous "Joysticks": Schanz screws in proximal/distal fragments act as long levers for precise rotation and angulation control.
  • Blocking (Poller) Screws: Placed just outside nail path to narrow the medullary canal, forcing the nail into a specific trajectory and correcting alignment. LOA: Not specified
  • Open Reduction: Last resort; limited incision with bone hooks or reduction clamps. Compromises fracture biology.

3.4 Reamed vs. Unreamed Nailing

Reamed Nailing = Standard of Care Reaming results in higher union rates vs. unreamed nailing by allowing a larger, stiffer nail and depositing autologous bone graft (reamings) at the fracture site. Ream until cortical "chatter"; final reamer 1.0–1.5 mm > nail diameter.

Unreamed nailing: Reserved for unstable polytrauma patients with significant chest injuries (minimize fat embolism) or some open fractures.

3.5 Nail Insertion and Rotational Control

Critical Pearl — Trochanteric Entry Insert the nail with its anterior bow rotated 90° to face medially. This directs the nail tip away from the medial cortex, preventing iatrogenic fracture. Gradually rotate back to correct orientation as it advances.

Rotational Control — the most common parameter to be malreduced: LOA: Not specified

  • Clinical: Ensure patella points directly anteriorly with hip in neutral rotation.
  • Contralateral comparison.
  • Fluoroscopic: "Lesser trochanter profile sign" on AP hip view compared to preoperative contralateral images.

3.6 Interlocking

Proximal Locking: Via radiolucent targeting jig attached to insertion handle. Options: static, dynamic, or reconstruction locking.

Distal Locking: Usually freehand with "Perfect Circle" technique — adjust C-arm until locking holes appear as perfect circles (indicating coaxial X-ray beam). Critical pitfall: Move the C-arm, not the patient's leg, to find the circles. Leg manipulation locks the fracture in malrotation.

Section 4: Retrograde Femoral Nailing

4.1 Rationale and Specific Indications

The fundamental advantage is that retrograde nailing completely avoids instrumentation of the proximal femur and hip joint. LOA: II

  • Ipsilateral femoral neck fractures
  • "Floating knee" injuries (ipsilateral femur + tibia fractures)
  • Morbidly obese patients
  • Polytrauma patients (bilateral femur fractures, acetabular fractures)
  • Pregnancy (reduced radiation to pelvis/uterus)
  • Distal third femur fractures

4.2 Surgical Technique

  1. Positioning: Supine; knee flexed 30–40° with sterile radiolucent bump under thigh (relaxes gastrocnemius, prevents distal fragment flexion deformity).
  2. Approach: 3–5 cm longitudinal incision over the front of the knee; split patellar tendon or parapatellar approach; enter the knee joint and expose the intercondylar notch.
  3. Entry Point: Center of intercondylar notch, ~1 cm anterior to PCL origin, in line with the distal tip of Blumensaat's line on lateral fluoroscopy.
  4. Canal Preparation: Guide wire passed retrograde; ream; insert nail from knee.
  5. Locking: Proximal (freehand, perfect circle technique); distal (targeting jig).

5.2 Segmental Femoral Fractures

High-energy injuries with compromised blood supply to the intermediate segment → higher risk for delayed union/nonunion. IMN remains the gold standard. LOA: II

  • Reduction: Percutaneous Schanz pin joysticks into the intermediate segment; limited open approach with percutaneous clamps.
  • Stability: Blocking (Poller) screws to guide the nail and control alignment of all three segments.

5.3 Ipsilateral Femoral Neck and Shaft Fracture

Priority: The Femoral Neck Anatomic reduction and stable fixation of the neck fracture must be achieved first. Complications (nonunion, AVN) are far more difficult to treat than shaft complications.
  • Sequence: Provisionally stabilize neck fracture with guide wires/screws before any reaming or shaft nailing.
  • Option 1 — Separate Implants (Preferred by many): Cannulated screws or DHS for the neck + retrograde IMN for the shaft. Allows independent, optimal fixation. LOA: Not specified
  • Option 2 — Single Cephalomedullary Nail: Long antegrade nail with proximal screws into femoral head. Elegant but technically demanding; some studies report higher neck nonunion rates vs. separate fixation. LOA: Not specified

Section 6: Postoperative Care and Rehabilitation

6.1 Immediate Postoperative Management

  • Pain: Multimodal — PCA, scheduled acetaminophen, short-course NSAIDs (ketorolac).
  • DVT Prophylaxis: Mechanical (pneumatic compression) + chemoprophylaxis (LMWH e.g., enoxaparin, or aspirin) for 2–4 weeks. LOA: II
  • Wound Care: Waterproof dressing; showering after 2 days; sutures/staples removed at 10–14 days.
  • Early Mobilization: Out of bed on postoperative day 1; immediate ROM exercises for hip and knee.

7.2 Nonunion

Failure to heal within 6–9 months. Categorized as hypertrophic (biologically active, mechanically unstable) or atrophic (biologically inert, avascular).

Risk Factors

  • Patient: Smoking (most significant — nicotine vasoconstriction), diabetes, malnutrition, older age, chronic steroids/NSAIDs. LOA: II
  • Fracture: High-energy, open, severe comminution, bone loss.
  • Surgical: Inadequate stability (too-small nail), distraction at fracture site, infection, unreamed nailing. LOA: II

Management

  • Exchange Nailing (gold standard): Remove existing nail → over-ream by 1–2 mm → insert larger-diameter nail. Addresses both mechanical stability and biological stimulus (autologous graft from reamings). LOA: II
  • Augmentative Plating + Bone Grafting: Leave nail in situ; apply compression plate across nonunion + iliac crest autograft.

7.3 Infection

Infection rate for closed IMN: 1–4%. LOA: II

  • Early (<3 months): If implant stable → aggressive debridement + culture-specific IV antibiotics. Nail can often be retained.
  • Chronic / Infected Nonunion: Staged approach: (1) Nail removal + canal debridement (RIA system); (2) Antibiotic cement spacer; (3) After 6 weeks of IV antibiotics and infection eradication → new IMN + bone grafting.

7.4 Hardware Failure

Almost never a problem with the implant itself — it is the end-stage manifestation of a biological failure (nonunion). The implant fatigues from millions of load cycles when bone fails to unite. Weakest points: interlocking screw apertures.

Management: Remove broken hardware (specialized extraction tools) → treat nonunion (exchange nailing + bone grafting).

Key Principle Prevention of hardware failure begins with the pursuit of anatomic and stable fixation at the index procedure.

Section 8: The Role of Implant Removal

8.1 Indications

Routine removal of asymptomatic hardware in adults is not recommended. LOA: Not specified

Table 12: Indications for Hardware Removal
CategoryDetails
Symptomatic Hardware (Most Common)Pain/irritation at nail ends (hip or knee); implant prominence in thin patients; foreign body sensation
InfectionChronic, low-grade infection / osteomyelitis requiring implant removal for eradication (biofilm)
Implant FailureBroken or bent nail → removed as part of revision procedure
Patient RequestYoung, active individuals with concerns about long-term implant presence; requires extensive counseling
AllergyTrue allergic reactions to titanium alloy (extremely rare)

8.2 Technical Challenges and Complications

Implant removal is not a minor procedure and can be significantly more challenging than the initial insertion. LOA: Not specified

  • Challenges: Bone overgrowth over screws; stripped screw heads; cold welding; mismatched instrumentation (different institution); broken implant extraction.
  • Complications: Refracture through screw holes (stress risers — highest risk in first months); neurovascular injury; infection (3–14%); failure to remove.
Decision Framework Hardware removal must be based on a clear indication with a thorough informed discussion about the benefits vs. the substantial risks of a second, often elective, surgery.
