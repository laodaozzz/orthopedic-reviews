Management of Locked Posterior Shoulder Dislocation:
A Defect-Based Treatment Algorithm

Evidence-based interactive educational summary covering patho-anatomy, diagnostic strategies, the three-tiered treatment algorithm, concomitant pathology, and recent surgical advances including 3D-printing technologies.

Systematic Reviews Meta-Analyses Case Series Expert Consensus

I Patho-anatomy & Diagnosis

1.1 Etiology & Pathomechanics

Locked posterior shoulder dislocation (LPDS) is a rare and frequently misdiagnosed orthopaedic injury, constituting only 2%–5% of all shoulder dislocations.[1][3] This rarity directly contributes to the high rate of missed diagnoses on initial presentation.

The injury mechanism is specific, resulting from forces that cause forceful internal rotation, adduction, and flexion of the shoulder.[3] Two classic etiologies account for the majority of cases:

  1. Convulsive Seizures or Electric Shock: Violent, tetanic muscle contractions cause the internal rotators (primarily the subscapularis) to overwhelm the external rotator cuff, driving the humeral head posteriorly.[1]
  2. High-Energy Trauma: A direct forceful blow to the anterior shoulder or a fall onto an outstretched hand in an internally rotated position produces the same posterior vector.[1]

The "locked" nature describes the mechanical incarceration of the humeral head: an anteromedial impaction fracture on the humeral head engages the posterior rim of the glenoid.[2] The etiology of seizure is particularly informative — the sustained violent contraction[3] not merely dislocates the head but carves the humeral head defect by forcefully impacting it against the glenoid rim and then holds it dislocated, creating the "locked" fracture-dislocation.[1]

💡 Key Concept
LPDS is often a severe osseous injury from its inception, rather than a simple soft-tissue dislocation that later becomes locked.[1]

1.2 The Critical Lesion: Reverse Hill-Sachs (RHL)

The pathognomonic finding is the reverse Hill-Sachs lesion (RHL) (also "McLaughlin lesion") — an impaction fracture on the anteromedial aspect of the humeral head.[1] It is reported in 40%–90% of patients following initial posterior dislocation.[1]

The size and location of this defect are the most critical factors determining glenohumeral joint stability following reduction.[8] While the shoulder is "locked" (dislocated), the pathologic articulation is relatively stable.[2] The true clinical problem — profound instability — arises after reduction, when the defect can "re-engage" the posterior glenoid rim, resulting in recurrent dislocation.[8]

1.3 Diagnostic Challenges

LPDS is the most commonly missed major joint dislocation. Up to 50% are missed during initial ED evaluation.[3] Contributing factors include:

  • Inadequate physical examination or subtlety of symptoms[1]
  • Clinical attention diverted to life-threatening inciting events (seizure)[1]
  • Patient misrepresentation of trauma mechanism[2]
  • Failure of standard radiographic imaging[2]

Acute Presentation

The arm is held in fixed internal rotation and adduction. The most telling sign is a fixed, rigid block to external rotation.[2] Forward flexion and abduction are severely limited. Visual inspection may reveal the "empty socket sign" (flattening of anterior contour).[14]

Chronic (Neglected) Presentation

Cases undiagnosed for >3 weeks are "neglected" or "chronic".[1] The locked arm can mimic adhesive capsulitis[15] or muscle weakness.[2] Consequences include progressive stiffness, AVN, and severe post-traumatic OA.[6]

⚠️ Critical Evidence
A multicenter study of 102 RHLs[17] demonstrated that the mean RHL defect size is significantly larger in chronic locked dislocations (64.1°) compared to acute dislocations (32.6°). The gamma angle (predictor of re-engagement) was also more severe in chronic cases (108.7° vs. 83.8°). A chronic lesion is not merely "late" — it is a different, more severe osseous injury.
Evidence Level III

1.4 Advanced Imaging & Defect Quantification

Standard AP and scapular-Y radiographs are notoriously misleading and a primary reason for missed diagnoses.[2]

  • CT Scan: The undisputed gold standard for diagnosis and surgical planning. 3D CT reconstruction allows accurate quantification of RHL defect size as a percentage of total articular surface.[2][17] Also superior for assessing posterior glenoid rim fractures or chronic glenoid erosion.[23]
  • MRI: Complementary; superior for evaluating soft-tissue pathology including posterior Bankart lesions, capsular stretching/tears, and rotator cuff integrity.[19][20]
📐 The "Bipolar" Concept
Stability depends on the relationship between the RHL and the posterior glenoid — a "bipolar" problem. The gamma angle is a key predictor of re-engagement; studies show a gamma angle >90° may warrant surgical intervention.[13] Bipolar bone loss (RHL + posterior glenoid loss) can transform a small, "non-engaging" RHL into an unstable, "engaging" one.[13]

II Defect-Based Treatment Algorithm

2.1 The Central Principle

Management is fundamentally dictated by RHL defect size (percentage of humeral head articular surface),[9] modified by:

  • Duration of dislocation (acute vs. chronic)
  • Patient age, functional demands, bone quality
  • Health of remaining articular cartilage[15]

The consensus evidence supports a three-tiered algorithmic framework:

SMALL  <20–25% — Generally stable post-reduction. Soft-tissue stabilization if needed.
MEDIUM  20–45% — "Reconstruction zone." Joint-preserving surgery required to fill/bypass defect.
LARGE  >45–50% — Unreconstructable. Salvage procedure (arthroplasty) mandated.
⚠ Note on Threshold Variability: Literature shows slight variability in percentage cutoffs (e.g., small/medium cited as 20% or 25%; medium/large cited as 40%, 45%, or 50%). These represent guidelines on a continuum of engagement risk. A "borderline" defect may be treated differently based on chronicity, glenoid bone loss, and surgeon experience.[28][18]

2.2 Small Defects (<20–25%)

For acute dislocations, closed reduction with adequate sedation is first-line.[28][35] The technique involves axial traction → adduction and internal rotation (to disengage RHL from glenoid rim) → gentle external rotation and abduction to guide the head back.

🚫 Critical Post-Reduction Step
The shoulder must be immobilized in external rotation (10°–20°) and slight abduction.[4] Traditional internal rotation sling is strictly contraindicated — it approximates the RHL to the posterior glenoid rim, inviting re-dislocation.[30]

2.3 Medium Defects (20–45%): The Reconstruction Challenge

2.3.1 Non-Anatomic: Modified McLaughlin Procedure

This is the workhorse for medium defects.[14] The rationale: non-anatomic "remplissage" (filling) of the defect physically prevents engagement and acts as a dynamic anterior soft-tissue restraint.[11]

  • Classic McLaughlin: Transfer of the subscapularis tendon into the RHL defect.[7]
  • Modified McLaughlin (Hughes/Neer): The preferred method. Osteotomy of the lesser tuberosity with intact subscapularis attachment; bone-tendon unit fixed into the defect (bone-to-bone healing).[2][8]

Studies demonstrate good-to-excellent middle-term functional outcomes (ASES, Constant scores) and high rates of maintained concentric reduction — even in neglected cases.[6][14]

2.3.2 Anatomic Reconstruction: Osteochondral Grafting

Anatomic restoration aims to restore native sphericity.[31] Options include fresh-frozen osteochondral allograft (typically femoral or humeral head)[9][31] or iliac crest autograft.[3]

🔑 Counter-Intuitive Finding (2024 Meta-Analysis)
A 2024 systematic review (155 shoulders) found anatomic humeral reconstruction associated with higher risk of radiological OA progression (21%) compared to McLaughlin (11%).[39] The accelerated OA is likely iatrogenic: anatomic grafting requires perfectly restoring sphericity — any incongruity creates abnormal shear forces. The McLaughlin bone-tendon complex is a more forgiving interface. A functionally stable joint may be superior to an imperfectly anatomic joint for long-term health.
Evidence Level II

2.3.4 Alternative: Rotational Humeral Osteotomy

A niche but valuable option: proximal humerus derotational osteotomy functionally rotates the humeral head, moving the RHL out of pathologic articulation.[15][38] Reserved for young, active patients with healthy cartilage and defects <40%.[15][38]

2.4 Large Defects (>45–50%) & Chronic/Arthritic Joints: Salvage Pathway

Joint-preserving reconstruction is no longer viable.[18] The treatment of choice is shoulder arthroplasty:

  • Hemiarthroplasty (HSA): Younger, higher-demand patients with healthy glenoid and functional rotator cuff.[34]
  • Total Shoulder Arthroplasty (TSA): For patients with large RHL plus glenohumeral arthritis. May offer superior outcomes vs. HSA in older populations.[18][34]
  • Reverse Shoulder Arthroplasty (RSA): For complex cases with large RHL combined with rotator cuff deficiency, significant posterior glenoid erosion, or high instability risk.[34]
⚠️ Prognostic Factor
A systematic review found a positive correlation between duration of dislocation and necessity for revision surgery following HSA or TSA.[34] Diagnostic and treatment delays are not benign — muscle wasting, capsular contractures, and progressive bone loss compromise even salvage arthroplasty.[6][16]
Evidence Level III

III Concomitant Pathology & Surgical Advances

3.1 The "Bipolar" Injury: Both Sides of the Joint

The RHL is rarely an isolated injury. Successful treatment requires a "both sides of the joint" philosophy.[27] Failure to identify and address concomitant posterior glenoid or labral pathology is a primary, preventable cause of surgical failure.[27]

📖 Clinical Pearl
A 2021 case report[27] describes a patient who underwent modified McLaughlin for a significant RHL. Post-operative CT confirmed persistent posterior subluxation. A second surgery — arthroscopic posterior Bankart repair — was required to re-attach the posterior labrum. Only after "repairing both sides" was the humeral head centralized.

Surgical planning must include thorough assessment for:

  • Posterior Bankart (Labral) Lesions: Detachment of the posterior inferior capsulolabral complex; must be addressed with arthroscopic suture anchor repair.[3]
  • Posterior Glenoid Rim Fractures ("Reverse Bony Bankart"): Creates bipolar bone loss. Significant posterior glenoid bone loss (>11%) may require posterior glenoid augmentation with autologous bone block.[13][42]
  • Subscapularis Tendon Integrity: Central to the McLaughlin procedure.[11] Complete subscapularis rupture must be identified and repaired concurrently. In arthroplasty, subscapularis repair significantly decreases postoperative dislocation risk after RSA.[54]

3.2 Technological Advances

3.2.1 The Arthroscopic Revolution

A significant trend since 2016 has been the adaptation of minimally invasive arthroscopic techniques.[56] The Arthroscopic Modified McLaughlin Procedure[57] provides excellent visualization and avoids the morbidity of open arthrotomy (sparing deltoid, avoiding large-scale subscapularis detachment).[59]

2024 studies validate the all-arthroscopic approach — combining closed reduction, arthroscopic McLaughlin (remplissage), and posterior labral repair — as safe and effective, even for neglected locked dislocations (up to 12 weeks) with RHL <40%. Patients showed marked improvements (e.g., forward flexion: 35° → 145°).[33]

⚖️ Nuanced Consideration
For truly chronic dislocations (>12 weeks), one study suggests that the open Neer modification (lesser tuberosity transfer) may yield better functional results than arthroscopic treatment.[61] The superior leverage, visualization, and soft-tissue release capabilities of an open approach may still be preferred for severe, long-standing contractures.

3.2.2 3D Printing & Patient-Specific Instrumentation (PSI)

3D-printing technology is emerging as a transformative tool:[62]

  • Pre-operative Planning: 3D-printed patient-specific models allow surgeons to physically analyze the RHL defect geometry.[12]
  • Patient-Specific Guides (PSI): Custom surgical guides enable exceptionally precise carving of osteochondral allografts for a perfect congruent match.[12]
  • Custom Implants: For highly irregular defects.[64]

Early data shows improved functional outcomes (higher Constant, lower DASH scores), lower pain, shorter operative times, and lower complication rates.[62]

🚀 Future Potential
3D printing may solve the central paradox of anatomic reconstruction. By using PSI to precisely shape allografts, surgeons can minimize incongruency — potentially making anatomic reconstruction the definitively superior joint-preserving option for medium defects.[12]

IV Synthesis & Clinical Recommendations

4.1 Evidence-Based Treatment Pathways

Treatment selection is dictated by three primary factors:

  1. RHL Defect Size: The percentage of articular surface loss is the primary determinant of stability.
  2. Chronicity: Duration determines tissue viability, defect severity, and the shift from reconstruction to salvage.
  3. Bipolar Assessment: Status of posterior glenoid and labrum is equally critical.
Table 3: Summary of Evidence-Based Pathways
Defect Category Evidence-Based Pathway Level of Evidence
Small (<25%) Stable. Acute → closed reduction + immobilization in external rotation.[35] Level IV
Medium (25–45%) "Zone of reconstruction." Modified McLaughlin[32] and anatomic grafting[10] both excellent. McLaughlin may have lower long-term OA risk.[39] Level III
Large (>45%) Unreconstructable → salvage arthroplasty. Implant choice: HSA/TSA/RSA based on glenoid/cuff status.[34] Level III

4.2 Key Clinical Recommendations

  1. Maintain a High Index of Suspicion: Any patient post-seizure, electric shock, or high-energy trauma with a fixed block to external rotation[2] must be considered to have LPDS until proven otherwise. Confirm with CT — standard radiographs are unreliable.
  2. Treat Both Sides of the Joint: Assess the posterior glenoid and labrum (MRI or CT arthrogram) before any procedure. Failure to address a concomitant posterior Bankart lesion[3] or significant glenoid bone loss[13] leads to surgical failure.[27]
  3. Embrace Modern Techniques: For medium-sized defects in subacute cases (<12 weeks), the all-arthroscopic approach combining modified McLaughlin with posterior labral repair offers excellent results with lower morbidity.[33] For anatomic allograft reconstruction, use 3D-printed PSI to maximize congruency.[12]
📝 Evidence Gap Identified: The primary unanswered question is the long-term (>10-year) comparative outcome of modern reconstructive techniques. Future research should focus on prospective RCTs comparing all-arthroscopic modified McLaughlin vs. 3D-guided anatomic allograft reconstruction for medium RHL defects. The source document notes this as a critical research priority.
