Nerve Injury in Pediatric Supracondylar Humerus Fractures

Etiology, Diagnosis & Treatment Algorithms — Evidence-Based Interactive Educational Summary

Executive Summary & Epidemiological Landscape

Supracondylar humerus fractures (SCHF) are the single most common elbow fracture in the pediatric population, constituting approximately 15% of all pediatric fractures and nearly 70% of all elbow injuries in the immature skeleton. Level I[1]

The injury is distinctive to the pediatric demographic, with a peak incidence between ages 5 and 7 years. This age distribution is directly linked to the developmental anatomy of the distal humerus; as the remodeling capacity evolves and the physis closes, incidence declines steadily until age 15, when adult-pattern injuries predominate. Level II[4]

The etiology is predominantly accidental, with falls from playground equipment (monkey bars, jungle gyms, slides) implicated in nearly 88% of high-energy mechanisms. Level III[6] While the vast majority are extension-type injuries from a fall onto an outstretched hand, the immense force transmits significant energy to surrounding soft tissues.

The incidence of nerve injury associated with SCHF ranges from 10% to 20%, with severe Gartland type III and IV fractures yielding rates as high as 20.6% to 42% in some prospective cohorts. Level II[7] This high prevalence reflects the extreme anatomical congestion of the distal humerus where the median, radial, and ulnar nerves traverse in close proximity to osseous structures.

Key Epidemiological Data

Most injuries are neurapraxias — transient physiological conduction blocks without anatomical discontinuity — that resolve spontaneously. However, the presence of a nerve deficit fundamentally alters the diagnostic and therapeutic algorithm.

The distribution of nerve involvement is highly predictable based on fracture displacement mechanics:

  • Anterior interosseous nerve (AIN) and median nerve — most frequently injured in extension-type fractures, especially with posterolateral displacement
  • Radial nerve — vulnerable in posteromedial displacement
  • Ulnar nerve — predominant neuropathy in the rare flexion-type fractures and a significant iatrogenic complication of cross-pinning fixation[7]

Etiology & Pathomechanics of Nerve Injury

The pathophysiology of nerve injury in SCHF is bipartite: traumatic injuries occurring at the moment of impact or displacement, and iatrogenic injuries resulting from therapeutic manipulation or surgical fixation.

Extension-Type Fractures (95–98% of all SCHFs) Level II[4]

The mechanism typically involves a fall onto an outstretched hand with the elbow in hyperextension. The olecranon acts as a fulcrum, snapping the thin supracondylar bone. The distal fragment displaces posteriorly while the jagged proximal metaphysis is driven anteriorly into the antecubital fossa, threatening the neurovascular bundle.

Median & Anterior Interosseous Nerve (AIN) Vulnerability

The AIN is the most frequently injured nerve in extension-type fractures, particularly with posterolateral displacement. The median nerve and AIN branch are tethered by the lacertus fibrosus and pronator teres heads, preventing escape from the injury zone. The anteromedial proximal spike creates a "tenting" effect.[7] A meta-analysis of over 5,000 fractures showed that AIN injury represents 34.1% of neurapraxias in extension fractures. Level I[12]

Because the AIN is purely motor (innervating flexor pollicis longus, flexor digitorum profundus to the index finger, and pronator quadratus), there is no sensory deficit — making this a "silent" palsy prone to underreporting.

Radial Nerve Susceptibility

The radial nerve is vulnerable in posteromedial displacement patterns, where the lateral spike places the nerve under tension. The nerve can be draped over the lateral spike or entrapped within the fracture gap during reduction.[17]

Combined Neurovascular Injury

Clinical Pearl

The median nerve and brachial artery share a common sheath. Median nerve palsy is a strong predictor of vascular compromise. In "pink pulseless hand" scenarios, a median nerve deficit should heighten suspicion of brachial artery entrapment.[19]

Flexion-Type Fractures (2–3% of SCHFs) Level III[16]

Flexion-type injuries carry a disproportionately high risk of ulnar nerve pathology. The posterior displacement of the proximal fragment directly threatens the ulnar nerve in the cubital tunnel. Ulnar neuropathy accounts for 91.3% of neurapraxias in flexion-type injuries, with a weighted event rate of 16.6%.[12]

Critical Warning

Any child with a flexion-type SCHF must be assumed to have an ulnar nerve injury until proven otherwise. Attempting repeated closed reductions in a flexion fracture with a new ulnar nerve deficit is ill-advised, as it may further crush an entrapped nerve.[16]

Iatrogenic Nerve Injury (2–6% of cases) Level I[21]

The primary culprit is the medial pin used in cross-pinning configurations. Three mechanisms exist:

  1. Direct Penetration (Neurotmesis): K-wire impales the nerve — rare but devastating
  2. Constriction: Nerve caught between pin and bone, causing ischemic compression
  3. Traction (Neurapraxia): Nerve tethered over pin; elbow extension post-op stretches the nerve[1]

Risk is significantly increased if the pin is placed with the elbow in hyperflexion, as the ulnar nerve subluxates anteriorly over the medial epicondyle. Level III[22]

Diagnostic Evaluation

The "Rock, Paper, Scissors, OK" Examination Level III[25]

This validated mnemonic provides a rapid, reproducible neurological screening in uncooperative children:

✊
Median Nerve
"Rock" (Make a Fist)
Tests flexor capability. Inability to flex index/middle fingers → "Hand of Benediction."
🖐️
Radial Nerve
"Paper" (Extend Fingers)
Tests extensor group. "Wrist drop" or inability to extend MCP joints is hallmark of radial palsy.[27]
✌️
Ulnar Nerve
"Scissors" (Finger Cross)
Tests interossei. Loss of function or 4th/5th digit "clawing" suggests ulnar involvement.[27]
👌
Anterior Interosseous Nerve
"OK" Sign
Isolates FPL & FDP index. "Flat pinch" (pad-to-pad) instead of tip-to-tip = AIN palsy. Most critical test — easily missed.[28]

The "Dimple Sign" Level III[18]

A distinct depression or pucker in the antecubital fossa skin indicating the sharp anteromedial spike has buttonholed through the brachialis muscle. This sign is:

  • A marker of high-energy injury and significant displacement
  • Strongly associated with entrapment of the neurovascular bundle
  • Often a contraindication to simple closed reduction with traction alone
  • An indication for open reduction if "milking" maneuver fails[18]

Radiographic Indicators

  • Anterior Humeral Line (AHL): On lateral view, should pass through middle third of capitellum. Disruption indicates extension deformity.[28]
  • Baumann's Angle: On AP view, assesses varus/valgus alignment. Significant varus collapse associated with posteromedial rotation and radial nerve risk.
  • Displacement Direction: Most predictive factor for nerve specificity — posterolateral = median/AIN risk; posteromedial = radial risk.[11]
  • Contralateral Imaging: Recommended as a patient-specific template for normal anatomy given the CRITOE ossification sequence.[32]

Advanced Diagnostics

EMG & Ultrasound Guidance

Early EMG (first 3 weeks) is discouraged — cannot distinguish neurapraxia from axonotmesis before Wallerian degeneration manifests. Reserve EMG for patients with no recovery at 8–12 weeks. Level III[33]

High-resolution ultrasound is increasingly recognized as valuable for visualizing nerve entrapment within callus and monitoring vascular flow in the pink pulseless hand.[7, 15]

Acute Treatment Algorithms

1. The "Pink Pulseless Hand" Paradigm

Historically, absent distal pulse was an absolute indication for aggressive vascular exploration. However, accumulated evidence demonstrates a high rate of spontaneous pulse return following fracture reduction and low limb ischemia due to robust collateral circulation. Level I[3, 15]

Pink Pulseless Hand Algorithm

1

Urgent Reduction (CRPP)

Fracture reduction is the first-line treatment, often relieving the kink in the brachial artery.[13]

2

Immediate Reassessment in OR

Evaluate pulse, perfusion, and nerve status post-fixation.

3

Decision Branches

Pulse Returns → Standard casting and observation.

Pulse Absent + Cold/White Hand → Immediate Vascular Exploration.[37]

Pulse Absent + Warm/Pink Hand → Watchful Waiting — strict hourly neurovascular checks for 24–48 hours. Doppler confirmation of arch flow.[3]

Important Nuance

A study of patients with persistent pulselessness at 72 hours revealed that 83% had actual brachial artery injuries. While the limb survives via collaterals, long-term implications of single-vessel flow are still being defined. The combination of a pulseless hand and median nerve palsy remains a "red flag" for physical entrapment.[39, 19]

3. Iatrogenic Nerve Injury Management

If a new nerve deficit appears post-operatively (most commonly ulnar after medial pinning):

  • Observation: ~90% are neurapraxias recovering spontaneously within 3–4 months[1]
  • Pin Removal: If deficit is profound or pin position is suspicious. However, systematic reviews suggest early wire removal does not statistically improve recovery rates compared to expectant management[1]
  • Algorithm: If fracture stable with lateral pins → remove medial pin. If medial pin needed for stability → maintain and observe, unless severe intractable pain suggests direct impalement.

Subacute & Chronic Management

Timeline of Recovery

The natural history of traumatic neurapraxia in SCHF is favorable. Mean time to clinical recovery is 2 to 3.5 months, with full recovery often taking up to 6 months. Level III[8]

  • Radial Nerve: Often first to recover (brachioradialis returns first)
  • Median/AIN: Slower; purely motor — no sensory precursors to guide monitoring
  • Ulnar Nerve: Often slowest, especially with flexion injuries or severe entrapment

Indications for Surgical Exploration

Exploration Decision Algorithm

A

Primary Exploration (Acute)

Open fractures (higher neurotmesis risk), cold pulseless hand (concurrent nerve inspection), inability to reduce (soft tissue interposition — often neurovascular bundle).[13]

B

Delayed Exploration (Subacute)

If no clinical or EMG evidence of recovery by 4–5 months → surgical exploration indicated.[42]

C

Radial Nerve Exception

Earlier exploration (3–4 months) if absolutely no brachioradialis function. Short distance to first muscle means recovery should be evident early.[33]

Common finding at delayed exploration: Nerve entrapment within fracture callus or dense fibrous scar — neurolysis is often curative. Lacerations (neurotmesis) are rare but require grafting.[9]

Reconstructive Options for Irreversible Injury

In the rare event (~1–5%) that nerve function does not return, reconstructive surgery is indicated. Children possess high neuroplasticity, allowing superior adaptation to transfers.

Tendon Transfers Level IV[44, 46]

FCR vs. FCU debate: Use of FCR is gaining popularity over FCU for finger extension transfer. FCU is the strongest wrist flexor; its transfer can weaken wrist function and lead to radial deviation deformity.[44]

Nerve Transfers: The Modern Frontier Level IV[47, 48]

Nerve transfers sacrifice a redundant nerve branch and coapt it directly to the injured distal nerve. By connecting close to the target muscle, regeneration distance is minimized, leading to faster recovery and preventing motor endplate death.

  • Radial nerve application: Redundant median nerve branches (FDS/FCR) → radial nerve branches (ECRB/PIN)
  • Outcomes: Comparative studies suggest nerve transfers result in superior grip strength and more physiologic muscle balance vs. tendon transfers. Patients achieve independent finger extension — often difficult with tendon transfers[47]
  • Advantage: Shorter operative times, younger patient age at intervention[47]

Conclusion & Best Practice Recommendations

  1. Prevention is Paramount: Lateral-only pinning for most fractures is the safest default. When medial pins are necessary, the strict "mini-open/elbow extension" technique is mandatory.[1, 13]
  2. Diagnostic Rigor: The "Rock, Paper, Scissors, OK" exam must be standard documentation. The "Dimple Sign" must be recognized as a warning of neurovascular entrapment.[25, 18]
  3. The Pink Pulseless Hand: Urgent CRPP followed by watchful waiting is the standard of care, supported by long-term data.[3] However, pulseless hand + median nerve palsy remains a "red flag."[19]
  4. Patience with Limits: Observation is default for post-injury palsy, but lack of recovery by 4 months warrants EMG and exploration. Emerging data supports early surgery (<8 h) for mixed nerve injuries.[10]
