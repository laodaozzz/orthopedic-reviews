Evidence-Based Orthopaedic Education: Glenohumeral Joint Osteoarthritis Management

Comprehensive Evidence-Based Management of Glenohumeral Joint Osteoarthritis

Introduction and Burden of Disease

Glenohumeral joint osteoarthritis (GHJ OA) is a progressively debilitating condition characterized by irreversible articular cartilage degradation, subchondral sclerosis, osteophyte formation, and complex morphological alterations of the glenoid vault. As the population ages and remains active, the epidemiological and economic burden has expanded exponentially, manifesting as profound pain, severe motion restriction, loss of independence, and reduced quality of life I.

The management challenge stems from the shoulder's unique biomechanics—it relies on dynamic muscular and static capsulolabral restraints rather than deep osseous architecture. The past two decades have seen a paradigm shift from hemiarthroplasty toward advanced anatomically restorative or biologically compensatory arthroplasty techniques, driven by 3D imaging, computational templating, and semi-constrained implant designs II.

Pathogenesis and Etiological Paradigms

Primary Glenohumeral Osteoarthritis

Primary GHJ OA is an idiopathic, progressive degenerative disease characterized by asymmetric posterior subluxation of the humeral head. This initiates a destructive cycle of eccentric posterior glenoid loading, driven by anterior soft-tissue contracture. The hallmark is posterior glenoid erosion leading to retroversion and biconcave morphology, while the rotator cuff generally remains intact II.

Secondary Glenohumeral Osteoarthritis

Secondary GHJ OA arises from identifiable preceding pathologies:

  • Capsulorrhaphy Arthropathy: Occurs after overly aggressive anterior stabilization (e.g., Putti-Platt), leading to iatrogenic posterior translation and accelerated posterior glenoid wear II.
  • Post-Traumatic and Iatrogenic Chondrolysis: Rapid cartilage dissolution following arthroscopic interventions, linked to intra-articular local anesthetic infusions or thermal necrosis II.
  • Inflammatory Arthropathies: Conditions like rheumatoid arthritis cause symmetric, concentric cartilage destruction with severe osteopenia and medialization II.
  • Cuff Tear Arthropathy (CTA): Results from massive, irreparable rotator cuff tears leading to superior humeral head migration and "acetabularization" of the acromion II.

Glenoid Morphology: The Modified Walch Classification

Accurate glenoid classification is prerequisite for preoperative planning. The original Walch classification (1999) had fair reliability. The Bercik Modification (2016) utilizing 3D CT reconstructions significantly improved reliability and introduced B3 and D subtypes II.

Table 1: Modified Walch Classification of Primary Glenohumeral Osteoarthritis
Type Subtype Morphological Description Wear Pattern
A A1 Centered humeral head with minor central erosion Concentric / No subluxation
A2 Centered humeral head with major central erosion; medialization Concentric / Profound medialization
B B1 Posterior joint space narrowing, sclerosis, osteophytes. No macroscopic erosion Posterior subluxation / Early posterior wear
B2 Biconcave glenoid with distinct paleoglenoid (anterior) and neoglenoid (posterior) Posterior subluxation / Asymmetric posterior erosion
B3 Monoconcave glenoid with severe posterior wear. No paleoglenoid remains Severe subluxation (>70%) OR retroversion (>15°)
C C Dysplastic glenoid with congenital retroversion >25° Congenital retroversion / Variable wear
D D Glenoid anteversion or anterior subluxation (Walch index <40%) Anterior subluxation / Anterior wear
Critical Distinction: B2 vs. B3

While retroversion and subluxation indices are similar, the absence of the paleoglenoid in B3 severely limits stable fixation with standard symmetric implants and traditional corrective techniques. 3D CT is essential for precise phenotyping II.

For secondary CTA, the Sirveaux-Favard classification (E0-E4) remains the standard for evaluating coronal plane glenoid erosion characteristic of CTA II.

Diagnostic and Preoperative Imaging Algorithm

3D CT as the Gold Standard

Two-dimensional CT scans are susceptible to positional artifacts, with scapular rotation altering glenoid version measurements by up to 10 degrees. 3D CT reconstruction provides significantly higher interobserver (ICC = 0.83) and intra-observer (ICC = 0.91) reliability II.

3D planning leads surgeons to select different implant sizes in 39-43% of cases and reveals underestimated posterior bone loss in 52% of patients. When coupled with patient-specific instrumentation, it reduces variability in component positioning II.

Limitation: While 3D planning improves radiographic accuracy, there remains a paucity of long-term evidence demonstrating statistically significant improvements in Patient-Reported Outcome Measures (PROMs) compared to expert 2D planning II.

Rotator Cuff Assessment: The Goutallier-Fuchs Classification

The status of the rotator cuff dictates the bifurcation between anatomic and reverse arthroplasty. The Fuchs modification of the Goutallier classification (MRI-based) remains the universal clinical standard II.

Table 2: Goutallier-Fuchs Classification of Rotator Cuff Fatty Infiltration
Grade Criteria Clinical Implication for Arthroplasty
0 Normal muscle architecture; no fatty streaks Optimal candidate for aTSA. High likelihood of excellent dynamic stability
1 Some isolated fatty streaks Excellent candidate for aTSA; muscle remains functional
2 Important fatty infiltration, but muscle > fat (<50% FI) Threshold of reversibility. Increased risk of cuff failure after aTSA
3 Equal fat and muscle (~50% FI) Functionally deficient cuff. High risk for aTSA failure; rTSA strongly considered
4 More fat than muscle (>50% FI) Irreversible degeneration. Absolute contraindication for aTSA; strict indication for rTSA

Surgical Decision-Making: aTSA vs. rTSA

Primary Osteoarthritis with Intact Cuff

I Based on strong evidence from multiple high-quality RCTs, the AAOS strongly recommends aTSA over hemiarthroplasty for primary GHJ OA with intact rotator cuff, demonstrating superior functional outcomes and pain relief.

Management of Complex Posterior Bone Loss (B2/B3)

For severe B2/B3 glenoids (>15° retroversion or >70% subluxation), rTSA is increasingly favored. The semi-constrained design converts destructive shear forces into stable compressive forces, forgiving residual retroversion III.

Augmentation techniques include:

  • Bony-Increased Offset RSA (BIO-RSA): Uses humeral head autograft to lateralize joint line, improving deltoid wrap and external rotation
  • Metal Augmented Baseplates: Provide immediate rigid fixation without graft resorption risk

Evidence Synthesis

I/II For younger, high-demand patients (<65 years) with intact cuff and minimal deformity, aTSA is recommended to maximize ROM and strength. For older patients, those with severe stiffness, or advanced B2/B3 wear, rTSA is recommended to minimize revision rates II.

Joint-Preserving Interventions: The CAM Procedure

The Comprehensive Arthroscopic Management (CAM) procedure is a systematic joint-preserving algorithm for young, active patients (<55 years). It addresses multiple pain generators: synovitis, capsular contracture, impingement, and neuropathy IV.

Surgical Steps

  1. Extensive debridement and synovectomy
  2. Chondroplasty and microfracture
  3. Global capsular release (360°)
  4. Inferior humeral head osteoplasty
  5. Axillary nerve decompression
  6. Biceps tenodesis and subacromial decompression

Outcomes and Predictors of Failure

Survivorship (avoiding arthroplasty): 92% at 1 year, 85% at 2 years, 77% at 5 years, 63% at 10 years IV.

Strict failure predictors:

  • Preoperative joint space <2 mm
  • Macroscopic humeral head flattening
  • Walch B2 or C glenoid
  • Markedly low critical shoulder angle

Note: Evidence gap—long-term PROM data for CAM compared to arthroplasty is limited.

Evidence-Based Treatment Algorithm

The contemporary management requires a highly individualized algorithm anchored in exhaustive diagnostics:

  1. Diagnostic Baseline: 3D CT with Modified Walch Classification + MRI with Fuchs/Goutallier criteria
  2. Young, High-Demand Cohort (intact cuff, >2mm joint space, Walch A1/B1): CAM procedure
  3. Standard Primary OA (intact cuff, Walch A1/B1/mild B2): aTSA I
  4. Complex Primary OA (intact cuff, Walch B2 >15° or B3): rTSA with metal augments or BIO-RSA III
  5. Biologically Deficient Cohort (Goutallier 3/4 or CTA): rTSA regardless of age or morphology
