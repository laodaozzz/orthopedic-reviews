Clinical Databases & Registries for Orthopaedic Research

STRATEGIC REVIEW 2024–2025

Executive Summary

The paradigm of orthopaedic research has fundamentally shifted over the last two decades, transitioning from a reliance on single-institution case series and relatively small-scale randomized controlled trials to the utilization of "big data" derived from massive administrative repositories and clinical registries. EVIDENCE LEVEL II

This transition has been driven by the necessity to detect rare adverse events—such as periprosthetic joint infection (PJI) or pulmonary embolism—which statistically require sample sizes in the tens or hundreds of thousands to analyze effectively, numbers practically unattainable through traditional prospective study designs. Furthermore, the increasing focus of healthcare policy on value-based care, bundled payments, and population health management has necessitated datasets that capture healthcare utilization across the entire continuum of care, rather than just the index surgical event.

Scope of This Review This report provides an exhaustive analysis of publicly available clinical databases and registries suitable for orthopaedic research, with a specific focus on Total Joint Arthroplasty (TJA), the rapidly expanding domain of outpatient arthroplasty, hip fractures, and orthopaedic trauma. Resources are categorized into three primary domains: US Administrative & National Databases, US Clinical Quality & Trauma Registries, and International & Specialized Registries.

1. United States Administrative & National Databases

Administrative databases are derived primarily from the billing and discharge records of hospitals and healthcare systems. They rely on International Classification of Diseases (ICD) codes to define diagnoses and procedures. Their primary strength is sheer volume and breadth—they capture the "real-world" practice of medicine across diverse settings. However, they are limited by reliance on coding accuracy and a lack of clinical granularity; variables such as range of motion, specific implant catalog numbers, or intraoperative details are typically absent.

1.1 Healthcare Cost and Utilization Project (HCUP) Family

The HCUP is a family of healthcare databases and related software tools developed through a Federal-State-Industry partnership sponsored by the Agency for Healthcare Research and Quality (AHRQ). HCUP databases are unique in that they are "all-payer," including data on patients covered by Medicare, Medicaid, private insurance, and those uninsured—a distinct advantage over Medicare-only datasets restricted to the elderly and disabled populations. EVIDENCE LEVEL I [1]

1.1.1 National (Nationwide) Inpatient Sample (NIS)

The NIS is the largest publicly available all-payer inpatient healthcare database in the United States and the workhorse of orthopaedic epidemiological research, cited in thousands of peer-reviewed publications. It contains data on approximately seven million hospital stays each year; when weighted, it estimates roughly 35 million hospitalizations nationally, representing a stratified sample of approximately 20% of discharges from U.S. community hospitals. EVIDENCE LEVEL II [3, 5]

⚠ Critical Methodological Consideration — Sampling Redesign (2012) Prior to 2012, the NIS was a sample of hospitals (all discharges from a selected hospital included). Post-2012, the design shifted to a sample of discharges from all participating hospitals. This change improved national estimate precision but complicated longitudinal analyses spanning the 2011–2012 divide, requiring revised trend weights. Additionally, the ICD-9-CM to ICD-10-CM/PCS transition in Q4 2015 introduced a "data break" where definitions of complications and comorbidities changed significantly. [5]

Key Variables for Orthopaedics:

  • Demographics: Age, sex, race, median household income for patient's ZIP code (quartile-stratified), primary payer. [5]
  • Clinical Coding: ICD-10-CM for diagnoses and ICD-10-PCS for procedures, enabling identification of specific fracture patterns (pertrochanteric vs. femoral neck) and surgical interventions (ORIF vs. hemiarthroplasty). Comorbidities assessed using the Elixhauser Comorbidity Index. EVIDENCE LEVEL II [7]
  • Hospital Characteristics: Teaching status, bed size, ownership, urban/rural location—enabling volume-outcome and centralization analyses.
  • Outcomes: In-hospital mortality, length of stay (LOS), total charges. NOTE: Does NOT capture post-discharge events (a patient dying one day after discharge appears as a survivor). Readmissions appear as new, unrelated records. [4]

1.1.2 Nationwide Ambulatory Surgery Sample (NASS)

One of the most significant limitations of the NIS in the modern era is its exclusion of outpatient procedures. With the removal of TKA and THA from the Medicare Inpatient Only (IPO) list, a massive volume of joint replacement surgery has migrated to the outpatient setting. Relying solely on the NIS will increasingly result in selection bias. EVIDENCE LEVEL II [10]

The NASS is the largest all-payer ambulatory surgery database in the U.S., producing national estimates of major ambulatory surgery encounters in hospital-owned facilities. It contains approximately 9.0 million unweighted encounters annually, weighted to represent nearly 12 million procedures. [10]

⚠ Facility Limitation NASS covers hospital-owned ambulatory facilities only. It significantly underrepresents or excludes freestanding Ambulatory Surgery Centers (ASCs) that are entirely physician-owned. Since many healthy TJA patients are treated in freestanding ASCs, NASS still represents only a specific slice of the outpatient pie. [13]

1.1.3 Nationwide Readmissions Database (NRD)

The NRD addresses the "linkage" problem inherent in the NIS. It utilizes verified patient linkage numbers to track individuals across hospitals within the same state over the course of a calendar year. This capability is indispensable for calculating 30-day, 60-day, or 90-day readmission rates for complications such as PJI, dislocation, or VTE. EVIDENCE LEVEL II [14]

Limitations: Linkage is state-specific—a patient operated in New York readmitted to New Jersey will not be linked. The NRD does not link across years (e.g., December to January), so readmissions for procedures performed late in the year may be lost. [14]

1.2 National Health and Nutrition Examination Survey (NHANES)

NHANES, conducted by the CDC's NCHS, combines interviews with physical examinations and laboratory testing. It uses a complex multistage probability design examining about 5,000 persons per year. Unlike HCUP data, NHANES provides biological measurements critical for bone health research. EVIDENCE LEVEL I [17]

Key Variables for Orthopaedics:

  • Bone Density (DXA): Dual-energy X-ray Absorptiometry data for femoral neck and lumbar spine—the reference standard for defining osteoporosis prevalence in the U.S. population. [18, 19]
  • Arthritis & Pain: Detailed questionnaires on joint pain, stiffness, swelling, and self-reported diagnoses of osteoarthritis and rheumatoid arthritis. [21, 22]
  • Functional Limitation: Physical examination components testing grip strength, gait speed, and balance—objective measures of frailty that billing codes cannot capture. [23]
  • Laboratory Data: Serum biomarkers including Vitamin D, calcium, CRP, and uric acid for studying metabolic bone disease and inflammatory arthropathies. [24]

Cost: NHANES data is free and publicly available. Restricted Data access (detailed geography, exact dates, sensitive genetic data) requires a proposal to the NCHS Research Data Center. [25, 26]

1.3 CMS Medicare Limited Data Sets (LDS)

For research targeting the elderly (age >65), Medicare data offers longitudinal tracking for the remainder of the patient's life. CMS makes data available primarily through Limited Data Sets (LDS) and Research Identifiable Files (RIF). EVIDENCE LEVEL II [27]

Key Files:

  • MedPAR: Records for 100% of Medicare inpatient beneficiaries; similar to NIS but specific to Medicare with longitudinal tracking. [28]
  • Carrier Files: Fee-for-service claims from professional providers—crucial for capturing surgeon fees, anesthesia services, and office visits. [29]
  • Standard Analytic Files (SAF): Available as 5% (sample) or 100% files for inpatient, outpatient, SNF, and home health agencies. [30]

Access Cost: 5% sample files cost approximately $1,000–$1,700/year. 100% files are significantly more expensive, with initial project fees of approximately $20,000. Application is managed through the Research Data Assistance Center (ResDAC). [31, 32]

2. United States Clinical Quality & Trauma Registries

Unlike administrative databases reliant on coders, clinical quality registries utilize trained clinical reviewers (often nurses) to abstract data directly from patient charts. This results in "high-fidelity" clinical data including operative time, precise antibiotic timing, and specific laboratory values.

2.1 ACS National Surgical Quality Improvement Program (ACS-NSQIP)

ACS-NSQIP is widely regarded as the premier risk-adjusted surgical outcomes database in the world. Originally developed by the VA, it employs a systematic sampling method (typically an 8-day cycle) to ensure a representative sample from participating hospitals. EVIDENCE LEVEL I [7]

Key Variables:

  • Risk Adjustment: Over 150 variables including detailed preoperative comorbidities (dyspnea, functional status, wound infection history), intraoperative details (anesthesia type, ASA class, operative duration), and labs (hematocrit, albumin, creatinine).
  • 30-Day Outcomes: Complications defined by strict clinical criteria, not billing codes. Surgical Site Infection categorized as Superficial, Deep, or Organ/Space per CDC definitions. [33]
  • Procedure Targeted Options: The targeted module allows collection of extra variables for TJA and hip fracture, including implant fixation type and ambulation status. [34, 35]
Access Restriction The NSQIP Participant Use Data File (PUF) is generally not available for public purchase. Access is restricted to researchers at hospitals that actively participate in the ACS-NSQIP program. Non-affiliated researchers can sometimes access data through collaboration with a PI at a participating site. [36, 37]

2.2 National Trauma Data Bank (NTDB) / Trauma Quality Programs (TQP)

The NTDB is the largest aggregation of trauma registry data in the U.S. with over 7.5 million records. The ACS has transitioned this resource into the Trauma Quality Programs (TQP) Participant Use File. It represents a convenience sample from Level I–V trauma centers. EVIDENCE LEVEL II [38]

Key Variables:

  • Injury Scoring: ISS, Glasgow Coma Scale (with sub-scores), and AIS codes for every body region. [39, 40]
  • Physiology: Admission vital signs (systolic BP, pulse, respiratory rate) for validating triage scores and mortality prediction models.
  • Outcomes: Mortality, ICU LOS, ventilator days, and discharge disposition.

Cost: $1,100 per admission year; fee generally waived for active TQIP participant trauma centers. [38]

3. Implant & Disease-Specific Registries

3.1 American Joint Replacement Registry (AJRR)

The AJRR, operated by the AAOS, is the largest hip and knee replacement registry by procedural volume, holding data on over 3 million procedures from hospitals, ASCs, and practice groups. EVIDENCE LEVEL II [42]

Strategic Evolution — Fracture & Trauma Registry (FTR) Transition Starting January 1, 2026, the standalone FTR will transition: hip fracture data integrates into the AJRR, while proximal humerus fracture data migrates to the Shoulder & Elbow Registry (SER). This consolidation positions the AJRR as the central hub for analyzing the intersection of trauma and arthroplasty. [43, 63]

Key Variables:

  • Level III Data: Implant catalog/lot numbers, surgical approach (anterior vs. posterior), and Patient-Reported Outcome Measures (PROMs) including HOOS and KOOS scores. [44]
  • CMS Linkage: Ability to link with CMS claims data enables tracking of longitudinal outcomes (revision surgery at different facilities) for Medicare patients, reducing loss to follow-up. [45]

Access: Managed through the AAOS Registry Analytics Institute (RAI). Researchers submit hypothesis-driven proposals during specific cycles. Funding opportunities include travel grants up to $3,500. [46, 47]

3.2 International Registry Powerhouses

Australian Orthopaedic Association National Joint Replacement Registry (AOANJRR)

Widely considered the global benchmark for device surveillance with near 100% capture. It has been instrumental in identifying and forcing the recall of poorly performing prostheses (e.g., metal-on-metal hips). Primary value lies in device-specific revision rates. EVIDENCE LEVEL I [48]

Access: Free for contributing surgeons via ad hoc reports; external access operates on cost-recovery basis. [49, 50]

National Joint Registry (NJR) — United Kingdom

Monitors hip, knee, ankle, elbow, and shoulder replacements across England, Wales, Northern Ireland, and the Isle of Man with over 3 million records. [51]

Cost: Clear cost-recovery policy—£500 + VAT for direct dataset dissemination; £600 + VAT per half-day for analyst time. [52]

UK Biobank

A large-scale biomedical database with in-depth genetic and health information from 500,000 UK participants. Unique for integration of genomics (GWAS), imaging (MRI/DXA), and longitudinal health records—allowing "upstream" research into disease etiology. [53, 54, 55]

Cost: Tiered pricing from £3,000–£9,000; reduced fees (~£500) for students and researchers from low/middle-income countries. [56, 57]

German Arthroplasty Registry (EPRD)

Distinguishes itself through systematic linkage with statutory health insurance claims data, closing the "loss to follow-up" loop by tracking revisions even if patients move to non-participating hospitals. [60]

TARN / National Major Trauma Registry (NMTR)

Historically the lead UK/European trauma registry. In 2024, TARN's functions transitioned to the NMTR under NHS England. Its legacy reports established critical baselines—e.g., falls from standing height as the dominant mechanism of major trauma in the elderly ("silver trauma"). [58, 59]

4.1 The Volume vs. Detail Trade-off

  • Using NTDB: Ideal for "big picture" epidemiological questions (e.g., "How does insurance status affect mortality in pelvic fractures?"). Massive sample size allows disparity analysis across thousands of patients. However, it does not capture "return to work" or "pain scores." [41]
  • Using a Hospital Registry: Ideal for functional questions (e.g., "Does surgical approach affect return to work at 6 months?"). But sample sizes are limited.

5. Practical Guide to Access & Regulatory Compliance

5.1 Data Use Agreement (DUA) Requirements

Almost all datasets require a legally binding DUA signed by the researcher and often their institution's legal representative. DUAs strictly prohibit any attempt to identify patients. In the NIS, researchers cannot report results for any group with fewer than 11 patients (cell suppression). DUAs typically restrict data to a specific research project. [9]

5.3 Institutional Review Board (IRB)

  • Public Use Files (PUF): Datasets like NIS or TQP PUF are often considered "de-identified" and may qualify for exempt or expedited IRB review.
  • Restricted Files: CMS Research Identifiable Files or restricted NHANES data always require full IRB approval, as the risk of re-identification is non-zero.

6. Future Horizons & Conclusion

6.1 Integration of Patient-Reported Outcomes (PROMs)

The historic reliance on "revision" as the primary endpoint for arthroplasty success is fading. Registries like the AJRR and international counterparts are aggressively integrating PROMs (KOOS, HOOS, EQ-5D) to measure the quality of the result, not just the absence of failure. Future database research will increasingly focus on "value"—defined as improvement in PROM score per dollar spent. EVIDENCE LEVEL III [63]

6.2 Machine Learning & Predictive Modeling

The sheer volume of data in registries creates fertile ground for machine learning (ML). Researchers are using ML algorithms on these datasets to predict mortality in trauma patients or readmission risk in arthroplasty with greater accuracy than traditional regression models. The UK Biobank's multi-modal data is particularly suited for AI-driven discovery of novel risk factors. EVIDENCE LEVEL III [64, 65]

6.3 Conclusion

Strategic Database Selection Summary
  • National Epidemiology & Cost: HCUP family (NIS/NASS) — account for outpatient shift
  • Clinical Quality & Complications: ACS-NSQIP — clinical fidelity for risk adjustment
  • Trauma: TQP PUF — with NHANES for biological context on bone health
  • Implant Survival: AJRR and international peers (AOANJRR, NJR)
  • Etiology & Genomics: UK Biobank, NHANES

Success requires a synergistic approach: using administrative data to define the scale of the problem, registries to understand clinical nuance, and biological databases to explore underlying etiology. By navigating the trade-offs of scope, access, and cost detailed in this report, investigators can leverage these powerful tools to drive evidence-based improvements in patient care.
