Postoperative Delirium in Elderly Hip Fracture Patients

Comprehensive Evidence-Based Review: 2024–2025 Clinical Guidelines, Preventive Strategies & Management

📌 Guideline Update 2024–2025 · ESAIC · AAOS · NICE · FFN

0 Introduction

The global demographic shift toward an aging population has precipitated a concurrent and dramatic rise in fragility fractures. Among these, hip fractures represent one of the most critical, devastating, and resource-intensive orthopedic emergencies. In the United Kingdom alone, the National Hip Fracture Database (NHFD) recorded 71,885 hip fractures in 2024, a figure projected to double by 2060.[1]

Within this highly vulnerable demographic, postoperative delirium (POD) emerges as one of the most frequent, deleterious, and historically mismanaged neurological complications.[2] Defined as an acute, fluctuating syndrome characterized by inattention, altered consciousness, and disorganized thinking, POD is no longer viewed merely as a transient postoperative nuisance; it is increasingly recognized as a profound indicator of acute brain failure that carries devastating long-term implications for morbidity, mortality, and permanent cognitive decline.[3]

Paradigm Shift (2024–2025): Major global societies — AAOS, ESAIC, NICE, BGS, and FFN — have moved aggressively away from reactive pharmacological management toward proactive, multicomponent preventive strategies. Pharmacological interventions for active delirium lack robust efficacy, do not shorten duration, and often carry lethal neurocognitive adverse profiles.[5]

This review synthesizes the latest high-impact literature, meta-analyses, and clinical guidelines from 2024–2025 covering epidemiology, risk factors, diagnostic modalities, and the vanguard of preventive and therapeutic strategies. It is optimized for advanced perioperative, anesthetic, and orthogeriatric teams.

The incidence of POD is highly variable across the literature, depending on diagnostic criteria, screening frequency, and cohort demographics, with raw published rates ranging from 10.09% to 51.28%.[14] However, a comprehensive meta-analysis of 13 cohort studies (N=18,850) established a pooled prevalence of 23%.[9] The 2025 NHFD report analyzing 16,476 patients assessed with the 4AT found 3,386 (21%) exhibited scores indicative of delirium.[10]

Critical Insight — Delirium as a Dementia Catalyst: A secondary analysis of the STRIDE trial demonstrated that POD exerts a profound neurotoxic effect, accelerating cognitive decline predominantly in patients who were cognitively unimpaired prior to surgery. In these patients, POD was associated with a decline of 1.52 MMSE points and 1.22 CDR-SB points over one year.[3,4] Conversely, delirium did not significantly alter the trajectory in patients with pre-existing cognitive impairment.[17] Prevention of POD is now recognized as a critical priority to mitigate a modifiable risk factor for incident dementia.

2 Predisposing & Precipitating Risk Factors Level II–III Evidence

The pathogenesis of POD is best understood through a vulnerability-trigger model: predisposing baseline characteristics (vulnerabilities) interact dynamically with precipitating physiological stressors (triggers) to overwhelm cerebral homeostasis. The 2024 ESAIC guidelines assign a "strong" recommendation to meticulous preoperative risk factor assessment.[6]

REGAIN Trial Landmark Finding (Level I — RCT): The massive REGAIN trial (N=1,600, 46 hospitals) conclusively demonstrated that spinal anesthesia was not statistically superior to general anesthesia regarding the primary composite outcome of death or inability to walk at 60 days, nor did it show superiority regarding POD incidence.[20] It is not the type of anesthesia but the management of the anesthetic state — excessively deep sedation (burst suppression), uncorrected hypoxia, and hemodynamic lability — that drives delirium risk.[2]

4 Evidence-Based Preventive Strategies Level I–II Evidence

Because pharmacological treatments for active delirium consistently show minimal efficacy[5], clinical consensus asserts that primary prevention is the only definitive strategy. Prevention requires a structural overhaul of the entire perioperative care pathway, relying on synergistic, interdisciplinary interventions.

4.1 Orthogeriatric Co-Management

Orthogeriatric co-management is defined as the highly integrated, collaborative care by both an orthopedic surgical team and a specialized geriatric or internal medicine team, beginning at admission and continuing through discharge.[31] The FFN, WHO, and BOA cement this as a key quality indicator for global hip fracture care.[26]

By holistically addressing hidden vulnerabilities — complex polypharmacy, undiagnosed metabolic derangements, acute malnutrition, occult infections, and subtle cardiac arrhythmias — geriatricians effectively raise the patient's physiological reserve prior to surgery. Meta-analyses reveal an NNT of only 5.3 to prevent one case of delirium, cementing co-management as one of the highest-yield interventions in modern perioperative medicine.[32]

4.2 ERAS & Accelerated Surgical Timing

A non-negotiable cornerstone of any hip fracture ERAS program is expedited surgery: within 48 hours of admission, ideally within 24 hours.[22] Prolonged wait times expose elderly patients to agonizing pain, enforced fasting, strict bed immobilization, and surging systemic inflammation — a toxic combination that rapidly precipitates acute brain failure.

Postoperatively, ERAS mandates: early mobilization within 24 hours with unrestricted weight-bearing as tolerated[26,45]; aggressive correction of preoperative Vitamin D deficiency; treatment of malnutrition; minimization of fasting through carbohydrate loading; and prompt urinary catheter removal.[26]

4.3 Fascia Iliaca Compartment Block (FICB)

Inadequate pain control is a primary driver of delirium, yet systemic opioid analgesia introduces a paradoxical risk of neurotoxicity.[21] The FICB, administered under ultrasound guidance immediately upon admission, provides profound target-specific analgesia without systemic neurotoxic effects.[35]

Clinical Impact of FICB Adoption: Shifting block utilization from 2.2% to 52.9% reduced preoperative MMEs from 38 → 16.9 and postoperative MMEs from 34 → 12.1. Institutional cohorts reported delirium drops from 6% → 0%.[21] Broader OCPP incorporating FICB reduced delirium from 44% → 29%.[8]

4.4 Opioid-Sparing Multimodal Analgesia (MMA)

MMA relies on strategic co-administration of agents targeting different nociceptive pathways, achieving superior pain control while drastically lowering systemic opioid doses.[38]

  • Acetaminophen (paracetamol): 10–15 mg/kg PO every 4–6 hours (max 5000 mg/day; ≤2000 mg/day in frail populations)[37]
  • Celecoxib: 100–200 mg PO every 12 hours (if renal function permits)[36]
  • Gabapentinoids (gabapentin): 100–200 mg PO every 8 hours (use with caution in extreme elderly due to sedating properties)[36]
  • Adjuncts: Systemic lidocaine infusions and perioperative dexamethasone exhibit opioid-sparing effects in advanced protocols[48]

5 Guidelines for Non-Pharmacological & Pharmacological Treatment Level V – Guideline Consensus

When primary prevention fails and POD manifests, management requires a disciplined, algorithm-driven approach. Guidelines from AGS, NICE, and ESAIC uniformly stress that treatment must first identify and reverse underlying physiological drivers (e.g., hypoxia, sepsis, urinary retention, uncontrolled pain) before resorting to psychoactive medications.[5]

5.1 Non-Pharmacological Multicomponent Bundles

The unanimous first-line treatment — and core of universal prevention — is immediate implementation of non-pharmacological bundles.[5] Key components:

  • Cognitive Reorientation: Continuous orientation to time, place, and person. Involvement of family members and familiar care partners to provide psychological anchoring.[5]
  • Environmental Optimization: Strict sleep hygiene protocols — minimize nighttime vital sign checks, reduce ward noise, dim lights, provide large-faced clocks and natural window light.[5]
  • Sensory & Physiological Repletion: Return visual and hearing aids immediately; aggressive hydration, nutrition, and bowel management to prevent constipation-induced agitation.[5]
  • Early Mobilization: Rapid cessation of bedrest; removal of physical tethers (IV lines, monitors, urinary catheters) to encourage ambulation and restore proprioception.[5]
Pharmacological Limitations: AGS guidelines explicitly note that pharmacological treatment has not been shown to modify the underlying duration, severity, or mortality of delirium; it merely suppresses outward behavioral symptoms.[5] Administering antipsychotics to patients with potential undiagnosed Lewy Body dementia or prolonged QTc carries high risk of lethal cardiovascular and neurological complications.[7]

5.2 Hypoactive Delirium — Special Considerations

Hypoactive delirium — characterized by extreme lethargy, withdrawal, and somnolence — carries a significantly higher mortality rate than hyperactive subtypes, largely due to clinical under-recognition and complications like aspiration pneumonia or deep vein thrombosis.[5] AGS guidelines warn that pharmacologic treatment of hypoactive delirium is unwarranted and potentially harmful, as it deepens dangerous somnolence, unless used very briefly to manage hidden, frightening internal delusions.[5]

6 Conclusion

Postoperative delirium in the elderly hip fracture population represents a critical physiological juncture where severe surgical trauma, systemic frailty, and pre-existing neurological vulnerability violently intersect. The result is a cascade of acute brain failure driving severe morbidity, astronomical healthcare costs, and irreversibly accelerated long-term cognitive decline.

As evidenced by the paradigm-shifting 2024–2025 guidelines from ESAIC, AAOS, NICE, and the FFN, the clinical approach has irrevocably shifted from reactive pharmacological suppression toward aggressive, interdisciplinary primary prevention. The universal implementation of:

  • Orthogeriatric co-management models
  • Rigid adherence to ERAS pathways requiring fixation within 48 hours
  • Rapid deployment of the 4AT screening tool upon admission
  • Aggressive use of opioid-sparing modalities — FICB and structured MMA

are no longer optional suggestions. They are the mandatory, ethical standard of care. Meticulous perioperative orchestration is the single most potent neurological intervention available to the clinical team.
