1. Introduction: The Public Health Imperative

1.1 Epidemiology and Impact

Geriatric hip fractures represent a formidable challenge for modern healthcare systems. An estimated 258,000 hip fractures occurred in the United States in 2010, at an annual economic cost of $17–20 billion.[2] These injuries are a leading cause of disability-adjusted life-years lost among older adults, fundamentally altering their trajectory of aging.[3]

⚠ Mortality Data

Mortality rates are alarmingly high: a five- to eight-fold increased risk in the first three months post-injury, culminating in 30-day mortality rates of 5–10% and one-year mortality rates of 12–37%.[4]

Beyond mortality, the impact on function and independence is devastating. Many survivors fail to return to their prior living circumstances or regain their pre-fracture level of mobility. Over 90% of fractures occur in individuals over age 65, typically resulting from a low-energy fall in the context of underlying osteoporosis.[6] This demographic is characterized by a high prevalence of frailty, cognitive impairment, and multiple medical comorbidities.[8]

1.2 The Foundational Principle: Multidisciplinary Orthogeriatric Co-Management

A consensus has emerged among major international health organizations: the most effective model of care is a structured, integrated, and multidisciplinary one — the "Hip Fracture Programme" or orthogeriatric care model.[2] This is the central recommendation from the AAOS NICE BGS FFN.

This model is predicated on co-management where orthopedic surgeons, geriatricians, anesthesiologists, specialized nurses, physical and occupational therapists, dietitians, and social workers collaborate from admission through to discharge.[4,9]

✓ Evidence for Orthogeriatric Care

Systematic reviews and meta-analyses demonstrate that orthogeriatric care models are associated with significantly reduced mortality, lower in-hospital complications (pressure ulcers, delirium), and superior functional and cognitive recovery.[4] Strong Evidence

1.3 A Systems-Based Approach to a Systemic Injury

The evolution of hip fracture management guidelines reveals a critical shift: from viewing a hip fracture as an isolated surgical problem to recognizing it as a systemic medical event occurring in a frail individual.[17] The primary drivers of excess morbidity and mortality are not surgical failures, but rather medical complications such as delirium, pneumonia, urinary tract infections, and VTE.[17]

By embedding geriatric expertise directly into the orthopedic care pathway, the complex medical, functional, and psychosocial needs of the patient can be addressed proactively.[1] Therefore, the single most significant advance in improving outcomes has not been a new surgical implant, but rather a new, systems-based model of care delivery.

2. Preoperative Pathway

2.1 Rapid Diagnosis and Initial Assessment

Standard radiographic evaluation includes an AP view of the pelvis and both AP and cross-table lateral views of the affected hip.[22] Frog-leg lateral views are explicitly not recommended, as the required manipulation can displace a previously non-displaced fracture.[22]

For suspected occult fractures (clinically suspected, radiographs negative), MRI is the modality of choice with the highest sensitivity and specificity.[24] If MRI is unavailable within 24 hours or contraindicated, CT is an acceptable alternative.[14]

📋 Fast-Track Admission

Guidelines recommend a protocol-driven, fast-track admission to an appropriate ward, ideally within four hours of presentation, to initiate the multidisciplinary care plan without delay.[6]

2.2 Medical Optimization and Timing of Surgery

There is a strong and consistent body of evidence demonstrating that surgical delay beyond 48 hours is an independent predictor of increased mortality and postoperative complications.[5]

⏱ Critical Recommendation: Time to Surgery

Major international guidelines converge on: perform surgery within 48 hours of admission. The 2021 AAOS guidelines refine this to a target of 24 to 48 hours, with high-performing centers often achieving surgery within 24 hours.[1] Strong Evidence

The preoperative period is for rapid, focused medical optimization, not protracted investigation. Key correctable comorbidities include:

  • Severe anemia
  • Active anticoagulation
  • Significant volume depletion or electrolyte imbalance
  • Uncontrolled diabetes (e.g., ketoacidosis)
  • Acute, unstable cardiopulmonary conditions

Anticoagulation Management

Limited evidence supports proceeding without delay for patients on antiplatelet agents (aspirin, clopidogrel).[25] For direct oral anticoagulants (DOACs), a recent international consensus suggests surgery can reasonably be performed <36 hours from last dose in many cases, balancing bleeding risk against harms of delay.[34] Warfarin requires active reversal with Vitamin K and/or PCC to achieve INR <1.5.[15]

2.3 Pain Management: The First Step in Delirium Prevention

Uncontrolled pain is a primary precipitating factor for delirium.[20] Pain must be assessed immediately, within 30 minutes of analgesia, and regularly thereafter using a validated scale.[14]

A multimodal analgesic strategy is strongly recommended:

  • Foundation: regular, scheduled paracetamol (acetaminophen)[28]
  • Opioids reserved for breakthrough pain, used judiciously[28]
  • Preoperative regional nerve blocks (fascia iliaca or femoral nerve block) — the most impactful intervention[2] Strong Evidence
✗ Abandoned Practice

Routine preoperative skin or skeletal traction provides no benefit and should be abandoned.[17] Strong Evidence

2.4 Anesthesia Assessment

Multiple large-scale studies and systematic reviews have failed to show a significant difference in major outcomes (mortality, delirium) between general and neuraxial (spinal) anesthesia.[8]

✓ Anesthesia Recommendation

Current strong recommendations from AAOS and NICE: either general or spinal anesthesia is appropriate, with similar outcomes expected.[2] The final decision should be individualized.[27] Strong Evidence

3. Surgical Treatment: Evidence-Based Implant Selection

3.1 Overarching Surgical Principles

The fundamental goal of surgical intervention is to create a construct stable enough to permit immediate, unrestricted full weight-bearing.[14] Early mobilization is the single most important factor in preventing complications associated with prolonged recumbency.[19]

Non-operative management is reserved only for patients with extreme medical comorbidities where operative mortality risk is prohibitive, or for non-ambulatory patients with minimal pain from a stable, impacted fracture.[17]

3.2 Technical Considerations with Strong Evidence

  • Cemented Femoral Stems: In osteoporotic bone, cementation achieves better initial fixation, reduces periprosthetic fracture risk, and is associated with improved short-term outcomes.[1] Strong Evidence
  • Surgical Approach: Moderate evidence shows no clear difference in major outcomes based on surgical approach (anterior, lateral, posterior). Choice based on surgeon preference.[1] Moderate Evidence
  • Tranexamic Acid (TXA): Routine administration significantly reduces intraoperative blood loss and need for transfusions.[30] Strong Evidence

4. Postoperative Management

4.1 Early Mobilization and Rehabilitation

The postoperative period is defined by an aggressive, proactive approach to prevent complications, with early mobilization as the central organizing principle. Unless contraindicated, patients should undergo assisted transfer out of bed and begin ambulation on postoperative day one.[3]

Formal physiotherapy assessment must occur on postoperative day one, followed by at least daily physiotherapy throughout the hospital stay.[3,14]

4.2 Delirium Prevention and Management

Postoperative delirium is the most common and one of the most serious complications, with incidence rates as high as 65%.[20] It is associated with poorer recovery, longer stays, increased mortality, and long-term cognitive decline.[45]

🛡 Key Delirium Prevention Strategies
  • Orthogeriatric co-management — routine geriatrician involvement[46]
  • Effective, opioid-sparing multimodal analgesia[2]
  • Avoid deliriogenic medications (benzodiazepines, anticholinergics)[35]
  • Maintain adequate oxygenation, hydration, nutrition, correct electrolytes[29]
  • Promote normal sleep-wake cycle, minimize nighttime disruptions[47]
  • Ensure glasses and hearing aids are available[47]
  • Encourage family presence and reorientation[47]
  • Early mobilization[2]

Pharmacological management with antipsychotics reserved only for severe agitation posing risk; used with caution for the shortest duration.[46]

4.3 Thromboprophylaxis (VTE Prevention)

Patients are at very high risk for DVT and PE, with fatal PE rates of 1.4–7.5% in the first three months without prophylaxis.[17] Routine pharmacological thromboprophylaxis (LMWH, fondaparinux, or other anticoagulant) is strongly recommended, initiated pre- or postoperatively and continued for at least 10 days, often up to 35 days.[17] Strong Evidence

4.4 Nutritional Support

Malnutrition is highly prevalent and an independent risk factor for complications.[51]

4.5 Blood Transfusion Strategy

✓ Restrictive Transfusion Threshold

Strong evidence supports a restrictive strategy: in asymptomatic postoperative patients, transfusion is not recommended unless Hgb < 8 g/dL.[22] Strong Evidence

4.6 Other Key Postoperative Points

  • Postoperative analgesia: Continue multimodal approach (scheduled paracetamol + judicious breakthrough opioids). Avoid NSAIDs in frail elderly.[27,29]
  • Pressure ulcer prevention: Risk assessment on admission; pressure-relieving mattresses, frequent repositioning, meticulous skin care.[3]

5. Post-Acute Rehabilitation

5.1 The Role of Physical and Occupational Therapy

Rehabilitation is a central and indispensable component of the care pathway. The overarching goal is to restore function to the pre-fracture baseline, enabling return to previous living circumstances.[13] Supervised PT and OT delivered across the full continuum of care significantly improves outcomes.[24] Recovery is long — basic mobility may recover within months, but complex IADLs can take up to a year.[58]

5.2 Physical Therapy (PT) Interventions

1Assessment: Use standardized outcome measures — gait speed, Timed Up-and-Go (TUG), 5-Times Sit-to-Stand, New Mobility Score, knee extension strength.[3]
2Active Exercise Program: Progressive resistive strength training (quadriceps, hip abductors), weight-bearing activities, balance training, functional mobility training (transfers, walking, stairs).[3]
3Extended Therapy: Functional deficits and elevated fall risk persist long after initial rehab. Strongly recommend extended therapy beyond 8–16 weeks: outpatient PT, progressive home programs, or community exercise programs.[3]
4Cognitive Impairment: Patients with mild-to-moderate dementia derive significant benefit from rehabilitation and should not be excluded.[3]

5.3 Occupational Therapy (OT) Interventions

OT focuses on recovery of ADLs (dressing, bathing, grooming, toileting) and IADLs (meal preparation, home management).[43]

  • Direct ADL training with adaptive equipment
  • Home environment assessment for fall hazards
  • Home modifications (grab bars, raised toilet seats)
  • Patient and family education on fall prevention and safe mobility[59]

6. Secondary Prevention

6.1 The High Risk of Subsequent Fracture

A geriatric hip fracture is a sentinel event. A patient who has sustained one fragility fracture has at least double the risk of another, with risk highest in the first one to two years.[62]

6.2 Osteoporosis Evaluation and Treatment

All patients presenting with a hip fracture should be evaluated for osteoporosis and initiated on treatment, ideally before hospital discharge.[5]

6.3 Falls Prevention

Since the vast majority of hip fractures result from a fall, a comprehensive falls risk assessment and multifactorial prevention plan are critical.[5]

🛡 Key Falls Prevention Interventions
  • Exercise: The single most effective intervention; must include strength + balance training. Tai Chi has strong evidence.[69] Strong Evidence
  • Medication Review: Identify and reduce fall-risk-increasing drugs (sedatives, anticholinergics, orthostatic hypotension-causing antihypertensives). Minimize polypharmacy.[22]
  • Vision and Hearing: Regular examinations; consistent use of prescribed aids.[70]
  • Home Safety: OT assessment; remove throw rugs, improve lighting, install grab bars, clear walkways.[22]

6.4 Fracture Liaison Service (FLS)

Despite clear evidence, a significant "care gap" exists where many patients are discharged without osteoporosis or fall risk being addressed.[9] The Fracture Liaison Service (FLS) model — a coordinator-based, systematic program — has been developed to close this gap.[63]

An FLS systematically identifies all patients with fragility fractures, initiates bone health and fall risk assessments, and ensures appropriate treatment and referrals before discharge.[16] FLS models are proven clinically effective and cost-effective, significantly increasing treatment rates and reducing subsequent fracture rates.[72]

7.2 Areas of Ongoing Research and Gaps in Evidence

While core principles are well-established, several areas require further investigation:

  • Optimal "dose" (frequency, intensity, duration) of postoperative rehabilitation
  • Precise timing of surgery for patients on novel oral anticoagulants[34]
  • Pathophysiology of postoperative delirium for targeted prevention strategies
  • Most effective ways to implement orthogeriatric models and FLS across diverse healthcare systems

7.3 Final Statement

Optimizing outcomes for the growing population of older adults with hip fractures is a clinical and public health imperative. The evidence is unequivocal: this requires a dedicated, systems-level commitment to implementing integrated, evidence-based, multidisciplinary care pathways — treating the whole person, from acute medical needs and surgical repair to functional rehabilitation and long-term health.
