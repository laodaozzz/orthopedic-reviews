Strategies for Preventing Varus Collapse & Screw Cut-Out in Osteoporotic Proximal Humerus Fractures

Interactive Evidence-Based Educational Summary · Based on Provided Source Document

I. Biomechanical Basis of Fixation Failure

A. The Centrality of the Medial Column

The primary catastrophic failure mode in locking plate fixation of proximal humerus fractures (PHFs) is secondary varus collapse. This is fundamentally a failure of the bone-implant construct, not the implant itself, and is overwhelmingly dependent on the integrity of the medial column, specifically the medial calcar.Level III [7]

The medial calcar serves as the primary load-bearing buttress against axial and varus-directed forces. A landmark cadaveric study demonstrated that simulating medial comminution decreased the mean load to failure by 48% and the mean energy to failure by 44%.Level V [6] This establishes that loss of medial support is not a minor variable but the single most critical biomechanical deficit.

⚠ Critical Causal Chain of Mechanical Failure

Osteoporosis → Complex fractures with medial comminution → Biomechanical void on the compression side → Cantilever bending forces concentrated on plate & bone-screw interface → Cyclical fatigue failure of bone → Varus collapse.

A standard locking plate applied laterally cannot function as a "load-sharing" construct when a medial void is present. In weak osteoporotic bone, this high cyclical strain leads to micro-motion and eventual fatigue failure of the bone, not the implant. The humeral head rotates into varus, clinically identical to varus collapse. [6]

B. The Bone-Implant Interface and Screw Cut-Out

Screw cut-out (secondary screw perforation, SSP) occurs when proximal screws migrate through the humeral head into the glenohumeral joint, reported in up to 14% of cases.Level IV [8]

The fundamental cause is poor bone quality (osteoporosis) resulting in compromised screw purchase. [1] Critically, bone mineral density (BMD) is not uniform throughout the humeral head. Biomechanical "mapping" studies demonstrated that the superior-anterior region possesses the lowest trabecular BMD and screw pullout strength, while the central, inferior, and posteromedial regions are the densest and most robust for fixation.Level V [19] This means screw position is a primary determinant of construct stability. [20]

C. The Coupled Failure Cascade

🔗 Varus Collapse Drives Screw Cut-Out

These two failure modes are not independent. Loss of reduction is "frequently associated with" secondary screw perforation. [24] The mechanical sequence:

  1. Fracture fixed but medial column remains unstable
  2. Cyclical loading initiates progressive failure → humeral head tilts into varus
  3. Screws locked to plate → entire fixed-angle construct rotates as single unit
  4. Superior-most screws driven through the superior humeral head, perforating the articular surface

This reveals the paradox of locking plate design: while engineered for osteoporotic bone, the rigid fixed-angle construct transfers 100% of micro-motion and failure load directly to the bone-screw interface. [17] In osteoporotic bone, the bone is the weak link—the rigid screws can "cheese-cut" through soft cancellous bone, explaining the gap between promising in vitro biomechanical results and high in vivo complication rates. [3]

📋 Pre-Operative CT Is Essential

Plain radiographs are insufficient to fully assess medial comminution. A CT scan is mandatory to determine medial support status and classify the fracture by its "medial stability."Level III [16] This pre-operative assessment dictates the entire surgical plan. If the calcar is comminuted, the surgeon must enter the OR with a definitive plan for medial augmentation or be prepared to pivot to arthroplasty.

III. Core Principles for Optimizing the Locking Plate Construct

A. Anatomic Reduction and Calcar Contact

Anatomic reduction is described as the "key to fixation success."Level III [9] AO Foundation principles emphasize achieving "perfect anatomical reduction" to restore the native neck-shaft angle and humeral head height before any implant is applied. If perfect reduction is not possible, a "slightly impacted but stable reduction" is acceptable, creating a bone-on-bone buttress that "auto-augments" the medial column. [38]

Techniques to hold reduction before plating include provisional K-wire fixation and intraosseous "tension band" sutures passed through the plate's suture eyelets. [9]

B. Plate Positioning Technical Pearls

  • Avoid Impingement: Position the plate 5–8 mm distal to the superior edge of the greater tuberosity.Level V [41]
  • Optimize Calcar Screw Trajectory: "Proximal" plate placement often leads to "missing" the calcar. "Distal" or "neutral" placement is biomechanically superior as it allows calcar screws to properly engage the inferomedial buttress.Level V [43]
  • Anatomic Landmarks: Plate should be lateral to the bicipital groove. [8] Pectoralis major insertion is a reference landmark for height. [10]

🔗 The Locked Triad

Reduction, Plate Position, and Screw Placement are interdependent. A compromise in one requires compensation in another. If calcar is comminuted and standard principles cannot compensate, the procedure will fail unless advanced augmentation strategies are employed.

IV. Advanced Augmentation Strategies

When pre-operative CT or intra-operative assessment reveals significant medial comminution, varus deformity, or severe osteoporosis, standard locking plate fixation is insufficient. Medial column augmentation is mandatory.

Strategy 1: Inferomedial "Calcar Screw" (Medial Support Screw – MSS)

The most basic augmentation: deliberate placement of one or more screws in the most inferomedial plate holes, directed to "catch" the calcar fragment and act as a fixed-angle buttress resisting varus collapse.Level III [8, 9]

Technique requirements: Place in the medial quarter of the humeral head, as close to inferior as possible, ideally within 12 mm of the calcar, purchasing both proximal (head) and distal (calcar) fragments. [14, 45] Clinical studies show calcar screws are associated with significantly less secondary loss of reduction [47] and lower failure risk. [7, 14] However, the supporting role of calcar screws alone is "limited" and may be insufficient with severe varus or significant medial comminution. [26]

Strategy 2: Endosteal Fibular Strut Allograft

For more significant structural deficits, a 5–7 cm fibular segment is placed into the intramedullary canal as a permanent endosteal strut.Level III [8]

Indications: Posteromedial calcar comminution, metadiaphyseal extension, severe osteoporosis, and nonunions. [13] The fibula acts as a true medial column replacement—providing solid internal buttress, improving construct stiffness, preventing varus collapse, and preventing tuberosity over-reduction. [8] Clinical studies confirm high union rates and satisfactory outcomes even in complex cases. [50]

Strategy 3: Cement Augmentation (PMMA & Calcium Phosphate)

Cement augmentation addresses two problems: (1) filling the metaphyseal void from comminution, and (2) improving screw purchase in soft, osteoporotic bone.Level V [17, 46]

Biomechanical studies consistently show void filling significantly enhances fixation stability, increases cycles to failure, and reduces risk of varus deformity and screw cut-out. [46] One study demonstrated that calcium phosphate cement augmentation significantly decreased intra-articular screw penetration.Level III [56]

Two primary techniques:

  • Manual Placement (Void Filling): 4–6 mL of medium-viscosity PMMA (partially cured 5–7 min) placed directly into the metaphyseal defect. [55]
  • Fenestrated Screws: Cement injected through specialized cannulated screws, creating a robust cement-bone composite around threads for "superior fixation." [60]

Intra-Operative Triage for Augmentation

🎯 Matching Strategy to the Problem
  • Minimal Instability (focal crack, good apposition): Medial Support Screws may suffice. [47]
  • Major Structural Defect (entire calcar/metaphysis comminuted): Fibular Strut allograft required. [49]
  • Poor Material Quality (soft, spongy bone): Cement Augmentation indicated. [56]

V. Comparative Effectiveness of Augmentation Strategies

Fibular Strut vs. Medial Support Screws (MSS)

A direct biomechanical comparison found the fibular graft construct to be significantly superior in all measured parameters: less displacement under cyclic loading (p=0.012), higher maximum failure load (p=0.012), and higher initial stiffness (p=0.001).Level V [64] Clinical evidence corroborates this: the fibular allograft group had better functional outcomes (CMS, ASES, DASH) and a significantly lower complication rate compared to plate alone.Level III [65]

Cement Augmentation vs. Structural Allografts

A finite element analysis found that a PHILOS locking plate combined with bone cement showed structural stability similar to the PHILOS plate combined with fibular allograft.Level V [66] The choice may depend on the type of defect (structural vs. material), surgeon preference, and allograft availability.

VI. The Tipping Point: When ORIF Fails and Arthroplasty Is Indicated

Contraindications for ORIF

  • Pre-existing pathology: Symptomatic glenohumeral osteoarthritis or advanced cuff tear arthropathy [67]
  • Non-reconstructible articular surface: Severe head-splitting fracture or impacted anatomic neck fracture with high AVN risk [8]
  • Patient factors: Advanced dementia, Parkinson's disease, very low-demand or nursing home–bound status [29, 30]

Primary Reverse Shoulder Arthroplasty (RSA)

RSA has emerged as the definitive alternative. Specific indications for primary RSA include: [34, 70]

  1. Displaced 3- and 4-part fractures in elderly patients (>65–70)
  2. Highly comminuted, non-reconstructible tuberosities
  3. Severe osteoporosis where screw purchase is impossible
  4. Pre-existing massive/irreparable rotator cuff tear
  5. Salvage of failed ORIF (malunion, nonunion, AVN)

📊 ORIF: High-Risk, High-Reward

ORIF offers the potential to preserve the native joint but carries a greater than 1-in-10 chance of requiring another major surgery at 10 years. RSA is the "safe and predictable" option with very high long-term implant survival (~93%) and very low reoperation rate (<4%). For complex 3- and 4-part fractures in the elderly, evidence increasingly favors RSA for its durability. [69]

VII. Post-Operative Protocols

The "Consensus Gap"

The most striking finding is the complete lack of scientific consensus or universally accepted, evidence-based rehabilitation guidelines. [86] Protocols are highly variable across institutions. [92] Sling durations range from one to six weeks. [93]

⚖ Tailoring Protocol to Construct Stability

The post-operative protocol must be tailored to intra-operative construct stability. An overly aggressive protocol on a weakly-fixed, non-augmented construct could precipitate fatigue failure and varus collapse. [1] An overly conservative protocol on a robustly-augmented construct leads to unnecessary stiffness. [96]

VIII. Evidence-Based Treatment Algorithm

The prevention of varus collapse and screw cut-out is a comprehensive strategy spanning pre-operative planning, meticulous surgical execution, and matched post-operative protocols.

Elderly (>65) with Osteoporotic PHF [29]

IF Low-demand / nursing home-bound OR medically unfit → Non-Operative (Conservative) Treatment (Goal: Pain control) [34]

IF Active / independent → Proceed to Step 3 (Goal: Restore function)

IF Non-reconstructible articular surface (head-split) OR pre-existing CTA/OA → Primary RSA [8]

IF Reconstructible → Proceed to Step 4

IF Medial Calcar INTACT (stable 2-part, valgus-impacted) → Standard ORIF (anatomic/impacted reduction, 5+ subchondral screws including calcar screws) [9]

IF Medial Calcar COMMINUTED (3/4-part, varus, medial hinge disruption) → Proceed to Step 5 [6]

IF Severe comminution (esp. tuberosities) AND/OR severe osteoporosis AND/OR patient >75–80 → Primary RSA (predictable outcome, superior durability, lowest reoperation rate) [32, 69]

IF Moderate comminution AND patient desires native joint AND acceptable bone quality → Proceed to Step 6

IF Problem is poor bone quality / metaphyseal void → Plate + Cement Augmentation [55]

IF Problem is major structural defect (calcar/metaphyseal comminution) → Plate + Endosteal Fibular Strut (+ MSS) [49]
