Intramedullary Augmentation in Osteoporotic Proximal Humerus Fractures: PMMA Rod Fabrication, Technique & Comparative Efficacy

Comprehensive evidence-based educational review — adapted from provided literature

1

Introduction: The Osteoporotic Conundrum

Displaced proximal humerus fractures (PHFs) in the elderly represent one of the most persistent challenges in orthopedic trauma surgery. The proximal humerus is the third most common site of osteoporotic fracture, trailing only the proximal femur and distal radius. LOE I

Approximately 80% of PHFs are nondisplaced or minimally displaced and can be managed conservatively. However, the remaining 20% involve complex, unstable 3- and 4-part fracture patterns that demand surgical intervention to restore function and mitigate pain.

The osteoporotic humeral head has been described as an "eggshell" — a fragile cortical shell encasing sparse, structurally compromised cancellous bone. LOE III

The Locking Plate Revolution and Its Limitations

Locking compression plate (LCP) technology, introduced in the early 2000s, theoretically offered "toggle-free" fixation for osteoporotic bone by deriving stability from the screw-plate interface. However, clinical experience over two decades has tempered this optimism:

  • Complication rates remain unacceptably high: ranging from 16% to nearly 50% in various series. LOE I
  • Prevailing failure modes: secondary varus collapse, screw cut-out, and intra-articular screw penetration. LOE II
⚠ Critical Concept: The Medial Hinge

When the medial cortex is comminuted or disrupted, the lateral locking plate functions as a cantilever beam, subjecting the bone-implant interface to high bending moments during shoulder abduction and rotation. In the absence of a medial buttress, cyclic loading leads to micromotion, screw loosening, and ultimately, varus collapse of the humeral head. LOE II

2

Pathomechanics of Failure & Rationale for Augmentation

2.1 Mechanics of Varus Collapse

In a standard locking plate construct with medial comminution, the center of rotation of the humeral head lies medial to the plate. Axial loads generate a varus moment arm. In osteoporotic bone, the cancellous bone offers minimal resistance, and the screws effectively "windshield wiper" within the soft bone of the head, expanding screw tracks until the hold is lost. LOE I

2.2 Principle of Medial Column Reconstruction

A structural strut placed in the intramedullary canal bridges the fracture gap and physically transmits compressive loads from the humeral head directly to the diaphyseal shaft. This load-sharing mechanism has two profound effects:

  • Stress shielding of the implant: FEA demonstrates that intramedullary struts significantly reduce von Mises stress on the locking plate and proximal screws. LOE II
  • Enhanced screw anchorage: Screws traverse plate → lateral cortex → dense strut → potentially medial cortex, creating "quadricortical" fixation that drastically increases pullout strength and resists toggling. LOE II

2.3 Void Filling vs. Structural Strutting

📋 Key Distinction

Void filling (e.g., calcium phosphate cement injection) provides compressive resistance but lacks bending stiffness. A PMMA rod or fibular allograft acts as a true structural member, providing resistance to both axial compression and bending moments. LOE III

Unlike calcium phosphate cements (brittle, primarily osteoconductive), PMMA is mechanically robust in compression and can be reinforced with a metal core to improve its bending modulus, making it a suitable mechanical substitute for the cortical bone of a fibular allograft. LOE III

Detailed PMMA Rod Fabrication Methodologies

Unlike fibular allografts (finished tissue-bank products), the PMMA bone cement rod is a surgeon-fabricated device. The literature describes several methods, primarily adapted from antibiotic-loaded cement nail techniques for long bone osteomyelitis.

3.1 The "Chest Tube" Molding Technique (Gold Standard)

This is the most versatile, widely cited, and reliable method for creating uniform, cylindrical PMMA rods. LOE IV

Essential Materials

  • PMMA Bone Cement: High-viscosity cement (e.g., Palacos R+G, Simplex P). The "R+G" denotes inclusion of Gentamicin for prophylactic benefit. LOE IV
  • Thoracic Catheter (Chest Tube): Range of 24–40 Fr. Diameter (mm) = Fr ÷ 3.
  • Reinforcement Core: 2.0–3.0 mm K-wire, Steinmann pin, or flexible reamer guide wire to increase bending stiffness and facilitate retrieval. LOE IV
  • Sterile Lubricant: Sterile mineral oil is the gold standard. LOE IV
  • Cement Gun/Syringe: Pressurized delivery to eliminate air voids.

Step-by-Step Protocol

  1. Pre-operative Sizing: Analyze preoperative X-rays or CT of the contralateral humerus to estimate isthmus diameter. Confirm intraoperatively with flexible reamers. Example: canal diameter 10mm → 30 Fr chest tube; 12mm → 36 Fr tube. LOE IV
  2. Mold Preparation: Liberally lubricate the chest tube lumen with sterile mineral oil. Critical: without lubrication, PMMA will bond to the silicone/PVC, making removal difficult and damaging the rod surface. LOE IV
  3. Cement Mixing & Antibiotic Loading: Mix polymer and monomer. If additional antibiotics desired (e.g., Vancomycin), add powder to cement powder before monomer. Up to 2g antibiotic per 40g cement is considered safe for mechanical integrity. Vacuum mixing recommended to reduce porosity and increase fatigue life. LOE IV
  4. Injection & Core Placement: Transfer cement to gun in liquid/early dough phase. Inject into tube, withdrawing gun slowly to prevent air entrapment. Immediately insert metal core centrally. Critical: One end should be bent into a hook or loop — this "retrieval eyelet" is essential for potential future removal. LOE IV
  5. Ex Vivo Curing: Allow to cure on the back table. PMMA polymerization is exothermic (can exceed 80°C). Curing ex vivo completely eliminates the risk of thermal necrosis to the endosteum and surrounding soft tissues — a significant safety advantage over in situ techniques. LOE III
  6. Demolding & Finishing: After full hardening (~10–15 min), slice chest tube longitudinally. Inspect rod for sharp ridges, flash, or irregularities. Trim with rongeur/rasp to ensure smooth surface. LOE IV
⚠ Safety Note: Ex Vivo Curing

The ex vivo fabrication approach is a mandatory safety requirement. In situ curing carries unacceptable risk of thermal necrosis and is not recommended in modern practice.

3.2 The "Syringe" Molding Technique

For very narrow canals or resource-limited settings: a 10–20 ml syringe (nozzle cut off) can serve as a mold. The plunger can compress cement to reduce voids. Limitation: produces rods of fixed diameter and limited length. LOE IV

3.3 The "In Situ" or "Blob" Technique (Void Filling)

Involves injecting high-viscosity cement directly into the metaphyseal void after reduction (allowed to cure to "dough phase"). Risks: thermal injury, extravasation, and less protection against diaphyseal toggle compared to a long rod. LOE III

3.4 3D-Printed Custom Molds

Emerging technology: preoperative CT → 3D model of canal → sterilized 3D-printed negative mold → intraoperative casting of a patient-specific spacer. LOE V

  • Advantage: Perfect fit, maximal contact area.
  • Disadvantage: Requires lead time, engineering support, and cost. Currently less applicable to acute trauma than the chest tube method.

Surgical Technique: Implantation & Fixation

4.1 Approach & Exposure

Standard deltopectoral approach. Heavy non-absorbable sutures (#2 or #5 Ethibond) placed in tendon-bone interface of supraspinatus, infraspinatus, and subscapularis to control tuberosity fragments. LOE IV

4.2 Canal Preparation & Strut Insertion

  • Canal Access: Avoid aggressive reaming to prevent thinning osteoporotic cortex. Use a sound or blunt reamer.
  • Insertion: Prefabricated rod inserted manually.
  • Positioning: Proximal end should sit just below the cartilage, supporting the inferomedial quadrant (calcar). Too high blocks reduction; too low fails to support the head. LOE II
  • Reduction: Head fragment reduced onto the rod, which acts as a "dowel" preventing varus tipping. Tuberosities reduced and secured with tag sutures.

4.3 Plate Application & Screw Fixation

  • Plate Position: 5–8 mm distal to the superior tip of the greater tuberosity to avoid subacromial impingement. LOE I
  • Drilling Through PMMA: Requires sharp drill bit and firm pressure. The surgeon will feel distinct resistance upon entering cement. Ensure the drill does not "skive" off the rod's curved surface.
  • Screw Insertion: Locking screws cut threads into the PMMA, creating a "cement-screw" mechanical interlock significantly stronger than bone-screw interface in osteoporotic bone. The screw is anchored at the plate (lateral) and rod (central), creating a rigid beam construct with very short working length. LOE II
  • Calcar Screws: Inferomedial screws should pass immediately inferior to or through the inferior portion of the rod to buttress the medial calcar. Biomechanical studies confirm oblique insertion maximizes resistance to varus forces. LOE II

Biomechanical Evidence: PMMA vs. Plating Alone

5.1 Stiffness & Stability

Biomechanical studies using synthetic osteoporotic bone models demonstrate that intramedullary augmentation significantly enhances construct stability: LOE II

  • Intercyclic Motion: PMMA strut reduced intercyclic motion significantly up to 10,000 cycles (p < 0.001).
  • Gap Deformation: Residual deformation of fracture gaps (simulating comminution) was significantly lower in the augmented group.

5.2 Finite Element Analysis (FEA) Findings

In standard plating, high stress concentrations occur at the screw-bone interface and plate holes — areas prone to fatigue failure. LOE II

  • Stress Redistribution: The strut absorbs compressive loads, relieving the plate. Oblique insertion of the strut may offer advantages in resisting traction forces.
  • PMMA vs. Fibula (FEA): Both provide similar biomechanical stability, reducing von Mises stress on humerus and locking plate under axial, oblique, and torsional loading. LOE II

5.3 Screw Anchorage Comparison

🔵 PMMA Rod

Screw threads cut into cement creating a mechanical interlock — immediate and rigid. However, if cement cracks or interface debonds, fixation is lost instantly.

🟢 Fibular Allograft

Screw threads purchase cortical bone. Also rigid, but fibula has potential for biological incorporation (creeping substitution), theoretically enhancing long-term durability.

6

Comparative Efficacy: PMMA Rod vs. Fibular Allograft

6.1 Clinical Outcomes & Functional Scores

Fibular Allograft (FA)

  • ASES Scores: Major meta-analysis demonstrated statistically significant improvement with FA vs. plating alone (Weighted Mean Difference = 5.08, p < 0.00001). LOE I
  • DASH & ROM: Significant improvements in DASH scores and range of motion (abduction/external rotation). LOE II
  • Radiographic Maintenance: Definitively proven to maintain Humeral Head Height (HHH) and Neck-Shaft Angle (NSA) significantly better than plating alone. LOE I

PMMA Rod

  • Early Results: One study reported improved DASH and Constant Scores with cement augmentation vs. bone grafting at 3 and 6 months (p ≤ 0.007). LOE III
  • At 12 Months: Broader meta-analysis found no significant difference in functional scores or quality of life between cement-augmented and non-augmented groups. LOE I
  • vs. Bone Grafts: Cement augmentation was noted as "possibly inferior to bone grafts" regarding pain relief and functional improvement, despite comparable radiographic outcomes. LOE I
📊 Evidence Interpretation

While PMMA prevents radiographic collapse (mechanical success), this does not always translate to better patient-perceived functional outcomes at 12 months. This may relate to the biological inertness of cement and its alteration of humeral modulus.

Discussion: Synthesizing the Evidence

The literature reveals a clear dichotomy in management of osteoporotic PHFs:

🟢 Fibular Allograft — The Biological Ideal

Reconstructs anatomy with "like" material. Offers incorporation potential. Superior functional scores in large meta-analyses. Preferred for: active elderly patients with reasonable life expectancy seeking a "living" construct that remodels over time.

🔵 PMMA Rod — The Pragmatic Solution

A "bailout" or "utility" option. Available at 2 AM in a rural hospital. Inexpensive. Biomechanically comparable to fibula for preventing varus collapse short-term. However, it is biologically dead, will never heal, and creates a permanent stress riser.

The "Ideal" PMMA Patient

  • Low-demand, low-mobility patients: Goal is pain relief and fracture union rather than maximal ROM.
  • Pathologic fractures: Where bone stock is compromised by tumor/osteolysis and biological graft healing is unlikely. LOE IV
  • Resource-limited settings: Where cost or availability of allografts precludes their use.
  • High infection risk: PMMA can be loaded with high-dose antibiotics (Vancomycin, Tobramycin) for dual function as structural support and local antibiotic delivery — superior to fibula in open fractures or infected non-union revisions. LOE IV
✅ The Fabrication Imperative

The success of the PMMA technique is heavily dependent on fabrication method. The Chest Tube Technique is not merely a suggestion but a requirement for safety. The risks of in situ curing (thermal necrosis, extravasation) are too high to justify the "blob" technique in modern practice. A central metal core with retrieval eyelet is a mandatory safety step. LOE IV
