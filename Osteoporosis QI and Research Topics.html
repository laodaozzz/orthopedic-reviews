Part 1: The Critical Care Gap — Defining the Opportunity for Improvement

1.1 Quantifying the Osteoporosis "Treatment Gap"

Osteoporosis is a widespread, largely preventable systemic disorder characterized by progressive bone loss and microarchitectural deterioration, predisposing patients to fragility fractures. The clinical and economic burden is staggering: approximately 9 million Americans suffer from osteoporosis, with an additional 48 million having low bone mass.Ref 1 Worldwide, one in three women and one in five men over age 50 will experience an osteoporotic fracture in their lifetime.Ref 2 The annual cost of treating these fractures equals or exceeds that of myocardial infarction, stroke, or breast cancer.Ref 2

⚠ The Treatment Gap Is Widening An analysis of postmenopausal women in Europe at high fracture risk found that the average treatment gap was 71% in 2019, a significant increase from 55% in 2010. This translates to a burden of 14.0 million untreated, eligible women in 2019, up from 10.6 million in 2010.Ref 6

The gap is most egregious in secondary fracture prevention. A fragility fracture is a sentinel event, yet it rarely triggers an appropriate clinical response. National data consistently show that fewer than one-third of patients who sustain a fragility fracture are subsequently evaluated and treated for osteoporosis;Ref 1 some studies place this figure even lower, at less than 20%.Ref 3 These post-fracture patients face the highest risk for subsequent fractures, which carry devastating increases in morbidity, disability, and mortality.Ref 3

1.2 Diagnosing the Gap (Part A): Physician-Level Barriers and "Clinical Inertia"

The evidence overwhelmingly suggests the primary driver is not a lack of diagnostic tools or effective medications, but rather a profound "clinical inertia" — defined as the "recognition of the problem, but failure to act."Ref 8

The problem is not failure of diagnosis; it is failure of therapeutic initiation. This "therapeutic inertia" is the most important factor contributing to low treatment rates.Ref 9

A nationwide survey of 359 physicians (endocrinologists, PCPs, and geriatricians) quantified the top-rated barriers to osteoporosis screening:Ref 10

  1. Patient nonadherence (63% of physicians)
  2. Physician concern about cost (56%)
  3. Clinic visit time constraints (51%)
  4. Screening being "low on the priority list" (45%)
  5. Patient concern about cost (43%)

Notably, physicians with more patient-facing time (3–5 days/week) were more likely to place osteoporosis screening "low on the priority list."Ref 10 This strongly suggests osteoporosis is a victim of the "tyranny of the urgent" — in a 15-minute primary care visit, bone health is consciously deprioritized in favor of more visible conditions like diabetes, hypertension, or cardiovascular disease.

1.3 Diagnosing the Gap (Part B): System- and Patient-Level Barriers

Patient-Level Barriers

  • Lack of Awareness and Risk Perception: Many patients lack fundamental awareness of osteoporosis as a serious disease.Ref 11 A qualitative study found patients were often "overly-confident" and did not realize their personal risk.Ref 12
  • Economic and Safety Concerns: Cost is a major barrier for both patients and physicians.Ref 10 Concerns about rare side effects (atypical femur fractures, osteonecrosis of the jaw) are significant deterrents.Ref 11

System-Level Barriers

  • Care Fragmentation: There is a critical lack of communication between hospital-based teams (orthopaedics, ED) and primary care.Ref 3 Orthopedic surgeons consistently state post-fracture osteoporosis management should "rest with the PCP,"Ref 13 yet this handoff consistently fails.
  • Access and Guideline Disparity: Lack of access to BMD testing in some areas,Ref 11 compounded by varying screening recommendations across organizations.Ref 16

1.4 Implications for Project Design

Successful QI projects cannot be limited to "educating" physicians or adding EMR alerts. They must systematically dismantle specific barriers by:Source synthesis

  1. Reducing the Friction of Acting: Automate processes, use EMR templates, and leverage non-physician team members.
  2. Creating Accountability: Move osteoporosis from "low priority" to a tracked, managed condition with performance dashboards.
  3. Addressing Downstream Patient Factors: Couple provider-facing interventions with patient-facing solutions (adherence monitoring, education, cost support).

Part 2: Foundational QI Projects — Systematizing Screening & Risk Assessment

2.1 Using the EMR as an Active Intervention Tool

The most feasible and scalable QI projects leverage the Electronic Medical Record to transform it from a passive documentation tool into an active engine for population health. These projects directly address "low priority" and "time constraint" barriers by automating identification, standardizing workflows, and shifting tasks to the broader care team.

2.2 Level 1: EMR-Based Identification and Provider Nudging

Case Study: Denver Health — Aromatase Inhibitor / ADT Cohort Ref 20

Problem: Only 31.2% of patients on aromatase inhibitors (AI) or androgen deprivation therapy (ADT) received a recommended baseline DXA scan.

Intervention: EMR-based alert (EPIC) reminding any provider ordering AI/ADT that a DXA scan should be performed, supported by education for pharmacists, nurses, and PCPs.

Outcome: DXA scan rates increased from 31.2% (baseline) to 44.2% (2020), 59.4% (2021), and 54.1% (2022) — immediate and sustained improvement.

This model is highly feasible as it leverages existing EMR alert functionality. It could be adapted for patients initiating long-term glucocorticoid therapy or those with new fragility fracture ICD-10 codes.

2.3 Level 2: Workflow Integration of Fracture Risk Assessment Tools (FRAX/SCORE)

Case Study: PDSA-Driven FRAX Implementation Ref 21

Problem: A low-income primary care clinic had no standardized fracture risk assessment tool, leading to inconsistent DXA referrals.

Intervention: Formal Plan-Do-Study-Act (PDSA) cycle to implement the FRAX tool, including pre-implementation staff training (improved knowledge by 25.8%) and standardized clinical processes.

Outcome: 96.3% FRAX adherence among 162 eligible women; 98.6% of DXA referrals were completed for the 71 women identified as high-risk.

A similar project used the SCORE (Simple Calculated Osteoporosis Risk Estimation) tool, where a score ≥6 automatically indicated DXA referral, also resulting in a statistically significant increase in screening.Ref 22

2.4 Level 3: Proactive Population Health Management (EMR Recall Campaigns)

Case Study: EMR-Based Osteoporosis Recall Campaign Ref 24

Methodology:

  1. EMR Query: Identified target population (N=394) aged 65–80, active patients, no BMD on file.
  2. Proactive Outreach: Mailed invitation packets with risk questionnaire and educational brochures.
  3. Standardized Visit: Special "EMR osteoporosis visit template" for 10–15 min recall appointments using FRAX tool to stratify into low/medium/high risk.
  4. Action: Medium/high-risk patients recommended for BMD scans and follow-up visits.

Outcome: Of 394 patients recalled, 55 came in (14% recall rate). Of these, 40% were medium or high-risk — patients completely lost to follow-up were successfully activated into a structured care pathway.

⚠ Critical Insight: The EMR Tool Alone Is Not Enough A contrasting study implementing an EMR dashboard with embedded risk calculators for 84 physicians found that physicians still failed to calculate fracture risk in 71% of patients with completed BMD and failed to treat 37% of high-risk patients.Ref 14 The successful FRAX projectRef 21 was a PDSA-guided QI project with training, workflow redesign, and standardized processes — not a passive tool deployment. The real QI project is building the socio-technical system around the tool.

Part 3: High-Impact System Redesign — Fracture Liaison Service (FLS) Models

3.1 The Clinical Imperative: FLS as the Gold Standard

The single most effective, evidence-based, and widely endorsed solution for secondary fracture prevention is the Fracture Liaison Service (FLS).Ref 29 An FLS is a systems-level, multidisciplinary program coordinated, typically by a nurse, to "systematically identify, assess, treat, and monitor" patients aged 50+ who have sustained a fragility fracture.Ref 7

Overwhelming Evidence for FLS
  • Significantly reduces treatment gap; increases treatment initiation and adherenceRef 30
  • Highly cost-effective in any healthcare setting (ICER: €409 per QALY gained)Ref 31
  • Reduces hospital bed days, surgeries, and need for institutional careRef 7
  • Proven to reduce subsequent fractures and decrease mortalityRef 7

3.2 FLS Models: Adapting for Community and "Open" Settings

There are four major types of FLS models defined by the level of service provided:Ref 2

FLS in "Open" Community Settings

Most community hospitals operate in an "open" system with disconnected EMRs. A QI demonstration project found a successful FLS is feasible with two key adaptations:Ref 33

  1. A Non-Physician Champion: Led by an NP or orthopedic nurse serving as FLS coordinator.
  2. An Independent Tracking Tool: A cloud-based FLS application independent of the hospital EMR served as the central nervous system, allowing the coordinator to track all patients and manage follow-up tasks.

3.3 Implementation Blueprints

Blueprint 1: Stakeholder-Driven Model Ref 34

  1. Identify FLS Champion (~50% ortho surgeons, ~50% specialists)
  2. Create Multi-Disciplinary Stakeholder Group (ortho, geriatrics, PCP, radiology, pharmacy, administrators)
  3. Determine Baseline Performance (chart audit or quality measures)
  4. Plan Communication & Long-Term Management (model selection, roles, monitoring responsibility)

Blueprint 2: "FLS-Lite" Model Ref 23

  1. Patient ID: Clinical Specialist runs monthly EMR query for ICD-10 codes (fragility fractures in patients 50+)
  2. Inpatient Action: "Bone Health Checklist" ensures foundational care before dischargeRef 49
  3. Transitional Care: Specialist contacts patient within 72 hours of discharge by telephone, educates on risk, and facilitates PCP follow-up
  4. PCP Handoff: Structured, closed-loop referral that is tracked
⚠ The Critical "Symbiosis" Problem A Type B or C FLS that simply "informs" the PCP is destined to fail. It sends high-risk patients back to the very primary care environment already suffering from clinical inertia.Ref 9 A successful community FLS must be symbiotic — it must be a transitional care program linking hospital identification (Part 3) with primary care workflow optimization (Part 2).

3.4 The Business and Quality Case

Quality Metrics

  • HEDIS Measure: "Osteoporosis Management in Women Who Had a Fracture" — tracks women 67–85 receiving DXA or pharmacotherapy within 6 months of fracture.Ref 35, 36
  • Medicare Advantage: FLS implementation directly improves health plan quality ratings tied to reimbursement.Ref 37

Cost-Effectiveness

  • FLS programs are "highly cost-effective" compared to usual care regardless of intensity or setting.Ref 31
  • A simulation of 1,000,000 patients found FLS would avoid 53,090 lifetime subsequent fractures.Ref 38
  • Avoided fractures translate directly to reduced hospital bed days, fewer surgeries, and less institutional care.Ref 7

Poor adherence to osteoporosis medication is a "major problem" and the physician's #1 reported barrier to screening.Ref 10

Physician-reported barrier: "Patient nonadherence" (Ref 10)

The most significant factors adverse to adherence are aging, polypharmacy, and smoking habits.Ref 17 Complex dosing requirements (e.g., oral bisphosphonates on empty stomach, remaining upright) create high barriers for patients unable to follow directions.Ref 39

4.2 Interventions to Improve Adherence

A systematic review of 15 studies (12 RCTs) found that multicomponent interventions based on patient education and counseling were the most effective at increasing adherence.Ref 40 Other effective strategies include:

  • Monitoring and follow-up via telephone calls or emailRef 17
  • Proactive care interventions (medication monitoring, decision aids)Ref 17
  • Simple, mailed patient reminders with "motivational messaging" and provider signatureRef 41

4.3 Rethinking Patient Education and Fall Prevention

A systematic review of group-based, face-to-face patient education found "inconclusive" results for most outcomes except physical function.Ref 43 This implies that active educational components (strength training, exercise, home safety modifications) are effective, while passive information-dumping is not.Ref 44

In-Hospital Fall Prevention: A QI Goldmine Ref 46

A cross-sectional study of 60 hospital units revealed massive variation in fall prevention practices:

  • Low-resource practices (bed locked/lowered, nonskid socks): Used consistently (72–73%)
  • Resource-intensive practices (scheduled toileting): Used very inconsistently (only 15%)
  • Interdisciplinary practices (post-fall huddles): Very limited (only 12%)

This provides a clear, actionable target for a hospital-based QI project.

4.4 "Low-Hanging Fruit" QI Projects

Case Study 1: Improving Vitamin D & Calcium Supplementation Ref 47

In nursing homes, remarkably simple interventions — an information sheet for nurses and adding the topic to admission meetings — doubled the rate of adequate supplementation from 32% to 65%.

Case Study 2: The Orthopaedic Care Checklist Ref 49

A single-page checklist for bone protection in orthopaedic wards achieved:

  • +37% increase in consultant-led osteoporosis plans
  • +20% enhanced bone-sparing treatment administration
  • Eliminated unsafe prescriptions
  • +27% improved discharge information communication
  • Staff survey: "well-received, easy-to-use, and educational"
Strategic Value of "Low-Hanging Fruit" These simple projects are ideal cultural primers for larger change. They force interdisciplinary coordination, educate staff, and prove the value of standardized processes. An organization that has successfully implemented a post-fracture checklist is infinitely more prepared to launch a post-fracture liaison service.

Part 5: Project Frameworks & Recommendations for Implementation

5.2 Project Charter 1: EMR-Driven Population Health (Primary Care)

Proactive Osteoporosis Prevention Program (POPP)

Objective: Close the primary prevention and screening gap by leveraging the EMR and care team to systematically identify, risk-stratify, and initiate care for all eligible at-risk patients.Ref 23

Target Population: Women ≥65, Men ≥70; high-risk cohorts (AI/ADT,Ref 20 glucocorticoids,Ref 4 postmenopausal women 50–64 with risk factorsRef 21)

Phased Implementation:

  1. Phase 1 (Identify): Build EMR patient registry; perform gap analysis (no DXA on file)Ref 24
  2. Phase 2 (Stratify): Opportunistic visits → FRAX tool in rooming processRef 21; Gap patients → automated EMR data risk stratificationRef 25
  3. Phase 3 (Act): Panel management recall campaign with dedicated template-driven visitsRef 24

Rationale: Directly tackles "time constraints" and "low prioritization"Ref 10 by shifting identification burden from physician to care team and EMR.

5.3 Project Charter 2: "FLS-Lite" Symbiotic Model (Community Hospital)

Fracture Accountability and Coordination Team (FACT)

Objective: Close the secondary prevention gap with a feasible hospital-based identification system forming a robust, closed-loop referral with primary care.Ref 3

Target: All patients ≥50 admitted to ED/inpatient with fragility fracture (ICD-10).Ref 23

Methodology:

  1. Identify: Clinical Specialist runs weekly/monthly EMR queryRef 23
  2. Inpatient Action: "Bone Health Checklist" in chart before dischargeRef 49
  3. Transitional Care: Phone contact within 72 hours of dischargeRef 23
  4. PCP Handoff: Structured, closed-loop referral tracked to completion

Rationale: Combines the feasible FLS-Lite model with the proven checklist and active transitional care to manage the handoff rather than just execute it.

5.4 Research Study: Adherence Intervention Trial

Pragmatic RCT: Pharmacist-Led Adherence Intervention

Research Question: In primary care patients newly initiated on oral bisphosphonates, does a multi-component adherence intervention (education + pharmacist monitoring) improve medication persistence at 1 year vs. standard care?

Intervention Group:

  1. Standardized "motivational" education at time of prescribingRef 41
  2. Pharmacist-led follow-up calls at 1, 3, and 6 monthsRef 17

Primary Outcome: Persistence (no refill gap >90 days)Ref 19 or Medication Possession Ratio (MPR)Ref 42 at 12 months.

Rationale: If this low-cost intervention significantly improves adherence, it directly solves the #1 problem causing physician inertia,Ref 9 justifying health system investment in such services.

5.5 Final Conclusion

From Fragmented Care to Integrated Bone Health Systems

The failure to prevent and treat osteoporosis is not due to a lack of effective treatments, but to a systemic breakdown rooted in clinical inertia and fragmentation.Ref 3

The solutions are deeply interconnected: an FLS-Lite will only succeed if primary care is optimized to receive patients. A PCP's willingness to initiate treatment is directly related to confidence in patient adherence.

Recommendation: Leaders should adopt a "start simple" approach. A one-page orthopaedic checklist or nurse-driven Vitamin D protocol builds interdisciplinary teams, demonstrates QI value, and establishes a baseline culture of bone health accountability.Ref 49 These "easy wins" are the foundation upon which larger, transformative systems of care can be built.
