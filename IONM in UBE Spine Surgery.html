1. The Evolving Paradigm of Minimally Invasive Spine Surgery

The surgical management of degenerative, traumatic, and neoplastic spinal pathology has undergone a profound evolution over the last two decades, transitioning from traditional open, highly tissue-disruptive procedures to sophisticated, target-specific minimally invasive techniques. Within this rapidly expanding modern armamentarium, Unilateral Biportal Endoscopy (UBE), frequently referred to synonymously as Biportal Endoscopic Spine Surgery (BESS), has emerged as a highly efficacious, transformative modality for the treatment of a wide spectrum of spinal diseases.[1]

Utilizing two distinct, tightly localized percutaneous portals—one dedicated strictly to high-definition endoscopic visualization and the other serving as an independent working channel for surgical instrumentation—UBE affords spine surgeons unparalleled optical magnification, enhanced multi-directional maneuverability, and the distinct ability to utilize standard, familiar spine instruments without the rigid geometrical constraints inherently associated with single-portal tubular microendoscopy.[2]

However, the defining biophysical characteristic of the UBE technique is its absolute reliance on a continuous, pressurized saline irrigation environment. This dynamic aqueous medium is fundamentally critical for maintaining a pristine optical field, continuously clearing osseous debris and hemorrhage, and providing a hydrostatic tamponade effect against epidural venous bleeding.[4] While the minimally invasive advantages of UBE are extensively documented, the introduction of high-flow fluid dynamics and aggressive radiofrequency (RF) plasma ablation within the rigidly confined epidural space introduces unique, procedure-specific iatrogenic risks. These specific risks include:

  • Irrigation fluid-induced epidural hypertension[5]
  • Insidious thermal injury to adjacent neural elements[5]
  • Iatrogenic mechanical trauma from manipulation of bulky instruments within a highly magnified but spatially restricted neuroforaminal corridor[5]

To systematically mitigate these distinct operative risks, Intraoperative Neurophysiological Monitoring (IONM) has been increasingly integrated into the standard UBE surgical workflow.[9] IONM serves as an indispensable, real-time diagnostic adjunct, offering immediate neurophysiological feedback regarding the functional integrity of the descending corticospinal tracts, the ascending dorsal column–medial lemniscus pathways, and the traversing and exiting spinal nerve roots.

Yet the direct clinical translation of conventional open-spine IONM protocols into the unique water-based environment of UBE presents complex, multi-factorial biophysical and clinical challenges. The application of standard electrical stimulation modalities is fundamentally altered by the high conductive properties of normal saline. Furthermore, the EBM literature surrounding the routine use of IONM in UBE remains highly fragmented, relying heavily on lower-tier retrospective cohort analyses rather than definitive, well-powered prospective randomized controlled trials.[12]

This report provides a deep, multi-dimensional literature review synthesizing the latest clinical studies, systematic reviews, and meta-analyses from high-impact publications (2021–2026), explicitly including The Spine Journal, European Spine Journal, and Journal of Neurosurgery: Spine.[43,51]

2. Neurophysiological Modality Utility in a Continuous Saline Environment

The foundational and most formidable biophysical challenge of utilizing IONM during UBE lies in the complex physics of electrical conduction within an actively flowing aqueous medium. Standard open or minimally invasive tubular spine surgery relies on the relatively high electrical impedance of ambient room air, retractors, and exposed osseous or muscular tissue. In contrast, the entirety of a UBE procedure is performed submerged in a continuous, high-turnover flow of 0.9% normal saline.[4]

2.1 The Biophysics of the Current Shunting Effect

Normal saline (0.9% NaCl) is a highly conductive electrolytic solution, possessing an electrical impedance that is magnitudes lower than that of dense cortical bone, ligamentum flavum, or the myelin sheaths of spinal nerve roots. According to fundamental principles of electrical physics (Ohm's Law), when an electrical stimulus is applied to a targeted tissue bed submerged in saline, the injected current inherently follows the path of least resistance. Instead of penetrating the relatively higher-impedance perineurium and heavily myelinated sheaths of a traversing spinal nerve root, a substantial fraction of the delivered current rapidly dissipates spherically into the surrounding fluid medium. This biophysical phenomenon is known clinically as the "current shunting effect."[14]

⚠️ Clinical Consequence
The current shunting effect severely compromises the baseline reliability, sensitivity, and specificity of all stimulation-based IONM modalities in endoscopic environments. It directly leads to inadequate delivery of stimulus current to target nerve tissue, resulting in highly dangerous false-negative findings. A surgeon may aggressively probe a tissue mass or pedicle wall, receive no EMG response, and falsely conclude that no neural structures are in proximity.[14] Conversely, if stimulation intensity is increased to non-physiological levels (>20 mA) to overcome the shunting, the current may spread extensively through the fluid, causing false-positive results.[17]

2.2 Triggered Electromyography (t-EMG) and Direct Nerve Stimulation Constraints

Triggered EMG (t-EMG) is a highly utilized, active stimulation modality traditionally employed to map neural structures, identify nerve root locations in epidural fibrosis, and verify cortical bone integrity during pedicle screw preparation (critical in UBE-TLIF). In UBE, t-EMG utility is heavily compromised by the current shunting effect.[14] A frank medial or inferior pedicle cortical breach—which would typically yield a low stimulation threshold (e.g., <10 mA) in an open environment—might not elicit a CMAP response until much higher, potentially neurotoxic currents are applied.

Mitigating Strategies for t-EMG in UBE

1
Transient Fluid Evacuation ("Dry Field" Technique): Temporarily halt the irrigation pump and actively aspirate fluid from the working field prior to stimulation, drastically increasing local impedance.[21]
2
Monopolar over Bipolar Probe: Monopolar stimulation is preferred in fluid environments. Bipolar probes suffer immediate short-circuiting through saline between the prongs. Monopolar probes use a distant return electrode, reducing localized shunting.[18]
3
Hyper-Insulated Instrumentation: Specialized endoscopic probes with only the microscopic distal tip exposed concentrate current density, partially overcoming the lower resistance of surrounding fluid.[22]
4
Irrigant Osmolarity Manipulation: Experimental models suggest temporary use of lower osmolar, non-electrolytic fluids (e.g., half-normal saline) to reduce medium conductivity. However, 0.9% normal saline remains universal standard due to physiological compatibility.[23]

2.3 Free-Running EMG (s-EMG): The Endoscopic Gold Standard

Unlike t-EMG, free-running or spontaneous EMG (s-EMG) is a passive modality that does not rely on exogenous electrical stimulation. It continuously monitors multiple target muscle groups for spontaneous motor unit action potentials (MUAPs) triggered by external mechanical irritation, abrupt traction, or thermal stress on the corresponding spinal nerve root.[10]

Because s-EMG operates strictly as a passive recording modality, it is completely immune to the current shunting effect. In the contemporary EBM literature, s-EMG is consistently highlighted as the most robust, sensitive, and clinically reliable modality for real-time detection of iatrogenic irritation during UBE decompression procedures.[10] When a surgical instrument mechanically agitates or compresses the nerve root, or when an RF probe elevates local epidural temperature to neurotoxic levels, the s-EMG system immediately registers high-frequency neurotonic discharges (complex "A-trains" or sustained burst activities). This instantaneous biofeedback allows the surgeon to immediately freeze the offending maneuver before permanent axonal injury can occur.[10]

2.4 Motor and Somatosensory Evoked Potentials (MEPs and SSEPs)

MEPs involve transcranial electrical stimulation of the motor cortex via scalp electrodes, with resulting compound muscle action potentials recorded via subdermal needle electrodes in peripheral target musculature. SSEPs involve peripheral electrical stimulation of major mixed nerves (e.g., posterior tibial or ulnar nerves), with cortical responses recorded via scalp electrodes.[21] Because both stimulation and recording sites are located far outside the localized surgical field, MEPs and SSEPs are completely unaffected by the epidural saline environment and associated shunting effects.

During UBE, particularly in complex supra-lumbar applications, continuous MEPs and SSEPs are paramount for detecting global or regional ischemic events within the spinal cord parenchyma—specifically critical during cervical and thoracic UBE where the spinal cord is vulnerable to irrigation fluid-induced hydrostatic pressure spikes.[6] Significant, reproducible decrements in MEP/SSEP waveform amplitude (>50% amplitude reduction) or prolongations in latency (>10% increase) serve as definitive critical alerts for impending spinal cord compromise.[26]

3. Pathophysiological Mechanisms of Injury Prevention via IONM

While UBE drastically minimizes paraspinal muscle crush injury and preserves the posterior ligamentous complex compared to traditional open surgery, the unique biportal endoscopic environment introduces highly specific, novel mechanisms of potential iatrogenic injury. Multimodal IONM serves as the primary defense mechanism against these threats.

3.1 Irrigation Fluid-Induced Hydrostatic Pressure (Epidural Hypertension)

UBE inherently mandates continuous, pressurized fluid irrigation. The epidural space is an enclosed, rigid anatomical compartment largely governed by principles analogous to the Monro-Kellie doctrine. Rigorous clinical studies demonstrate that standard, uncomplicated UBE procedures operate at an average epidural hydrostatic pressure of approximately 17.98 mmHg.[4] However, when fluid outflow is transiently obstructed (a common occurrence when working cannulae are not properly vented, instruments occlude the portal, or debris clogs the outflow tract), intra-canal pressure can spike dramatically.

Table 2. Epidural Hydrostatic Pressure by Irrigation System Type (from source)
Irrigation System Mean Pressure (mmHg) Variability Clinical Implication
Gravity-based irrigation ~23.86 mmHg Highly variable with frequent unmeasured spikes Higher risk of epidural hypertension and cord ischemia
Digital infusion pump ~12.10 mmHg Tightly regulated Significantly lower risk profile; preferred system[4]
Average uncomplicated UBE ~17.98 mmHg Moderate Baseline reference for safe operation[4]

When artificial epidural fluid pressure functionally exceeds the mean arterial perfusion pressure of the spinal cord's microvasculature, a state of critical ischemia rapidly ensues.[5] In the lumbar spine, this may result in transient, generally reversible cauda equina ischemia. However, in the cervical and thoracic spine, elevated hydrostatic pressure can rapidly precipitate irreversible myelopathy and permanent cord infarction.[6] Furthermore, retrograde transmission of high pressure via CSF into the cranial vault can lead to acute intracranial hypertension, manifesting as severe headaches, neck pain, seizures, or pseudohypoxic brain swelling.[5]

The viability of cord perfusion is determined by: CPP = MAP − EHP, where CPP is cord perfusion pressure, MAP is systemic mean arterial pressure, and EHP is epidural hydrostatic pressure.

🚨 IONM Alert Protocol for Epidural Hypertension
Upon an MEP or SSEP alert during UBE:
  1. Immediately reduce irrigation pump pressure below 30 mmHg[5]
  2. Aggressively vent working portal and endoscopic cannula to clear soft-tissue blockages and restore free outflow[5]
  3. Augment systemic MAP via vasopressors (phenylephrine or ephedrine) to drive blood flow across ischemic watershed zones[32]

3.2 Radiofrequency (RF) Plasma Thermal Injury

Hemostasis in the epidural space during UBE heavily relies on RF probes, which generate intense, localized thermal energy.[33] Intraoperative thermal injury is widely identified as a leading cause of iatrogenic nerve root injury and postoperative dysesthesia in UBE.[7] Neural injury occurs via two mechanisms:

  • Direct Contact Injury: Inadvertent physical contact of the active, superheated RF probe tip with the dural sac or epineurium of the exiting nerve root.
  • Indirect Heating ("Immersion Heater Effect"): Continuous RF activation within a constrained fluid cavity raises ambient temperature. If saline flow is inadequate as a convective heat sink, the epidural fluid rises exponentially in temperature, inducing indirect thermal coagulation of neural proteins even without probe contact.[8]
🛡️ IONM Defense: s-EMG Thermal Surveillance
Visual cues through the endoscope are entirely insufficient to gauge ambient fluid temperature—water at 37°C looks identical to water at 50°C. As local fluid temperature approaches the critical threshold for neural thermal injury (typically 42–45°C), thermal agitation of the axonal membrane alters voltage-gated channel kinetics, resulting in spontaneous depolarization.[10] This manifests instantly on s-EMG as sustained, high-frequency neurotonic discharges ("A-trains"), prompting the surgeon to deactivate the RF probe and flush with cold saline.[5,38]

3.3 Mechanical Trauma and Spatial Constraints

UBE is inherently constrained by a narrow, fixed working angle. In complex procedures requiring extensive contralateral decompression via a unilateral approach ("over-the-top" decompression) or during forceful insertion of large interbody cages in UBE-TLIF, significant, non-physiological mechanical traction is routinely placed on the thecal sac and traversing nerve roots.[35]

Aggressive mechanical retraction within Kambin's triangle (bordered by the exiting nerve root, the traversing nerve root, and the superior endplate of the caudal vertebra) during cage insertion is a documented vector for severe postoperative dysesthesia, neuropathic pain, and frank motor deficits.[40] Continuous s-EMG is hyper-sensitive to this mechanical deformation, detecting the exact millisecond that traction exceeds physiological tolerances, allowing the surgeon to release tension, widen the corridor via further bony decompression, or adjust the implant trajectory prior to irreversible crush injury.[40]

4. Evidence-Based Medicine Hierarchy & Critical Appraisal (2021–2026)

4.1 The Prevailing Level of Evidence

A critical, systematic analysis of the current bibliometric landscape reveals a stark methodological reality: the vast majority of clinical consensus regarding UBE efficacy and IONM utility is heavily predicated on Level III (retrospective comparative cohorts) and Level IV (retrospective case series) evidence.Level III–IV[11]

While the gross volume of academic publications regarding UBE has surged exponentially over the last five years[43], the literature remains heavily skewed toward technical notes, surgical technique descriptions, learning curve analyses (often utilizing Cumulative Sum methodology)[1], and retrospective registry reviews.[12]

4.2 Meta-Analyses and the Absence of Level I RCTs

Numerous systematic reviews and meta-analyses aggregating Level III and Level IV studies have been published recently. These consistently demonstrate that UBE is strictly non-inferior to traditional microscopic decompression in long-term functional outcomes (ODI, VAS for back/leg pain) and overall complication rates, with statistically significant superiority for UBE in early perioperative metrics including reduced blood loss, diminished postoperative pain, and shorter hospital stays.[12]

🔍 Critical Evidence Gap
There is a conspicuous and absolute absence of Level I Randomized Controlled Trials (RCTs) specifically investigating the isolated efficacy of IONM within the UBE environment. While highly cited Level I RCTs do exist comparing the overall UBE technique to microscopic surgery (e.g., Park et al., The Spine Journal[51]), these randomize patients based on the surgical approach (UBE vs. Micro), not the monitoring status. They do not randomize to "IONM" versus "No IONM" cohorts within the UBE arm.

Limitation noted: The source document does not identify any Level I RCTs for IONM in UBE. Evidence supporting IONM integration is extrapolated entirely from retrospective, non-randomized cohorts.[11] For example, Bai et al. (2025) conducted a retrospective comparative analysis of 127 patients concluding IONM provided timely neurological information and reduced early postoperative leg pain (Level III).[10]

The persistent lack of Level I RCTs for IONM in UBE reflects a broader ethical conundrum: because IONM has been established as beneficial in complex spinal deformities and tumor resections, many IRBs view intentional withholding of IONM in high-risk procedures as ethically precarious and legally indefensible. Advancement of the highest-tier literature thus relies on large-scale, multicenter prospective observational registries and propensity-score matched cohorts.[13]

5. Clinical Consensus: "Standard of Care" Paradigms

The medicolegal and clinical concept of a universal "standard of care" regarding IONM in UBE is highly nuanced and strictly anatomically dependent. The current global clinical consensus dictates a highly stratified, risk-adjusted approach.[54]

5.1 Routine Lumbar UBE Decompression: An Elective Adjunct

For primary, uncomplicated single-level lumbar disc herniations or routine central/lateral recess lumbar spinal stenosis, routine multimodal IONM is not currently mandated as a strict, universal standard of care across all jurisdictions.[54] Rationale:

  1. High-Definition Visualization: 4K endoscopic magnification allows meticulous visual confirmation of nerve roots, reducing blind mechanical trauma.[43]
  2. Low Baseline Anatomical Risk: The risk of catastrophic irreversible spinal cord injury is anatomically absent in the lower lumbar spine. The cauda equina possesses significantly greater resilience to transient ischemia than the solid spinal cord above the conus.
  3. Cost-Effectiveness and ERAS Protocols: IONM requires dedicated technical teams, neurophysiologists, and anesthetic modifications (TIVA with propofol/remifentanil to avoid MEP suppression by volatile agents[21]). For routine decompressions, the cost-per-QALY gained is debated.[54]

5.2 High-Risk Applications: C-UBE, T-UBE, and UBE-TLIF

As indications expand into complex pathologies, the consensus shifts dramatically. Multimodal IONM (s-EMG + MEPs + SSEPs) is overwhelmingly recommended and, in the vast majority of leading academic centers, strictly established as standard of care.

Cervical UBE (C-UBE) and Thoracic UBE (T-UBE)

Operating directly adjacent to the vulnerable spinal cord, even minor transient spikes in hydrostatic pressure or millimeters of over-retraction can induce permanent tetraplegia or paraplegia.[6] The margin for technical error is effectively zero. Continuous MEP and SSEP monitoring is deemed absolutely, medicolegally, and clinically essential.

UBE-TLIF (Transforaminal Lumbar Interbody Fusion)

UBE-TLIF represents the absolute pinnacle of biportal endoscopic complexity.[55] It requires forceful insertion of large interbody cages through Kambin's triangle under pressurized fluid medium. The risk of stretching or crushing the exiting nerve root (including the dorsal root ganglion) is significantly higher than in non-fusion decompression.[40] Continuous s-EMG is hyper-critical for immediate alert during cage trialing, while specialized insulated probes within briefly aspirated "dry" fields verify pedicle cortical integrity.[14]

5.3 Economic and ERAS Considerations

Recent economic evaluations demonstrate that extended endoscopic lumbar procedures explicitly utilizing IONM can be significantly more cost-effective per QALY gained ($15,536/QALY) compared to unmonitored traditional open transforaminal fusions ($32,869/QALY).[54] However, variation in surgeon comfort, regional healthcare resources, and legal environments ensures that the absolute "standard of care" remains a dynamic, continuously evolving consensus.

6. Future Directions & Technological Synergies

The inherent biophysical limitations of standard IONM modalities within the conductive UBE environment are actively driving rapid technological innovation.[22]

1
Probe Redesign and Hyper-Isolation: Advanced, highly insulated, purely endoscopic bipolar stimulation probes designed for submerged environments aim to completely mitigate the current shunting effect, restoring t-EMG reliability without continuous aspiration.[22]
2
Automated, Closed-Loop Fluid Management: Real-time, closed-loop pressure-sensing digital irrigation pumps capable of dynamically adjusting inflow pressure based on continuous epidural pressure feedback will largely negate pressure-induced ischemic events.[4]
3
Intelligent, Neuro-Protective RF Generators: Next-generation RF devices featuring integrated micro-temperature sensors and automatic failsafe shut-off algorithms to prevent epidural fluid from reaching neurotoxic thermal thresholds (currently detectable via s-EMG A-trains).[36]

7. Conclusion

The successful integration of Intraoperative Neurophysiological Monitoring into Unilateral Biportal Endoscopic spine surgery represents a highly sophisticated intersection of advanced neurophysiology, complex biophysics, and cutting-edge minimally invasive surgical technique.

The unique, highly conductive, continuous saline environment of UBE fundamentally alters the diagnostic utility of standard IONM modalities. The current shunting effect compromises active stimulation-evoked responses (t-EMG), necessitating specific workarounds such as transient field aspiration or hyper-insulated probes. Conversely, passive recording modalities (s-EMG) are entirely impervious to fluid conductivity and serve as the most vital continuous monitor for mechanical traction, root compression, and RF thermal irritation. Long-tract modalities (MEPs and SSEPs) remain essential for detecting global ischemic events induced by irrigation fluid-induced epidural hydrostatic hypertension.

A rigorous appraisal of the recent EBM literature reveals a landscape dominated by Level III and Level IV retrospective data, with a critical dearth of Level I RCTs specifically evaluating IONM in UBE—largely due to intractable ethical constraints.[11,13]

The global clinical consensus is heavily stratified by procedural anatomy and risk. For routine lumbar decompressions, IONM remains an elective, cost-dependent consideration. However, as UBE indications expand into cervical, thoracic, and UBE-TLIF reconstructive fusions, multimodal IONM transforms from an elective adjunct into a critical, absolute standard-of-care necessity.[6,40,55]
