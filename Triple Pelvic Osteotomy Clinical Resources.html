1. Introduction to Developmental Dysplasia of the Hip

Developmental dysplasia of the hip (DDH) encompasses a broad and complex spectrum of anatomical abnormalities affecting the coxofemoral joint, ranging from subtle acetabular dysplasia and microinstability to frank subluxation and complete joint dislocation.1

The fundamental structural deficit in the mature or maturing dysplastic hip is a shallow, lateralized, and frequently anteverted acetabulum that fails to provide sufficient anterior and lateral coverage of the femoral head.4 This anatomical insufficiency drastically reduces the contact area between the femoral head and the acetabular dome, leading to a pathological concentration of weight-bearing forces.6

Natural History of Untreated DDH

The natural history of untreated or residually dysplastic hips invariably progresses toward early-onset, secondary degenerative osteoarthritis due to abnormal joint biomechanics, localized chondral overload, and subsequent labral pathology.1

1.1 Conservative Management & Indications for Surgery

In infantile DDH, conservative management utilizing devices such as the Pavlik harness often yields successful concentric reduction, capitalizing on the immense remodeling potential of the infant's cartilaginous anlage.2 However, when diagnosis is delayed or conservative measures fail, the structural limits of spontaneous acetabular remodeling necessitate surgical intervention.1

The overarching objective of any pelvic osteotomy is to surgically detach, reorient, and stabilize the acetabulum to normalize the biomechanical relationship between the femoral head and the acetabular roof.1 By restoring a concentric and stable joint, surgeons aim to alleviate pain, improve functional kinematics, and significantly delay or preclude the need for total hip arthroplasty.1

2. The Surgical Armamentarium: Pelvic Osteotomy Classification

Surgical correction of idiopathic DDH remains one of the most technically demanding challenges in orthopedic surgery.1 Interventions are broadly categorized into procedures directed at the proximal femur and procedures directed at the pelvis.9

Pelvic osteotomies are indicated when the primary pathology is a deficient acetabulum, as evidenced by an increased acetabular index and a lack of lateral ossification.9 These procedures are subclassified into:

2.1 Reshaping Osteotomies: Pemberton & Dega

The Pemberton osteotomy involves a pericapsular, incomplete transiliac cut that hinges the acetabular roof downward over the femoral head, relying on the tri-radiate cartilage as a fulcrum.4 The Dega osteotomy similarly utilizes an incomplete transiliac cut but leaves the inner cortex of the ilium intact, directing the acetabular roof laterally and anteriorly.13

These procedures are highly effective but are strictly limited to younger pediatric patients—typically under age six to eight—whose triradiate cartilage remains open and pliable.1

2.2 The Salter Innominate Osteotomy

The quintessential redirectional procedure involves a single transverse cut through the ilium extending to the sciatic notch, with the distal fragment hinged on the pubic symphysis to provide anterolateral coverage.4 However, its success is entirely dependent on the elasticity of the pubic symphysis.4 As children mature beyond age eight, the symphysis loses elasticity and can no longer rotate adequately.4

2.3 The Triple Pelvic Osteotomy: When It Becomes Mandatory

When the symphysis becomes rigid in older children, adolescents, and adults, the TPO becomes the mandatory joint-preserving intervention.4 By executing complete osteotomies through all three bones of the hemipelvis—the ilium, ischium, and pubis—the TPO completely frees the acetabulum from the axial skeleton, permitting a massive degree of spherical correction.4

TPO vs. Chiari (Salvage)

Comparative studies have consistently demonstrated that the TPO provides superior radiologic and functional outcomes, particularly concerning the final Stulberg classification, making it the preferred intervention when a congruent reduction can be achieved.9

3. Evolution & Biomechanical Rationale

3.1 The Steel TPO (1965–1973)

The original concept of osteotomizing three pelvic bones was pioneered by Le Cœur in 1965 and popularized by Steel in 1973.16 Early iterations placed the ischial cut distally near the ischial tuberosity and the pubic cut near the symphysis pubis.16

Key Biomechanical Limitations of Steel Technique

  • The axis of rotation remained physically distant from the true center of the hip joint, creating a long, cumbersome lever arm.16
  • Heavy tethering by sacropelvic ligaments (sacrospinous and sacrotuberous) restricted rotation.16
  • Unwanted translational shifts and excessive anterior tilt were common.16
  • Highly rigid internal fixation required during extended healing.16

3.2 The Tönnis Modification (1981): A Paradigm Shift

Tönnis introduced a highly influential modification, shifting osteotomy sites into a juxta-articular position, as close to the acetabular cartilage as safely possible.22

By moving the ischial cut proximal to the sacrospinous and sacrotuberous ligaments, the Tönnis technique effectively decoupled the acetabular fragment from massive restraining forces.16 This allowed for much freer, more purely spherical rotation centered directly over the femoral head.16

Advantages of the Tönnis Juxta-Articular Approach

  • Unhindered spherical mobility of the acetabulum16
  • Larger bone contact area → superior intrinsic stability → faster union16
  • Preserved physiological sacropelvic ligament tension24
  • Minimized pelvic cavity narrowing27

3.3 Contemporary Context: TPO vs. Bernese PAO

The Ganz Bernese periacetabular osteotomy (PAO) has gained prominence in adult hip preservation due to its single anterior incision and intact posterior column.26 However, it has a notoriously steep and unforgiving learning curve, with early series demonstrating significant rates of major complications including intra-articular crack propagation and neurovascular injuries.27

The TPO remains heavily utilized in the pediatric and adolescent demographic due to a much shallower learning curve, direct visualization of bone cuts, and demonstrably lower risk of catastrophic intra-articular fractures.28 Advanced modifications (e.g., interlocking iliac cut by Kumar and O'Hara) provide geometric stability approaching PAO integrity.28

5. Surgical Technique: Modern Dual-Incision Modified Tönnis TPO

The following procedural guide utilizes the modern, dual-incision, modified Tönnis technique, designed to maximize surgical safety, optimize femoral head containment, and facilitate highly stable fixation.11

Step 1: Preoperative Planning

Three-dimensional CT scans assess acetabular version and bone stock.5 Standardized templating tools (e.g., Hip2Norm software) digitally correct pelvic tilt and model the required acetabular reorientation.28 Patient positioned supine on a radiolucent operating table.22

Step 2: Medial Approach — Pubic & Ischial Osteotomies (Vladimirov Modification)

A single medial incision in the medial groin, 0.5–1.0 cm distal to the inguinal crease over the adductor longus tendon.22 The interval between adductor longus (retracted medially) and pectineus (retracted laterally) is developed, shielding the anterior femoral neurovascular bundle.22

Pubic osteotomy: The cut connects the anterior aspect of the anterior obturator tubercle to the highest projection of the obturator sulcus, directed strictly caudal-to-cranial to safeguard the corona mortis vascular anastomosis.22

Ischial osteotomy (Li modification): The cut is between the lower border of the posterior obturator tubercle and the peak of the ischial spine, driven lateral-to-medial. A palpating finger on the posterior obturator tubercle acts as a backstop to protect the pudendal nerve and internal pudendal vessels.22

Step 3: Anterior Approach — Iliac Osteotomy

A modified Smith-Petersen or "bikini" incision centered over the ASIS.28 The sartorius–tensor fasciae latae interval is developed; the lateral femoral cutaneous nerve is protected medially.32 Meticulous subperiosteal dissection posteriorly protects the superior gluteal neurovascular bundle and sciatic nerve.29

The iliac cut extends from just proximal to the AIIS posteriorly, exiting into the greater sciatic notch.4 In the interlocking modification, a three-sided geometric cut with a reciprocating saw allows bones to physically interlock upon rotation.28

Step 4: Acetabular Mobilization, Redirection & Fixation

A heavy reduction forceps or Schanz pin "joystick" manipulates the fragment laterally and anteriorly.21 Critical caution: Excessive external rotation induces iatrogenic acetabular retroversion → anterior FAI.5 Intraoperative fluoroscopy confirms correction and absence of the crossover sign.6

Fixation: structural autograft from the iliac crest impacted into the iliac gap; two or three 3.0 mm threaded K-wires or 4.5 mm pelvic reconstruction screws secure the construct.4 Dynamic testing verifies stability through full range of motion.24

7.2 Functional Outcomes

Clinical functional scoring reflects profound anatomical corrections:

  • Harris Hip Score: Increases from fair/poor ranges (70–74 points) to excellent (90–100 points).6
  • Oxford Hip Score: Typically doubles from pre-operative baselines (e.g., 19.6 → 39.4), correlating with decreased daily pain and improved mobility.28
Level III Evidence

7.3 Long-Term Survivorship

Kaplan-Meier survivorship analyses demonstrate survival rates (avoidance of conversion to THA) exceeding 85.3% at 15 years.38 Long-term studies spanning 18–32 years confirm the TPO serves as a robust modality to stall degenerative progression, provided a perfectly concentric reduction is achieved.37

Negative Prognostic Factors Predicting Early Failure

  • Advanced pre-operative osteoarthritic changes
  • Patient age > 35 years at the time of surgery
  • Pre-operative Body Mass Index > 2538
Level III Evidence

8. Postoperative Rehabilitation Protocol

Modern stable fixation protocols utilizing robust screw constructs or interlocking geometry frequently eliminate the need for casting entirely.25

  • Weight-bearing: Strict toe-touch or non-weight-bearing for 6–12 weeks to allow callus formation and graft integration.25
  • VTE prophylaxis: Low-molecular-weight heparin or targeted oral anticoagulants for high-risk patients.35
  • Radiographic monitoring: Serial imaging at 6 and 12 weeks to confirm graft incorporation, hardware integrity, and maintenance of corrective angles.36
  • Early mobilization: Active-assisted ROM on CPM machine to preserve joint mechanics and provide synovial nourishment.28
