Executive Summary

The surgical incision is the foundational step of orthopedic intervention, constituting the portal through which all reconstructive, fixation, and ablative procedures are conducted. It is not merely a technical prerequisite but a biological determinant that significantly influences postoperative morbidity, functional recovery, and rehabilitation kinetics.

The evolution of incision planning has transitioned from the rigid, cadaver-derived maps of the 19th century to a dynamic, evidence-based understanding of cutaneous mechanobiology and functional anatomy. This report synthesizes the current literature across four critical domains:

  • Biomechanical skin tension lines — including the critical distinction between Langer's lines, RSTL, and BEST lines in the lower extremity
  • Comparative instrumentation outcomes — scalpel vs. electrosurgery vs. advanced RF (ACE blade)
  • Internervous surgical planes — a granular anatomical atlas for safe extensile exposure across the appendicular and axial skeleton
  • Extensibility and soft tissue management — adhering to AO and OTA principles in high-energy trauma
Guiding Principle The incision is an integral part of the soft tissue resuscitation, governed by the principles of debridement and vascular preservation. Evidence mandates departure from historical dogma when it conflicts with biomechanical reality.

Part I: The Biomechanics of the Cutaneous Envelope

The skin is a complex, anisotropic, viscoelastic material. Its mechanical behavior under stress — specifically the stress applied by a surgical incision and subsequent closure — dictates the quality of the resulting scar and the risk of wound complications such as dehiscence, necrosis, and hypertrophy. The primary objective of incision planning is to align the surgical vector with the intrinsic lines of minimal tension.

1.1 Historical Paradigms: The Legacy and Limitations of Langer

The historical bedrock of incision planning is the concept of Langer's Lines (cleavage lines). Described by the Austrian anatomist Karl Langer in 1861, these lines were mapped by piercing the skin of cadavers with a circular awl. Langer observed that the resulting wounds distorted into ellipses due to the intrinsic tension of the dermal collagen network.1

Critical Limitation of Langer's Model Langer's subjects were cadavers in rigor mortis. His maps reflect the static, passive tension of the collagen reticulum in a stiffened state, devoid of the dynamic forces exerted by muscle tone, joint position, and gravity in a living patient. While Langer's lines accurately describe the structural orientation of connective tissue fibers in the reticular dermis, they fail to account for the dynamic anisotropy of living skin.1

Evidence suggests that incisions made strictly parallel to Langer's lines often cross natural skin creases perpendicularly, particularly on the limbs and trunk, leading to widening scars and "railroad track" deformities.2

1.2 The Dynamic Shift: Relaxed Skin Tension Lines (RSTL)

To address the static limitations of Langer's model, subsequent researchers introduced concepts grounded in living anatomy:

  • Kraissl's Lines: Defined by the orientation of collagen fibers perpendicular to the direction of underlying muscle contraction. In the living body, muscle action generates tension lines at right angles to the fibers. Incisions placed parallel to these dynamic tension lines — and thus perpendicular to the muscle fibers — heal with reduced tension. These lines are often visible as "wrinkle lines" in elderly patients.1
  • Relaxed Skin Tension Lines (RSTL): The most practical guide for elective incision planning in many anatomical regions. RSTLs are determined by the "pinch test": when skin is pinched and relaxed, furrows and ridges form. The RSTL runs parallel to these furrows. Biomechanically, the skin possesses its greatest extensibility perpendicular to the RSTL. Therefore, an incision placed parallel to the RSTL experiences the least amount of "pull" attempting to distract the wound edges.2

1.3 The Lower Limb Paradox: BEST Lines vs. RSTL

The most significant divergence from traditional tension maps occurs in the lower extremity, particularly the leg (tibia/fibula region). The skin in this region is tightly adherent to the underlying fascia and bone, with limited redundancy.

Key Evidence — BEST Lines Recent biomechanical studies utilizing tensiometers to measure wound closure forces have led to the definition of Biodynamic Excisional Skin Tension (BEST) lines. These studies involved excising circular defects and measuring the force required to close them in various vectors, revealing a striking finding: the skin of the lower leg exhibits orthotropy (directional dependence) that favors vertical closure.4

Contrary to the visual cues of transverse wrinkle lines (RSTL) often seen on the anterior shin, measurements show that vertical incisions (parallel to the long axis of the tibia) consistently require significantly less force to close than horizontal or oblique incisions.4

  • Implication for Trauma: This supports the standard use of longitudinal incisions for tibial nailing and plating. A transverse incision on the shin fights against the significant longitudinal tension of the pre-tibial skin.
  • Implication for Oncology: For excising skin lesions on the leg, the long axis of the ellipse should be vertical (following the BEST line), rather than transverse (following the RSTL), to minimize the risk of dehiscence and the need for skin grafting.4

1.4 Geometric Principles of Excision

The Fusiform Excision

The standard elliptical excision is designed with a length-to-width ratio of 3:1 to 4:1. This geometry prevents the formation of "dog-ears" (standing cones) at the apices. The long axis must align with the RSTL or BEST lines to ensure the closure vector recruits the maximal skin laxity.6

The Parallelogram Excision

When a lesion or surgical target is oriented obliquely to the lines of minimal tension, a standard ellipse would necessitate removing excessive healthy tissue. The parallelogram excision offers a geometric solution: by converting the excision into a parallelogram where the shorter sides align with the skin tension lines, the surgeon can reduce the total area of healthy tissue excised and distribute tension more evenly.7

1.5 Striae Distensae as Biomechanical Indicators

Striae distensae (stretch marks) provide an intrinsic, patient-specific map of skin stress history. Striae always form perpendicular to the direction of maximal tension. Anatomical studies utilizing histological analysis have confirmed that both skin tension lines and the primary orientation of dermal collagen fibers run perpendicular to striae. Therefore, incisions should be placed perpendicular to the striae (i.e., crossing them at 90 degrees) to align with the lines of minimal tension.8

Part II: The Physics of Dissection — Instrumentation & Wound Healing

The choice between the "cold steel" scalpel and electrosurgery (diathermy/cautery) is often dictated by tradition rather than evidence. The classical dogma — "scalpel for skin, diathermy for deep tissue" — is based on the premise that thermal injury to the skin edges promotes infection and scarring. Contemporary evidence challenges this view.

2.2 Comparative Outcomes: Systematic Review Evidence

Extensive meta-analyses of randomized controlled trials (RCTs) comparing scalpel versus electrosurgical skin incisions have yielded consistent findings across general and orthopedic surgery.

2.2.1 Incision Time and Operative Efficiency

Meta-analyses consistently demonstrate that electrosurgical incisions are significantly faster than scalpel incisions. The efficiency gain is derived from the simultaneous cutting and hemostasis, eliminating the "stop-and-start" sequence of scalpel incision followed by hemostatic clamping.11 Level I

2.2.2 Intraoperative Blood Loss

Electrosurgery results in significantly lower early blood loss compared to scalpel incisions. While the absolute difference in milliliters may be small for minor procedures, the reduction is statistically significant and clinically relevant in large exposures (e.g., hip arthroplasty, spine surgery).14 Level I

2.2.3 Postoperative Pain

A counterintuitive but robust finding across multiple studies is that patients undergoing electrosurgical incisions report lower pain scores in the first 24 to 48 hours postoperatively. The proposed mechanism is thermal deafferentation: the heat of the electrosurgical blade ablates the sensory nerve endings at the wound edge, preventing the transmission of nociceptive signals.11 Level I

2.2.4 Infection and Wound Healing

The fear that thermal necrosis increases infection risk has not been substantiated in general surgery populations. Systematic reviews found no significant difference in surgical site infection (SSI) rates between scalpel and electrosurgery.18 Level I

Total Knee Arthroplasty (TKA) — Special Consideration The pre-patellar skin is thin, less vascular, and subject to high tension during flexion. Some retrospective studies suggest a higher rate of wound complications (dehiscence, drainage) with electrosurgical incisions in TKA, theoretically due to extensive thermal damage to the subdermal plexus in thin flaps.20 However, other matched studies found no difference in healing or infection.21 Consensus suggests care must be taken to minimize collateral thermal damage (low power, pure cut mode) in areas of thin subcutaneous coverage. Level III

2.3 Advanced Electrosurgical Technology: The ACE Blade

To mitigate thermal damage, new blade geometries have been developed. The ACE Blade (Advanced Cutting Effect) utilizes a tapered geometry and a specialized coating to concentrate RF energy at the leading edge, allowing for cutting at lower power settings with reduced thermal spread.

  • Histological Evidence: Porcine studies comparing ACE blades, standard PTFE-coated blades, and scalpel incisions revealed that at "Time 0" (immediate), standard electrosurgery produced a wide zone of thermal damage. The ACE blade produced minimal damage, significantly less than standard cautery.22
  • Healing Trajectory: At 6 weeks, incisions made with the ACE blade showed dermal scar widths and wound tensile strengths comparable to scalpel incisions, and significantly superior to standard electrosurgery. The inflammatory response was milder in ACE incisions.22
Clinical Recommendation For standard orthopedic incisions through robust muscle and fascia (hip, spine, thigh), standard electrosurgery is efficient and safe. For incisions in cosmetically sensitive areas or areas with compromised vascularity (foot and ankle, dorsal hand), the scalpel or advanced low-thermal blades (ACE, PlasmaBlade) are preferable to maximize biologic preservation.

Part III: Anatomical Atlas of Internervous Planes

The safety of deep surgical dissection relies on the internervous plane — the cleavage line between muscles supplied by different nerves. Utilizing these planes prevents iatrogenic denervation of the muscle bellies bordering the approach.

3.1 The Shoulder and Humerus

3.1.1 Anterior Approach to the Shoulder (Deltopectoral)

This is the "workhorse" approach for arthroplasty and fractures of the proximal humerus.

  • Internervous Plane: Deltoid (Axillary Nerve) ↔ Pectoralis Major (Medial and Lateral Pectoral Nerves)24
  • Key Landmark: The cephalic vein lies within the deltopectoral groove. It is typically retracted laterally with the deltoid to preserve venous drainage into the axillary vein.
  • ⚠ Danger: The musculocutaneous nerve enters the coracobrachialis 2.5 to 8 cm distal to the coracoid process. Excessive medial retraction of the conjoined tendon puts this nerve at risk of traction injury (neurapraxia).25

3.1.2 Anterior Approach to the Humerus (Anterolateral)

  • Internervous Plane: Brachialis (Musculocutaneous N.) ↔ Brachioradialis (Radial N.)26
  • Nuance: Distally, the radial nerve supplies the lateral aspect of the brachialis in many individuals, making this technically a dual-innervation zone.
  • ⚠ Danger: The radial nerve must be identified in the groove between brachialis and brachioradialis before deep dissection.

3.2 The Elbow and Forearm

3.2.1 Lateral Approach to the Elbow (Kocher)

  • Internervous Plane: Anconeus (Radial N.) ↔ Extensor Carpi Ulnaris / ECU (Posterior Interosseous Nerve / PIN)27
  • Anatomy: The plane is often marked by a fat stripe between the two muscles.
  • ⚠ Danger: The PIN winds around the radial neck within the supinator. The forearm must be pronated during this approach — this rotates the radial tuberosity posteriorly and moves the PIN anteriorly and medially, away from the surgical field.27

3.2.2 Volar Approach to the Forearm (Henry)

  • Internervous Plane (Proximal): Brachioradialis (Radial N.) ↔ Pronator Teres (Median N.)
  • Internervous Plane (Distal): Brachioradialis (Radial N.) ↔ Flexor Carpi Radialis / FCR (Median N.)30
  • Deep Structures: The radial artery runs beneath the brachioradialis in the middle third. The superficial radial nerve runs under the brachioradialis and must be protected during lateral retraction.
  • Forearm must be supinated during this approach to move the PIN laterally and posteriorly as the supinator is incised along its medial insertion.30

3.2.3 Dorsal Approach to the Forearm (Thompson)

  • Internervous Plane: ECRB (Radial N.) ↔ EDC (PIN)32
  • High Risk This approach places the PIN directly in the dissection field as it emerges from the supinator. It must be visualized and protected at all times.

3.3 The Wrist

3.3.1 Dorsal Approach

Not a true internervous plane — dissection proceeds between the 3rd compartment (EPL) and 4th compartment (EDC/EIP). The EPL tendon is released from its canal around Lister's tubercle and transposed radially.34

3.3.2 Volar Approach to the Distal Radius (Modified Henry)

  • Plane: FCR tendon (Median N.) ↔ Radial Artery (vascular structure)
  • The incision is made over the FCR tendon, retracted ulnarly (protecting the median nerve), with the radial artery retracted radially.36

3.4 The Spine: Wiltse Paraspinal Approach

For posterior lumbar surgery, minimizing muscle trauma is paramount to preventing "failed back" syndrome related to paraspinal atrophy.

  • Internervous Plane: Multifidus (medial muscle group) ↔ Longissimus (lateral muscle group). Both are innervated by medial branches of the dorsal rami, but the cleavage plane lies between muscles supplied by different branches.37
  • Landmark: The plane is found approximately 3–4 cm lateral to the midline at L4/L5. Perforating vessels mark the septum.
  • Utility: Direct access to facet joints, pedicles, and transverse processes without stripping muscles from spinous processes.38

3.5 The Hip

3.5.1 Anterior Approach (Smith-Petersen)

  • Superficial Plane: Sartorius (Femoral N.) ↔ TFL (Superior Gluteal N.)
  • Deep Plane: Rectus Femoris (Femoral N.) ↔ Gluteus Medius (Superior Gluteal N.)26
  • ⚠ Danger: The Lateral Femoral Cutaneous Nerve (LFCN) crosses superficially and is highly variable. The ascending branch of the lateral femoral circumflex artery must be ligated.

3.5.2 Posterior Approach (Moore/Southern)

Not a true internervous plane — involves splitting the Gluteus Maximus (supplied by Inferior Gluteal Nerve). The split is performed proximally and laterally where the bulk of the muscle remains innervated. Deep dissection requires tenotomy of the short external rotators.40

3.6 The Knee

3.6.1 Medial Parapatellar Approach

The standard approach for TKA. Incision through the quadriceps tendon, medial to the patella, and through the medial patellar retinaculum. Disadvantage: destabilizes the extensor mechanism and interrupts the blood supply to the patella from the medial side.

3.6.2 Subvastus and Midvastus Approaches

  • Subvastus: Elevates the entire VMO without cutting its tendon. Preserves quadriceps integrity, leading to faster SLR recovery.41 Level I
  • Midvastus: Splits VMO fibers (usually 2–3 cm into muscle). A compromise offering better exposure than subvastus but less trauma than parapatellar. Meta-analyses show earlier flexion recovery with equivalent long-term results.42 Level I

3.7 The Ankle and Foot

3.7.1 Anterior Approach to the Ankle

  • Internervous Plane: None — both EHL and EDL are innervated by the Deep Peroneal Nerve.
  • The anterior tibial artery and deep peroneal nerve lie deep to the interval. The superficial peroneal nerve crosses the surgical field in the subcutaneous tissue and must be identified.44

3.7.2 Lateral Approach to the Hindfoot (Ollier's)

  • Internervous Plane: Peroneus Brevis (Superficial Peroneal N.) ↔ Peroneus Tertius (Deep Peroneal N.)44
  • Wound Healing Risk This incision is notorious for wound healing problems due to disruption of the angiosomes supplied by the lateral tarsal artery.

Part IV: Incision Planning for Extensibility & Utility

4.1 Crossing Flexion Creases

Incisions crossing joint flexion creases at 90 degrees are contraindicated due to the risk of contracture. As the scar matures, it shortens; a straight line across a joint acts as a tether (bowstring).

  • The Bruner Incision (Zig-Zag): Used in hand surgery. Makes 90-degree turns at each flexion crease, creating triangular flaps that distribute extension force across oblique limbs.46
  • Mid-Axial Incision: Placed on the lateral aspect of the digit, connecting the dorsal ends of the flexion creases. This line represents the "neutral axis" of motion where skin movement is minimal during flexion/extension. Preferred for drainage of septic flexor tenosynovitis.47

4.2 The Utilitarian Incision

In orthopedic oncology and complex trauma, incisions must be "utilitarian" — capable of being extended to expose the entire bone or joint without compromising future reconstructive options.

  • Shoulder: Anterior deltopectoral + posterior limb along the scapular spine; can be connected for total scapulectomy or forequarter amputation.48
  • Pelvis: Extended Iliofemoral Approach exposes the entire lateral ilium. Modern practice favors combined approaches (Anterior + Posterior) to reduce morbidity of massive muscle stripping.49

4.3 Extensile Measures in Trauma

  • Extensile Henry: Volar forearm approach can be extended distally across the wrist with a zig-zag to avoid the median nerve palmar cutaneous branch.51
  • Extensile Lateral Elbow: The Kocher approach can extend proximally to the humerus, but the radial nerve crosses from posterior to anterior through the lateral intermuscular septum and must be protected.27

4.4 Soft Tissue Management in Open Fractures

The AO Principles and OTA Classification emphasize that the incision in open fractures is dictated by the Zone of Injury.

Open Fracture Incision Principles
  • The traumatic wound should be extended longitudinally to allow thorough debridement.
  • Transverse extensions (T-incisions) are discouraged — they create devascularized skin corners that invariably necrose.52
  • High-grade skin injuries (loss of coverage) mandate coordination with plastic surgery for flap coverage within 5–7 days to prevent deep infection.53

The OTA Open Fracture Classification provides a more granular assessment of soft tissue injury (Skin, Muscle, Arterial contamination) than the Gustilo-Anderson system.53

Part V: Complications & Special Considerations

5.1 Neuromas and Nerve Injury

Superficial sensory nerves are frequently the source of chronic postoperative pain (neuroma).

  • Superficial Radial Nerve (SRN): Extremely vulnerable in approaches to the distal radius. Runs subcutaneous over the anatomical snuffbox. High Risk
  • Sural Nerve: At risk in the posterolateral approach to the ankle. Moderate Risk
  • Prevention: Incisions should be planned to avoid known nerve courses. Nerves should be formally identified and retracted. Blunt dissection in the subcutaneous layer is safer than sharp dissection.

5.2 Hypertrophic Scarring

Scar hypertrophy is directly correlated with tension. The mechanism involves mechanotransduction pathways in fibroblasts (FAK-ERK signaling) activated by mechanical tension, leading to excess collagen deposition (fibrosis).

Evidence-Based Mitigation Strategies
  • Adhering to BEST lines (vertical on lower leg)
  • Using deep dermal sutures to offload tension from the epidermis
  • Utilizing parallelogram excision technique for misaligned lesions7

5.3 Periprosthetic Joint Infection (PJI)

The incision is the first line of defense against PJI.

  • Previous incisions are a major risk factor. In TKA, parallel vertical incisions can lead to skin bridge necrosis.
  • A minimum distance of 7 cm between incisions is recommended to maintain vascularity from the lateral genicular network.55
  • If the old incision is viable, it should be used; if not, the new incision should be placed as far laterally as possible to preserve medial blood supply.
  • Closure: Water-tight closure of the arthrotomy is critical. Type of suture material (barbed vs. standard) is less important than minimizing dead space and tension.

Conclusion

The orthopedic surgical incision is a high-stakes convergence of biology and mechanics. The evidence mandates a departure from historical dogmas when they conflict with biomechanical reality — most notably in the adoption of vertical BEST line incisions for the lower leg. The safety of these exposures rests on the precise anatomical utilization of internervous planes, which preserve the neuromuscular integrity of the limb.

The modern surgeon should embrace electrosurgery as a superior tool for efficiency and pain control in robust tissues, while reserving the scalpel and advanced blades (ACE) for delicate soft tissue envelopes. In the arena of trauma, the incision is an integral part of the soft tissue resuscitation, governed by the principles of debridement and vascular preservation. By rigorously applying these evidence-based guidelines, orthopedic surgeons can optimize the biological environment for healing, functional recovery, and long-term implant survival.
