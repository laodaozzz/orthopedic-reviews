Surgical Management of Displaced Proximal Humerus Fractures in Adolescents

A Comparative Analysis of K-Wire, ESIN, and Plate Fixation: Evidence-Based Synthesis for Clinical Decision-Making

1. Management Principles for Displaced Proximal Humerus Fractures

1.1. The Adolescent Patient: A Unique Biological Dichotomy Level IV

Management of proximal humerus fractures in adolescents presents a distinct challenge, positioned between pediatric and adult orthopaedic principles. These fractures peak between ages 11-15 years, with treatment decisions dominated by a critical biological dichotomy: significant remodeling potential versus approaching skeletal maturity. Ref 1

The proximal humeral physis contributes 80% of humerus longitudinal growth. While non-operative management is standard in younger children (<10 years), adolescents represent "distinct surgical populations" with operative rates increasing from 8.5% in younger adolescents (10-13 years) to 19.8% in older adolescents (14-18 years). Ref 8

Key Decision Point: Clinical decision-making bifurcates at approximately 13 years of age. Before this point, risks of surgery are weighed against high remodeling probability. After this point, risk of residual deformity from incomplete remodeling outweighs surgical risks. Ref 9, 10

1.2. Defining the Threshold for Surgical Intervention Level IV

While minimally displaced fractures (Neer-Horowitz Type I/II) are managed non-operatively, significant controversy exists regarding the precise threshold for surgical intervention in severely displaced Type III/IV fractures.

  • Royal Children's Hospital Guideline: Accept up to 30° angulation and 30% displacement. Prompt referral for >30° angulation. Ref 6
  • Orthobullets Consensus: Accept up to 45° angulation or 2/3 shaft displacement. Ref 7
  • Prospective Multicenter Study (2023): Defined displaced cohort as >30° angulation or >50% displacement. Operative group: mean 59.6° angulation vs. 45.3° in non-operative group. Ref 10

Severe displacement (Neer-Horowitz III/IV) is the strongest predictor for surgery (OR = 10.00), with operative rates reaching 60% in this cohort. This aggressive threshold is justified by comparative outcomes: conservative treatment success drops from 91% to 82% in severely displaced fractures, while surgical fixation maintains 95-98% success rates. Ref 11, 14

1.3. The Irreducible Fracture: Absolute Indication for Open Reduction Level IV

Failure to achieve acceptable closed reduction is an absolute indication for open reduction. Impediments include periosteum (90.0% cases), long head of biceps tendon (90.0%), and deltoid muscle (70.0%). The biceps tendon is consistently the most common entrapped structure. Ref 9

2. Comparative Analysis of Functional and Radiological Outcomes

2.1. Elastic Stable Intramedullary Nailing (ESIN): Evidence-Based Method of Choice Level I

ESIN is identified as the "method of choice" for severely displaced fractures, demonstrating consistent superiority across multiple outcome metrics.

ESIN Strengths:
  • Functional outcome: 98% "good to excellent" results (p = 0.01 vs. other methods) Ref 5
  • Radiological outcome: 96% "good to excellent" results Ref 14
  • Arm length discrepancy: Only 4% incidence Ref 18
  • Allows early mobilization, preventing shoulder stiffness Ref 1

2.2. Kirschner Wire (K-Wire) Fixation: Traditional Standard Level IV

Percutaneous pinning remains common due to surgeon familiarity, with excellent functional results but notable limitations.

K-Wire Profile:
  • Functional outcome: 95% success rate Ref 14
  • Radiological outcome: 88% "good to excellent" (lowest among methods) Ref 14
  • Procedural limitation: 35% conversion to open reduction in comparative study Ref 20
  • Arm length discrepancy: 19% incidence Ref 18

2.3. Plate Osteosynthesis (ORIF): Evolving Role for Rigid Fixation Level IV-V

Plate fixation has specific, niche indications in complex adolescent fractures.

Plate Indications:
  1. Irreducible fractures requiring open approach Ref 17
  2. Highly unstable or comminuted fractures
  3. Older adolescents nearing skeletal maturity where physeal concerns are minimal

Evidence for plates is sparse and contradictory, reflecting selection bias. However, recent data shows promising results: a 2024 case series reported 100% ASES scores and 99.4% Simple Shoulder Test scores in 18 adolescents. Ref 17

2.4. Comparative Metrics: Healing, Mobilization, and Recovery

Time to union is rapid across all modalities: ESIN 7.6 weeks, K-wires 8.4 weeks, ORIF cohort 4.0 weeks. ESIN is favored for allowing earlier mobilization, with patients returning to sports at mean 3.0 months post-surgery. Ref 9, 15

3. Comprehensive Profile of Complications

3.1. Overall Complication Rates: A Deceptive Similarity

Meta-analyses report similar overall complication rates across methods: ESIN 7%, K-wires 9%, conservative 8% (p = 0.92). However, these mask profound qualitative differences in severity. Ref 3

3.2. K-Wire Complications: Infection, Migration, and Instability

  • Pin-Tract Infection: Most common complication, typically superficial. Ref 3
  • Hardware Migration: Risk with smooth pins, may lead to loss of reduction. Ref 3
  • Shoulder Stiffness: Due to longer immobilization requirements. Ref 23
  • Neurovascular Risk: Axillary nerve at risk with lateral pins, musculocutaneous nerve with anterior pins. Ref 7

3.3. ESIN Complications: Hardware Prominence

  • Nail Protrusion/Skin Irritation: Most frequent adverse event. Ref 35
  • Subacromial Impingement: If nails advanced too far proximally. Ref 37
  • Procedural Evolution: Two-nail technique significantly reduced complication rates vs. one-nail technique. Ref 14

3.4. Plate Complications: High Stakes of Open Reduction

  • Avascular Necrosis (AVN): Most feared complication (1.6-16.4% incidence). Ref 30
  • Screw Cut-Out/Penetration: Most common complication (13.7-14% incidence). Ref 34
  • Subacromial Impingement: From high plate positioning. Ref 31
  • Overall Rate: 13.4% in large series. Ref 30

4. Critical Differentiator: Physeal Injury and Arm Length Discrepancy

4.1. Physeal Disturbance Analysis

The implant's relationship with the proximal humeral physis is the most important factor guiding implant choice. The physis contributes 80% of humeral growth.

Physeal Risk Profiles:
  • K-Wire: Physeal-CROSSING. Must cross physis for stable fixation, creating iatrogenic risk. Ref 22
  • ESIN: Physeal-SPARING. Nails inserted retrograde, bypassing physis entirely. Ref 35
  • Plate: Physeal-SPARING. Screws placed into epiphysis and diaphysis, avoiding direct physis injury. Ref 5

4.2. Arm Length Discrepancy: The Definitive Metric

Long-term ALD rates provide the most compelling differentiator between methods:

ALD Rates at Final Follow-up:
  • K-Wires: 19%
  • ESIN: 4%
  • Conservative: 9%

While not statistically significant (p=0.22), the magnitude (19% vs 4%) is profoundly clinically significant. Ref 15

This ALD data reconciles contradictions in the literature. The 19% risk for K-wires likely represents combination of: (1) true physeal damage, and (2) mechanical failure/malunion at fracture site, as evidenced by their lower radiological success rate (88% vs 96% for ESIN).

5. Secondary Surgery: Revision Rates and Hardware Removal

5.1. Revision Surgery for Treatment Failure

Unplanned revisions are significant complications. For plates, one study reported 12.6% re-intervention rate for complications like screw penetration or AVN. Ref 30

5.2. Planned Second Surgery: Routine Procedure

In adolescents, hardware removal is not a complication but a routine, planned part of treatment for all internal implant types. This differs significantly from adult orthopaedics.

Hardware Removal Protocols:
  • K-Wire (Exposed): Simple clinic removal at 3-4 weeks, but higher infection risk. Ref 45
  • K-Wire (Buried): Lower infection risk but 100% secondary surgery required. Ref 44
  • ESIN: ~90% planned re-operation rate. Nails left 1cm proud to facilitate removal. Ref 9, 36
  • Plate: Standard removal at 3-6 months. Second open surgery required. Ref 5

5.3. Clinical Trade-Off

Surgeons face a clear trade-off:

  • Strategy A (Exposed K-wires): High infection risk BUT avoids second general anesthetic.
  • Strategy B (Buried implants): Guaranteed second surgery BUT minimizes infection risk and provides more rigid fixation.

6. Evidence-Based Treatment Algorithm

6.1. Expert Synthesis and Recommendations

Step 1: Indication for Surgery

Required for severely displaced fractures (Neer-Horowitz Type III/IV, or angulation >30-45°) in adolescents >13 years. Non-operative management fails in 18% of cases; surgical fixation provides 95-98% success. Ref 14

Step 2: Primary Surgical Choice: ESIN

Evidence-based method of choice with 98% functional success, 96% radiological success, and lowest ALD risk (4%). Ref 4, 15

Step 3: Secondary Surgical Choice: K-Wire

Adequate alternative with 95% functional success but 19% ALD risk. Consider for surgeons with less ESIN experience. Ref 1, 21

Step 4: Niche/Tertiary Choice: Plate Osteosynthesis

Reserved for specific complex indications: irreducible fractures, highly comminuted patterns, or older adolescents nearing skeletal maturity. Ref 5, 17

6.2. Proposed Treatment Algorithm

Treatment Decision Flowchart:
  1. Assess Age & Displacement: <13 years OR <30° angulation/50% displacement → Non-operative management
  2. If Surgical Indication Met: Attempt closed reduction in OR
  3. If CR Successful:
    • 1st Choice: ESIN (best outcomes, 4% ALD risk)
    • 2nd Choice: K-wire (good outcomes, 19% ALD risk)
  4. If CR Fails (Irreducible):
    • 1st Choice: ORIF with plate fixation (rigid stability)
    • 2nd Choice: Open reduction + ESIN/K-wire
