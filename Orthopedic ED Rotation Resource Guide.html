Section I: Acute Clinical Assessment & Triage Rules

Efficiency in the Emergency Department depends on rapidly differentiating benign soft tissue injuries from significant structural pathology. Validated clinical decision rules are the orthopedic resident's first line of defense against unnecessary imaging and missed fractures.

1.1 The Ottawa Ankle and Midfoot Rules

Since their development in 1992, the Ottawa Ankle Rules (OAR) have become the global standard for determining the necessity of radiography in acute ankle and midfoot injuries. Their implementation has been shown to reduce unnecessary radiographs by 30–40% without compromising diagnostic safety.I–II

Ankle Series Indications

An ankle radiographic series is indicated only if there is pain in the malleolar zone and any one of:

  1. Bone tenderness at the posterior edge or tip of the lateral malleolus — palpate distal 6 cm of fibula; posterior aspect differentiates avulsion fractures from ligamentous sprains.
  2. Bone tenderness at the posterior edge or tip of the medial malleolus — palpate distal 6 cm of tibia; posterior edge tenderness is highly predictive of fracture.
  3. Inability to bear weight — inability to take four complete steps both immediately after injury and at time of ED evaluation. A limp is acceptable.

Midfoot (Foot) Series Indications

A foot radiographic series is indicated if there is pain in the midfoot zone and any one of:

  1. Bone tenderness at the base of the fifth metatarsal — site of peroneus brevis insertion; suggestive of pseudo-Jones or Jones fracture.
  2. Bone tenderness at the navicular — keystone of the medial longitudinal arch; missed fractures risk osteonecrosis.
  3. Inability to bear weight — functional proxy for midfoot structural integrity.
Diagnostic Performance Sensitivity: 96.4–100% (near-perfect; negative test essentially rules out fracture)I
Specificity: 26–46% (modest; high false-positive rate is an acceptable trade-off in triage)II
Negative Likelihood Ratio: ~0.08 (conclusive shift against fracture)
Gender variation: Specificity may be lower in males (~43%) vs. females (~56%), but sensitivity remains >92% across both genders.III

Pediatric Considerations

Recent evidence supports OAR use in children >5 years. A study of 144 subjects (including pediatric patients) found sensitivity and negative predictive value of 100%, with inability to walk four steps being the single most sensitive indicator.III

The Buffalo Rule Modification

Directs the clinician to palpate the crest or midportion of the malleoli rather than the posterior edge. Tenderness over the crest is more specific for bony injury. Demonstrated sensitivity 100% and specificity up to 59%.II

Section II: Soft Tissue Management & Laceration Repair

2.1 Timing of Closure

The historical "golden period" of 6–8 hours has been challenged. Current evidence supports:

  • Clean object wounds (non-infected): Primary closure safely performed up to 18–19 hours post-injury without increased infection rates.II
  • Face and scalp: Window extends to 24 hours (rich vascular supply).
Indications for Delayed Primary Closure Heavy contamination (soil, feces, organic matter) · Deep puncture wounds · High-velocity crush injuries · Presentation >24 hours · Patient comorbidities (diabetes, immunosuppression)

2.3 Irrigation & Infection Control

  • High-pressure irrigation (60 cc syringe with splash guard) is superior to soaking.II
  • Potable tap water is as effective as sterile saline for uncomplicated lacerations.II
  • Non-sterile gloves show no difference in infection rates vs. sterile surgical gloves for routine repair.II
  • Antibiotics: NOT indicated for simple lacerations. Reserved for mammalian bites, crush injuries, open fractures, and wounds involving tendons/joints.

Section III: The Science & Art of Immobilization

The fundamental principle: "Padding is protection, molding is reduction."

3.1 Material Science & Safety

⚠ Thermal Injury Prevention Both plaster and fiberglass harden via an exothermic reaction. Heat generation is proportional to water temperature and layer count. Always use cool to lukewarm water. Excessive layers (>12–15) or hot water can cause full-thickness burns. This is a never event.Not specified in source — general clinical knowledge

Material Comparison

  • Fiberglass: Sets in 3–5 min; lighter, stronger, less moldable.
  • Plaster of Paris: Sets in 5–8 min; heavier, significantly more moldable — preferred for initial reductions.

3.2 Upper Extremity Splints

Sugar Tong Splint (Forearm/Wrist)

Indications: Distal radius fractures, forearm shaft fractures. Prevents supination/pronation while immobilizing the wrist.

  1. Measure from dorsal MCP joints → dorsal forearm → around elbow → to volar palmar crease.
  2. Apply stockinette and 2–3 layers of cast padding (extra at elbow).
  3. Apply wet slab from dorsal knuckles, around posterior elbow, up volar forearm to palm.
  4. Position: Elbow at 90° flexion; wrist neutral to slight extension (10–20°); forearm neutral rotation.
  5. Mold: Apply interosseous mold — compress between radius and ulna with flat palms. Never squeeze with fingertips.

Volar Short Arm Splint

Indications: Carpal fractures, wrist soft tissue injuries, non-displaced distal radius fractures.

  1. Measure from distal palmar crease (proximal to MCP joints) to proximal third of forearm.
  2. Apply padding and wet slab to volar surface.
  3. Position: Wrist ~20° extension ("soda can" position). Mold distal end to support metacarpal arch.
  4. Pearl: Do not wrap rigid material circumferentially — dorsal aspect should be soft (Ace wrap only) for swelling.

Thumb Spica Splint

Indications: Scaphoid fractures, de Quervain's, first metacarpal fractures (Bennett's/Rolando's), UCL injuries.

  1. Position: "Wine glass" — wrist 20° extension, thumb abducted and extended.
  2. Wrap around radial forearm and encompass the thumb (radial gutter or sandwich style).
  3. Pitfall: Ensure thumb is NOT adducted against index finger → web space contracture risk.

3.3 Lower Extremity Splints

Posterior Short Leg Splint

Indications: Ankle fractures, severe sprains, foot fractures, Achilles injuries.

  1. Position: Prone with knee flexed 90° (gravity assists dorsiflexion) or supine with assistant holding toes.
  2. Measure from plantar metatarsal heads up posterior calf to fibular head level.
  3. Pad fibular head generously — common peroneal nerve is superficial here. End splint 2–3 cm distal. Pad calcaneus.
  4. Apply wet slab posteriorly; mold aggressively around malleoli and arch.
  5. Critical: Maintain ankle at 90° dorsiflexion (neutral) while material hardens. Equinus → Achilles shortening.
  6. Consider adding a stirrup (medial calf → under heel → lateral calf) for M-L stability.

Coaptation Splint (Humerus)

Indications: Humeral shaft fractures.

Runs axilla → medial arm → around elbow → lateral arm to acromion. Uses hydrostatic column pressure and gravity traction to align fragments. Heavy padding at axilla to prevent irritation.

3.4 Complications of Immobilization

⚠ Up to 93% of splints placed by non-orthopedic providers in pediatric settings were improper. The orthopedic resident must be the safeguard.III
  • Thermal injury — hot water or excessive layers.
  • Pressure ulcers — most common at heel, malleoli, ulnar styloid. Use "nurse's corners" and extra felt at prominences.
  • Compartment syndrome (iatrogenic) — tight Ace wrap restriction. Rule: Wrap distal → proximal with moderate tension. Never apply rigid material 360° acutely.

Section IV: Orthopedic Trauma & ATLS Protocols

The orthopedic resident is responsible for Circulation (C) via pelvic stabilization and Disability (D) through neurologic exams per ATLS.

4.1 Pelvic Ring Disruptions & Hemorrhage Control

Pelvic fractures, especially "open book" (AP compression) injuries, are a leading cause of hemorrhagic death in blunt trauma. The pelvic binder acts to mechanically reduce pelvic volume, tamponading venous bleeding.

⚠ Critical Application Error The most common mistake is placing the binder too high over the iliac crests, which can internally rotate the innominate bones and open the posterior pelvis further. The binder must be centered over the greater trochanters.Source: ATLS 10th Ed.

Application Steps

  1. Internally rotate lower limbs (tape feet together) to reduce pelvic volume.
  2. Slide binder under patient; center over greater trochanters.
  3. Tighten until "click" (commercial device) or firm tension to close diastasis.
  4. Remove or reassess within 24 hours to prevent trochanteric pressure ulcers.

4.3 Acute Compartment Syndrome (ACS)

ACS is a limb-threatening surgical emergency where elevated intracompartmental pressure compromises capillary perfusion.

The Classic "5 Ps" Are Unreliable Pain, Pallor, Paresthesia, Paralysis, Pulselessness often represent irreversible late-stage ischemia. Do not wait for all five.

Early Diagnostic Indicators

  • Pain out of proportion to injury — most sensitive early sign.
  • Pain with passive stretch of muscles in the affected compartment.

The Diagnostic Standard: Delta Pressure (ΔP)

For obtunded or equivocal cases, intracompartmental pressure measurement is mandatory:

ΔP = Diastolic BP − Intracompartmental Pressure

A ΔP ≤ 30 mmHg is the threshold for diagnosing ACS and indicates the need for emergent fasciotomy.I

This accounts for perfusion pressure relative to tissue pressure and is more reliable than absolute pressure readings in hypotensive patients.

Section V: The Checklist of "Missed Diagnoses"

The ED environment is prone to diagnostic errors. The following occult injuries carry disproportionately high morbidity and medicolegal risk.

5.1 Upper Extremity Occult Injuries

Scaphoid Fracture

The Trap Most common carpal fracture; occult on initial X-rays in up to 20%. High risk of non-union and avascular necrosis (retrograde blood supply from radial artery).
  • Clinical signs: Anatomical Snuffbox tenderness (Sensitivity 87–100%), scaphoid tubercle tenderness, pain with axial thumb loading.II
  • Action: If X-rays negative but clinical signs positive → treat as fracture. Apply thumb spica splint; arrange MRI/CT or repeat X-rays in 10–14 days.II

Posterior Shoulder Dislocation

The Trap <5% of dislocations; missed in up to 50% of initial evaluations. Associated with seizures or electric shock.
  • Lightbulb Sign: Humeral head in internal rotation — symmetrical, rounded.Not specified
  • Rim Sign: Glenohumeral joint space >6 mm.
  • Trough Line: Vertical dense line (reverse Hill-Sachs lesion).
  • Definitive view: Axillary lateral or Velpeau — mandatory.

Perilunate & Lunate Dislocation

  • Spilled Teacup Sign (Lateral): Lunate tilts volar, resembling a spilled cup.
  • Piece of Pie Sign (AP): Lunate appears triangular due to rotation.
  • Gilula's Arcs: Disruption of carpal arcs on AP view.

Triquetral Fracture

Pooping Duck Sign (Lateral): Dorsal avulsion fragment posterior to lunate/triquetrum — resembles "poop" behind the "duck."

5.2 Lower Extremity Occult Injuries

Lisfranc Injury (Tarsometatarsal Dislocation)

The Trap Dismissed as "midfoot sprain." Mechanism: axial load on plantarflexed foot. The Lisfranc ligament (base of 2nd MT → medial cuneiform) lacks transverse intermetatarsal support.
  • Fleck Sign: Pathognomonic bony avulsion between 1st and 2nd metatarsal bases.Not specified
  • Alignment: Medial border of 2nd MT should align with medial border of middle cuneiform on AP.
  • Action: Weight-bearing films (if tolerated) often needed to reveal instability.

Segond Fracture

Small vertical avulsion of the lateral tibial plateau. Pathognomonic for ACL tear (75–100% association). Implies significant rotatory instability. Document complete knee exam and refer for MRI.II

Section VI: The Resident Consultant

6.1 The Art of the Consult

The 5 Cs of Consultation:

  1. Contact — Who is calling?
  2. Communicating — Patient details (age, mechanism, vitals).
  3. Core Question — What do they need?
  4. Collaboration — Joint plan.
  5. Closing the Loop — Confirm the plan and follow-up.
Consult Etiquette Always visualize the patient and images yourself. Never refuse a consult or disparage the ED physician. Acknowledge urgent consults (open fractures, dislocations, compartment syndrome) immediately.Source: Expert consensus

6.2 Documentation & Medicolegal Protection

  • Neurovascular Status: Document NV status before and after every reduction and splint application. "Neurovascularly intact distally" (NVID) is the most critical phrase.
  • Negative Findings: Explicitly document absence of compartment syndrome signs ("Calves soft, compartments compressible, pain proportionate").
  • Consent: Document informed consent for reductions — risks of sedation, nerve injury, failure of reduction.
