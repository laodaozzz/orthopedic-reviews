1. Introduction: The Evolving Paradigm of Proximal Femoral Trauma

The management of proximal femoral fractures—encompassing both intertrochanteric and subtrochanteric morphological patterns—represents one of the most complex, resource-intensive, and biologically demanding challenges within contemporary orthopedic trauma surgery. As the global population ages, the absolute incidence of these high-morbidity fragility fractures is projected to double by 2050. Level I

Recent epidemiological data reveals an alarming operative mortality rate of 17.47% following femoral intertrochanteric fractures, which, while severe, remains markedly superior to the devastating 34.6% mortality rate observed in cohorts managed non-operatively with conservative measures. Level III Consequently, surgical stabilization is not merely a functional intervention—it is a critical, life-saving imperative.

🔑 Core Paradigm Shift (2021–2026) The historical pursuit of absolute anatomical reduction has been superseded by a philosophy of morphology-specific biomechanical stabilization. Achieving a stable, load-sharing construct—even if intentionally non-anatomical—provides a vastly superior mechanical environment for secondary bone healing than an unstable anatomical reduction in osteoporotic geriatric bone.

The AO/OTA classification system serves as the fundamental framework. Intertrochanteric fractures (AO/OTA 31-A) span a gradient of instability: simple stable two-part fractures with an intact lateral cortex (31-A1), unstable multifragmentary patterns with detached posteromedial fragments (31-A2), and completely incompetent lateral wall with reverse obliquity (31-A3). Level V – Expert Opinion Subtrochanteric fractures (AO/OTA 32) occupy the adjacent diaphyseal-metaphyseal junction but exhibit distinctly more aggressive biomechanical behaviors due to the profound deforming forces of the pelvifemoral musculature. Level V – Textbook

2. Anatomical Constraints & Biomechanics of Deformity

The proximal femur is governed by a complex array of muscular attachments that exert massive deforming forces on fracture fragments the moment structural continuity is lost. In both low intertrochanteric and subtrochanteric fractures, the proximal fragment undergoes a characteristic "flexion, abduction, and external rotation" deformity. Level V – Textbook

The inability to neutralize these forces through closed manipulation on a fracture table is the primary reason why advanced reduction techniques and specialized implant constructs have become strictly necessary.

3. Evolution of Reduction Theories: Positive Cortical Support

3.1 Positive Medial Cortical Support (PMCS)

As the demographic profile of hip fracture patients has skewed older and more osteoporotic, the limitations of anatomical reduction have become apparent. When osteoporotic bone is reduced anatomically, the medial cortices are aligned but the low bone density causes the cortices to crush into the cancellous void under axial loading. This "negative support" environment results in the head-neck fragment displacing laterally or posteriorly, placing the entire load-bearing burden onto the metallic implant. Level V – Biomechanical

The orthopedic community has heavily validated Positive Medial Cortical Support (PMCS) as a superior, intentionally non-anatomical alternative. In a PMCS configuration, the medial cortex of the head-neck fragment is positioned superomedially to the medial cortex of the femoral shaft, physically resting upon the rigid cortical bone of the distal shaft. This establishes a structural buttress that facilitates controlled sliding and impaction along the lag-screw axis. Level V – Biomechanical / Clinical Guideline

📐 2025 Finite Element Analysis – Key Findings A highly detailed FEA study (2025) established 25 computerized models of intramedullary nail fixation for AO/OTA 31-A1.2 fractures using CT scans from a 65-year-old volunteer, subjected to joint reaction forces up to 2,967.7 N. Level V – FEA
  • Transitioning from negative to PMCS fundamentally altered stress distribution across the bone-implant construct.
  • Under negative support, nail stress concentrated at the proximal intersection (lag screw–nail junction) – the site most prone to fatigue fracture.
  • PMCS decreased proximal intersection stress while increasing distal intersection stress, distributing the load over a larger surface area via lever mechanics.
  • PMCS produced a steep decrease in von Mises stress at the fracture surface, minimizing pathological displacement and promoting callus formation.

3.2 Anterior Cortical Support (ACS)

Sagittal plane alignment is equally critical. The CRQC guidelines strictly require that acceptable reduction on lateral views must demonstrate smooth anterior cortical support (ACS), with translational displacement <50% of the total anterior cortex thickness. Not specified in source – guideline cited

When the head-neck fragment was positioned anteriorly to gain positive ACS in the 2025 FEA models, the overall stress load on the entire fixation system plummeted. The modern reduction algorithm therefore dictates: slight valgus angulation + positive medial, anteromedial, and anterior cortical support. This configuration is no longer a compromise but the definitively superior mechanical alternative for osteoporotic patients. Level V – Biomechanical / Clinical Guideline

4. Implant Selection: Navigating the Intramedullary Landscape

4.1 Intramedullary Nailing as Gold Standard

Intramedullary nailing is now universally acknowledged as the gold standard for unstable intertrochanteric (AO/OTA 31-A2, A3) and subtrochanteric fractures. Level I Cephalomedullary nails (CMNs) provide a significantly shorter lever arm than side-plate devices, transferring weight-bearing forces more directly down the mechanical axis.

4.3 Construct Length: Spanning vs. Non-Spanning Fixation

A high-impact, multicenter retrospective study (JBJS, August 2025) evaluated 913 patients (median age 80) with pertrochanteric and intertrochanteric fragility fractures (388 type 31-A1, 324 type 31-A2, and highly unstable variants). Level III – Multicenter Retrospective Cohort

📊 Key Finding: Spanning Fixation Eliminates Peri-Implant Fractures
  • Spanning (long) nails: 0% cumulative incidence of peri-implant fractures at 1 year
  • Non-spanning fixation: 3.3% incidence of secondary fractures at implant tip
  • Short non-spanning plates: 3.5% failure; Short non-spanning nails: 2.6% failure
  • Hazard ratio for spanning fixation: 0.14 (p < 0.01) – massive risk reduction
  • Contralateral fracture rates were not significantly different between groups (p = 0.77), confirming biomechanical superiority rather than selection bias

Helical Blade Performance: Equivocal Evidence

Biomechanical analyses using graded polyurethane (PUR) foam blocks simulating normal and osteoporotic bone densities found that a traditional threaded screw generates 177% more pull-out resistance in normal density and 198% more resistance in low-density (osteoporotic) foam compared to a helical blade. Level V – Biomechanical Clinical registry data showed no statistically significant differences between the two regarding mechanical failure (OR=0.71, p=0.25), reduction quality, or Harris Hip Scores. Level III However, subgroup analyses suggest the helical blade may perform better in healthier (ASA 1–2) geriatric patients, while in severe osteoporosis, it is susceptible to migration and back-out. 5. Biomaterial Augmentation: Mechanics Meets Biology

5.1 Polymethyl-methacrylate (PMMA) for Head Element Purchase

PMMA bone cement is a highly rigid, non-resorbable acrylic polymer. It is specifically indicated when securing the head element within osteoporotic bone is the principal mechanical challenge. It is recommended for frail elderly patients with unstable AO/OTA A2–A3 patterns with loss of the medial buttress, or when soft, devoid cancellous bone is encountered during reaming. Level not specified in source – narrative review

⚠ Critical Surgical Mandates for PMMA Augmentation
  • Augment only after sound fracture reduction (PMCS + slight valgus) and ideal head-element placement (low TAD, center-inferior AP, neutral lateral) are confirmed
  • Inject 2–6 mL through fenestrated helical blade or lag screw at medium viscosity transition
  • Continuous biplanar fluoroscopy is mandatory – halt immediately if linear spread toward joint space or venous channels is detected
  • Pre-injection contrast mapping recommended to visualize vascular architecture

Meta-analyses report 35–55% absolute reduction in mechanical failure rates and 30–50% reduction in cut-out signals, with patients progressing to full weight-bearing 3–7 days earlier. Level not specified – narrative review A prospective study noted 100% union rate at 13.6-month follow-up with zero implant failures. Level IV – Case Series

Health economic analysis (Markov model, German payer perspective) demonstrated that PMMA augmentation is a dominant economic strategy: absolute long-term cost savings of €50.3/patient and incremental QALY gains of 0.01/patient, as reduced revision surgery costs fully offset initial material expenditure. Economic analysis – level not classified in source

5.2 Calcium-Phosphate (CaP) for Medial Buttress Restoration

CaP cements (e.g., β-tricalcium phosphate) serve as biological void fillers with lower compressive strength (20–60 MPa vs. PMMA's 70–100 MPa). They are indicated for filling discrete metaphyseal structural defects at the calcar or lesser trochanter. CaP is osteoconductive and resorbable, gradually replaced by host bone over 6–12 months. Level not specified – narrative review

🚫 "No-Fly Zone" – Sliding Corridor The surgeon must deliberately avoid the geometric path the head element traverses during collapse/compression. Over-packing the void and cementing the sliding mechanism will force hardware cut-out under physiological loads. CaP should be placed as a structural strut outside the sliding corridor, reducing mean telescoping by ~2–4 mm and preventing extreme varus collapse.

6. Advanced Surgical & Reduction Techniques

6.1 Percutaneous Joystick & Soft Tissue Rebalancing

When longitudinal traction fails to correct severe sagittal plane flexion deformity, the percutaneous "joystick technique" involves inserting a Schanz pin, Steinmann pin, or Kelly clamp into the anterior cortex of the proximal fragment through a posterolateral stab incision, using it as a lever to manually push the flexed segment downward and internally rotate it. Level not specified – narrative review

The "soft tissue rebalancing technique" refines these principles using hemostatic forceps inserted through minimal incisions, using the surrounding intact soft tissue envelope as a fulcrum. Clinical studies report excellent functional outcomes with average LEFS score of 60.4 at final follow-up, while significantly minimizing operative time, soft tissue devascularization, and blood loss compared to open clamping. Level III – Comparative Study

6.2 Cerclage Wiring: Myths Debunked, Risks Quantified

Recent histological, anatomical, and ex-vivo biomechanical studies have definitively debunked the long-held "strangulation myth" – applying cerclage wire at physiological tensions causes no significant periosteal compression or measurable reduction in microvascular perfusion. Biomechanical – level not classified Clinical studies confirm that limited open reduction + intramedullary nailing + steel cable cerclage (for Seinsheimer III fractures) provides faster bone healing and improved early functional recovery. Level IV

⚠ Dose-Dependent Biological Risk of Cerclage Wires A 2025 dual-center retrospective analysis (148 patients, 2015–2025) demonstrated a highly significant, dose-dependent escalation in persistent wound drainage (>5 days) and deep surgical site infection as the number of cerclage wires increased, independent of surgical duration. The massive cumulative surface area of multiple cables provides an ideal niche for bacterial biofilm formation. Level III – Retrospective Cohort
Consensus: Deploy the absolute minimum number of wires required for mechanical stability.

6.3 Lateral Wall Reconstruction & Novel Nail-Plate Constructs

The lateral femoral wall acts as the primary stabilizing buttress for controlled lag-screw sliding. Preoperative 3D CT is now mandated. A critical thickness threshold of <20.5–21 mm (measured 3 cm below the vastus lateralis ridge at 135°) is an independent predictor of postoperative lateral wall blowout. Level III When DHS is deployed in lateral wall fractures, the reoperation rate skyrockets to 22% vs. 3% with intact lateral walls. Level III

The revolutionary Proximal Femoral Universal Nail (PFUN) system, engineered for AO/OTA 31-A3.3 fractures, integrates a standard CMN with a contoured lateral plate secured to the nail via transverse interlocking nuts, plus a dedicated lesser trochanteric screw. Level V – FEA

🔬 PFUN vs. Standard PFNA (FEA Results)
  • Maximum von Mises stress in proximal femur: 85.81% lower in PFUN (26.13 MPa vs. 48.55 MPa)
  • Peak stress at fracture ends: 27.27 MPa (PFUN) vs. 49.95 MPa (PFNA)
  • Total structural displacement: >15% reduced (13.81 mm vs. 15.93 mm)

7. Subtrochanteric Fractures: Expert Consensus & Unique Challenges

Subtrochanteric fractures occupy the exact transition between spongy trabecular bone and dense cortical bone, subjected to the absolute highest compressive and tensile bending moments in the entire skeleton. They are notorious for high rates of delayed union, non-union, and hardware fatigue failure. Level V – Textbook

7.2 Atypical Femoral Fractures (AFFs)

AFFs are insidious, atraumatic injuries directly associated with prolonged use of anti-resorptive medications (bisphosphonates, denosumab). Pathogenesis involves long-term suppression of osteoclastic remodeling, leading to silent accumulation of micro-damage in the lateral cortex of the subtrochanteric region. 8. Conclusion

The literature published between 2021 and 2026 has established a definitive, evidence-based treatment algorithm for proximal femoral fragility fractures. Key principles include:

  1. PMCS and ACS as biomechanically superior alternatives to anatomical reduction in osteoporotic bone
  2. Intramedullary CMNs with IDLS head elements and spanning (long) constructs as the gold standard
  3. Targeted biomaterial augmentation (PMMA for head-element purchase; CaP for medial buttress restoration)
  4. Minimally invasive reduction techniques (soft tissue rebalancing, percutaneous joystick) with judicious cerclage utilization
  5. Quantitative lateral wall assessment (threshold <20.5–21 mm) guiding implant selection and reconstruction strategies
  6. Multidisciplinary AFF management integrating curvature-matched long nails with urgent anabolic pharmacological rescue
