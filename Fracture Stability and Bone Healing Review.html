Biomechanics & Biology of Fracture Fixation

An Exhaustive Evidence-Based Review of Stability and Bone Healing · Source: User-Provided Literature Review (27 references)

1. Introduction & Global Epidemiological Burden

The human skeletal system comprises 206 distinct bones accounting for approximately 15% of total body weight.LoE V By volume, mature bone is a composite of 36% inorganic mineral (crystalline hydroxyapatite → compressive strength), 36% organic matrix (Type I collagen → tensile flexibility), and 28% water.LoE V

⚠ Staggering Global Burden Fracture incidence ranges from 2–5 per 100 person-years, with >178 million new cases annually worldwide. Roughly 200 million individuals have osteoporosis; one in three women over 50 will sustain a fragility fracture. Hip fractures carry a 1-year mortality approaching 30% and a cost of illness exceeding $30,000 per patient.LoE III

Dual demographic forces drive the rising tide: high-energy road traffic accidents and the expanding geriatric population with osteoporotic fragility fractures.LoE IV Optimizing surgical fracture management to ensure rapid, reliable, and mechanically sound bone healing is therefore a life-saving medical imperative.LoE V

2. Historical Evolution of the AO Principles

The modern science of fracture management is inextricably linked to the Arbeitsgemeinschaft für Osteosynthesefragen (AO Foundation).LoE V For centuries, conservative treatment—external plaster casting or prolonged skeletal traction—was standard, but these methods failed to control rotational instability and precipitated lethal systemic complications (DVT, PE, hypostatic pneumonia, muscular atrophy).LoE IV

The Paradigm Shift

Mid-20th century AO pioneers introduced open reduction and stable internal fixation (ORIF). The early phase prioritized perfect anatomical reduction and massive metallic implants for absolute mechanical stability—any micro-motion was deemed detrimental.LoE III

However, long-term data revealed a severe biological cost: extensive surgical stripping devascularized bone, causing cortical necrosis and susceptibility to fracture-related infections (FRI).LoE IV This catalyzed the evolution to "biological osteosynthesis"—acknowledging that mechanical, biomechanical, and biological requirements must exist in equilibrium.LoE III

The Four Modern Pillars

1

Fracture Reduction

Anatomical for articular; functional (length, alignment, rotation) for diaphyseal.

2

3. Damage Control Orthopedics & Soft Tissue Management

In polytrauma, Damage Control Orthopedics (DCO) serves as the life-saving first stage when definitive fixation is contraindicated.LoE III

Indications for DCO

  • Inability to be adequately resuscitated (ongoing massive fluid/blood transfusion requirements)
  • Persistent hypothermia and clinical coagulopathy
  • Severe metabolic derangements: high/worsening base deficit and elevated serum lactate
  • Severe traumatic brain injury
  • Severe pulmonary dysfunction requiring extensive ventilatory support
ℹ External Fixation in DCO Applied rapidly via percutaneous pins, external fixators bridge the zone of injury, minimize ongoing soft tissue trauma, decrease fat embolism risk, and facilitate patient transfer.LoE III

Staged Approach & MIPO

Only after soft tissue swelling has definitively subsided (indicated by reappearance of normal skin wrinkles) should definitive stabilization be performed. Modern practice increasingly uses Minimal Invasive Percutaneous Osteosynthesis (MIPO) to preserve the soft tissue envelope.LoE III

4. Absolute vs. Relative Stability

Stability is a continuous biomechanical spectrum. The degree of interfragmentary motion (IFM) directly dictates the biological healing pathway.LoE III

Definitions

Absolute stability: Complete abolition of all movement at the fracture site under physiological loading, achieved exclusively through active interfragmentary compression. Leads to direct (primary) bone healing.LoE III

Relative stability: Controlled, elastic, reversible movement permitted at the fracture site via splinting or bridging. Acts as a biological stimulus for callus formation. Leads to secondary (indirect) bone healing.LoE III

5. Perren's Interfragmentary Strain Theory

Stephan Perren formulated the seminal Interfragmentary Strain Theory, positing that healing tissue can only survive and differentiate within specific mathematical boundaries of mechanical strain.LoE III

Tissue Strain Tolerance Hierarchy

Fibrous connective tissue~17%Early soft callus
Dense fibrocartilage~10%Hard callus precursor
Lamellar bone~2%Final mineralized matrix

The 2–10% Strain Rule

✓ Classic Clinical Heuristic <2% strain → Primary (direct) bone healing via intramembranous ossification.
2–10% strain → Secondary (indirect) bone healing via endochondral ossification.
>10% strain → Persistent tissue rupture → hypertrophic non-union risk.LoE III

Modern Reframing (2020–2026)

Recent finite element analysis and in vivo microCT studies have challenged the rigid mathematical boundaries. Controlled ovine tibial osteotomy models demonstrated successful healing at gap-closing strains averaging 51%—directly contradicting the >10% = non-union dogma.LoE II

The resolution lies in distinct cellular responses: tensile (deviatoric) strain drives fibrous phenotypes; hydrostatic pressure (compression) drives chondrogenic differentiation. Shear conditions can facilitate healing in specific environments.LoE II

6. Primary (Direct) Bone Healing

Achieved when absolute stability suppresses strain below 2%. No external callus forms; healing occurs via intramembranous ossification and direct Haversian remodeling.LoE III

⚠ High-Stakes Biomechanics Early soft-tissue strength is ~1,000× less than intact cortical bone. Without external callus support, 100% of mechanical integrity depends on the implant. If biological healing is delayed, the implant will catastrophically fail from fatigue.LoE III

Key Signaling Cascade

  • Hematoma → hypoxic, acidic microenvironment → chemotactic signaling
  • Macrophages + platelets release TGF-β, PDGF, VEGF → recruit mesenchymal stem cells (MSCs)
  • 2–10% strain environment → MSCs differentiate to chondrocytes + fibroblasts → fibrocartilaginous soft callus
  • Radial callus expansion → ↑ area moment of inertia → strain drops below 2% → endochondral ossification → woven bone hard callus

Remodeling is governed by Wolff's Law—bone adapts to mechanical loads. At the biophysical level, piezoelectric charges generated by deformation of hydroxyapatite and collagen drive targeted remodeling:LoE III

9. The Lag Screw Principle

The most fundamental method of generating interfragmentary compression. Its singular function: forcefully compress one bone fragment against another.LoE III

Execution Requirements

  • Gliding hole (near cortex): drilled with a bit equal to or larger than the screw's outer thread diameter → threads bypass the near cortex.
  • Thread hole (far cortex): drilled with a bit matching the screw's core diameter → threads engage firmly.
  • As the screw is tightened, the head seats against the near cortex (pushing forward) while threads pull the far fragment backward → dynamic clamping → high-load compression.LoE III
⚠ Drill Trajectory Is Critical The drill path must be perpendicular (orthogonal) to the fracture plane. An oblique insertion creates a severe shear vector → iatrogenic malreduction.LoE III

Neutralization Plate Protection

A standalone lag screw offers negligible resistance to bending/torsion. It must be protected by a neutralization plate applied 90° orthogonal to the screw trajectory to minimize core stresses.LoE III

10. Compression & Buttress Plating

Dynamic Compression Plate (DCP)

For transverse diaphyseal fractures lacking surface area for lag screws. The plate holes feature inclined ramps; eccentric screw placement drives the plate across the bone, slamming fracture ends together under axial compression.LoE III

Critical Technique: Pre-Contouring ("Overbending")

A perfectly contoured plate concentrates compression on the near cortex, hinging open the far cortex. The plate must be overbent so its center stands off 1–2 mm over the fracture line. This pre-stress acts as a leaf spring, driving the far cortex together first → uniform circumferential compression.LoE III

The Axilla Concept (Oblique Fractures)

Plate positioned over the apex of the mobile fragment. Initial screws fix the plate to the stable fragment, forming a rigid "axilla." The eccentric compression screw drives the mobile fragment into this block → compresses while preventing shear.LoE III

Buttress (Antiglide) Plating

For shear/vertical split metaphyseal fractures. Deliberately under-contoured by 1–2 mm; when secured with neutral-mode bicortical screws, the spring effect applies constant counter-pressure against shear displacement. Often supplemented with through-plate lag screws.LoE III

11. Intramedullary Nailing

The biomechanical gold standard for relative stability in major diaphyseal fractures. Positioned within the central mechanical axis, the IM nail experiences vastly smaller bending moments than eccentric plates.LoE III

✓ Key Advantage: Immediate Weight-Bearing Patients can typically weight-bear as tolerated immediately post-op, generating the exact axial micro-motion needed for rapid callus formation. Plate constructs require limited weight-bearing (max 15 kg) for 8–12 weeks.LoE III

Poller (Blocking) Screws

In wide metaphyseal regions ("bell clapper" effect), standard nailing fails to prevent angulation. Poller screws are driven into the medullary space adjacent to the nail path, acting as artificial cortices to force central nail alignment.LoE III

12. Bridge Plating Principles

When the medullary canal is unavailable (prior hardware, narrow/deformed canals), bridge plating achieves relative stability extramedullarily. A long plate is slid submuscularly via MIPO, anchoring only the proximal and distal segments while the comminuted zone remains undisturbed.LoE III
