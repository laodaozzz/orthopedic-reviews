Beyond immediate acute pain, OVCFs alter spinal alignment, precipitate kyphotic deformity, compromise pulmonary function, and increase the risk of subsequent adjacent segment fractures (ASFs) and mortality [1].

While conservative management—comprising analgesia, orthotic bracing, and targeted physical rehabilitation—remains foundational for stable fractures, percutaneous vertebral augmentation (PVA) has revolutionized interventional management of intractable fracture-related pain. Techniques such as percutaneous vertebroplasty (PVP) and percutaneous kyphoplasty (PKP) involve fluoroscopically guided injection of polymethylmethacrylate (PMMA) bone cement into the collapsed vertebral body, stabilizing trabecular microfractures and providing immediate mechanical support [4].

This comprehensive literature review synthesizes current Level I and Level II evidence to address three critical clinical domains in OVCF management:

  1. Natural history of bone marrow edema (BME) on magnetic resonance imaging
  2. Biomechanical imperatives of cement distribution patterns
  3. Evidence-based diagnostic algorithm for post-augmentation residual pain

2 Natural History of Bone Marrow Edema on MRI

2.1 Pathophysiology & Radiological Principles

Bone marrow edema in the context of an acute OVCF represents a complex histological and physiological cascade. Axial compressive forces exceeding the load-bearing capacity of the osteoporotic trabecular network induce microfractures, focal hemorrhage, localized ischemia, and a robust inflammatory response [7]. This response is characterized by lymphocytic infiltration, fibrovascular granulation tissue formation, and increased interstitial fluid within the marrow cavity [8].

🔬 MRI Signal Characteristics

BME is identified by hypointense signals on T1-weighted imaging and hyperintense signals on T2-weighted, STIR, or T2 fat-suppressed sequences. It is the definitive diagnostic marker of an acute or subacute fracture, distinguishing active pathology from chronic, healed deformities [7].

Level II retrospective studies demonstrate that the morphological pattern of low-signal intensity on early T1-weighted images correlates directly with subsequent vertebral deformity types. Diffuse low-signal intensity patterns are highly predictive of:

  • Severe vertebral body height loss
  • Development of vertebral osteonecrosis
  • Manifestation of crush-type deformities
Level II Evidence

2.2 BME Resolution: Conservative vs. Post-PVP Management

The temporal evolution of bone marrow edema diverges significantly depending on the selected therapeutic modality.

Conservative Therapy — 0–12 weeks

Typically managed with hyperextension bracing, controlled bed rest, and early antiresorptive therapy. BME demonstrates a gradual, relatively linear reduction. Prospective Level II data indicates significant reductions in mean BME area at 60 and 90 days post-injury [10].

Conservative Therapy — 3–9 months

Complete resolution of the hyperintense STIR signal is rarely observed within the first three months, underscoring the protracted nature of trabecular remodeling and osteogenesis. BME reduction correlates directly with clinical symptom improvement (VAS, ODI) [10].

Post-PVP — <72 hours

PMMA injection induces immediate mechanical stabilization, preventing further trabecular friction. Exothermic polymerization causes localized thermal necrosis of neurovascular and inflammatory tissues. Pain relief is profound and immediate [12].

Post-PVP — 3–12 months

BME gradually decreases; however, approximately 29% of treated vertebrae still exhibit residual BME at 12 months. Once BME completely resolves post-PVP, it does not return unless a secondary fracture occurs [4].

⚠️ Critical Third-Order Insight: Clinical-Radiological Dissociation

While conservatively managed patients exhibit a positive correlation between edema reduction and pain relief, patients undergoing PVP experience a decoupling of these variables. Pain relief post-PVP achieves maximum reduction within 3 months and remains constant, while BME persists independently. The gradual decrease in BME post-PVP is wholly unrelated to pain relief, indicating that elimination of mechanical micro-motion is the primary driver of analgesia, rather than cessation of inflammatory edema [4].

2.3 Clinical Guidelines: Persistent STIR Signal at 4–5 Months Post-Injury

A recurring clinical paradox occurs when an asymptomatic or mildly symptomatic patient at 4–5 months post-injury demonstrates persistent STIR hyperintensity but CT-confirmed cortical sclerosis indicating mature osteoblastic activity and structural union.

The persistence of STIR hyperintensity in the presence of CT-confirmed sclerosis does not indicate non-union, pseudarthrosis, or active fracture. Instead, it represents highly vascularized woven bone undergoing active mechanotransduction and delayed micro-architectural remodeling [8]. This phenomenon parallels "asymptomatic bone marrow edema" (aBME) observed in elite athletes and military trainees, representing a physiological adaptation to sustained biomechanical stress [8].

✅ Evidence-Based Recommendations for Persistent BME with CT Sclerosis
  • Prolonged immobilization is contraindicated — Level I evidence demonstrates extended bed rest exacerbates sarcopenia, accelerates disuse osteopenia, increases DVT risk, and contributes to higher long-term mortality [16]
  • Full, unprotected weight-bearing should be actively encouraged for proper Wolff's Law-directed trabecular realignment [3]
  • Return to manual labor is permissible through a graded, phase-based rehabilitation program focusing on paraspinal, multifidus, and core musculature strengthening [3]
  • Pharmacological optimization: Bisphosphonates (e.g., zoledronic acid) or anabolic agents (e.g., teriparatide) to support trabecular mineralization [3]
  • Restrict chronic NSAID use — can inhibit prostaglandin synthesis essential for final osteogenesis, potentially delaying osseous integration [3]
Level I Evidence

3 Biomechanical Imperatives of Cement Distribution

Standard PMMA preparations exhibit an elastic modulus of 1700–3700 MPa and compressive strength of 85–114 MPa — exponentially higher than surrounding osteoporotic cancellous bone (elastic modulus: 10–900 MPa; compressive strength: 0.1–15 MPa) [18]. The augmented vertebra behaves as a rigid composite material, and the spatial distribution of this rigid cement mantle dictates long-term biomechanical success.

3.1 The Necessity of "Crossing the Midline"

Contemporary Finite Element Analysis (FEA) and cadaveric biomechanical models (Level II evidence) have definitively established that geometric pattern of distribution supersedes total volume in ensuring long-term structural stability [20].

❌ Blocky/Unilateral Distribution
Cement fails to cross sagittal midline → severe stress riser, asymmetric stiffness, accelerated micro-architectural collapse
✅ Symmetrical/Diffuse Distribution
Cement successfully crosses midline → balanced stiffness, superior resistance to failure, neutral coronal alignment
Level II Evidence — FEA & Cadaveric Studies [21]

3.2 Endplate-to-Endplate Distribution

In addition to coronal symmetry, the sagittal distribution of cement is paramount. "Endplate-to-endplate" distribution means PMMA makes direct structural contact with both superior and inferior cortical endplates [23].

If cement is deposited solely within the central trabecular void without endplate contact, the mechanical load must still pass through fragile osteoporotic cancellous bone. This results in progressive trabecular subsidence, loss of kyphotic correction, and late-onset height loss [27].

When cement achieves contact with both endplates, it acts as an internal structural pillar, directly transferring axial compressive loads safely through the rigid cement mantle, bypassing weakened cancellous bone. This significantly reduces the incidence of refracture and optimizes long-term functional prognosis [26].

🦴 Advanced Implant Technology

Third-generation PVA systems using expandable titanium mesh cages or stents (e.g., SpineJack) mechanically compress trabecular bone to restore height and create a defined cavity that ensures subsequent cement injection uniformly bridges superior and inferior endplates [5].

The superiority of the unilateral approach depends entirely on precise trajectory execution. The standard unilateral transpedicular approach restricts medial angulation, frequently depositing cement only in the ipsilateral hemi-vertebra [33].

The unilateral extrapedicular approach (UEA) overcomes this by establishing a more lateral skin entry point with a steeper medial trajectory, placing the cannula tip directly at or past the sagittal midline. When correctly executed, UEA delivers biomechanical stiffness, strength restoration, and protection against late-onset collapse that is functionally equivalent to bilateral transpedicular augmentation [33].

⚠️ Critical Technical Requirement

Current evidence dictates that unilateral extrapedicular augmentation is the preferred clinical modality, strictly contingent upon the trajectory successfully directing cement across the midline. This requires meticulous pre-operative CT or MRI planning [35].

4 Diagnostic Algorithm for Residual Pain

Despite high procedural efficacy, 11.5% to 16.3% of patients experience residual, persistent, or recurrent moderate-to-severe back pain following PVA [40].

4.1 Differential Etiology Spectrum

When a patient presents with persistent pain following "successful" PVP, four primary etiologies must be systematically differentiated [42]:

A
Adjacent Segment Fractures (ASFs) The rigid PMMA bolus transfers increased mechanical stress to adjacent endplates. Presents as new-onset, acute, severe mechanical pain weeks to months post-procedure. Diagnosis confirmed by new BME (high STIR signal) at adjacent level [45].
B
Facet Joint Osteoarthritis (Facetogenic Pain) Uncorrected kyphotic deformity shifts weight-bearing loads posteriorly onto facet joints. Described as axial, paramedian pain exacerbated by extension and lateral rotation [42].
C
Sacroiliac Joint (SIJ) Dysfunction Altered lumbopelvic parameters from thoracic/lumbar fractures overload the sacroiliac complex. Presents as referred pain to buttock, groin, or posterior thigh, mimicking radiculopathy [44].
D
Insufficient Contralateral Support & Micro-Instability Sub-optimal cement distribution (unilateral, uncrossed, blocky) leaves the unaugmented hemi-vertebra compromised. Disparate modulus of elasticity drives persistent micromotion at the cement-bone interface, forming an intravertebral fluid cleft — a localized pseudarthrosis or Kummell's disease [50].

4.3 Diagnostic Block Techniques

Before attributing residual pain to micro-instability, evidence-based algorithms mandate ruling out facet joint and SIJ pathology using provocative diagnostic blocks [44].

Medial Branch Blocks (MBB) for Facetogenic Pain

Under fluoroscopic guidance, a low volume (≤0.5 mL) of short-acting local anesthetic (1% lidocaine) is injected at the junction of the superior articular process and transverse process [47].

📋 Positive Block Criteria

≥80% relief of primary index pain during the anesthetic window, with restored ability to perform previously painful movements. A dual-block paradigm (using bupivacaine on a separate occasion) eliminates false-positive placebo responses. Positive dual blocks → radiofrequency ablation (RFA) indicated [55].

Sacroiliac Joint Provocative Injections

Indicated when ≥3 positive provocative maneuvers (FABER/Patrick's test, Thigh Thrust, Gaenslen's test, SIJ Distraction) are present. Fluoroscopically guided intra-articular injection of local anesthetic ± corticosteroid into the inferior SIJ capsule [44]. Significant immediate relief confirms SIJ syndrome.

4.4 Contralateral Revision Strategy

If ASFs are excluded on MRI and diagnostic blocks are negative, residual pain is isolated to the index vertebra. With imaging confirmation of uncrossed cement + active vacuum cleft → diagnosis of symptomatic micro-instability is established.

✅ Definitive Treatment: Contralateral Revision Augmentation

A secondary percutaneous access via the contralateral pedicle injects high-viscosity PMMA directly into the intravertebral cleft and remaining void. The new cement interdigitates with the original mass, abolishing pathological micromotion and locking the pseudarthrosis [45].

Outcomes: Mean VAS reduction >5 points, ODI reduction >40 points. Safety profile comparable to primary augmentation — no increase in leakage, infection, or neurological events [58].

Level III–IV Evidence

5 Conclusion

The clinical management of OVCFs demands rigorous synthesis of biomechanical engineering principles, physiological remodeling data, and precise diagnostic pathways. Key principles include:

  1. BME persistence at 4–5 months with CT sclerosis represents benign, active remodeling — not pathological fragility — permitting safe graded return to mobilization
  2. Unilateral extrapedicular augmentation maximizes perioperative safety but requires trajectory planning to ensure cement crosses the midline and engages both endplates
  3. Post-operative residual pain demands a strict algorithmic pathway using dynamic imaging and diagnostic blocks to isolate the true pain generator before revision surgery
  4. Contralateral revision augmentation effectively locks pseudarthrosis and restores spinal tensegrity in confirmed micro-instability cases
