1. The Event: Immediate Intraoperative Response

The contamination of a critical surgical instrument by dropping it on the floor is a predictable, undesirable event that represents a significant surgical flow disruption. It necessitates a standardized, immediate, and coordinated response from the entire surgical team. Guideline

1.1 Notification & Communication

All perioperative team members share the responsibility to verbally announce the event the moment it occurs. This serves as the trigger for the entire management protocol. The announcement must be unambiguous and directed to the RN circulator and the scrub person.1,2 Expert Opinion

Minimizing distractions, noise, and nonessential conversation is crucial to ensure correct communication and action.1

1.2 Retrieval & Containment Protocol

  • RN Circulator retrieves the instrument using standard precautions and PPE (gloves) to prevent self-contamination.1
  • Instrument is presented to the scrub person for positive identification.1
  • Instrument is immediately isolated from the sterile field—now considered biohazardous.1
⚠ Counted Items: Dual Risk Management If the instrument is a counted item, it must be segregated in a designated, visible location off the sterile field (e.g., separate prep table or basin). It must NOT be removed from the OR or discarded—it remains part of the count and must be included in all reconciliations.1 Failure to sequester it could trigger a false count discrepancy, leading to a costly RSI protocol including wound exploration and intraoperative radiography.

1.3 Decision-Making Framework

The team faces a critical decision: replace or reprocess.5

✅ Preferred Pathway: Replace The safest and most efficient action is to obtain a sterile replacement from backup inventory. The contaminated instrument is sent for standard terminal sterilization at case conclusion.5
🚨 Exception Pathway: IUSS (Last Resort) Immediate-Use Steam Sterilization (IUSS) should only be considered when all three conditions are met:5
  1. The instrument is unique or one-of-a-kind.
  2. It is absolutely critical for the immediate continuation of the procedure.
  3. No sterile replacement is available in the facility.
The surgeon must weigh clinical necessity against the 5.7–7.6 minute delay6 and the 1.52× increased SSI risk.27

1.4 Quality Improvement Data

The frequency of dropped instruments is a valuable quality metric. Data indicates the operating surgeon is most frequently responsible for instrument falls.6 Tracking these events can reveal systemic issues—suboptimal sterile field organization, inefficient passing techniques, or ergonomic challenges—enabling targeted training and process improvement.6

2. The Foundational Principle: Decontamination Is Non-Negotiable

🛡️ Core Principle "If a surgical instrument is not clean, it can't be sterile."9 Effective sterilization is entirely predicated on the meticulous and complete removal of all bioburden through a validated decontamination process. There are no shortcuts.

2.1 Point-of-Use Preparation

Decontamination begins the moment the instrument is retrieved in the OR.10

  • Wipe the instrument with a sterile surgical sponge moistened with sterile water to remove all visible debris.10
  • Do NOT use saline—chloride ions cause pitting, corrosion, and irreversible damage to the instrument's passivation layer.10
  • Treat with an enzymatic spray, foam, or gel, or cover with a water-moistened towel to keep bioburden moist during transport.9

2.2 Safe & Secure Transport

  • Place in a closed, puncture-resistant, leak-proof container.10
  • Label with a fluorescent orange/orange-red biohazard label (OSHA compliance).10
  • Acts as a physical and visual demarcation ensuring correct "dirty" workflow entry at SPD.

2.3 Full Decontamination Workflow

Performed in the SPD decontamination area (physically separated from clean/sterile areas) by trained personnel wearing appropriate PPE.11,13 Cleaning in OR scrub sinks is strictly prohibited.5

All steps must adhere to the manufacturer's Instructions for Use (IFU).9

Table 3: Sequential Decontamination Workflow
Step Procedure Key Considerations Ref.
1. Disassembly Completely disassemble multi-part instruments per IFU. Critical for cleaning/sterilant to contact every surface. 10
2. Cleaning Manual: soak in warm water + neutral pH enzymatic detergent; brush all surfaces, joints, hinges, serrations. Flush lumens. May follow with automated washer-disinfector if validated. Brush lumens entire length. All activities in decontamination area only. 9, 14, 16
3. Rinsing Thorough rinse to remove all residual cleaning agents. Final rinse with treated water (distilled/deionized/RO). Prevents mineral deposits and spotting. 9
4. Inspection Meticulous inspection under magnification and adequate lighting for debris, stains, or damage. If soil remains → fails inspection → return to Step 1. Only "clean" instruments proceed to sterilization. 13, 15

3. Immediate-Use Steam Sterilization (IUSS): The High-Risk Solution

3.1 Terminology Evolution: From "Flash" to IUSS

"Flash sterilization" is now considered outdated. The term "IUSS" was adopted to reflect the complete, multi-step procedure that includes the same rigorous cleaning and decontamination as terminal sterilization.20,23 "Flash" dangerously implied a hurried or incomplete process.

"Immediate use" = the shortest possible time between sterilizer removal and aseptic transfer to the sterile field. IUSS items are intended for use only in the current procedure and must never be stored.14,20

3.2 Risk Evidence

📊 Quantified Risk — Ref. 27 (2024 Retrospective Study) A 2024 retrospective observational study of ~24,000 surgical procedures found:27 Level III
  • IUSS associated with 1.52× higher risk of SSI vs. terminal reprocessing (RR=1.52; 95% CI, 1.10–2.11)
  • Transplant surgery: 2.47× higher risk
  • Plastic surgery: 3.64× higher risk
This data provides a powerful rationale for minimizing IUSS use and treating it as a significant deviation from standard of care.

3.3 Indications, Contraindications & Unacceptable Uses

Table 4: IUSS Decision Framework — Indications & Contraindications
Category Details Ref.
Accepted Indications Unique, non-replaceable instrument contaminated mid-procedure, essential to proceed. True emergent/unplanned situation with insufficient time for terminal sterilization. 5, 7
Unacceptable Uses Convenience; compensating for inadequate inventory; substitute for efficient workflow/planning; "needed for next case." 17, 27
Absolute Contraindications
  • Implants (screws, plates, joints) — except documented life/limb emergency with no other sterile implant17
  • Prion disease (CJD) contamination — requires specific extended protocols13
  • Single-use devices14
  • Manufacturer IFU prohibits IUSS24

3.4 IUSS Protocol: Step-by-Step Execution

Containment

After passing cleaning inspection, the instrument must be placed in a rigid, closed sterilization container system validated for IUSS by its manufacturer.17 Open trays, wrappers, or peel pouches are generally prohibited.

📋 Critical IFU Reconciliation Staff must locate, interpret, and reconcile three separate IFUs—for the instrument, the sterilizer, and the containment device—in a time-pressured environment.9 If conflicting parameters exist, the most conservative (longest time, highest temperature) must be used. Best practice: pre-validate and create quick-reference guides for critical instruments.

Cycle Selection & Parameters

Two primary cycle types are used:15,17

  • Gravity-Displacement: Steam displaces cooler air downward. Suitable for simple, non-porous, non-lumened metal instruments.
  • Dynamic-Air-Removal (Pre-vacuum): Pressure/vacuum pulses remove air before steam. Preferred for complex instruments, lumens, and porous items.
Table 1: Common IUSS Steam Sterilization Parameters (Based on ANSI/AAMI ST79)15
Sterilizer Type Load Configuration Min. Exposure Time Min. Exposure Temperature
Gravity Displacement Metal, Non-porous, No Lumens 3 minutes 270°F (132°C)
Gravity Displacement Metal with Lumens, Porous Items 10 minutes 270°F (132°C)
Dynamic-Air-Removal (Pre-vacuum) Metal, Non-porous, No Lumens 3 minutes 275°F (135°C)
Dynamic-Air-Removal (Pre-vacuum) Metal with Lumens, Porous Items 4 minutes 270°F (132°C)

⚠ Note: These are minimum recommended parameters. Manufacturer's IFU must be reconciled, and the most conservative parameters always followed. IUSS cycles typically have minimal dry time of 0–1 minute, resulting in a hot and potentially wet load.17

Monitoring Type Tool Purpose Placement Frequency

🚨 Implant Paradox If an implant must be processed via IUSS (rare emergency), a BI must be included in that load. The implant should be quarantined and not released until a negative BI result is obtained.29 However, the emergency nature often precludes waiting. If the implant must be released prior to the BI result, this constitutes a major deviation from standard of care requiring immediate notification of the surgeon and infection preventionist for patient follow-up and risk management.29

4. Completing the Cycle: Aseptic Transfer, Documentation & Traceability

4.1 Aseptic Transfer

  • Rigid container transported immediately to point of use after cycle completion.20
  • Personnel must protect themselves from hot surfaces and prevent container compromise.26
  • Upon opening, scrub person/RN circulator must first inspect the internal chemical indicator.26
  • Clear "pass" required before instrument handling. If "fail" or inconclusive → instrument is NOT sterile and must NOT be delivered to the sterile field.26

4.2 Mandatory Documentation

Every IUSS cycle requires meticulous documentation for traceability, QA, and regulatory compliance.23

📊 Documentation as a Diagnostic Tool The requirement to document the specific reason for IUSS is a powerful data-generation tool. Regular audits of these logs enable differentiation between true emergencies and systemic failures. If logs frequently cite reasons like "needed for next case," "insufficient inventory," or "loaner tray arrived late," it provides objective evidence of underlying systemic failures in inventory management, scheduling, or supply chain logistics.27

5. Alternatives & Best Practices for Minimizing IUSS Reliance

The ultimate goal is to make IUSS use exceedingly rare. The safest IUSS cycle is the one that is never run.

5.1 Sufficient Instrument Inventory

The single most effective strategy to reduce IUSS rates.17 Frequent IUSS use is a clear indicator of insufficient inventory. The 1.52× increased SSI risk27 provides strong clinical and financial justification for capital investment in backup instruments—often more cost-effective than treating a single SSI (extended stays, additional procedures, antimicrobial therapy, litigation).5

5.2 Loaner & Specialty Instrument Management

Implement and enforce robust policies:7

  • All loaned instrumentation must be delivered with sufficient lead time for SPD to perform complete inventory, inspection, decontamination, and terminal sterilization before the scheduled procedure.
  • Requires close collaboration between facility, surgeons' offices, and vendors.

6. Conclusion: Upholding a Culture of Sterility Assurance

The guiding principles are unambiguous and centered on patient safety:

  1. First and best option: Replace a contaminated instrument with a pre-sterilized duplicate.
  2. If replacement is impossible and the instrument is critical, begin with a full, non-negotiable decontamination workflow.
  3. IUSS is a high-risk, exception-based procedure—reserved only for true clinical emergencies.
  4. Execution must be flawless, guided by reconciled IFUs, verified through rigorous monitoring, and meticulously documented.
  5. The mark of excellence is making IUSS unnecessary through sufficient inventory, robust loaner management, optimized workflows, and advanced technologies.
✅ Key Takeaway Upholding this commitment requires continuous education, competency verification for all perioperative and sterile processing staff, and a robust quality improvement program that uses IUSS data to identify and rectify systemic vulnerabilities—fostering a pervasive culture of sterility assurance.
