1. Introduction: The Emerging Epidemic

The landscape of modern orthopaedic surgery is undergoing a fundamental shift. With the exponential rise in primary total hip arthroplasty (THA) over the past three decades, a parallel surge in periprosthetic femoral fractures (PFF) has emerged as one of the most complex challenges in reconstructive surgery. Level II

PFFs are now recognized as the third most common reason for revision hip arthroplasty, trailing only aseptic loosening and instability. These fractures carry morbidity and mortality rates comparable to — and in some cohorts exceeding — those of native hip fractures. Level II

⚠️ Projected Growth

Modeling predicts a 212% increase in PFF cases between 2016 and 2032 in the United States alone. This trajectory underscores the critical need for standardized management algorithms and evidence-based surgical decision-making. Level II (Ref. 4)

The Dual Nature of PFFs

Periprosthetic fractures are simultaneously fragility fractures indicative of compromised bone stock and mechanical failures of a complex implant-bone composite system. This duality necessitates a treatment approach that bridges the traditional divide between trauma surgery and reconstructive arthroplasty, requiring surgeons to possess mastery over both internal fixation techniques and complex revision arthroplasty systems. Level V (Ref. 1)

Economic Burden

The cost of managing PFFs is substantial and rising. The economic impact extends beyond the surgical episode to include prolonged rehabilitation, readmissions, and long-term disability. Cost-effectiveness analyses from 2024/2025 indicate that while revision arthroplasty has higher upfront costs, it may be more cost-effective long-term for active patients due to superior health-related quality of life (HRQoL) and lower reoperation rates. Level II (Ref. 46)

2. Classification Systems

The cornerstone of PFF management is accurate classification. Unlike descriptive systems elsewhere in orthopaedics, PFF classifications are prescriptive — they guide the surgeon directly toward a specific treatment algorithm.

2.1 Vancouver Classification System

Since its proposal by Duncan and Masri in 1995, the Vancouver Classification has established itself as the universal language for periprosthetic hip fractures. Its utility stems from its focus on three critical factors: fracture location, implant stability, and bone stock quality. Level IV (Ref. 5, 6)

Location → Implant Stability → Bone Stock → Treatment Decision

Type A: Trochanteric Fractures

  • Type AG (Greater Trochanter): Often from eccentric muscle contraction, direct trauma, or osteolysis. While frequently stable, can compromise the abductor mechanism leading to Trendelenburg gait. Level IV (Ref. 2)
  • Type AL (Lesser Trochanter): An isolated fracture may be a sentinel sign of medial wall compromise or subsidence of a tapered wedge stem. Level IV (Ref. 6)

Type B: The Diaphyseal Battleground Level IV

This is the most common and most surgically challenging category:

  • Type B1 (Stable Stem): The prosthesis remains well-fixed; the fracture is purely a disruption of bone integrity. (Ref. 6)
  • Type B2 (Loose Stem, Adequate Bone): The stem fixation is disrupted, but proximal bone stock remains adequate for revision. (Ref. 6)
  • Type B3 (Loose Stem, Poor Bone): The stem is loose, and the proximal femur is severely compromised — incapable of supporting a standard revision stem without augmentation. (Ref. 6)

Type C: Distal Fractures

Occur well below the stem tip. The implant is stable; treatment follows distal femoral fracture principles with the caveat of stress risers from the proximal implant. Level IV (Ref. 6)

2.2 Unified Classification System (UCS)

The UCS adapts Vancouver principles to all periprosthetic fractures, introducing categories for multi-implant scenarios. It has demonstrated high intra- and inter-observer reliability comparable to Vancouver. Level II (Ref. 8)

3. The Diagnostic Dilemma: Differentiating B1 and B2 Fractures

The most critical decision point in the entire PFF management algorithm is the differentiation between Vancouver B1 (stable stem) and B2 (loose stem). This single classification determines whether a patient undergoes internal fixation (ORIF) or revision arthroplasty. Level III (Ref. 13)

🚨 The Misdiagnosis Problem

Studies demonstrate that 20% to 47% of fractures diagnosed as B1 on preoperative X-rays are found to be loose (B2) upon intraoperative inspection. This high discordance rate highlights the inadequacy of plain films alone. Level III (Ref. 13)

3.1 Clinical History as a Diagnostic Tool

  • Start-Up Pain: Anterior thigh pain upon rising from seated position or during first steps — a hallmark of antecedent loosening. (Ref. 13)
  • Functional Decline: Deteriorating mobility or new reliance on assistive devices prior to the fall suggests pre-existing implant pathology. (Ref. 13)
  • Mechanism of Injury: Low-energy fractures (twisting, rising from chair) imply pre-compromised bone; high-energy trauma more likely causes true B1 in a well-fixed stem. Level III (Ref. 17)

3.2 Radiographic Signs of Loosening

  • Radiolucent Lines: Continuous lines >2 mm at the cement-bone or metal-bone interface. Level III (Ref. 18)
  • Pedestal Sign: Bony shelf or endosteal sclerosis at the stem tip — pathognomonic of proximal instability and distal load transfer. (Ref. 18, 21)
  • Subsidence/Migration: Any vertical subsidence or varus/valgus shift compared to prior images confirms B2 status. Level III (Ref. 20)
  • Digital Tomosynthesis: Emerging data shows superior sensitivity (63.3%) for detecting radiolucent lines vs. plain radiography (20.5%) or CT (50.2%). Level II (Ref. 22)

3.3 Intraoperative Stability Testing: The Gold Standard

✅ Intraoperative Protocol

The surgeon must expose the fracture site and proximal femur, then physically manipulate the stem (rotational and axial forces). Visible motion at the interface or extrusion of blood/fluid confirms loosening. If a stem thought to be stable moves intraoperatively, the surgical plan must immediately convert to revision arthroplasty. Level IV (Ref. 16)

The surgical team must always be prepared for revision — implants and instruments available — even when booking a "fixation" case. Level III (Ref. 13)

4. Surgical Management Strategies

The treatment hierarchy is dictated by fracture type, with overarching goals of restoring mechanical stability, preserving bone stock, and early mobilization to prevent recumbency-related complications.

4.1 Vancouver Type A: Trochanteric Fractures Level IV

  • Conservative: Type AG with <2.5 cm displacement and stable abductor mechanism → protected weight-bearing and abduction restrictions for 6–12 weeks. (Ref. 6)
  • Surgical Indications: Displacement >2.5 cm, symptomatic non-union, or hip instability.
  • Techniques: Tension band wiring, cerclage cables, trochanteric claw plates. Autologous bone grafting for severe osteolysis or non-union. (Ref. 2)

4.2 Vancouver Type B1: Internal Fixation Level IV

The standard of care is Open Reduction and Internal Fixation (ORIF) using a lateral locking compression plate spanning the full femur.

Locking Plate Constructs (Ref. 14, 24)

  • Proximal fixation via unicortical locking screws (avoiding the stem) or cerclage cables integrated into plate holes.
  • Cortical Strut Allografts: Historically augment fixation and restore bone stock. Biomechanical studies confirm increased construct stiffness. Level II (Ref. 25) However, recent clinical data shows modern locking plates alone achieve comparable union rates. Strut grafts are now reserved for severe medial comminution or profound osteopenia. Level II (Ref. 24)

Emerging: Dual Plating Level II

🔬 Dual Plating for Complex Patterns

For fractures extending into the supracondylar region or with significant comminution, adding an anterior or medial plate provides multi-planar stability, significantly increasing stiffness against varus collapse. Studies show high union rates (up to 98.5%) and low failure rates, allowing immediate postoperative weight-bearing — a paradigm shift from restricted protocols after single plating. MIPO techniques minimize soft tissue stripping. (Ref. 27, 28, 30)

4.3 Vancouver Type B2: The Surgical Controversy

Type B2 fractures represent the greatest area of debate in PFF management.

Gold Standard: Revision Arthroplasty (RA) Level II

  • Modular Tapered Fluted Titanium Stems: Current implant of choice. Modularity allows independent adjustment of leg length, offset, and version; fluted distal geometry provides aggressive scratch-fit fixation. 96–97% stem survival at mid-term follow-up. (Ref. 31, 32)
  • Cement-in-Cement Revision: For select cases with a well-fixed cement mantle — dramatically reduces operative time, blood loss, and risk of iatrogenic bone damage. Particularly valuable for frail elderly patients. Outcomes comparable to full revision with rigorous patient selection. Level III (Ref. 34, 35, 37)

Alternative: ORIF for B2 Fractures Level II

⚠️ ORIF as "Damage Control" for B2

Meta-analyses show ORIF in B2 fractures is associated with shorter operative time, less blood loss, lower infection rates (4.4% vs. 6.7%), and lower dislocation rates (1.7% vs. 7%). However, ORIF carries a higher risk of mechanical failure and refracture (7.1% vs. 4.2%). ORIF is indicated for patients with low functional demand, limited life expectancy, or ASA >3 that preclude revision. For active, healthy patients, Revision Arthroplasty remains superior. (Ref. 38, 40)

4.4 Vancouver Type B3: Addressing Bone Loss Level IV

  • Modular Stems: Viable if sufficient distal bone stock (>4 cm intact isthmus) exists. (Ref. 31)
  • Impaction Bone Grafting (IBG): Biological reconstruction with morselized allograft. Technically demanding; associated with high complication rates (subsidence, non-union) in B3 fractures. Now reserved for younger patients where restoring bone stock is critical. (Ref. 41, 42, 43)
  • Proximal Femoral Replacement (Megaprosthesis): Predictable solution for elderly, low-demand patients with massive bone loss. Allows immediate weight-bearing but carries higher dislocation rates. (Ref. 41)
  • Allograft-Prosthesis Composite (APC): Offers soft tissue reattachment potential but risks deep infection, non-union, and graft resorption. (Ref. 6)

4.5 Vancouver Type C: Distal Fractures Level IV

Lateral locking plates are standard. The plate must overlap the stem tip to prevent stress risers. Retrograde nailing is often impossible due to the hip stem or risk of creating an interprosthetic fracture. (Ref. 1)

5. Emerging Technologies & Future Directions

5.1 3D-Printed Custom Implants Level V

For catastrophic bone defects that defy standard classification (e.g., UCS Type D/E with massive pelvic and femoral loss), custom 3D-printed titanium implants are emerging as a limb-salvage solution. Using CT data, manufacturers create patient-specific implants with porous scaffolds matching the exact defect topography, facilitating osseointegration. While costs and lead times remain high, case reports demonstrate success in scenarios where conventional megaprostheses or allografts have failed. (Ref. 44, 45)

5.2 Economic Analysis Level II

A 2024/2025 Markov analysis found that revision arthroplasty is more cost-effective long-term for B2 fractures due to higher HRQoL and lower reoperation rates. However, for patients with life expectancy <2 years, ORIF is the economically rational choice, minimizing immediate resource utilization. (Ref. 46, 40)

6.1 Infection (PJI)

Meta-analyses consistently show higher infection rates in revision arthroplasty (~6.7%) vs. ORIF (~4.4%). A 2024 matched-cohort study found significantly higher rates of surgical site infection, UTI, and wound disruption in the revision group, attributed to longer operative time, increased blood loss, and extensive dissection. Level II (Ref. 38, 47)

6.2 Dislocation

Dislocation is significantly more common after revision (7%) than ORIF (1.7%). Revision exposure often requires takedown of the abductor mechanism or extensive scar release, compromising the soft tissue envelope. ORIF preserves the joint capsule and native stem version. Level II (Ref. 38)

6.3 Mortality

One-year mortality rates are generally comparable between ORIF and revision groups, driven largely by underlying patient frailty. However, systemic complications such as AKI and DVT are more prevalent in the revision cohort. Level II (Ref. 47)

7. Conclusion & Key Recommendations

🎯 Key Clinical Takeaways
  1. Assume Loosening: 20–47% of "B1" diagnoses on X-ray are B2 intraoperatively. Maintain a high index of suspicion. Level III
  2. The Intraoperative Test is Supreme: Preoperative imaging is a guide, but intraoperative stability testing is the judge. If the stem moves → convert to revision. Level IV
  3. B1 Management: Lateral locking plates are standard. Dual plating for distal or complex patterns to allow early weight-bearing. Level II
  4. B2 Management: Revision arthroplasty with modular tapered stems is the gold standard. ORIF is a valuable salvage procedure for the medically frail but carries higher mechanical failure risk. Level II
  5. B3 Management: Modular stems and megaprostheses have largely supplanted impaction grafting due to reliability and reproducibility. Level IV

As the "epidemic" of periprosthetic fractures accelerates, the future lies in continued refinement of diagnostic modalities to eliminate the "gray zone" of classification, and centralization of these complex cases to specialized centres capable of handling the dual demands of trauma and arthroplasty reconstruction.
