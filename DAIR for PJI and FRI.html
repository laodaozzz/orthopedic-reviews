Debridement, Antibiotics, and Implant Retention (DAIR)

Evidence-Based Guidelines and Clinical Outcomes in Periprosthetic Joint Infection & Fracture-Related Infection

I. Executive Summary

DAIR represents a critical implant-sparing surgical strategy in the management of acute orthopedic implant-associated infections, applied to both periprosthetic joint infection (PJI) following total joint arthroplasty and fracture-related infection (FRI) associated with osteosynthesis hardware. Level II

While two-stage revision remains a benchmark for infection eradication, DAIR offers a less morbid alternative with potential for superior functional outcomes, reduced healthcare costs, and preservation of bone stock when applied to a meticulously selected patient population. Level II

Core Triad of DAIR Success
  1. Accurate and timely diagnosis – using standardized, evidence-based criteria
  2. Radical surgical debridement – removal of all non-viable tissue
  3. Prolonged, pathogen-specific antimicrobial therapy – guided by intraoperative cultures

Ideal candidates present with an early postoperative or acute hematogenous infection, a short duration of symptoms, a well-fixed and stable implant, and a healthy soft-tissue envelope. Level II Host factors, particularly immunocompetence, are significant predictors of success. Level III

Clinical outcomes are variable: pooled infection control rates for PJI range between 61% and 72%. Level I For FRI, in appropriately selected early cases, DAIR can achieve clinical remission and fracture union rates approaching 86%. Level IV

II. Foundational Principles: Biofilm & Infection Classification

Biofilm Formation on Orthopedic Implants

The introduction of a foreign body fundamentally alters the local host environment, creating a unique niche for microbial colonization. The central challenge lies in biofilm formation—a structured consortium of microorganisms encased in a self-produced extracellular polymeric substance (EPS), or glycocalyx, which adheres to the implant surface. Not specified in source

The process begins within hours of bacterial inoculation. Over approximately four weeks, bacteria proliferate and produce the EPS matrix (polysaccharides, proteins, lipids, extracellular DNA), creating a barrier that confers profound resistance to both the host immune system and antimicrobial agents. Not specified in source

Key Pathophysiological Principle

Bacteria within a biofilm can be up to 10,000 times more resistant to antibiotics than planktonic counterparts, making surgical debridement an indispensable component of treatment. The biofilm also harbors "persister cells"—phenotypically dormant and highly antibiotic-tolerant—that serve as reservoirs for recurrence.

Evidence Gap: Diagnostic Framework Maturity

The PJI field benefits from a quantitative, validated scoring system enabling high-quality comparative research. The FRI consensus definition, while a monumental step forward, is fundamentally qualitative and lacks a unifying score. This heterogeneity makes pooling data across studies more challenging, and the FRI evidence base consequently remains less mature. Level II

IV. The DAIR Procedure: Technical Protocol

Preoperative Planning

A multidisciplinary team (MDT) approach—integrating orthopedic surgeons, infectious disease specialists, and clinical microbiologists—is paramount. Level II Critical steps include:

  • Antibiotic holiday: Cessation of all antibiotics for ≥2 weeks prior to surgery (if not septic) to maximize culture yield. Level III
  • Preoperative aspiration for organism identification and sensitivity planning. Level III
  • Host optimization: Glycemic control, nutritional status, smoking cessation. Not specified in source

Surgical Principles: Radical Debridement & Synovectomy

The cornerstone is a radical, extensive debridement of all infected and necrotic tissue. Level II The procedure involves:

  • Reopening the original incision; excision of sinus tracts in their entirety
  • Systematic debridement through all tissue planes
  • Radical synovectomy – removal of entire inflamed synovial lining
  • Dissection continues until only healthy, viable, bleeding tissue remains
  • High-volume irrigation with 3–8+ liters of sterile fluid; pulsatile lavage favored
  • Interest in dilute antiseptic solutions: 0.05% chlorhexidine gluconate, povidone-iodine, or 97% ethanol Level III

Modular Component Exchange: The Controversy

Exchange of modular components (polyethylene liner, femoral head) has been a long-standing principle to remove a significant septic focus. However, a 2021 systematic review and meta-regression of 65 studies revealed a critical temporal shift: Level I

Paradigm Shift in Modular Exchange Evidence

In studies published before 2004, modular exchange was positively associated with success. In studies published after 2004 (37 studies), this association vanished—no statistically significant benefit. This suggests that as debridement techniques and antibiotic protocols have improved, the relative importance of this single step has diminished. The decision must now be made case-by-case. Level I

Microbiological Sampling

  • Minimum 3–5 deep tissue samples from different areas around the implant Level III
  • New sterile instrument for each sample to prevent cross-contamination
  • Swabs are unreliable and should be avoided
  • Cultures held for extended period (up to 14 days) for slow-growing organisms

V. DAIR in Acute Periprosthetic Joint Infection

Patient Selection Criteria

IDSA clinical practice guidelines provide clear recommendations. Level II

  1. Acute Postoperative PJI: Within ~30 days of index arthroplasty, symptoms <3 weeks
  2. Acute Hematogenous PJI: Symptoms <3 weeks duration
  3. Implant stability: Well-fixed, no radiographic loosening
  4. Soft tissue integrity: Healthy, amenable to primary closure
  5. Immunocompetent host Level III
The "Time Window" Controversy

Rigid calendar-based cutoffs are an imperfect proxy for biofilm maturation. Data from the Dutch Arthroplasty Register found no significant difference in 1-year re-revision rates for DAIR performed <4 weeks vs. 4–12 weeks post-arthroplasty. Level III Duration of symptoms may be a more critical predictor than time from index surgery. Level III Future paradigm shifts will come from tools that directly assess biofilm maturity rather than relying on calendar-based guesses.

Prolonged, targeted antimicrobial therapy is as critical as surgical debridement. Level II

The Rifampin Paradox

Rifampin is positioned as the single most important medical adjunct for staphylococcal PJI. However, evidence reveals a nuanced reality: Level I

  • A 2021 meta-analysis of 64 studies found rifampin addition yielded a pooled risk ratio of only 1.10 (95% CI, 1.00–1.22)—statistically significant but modest. Level I
  • A 2023 study demonstrated that with rifampin-resistant staphylococcal PJI treated with DAIR, the infection relapse rate was an alarming 80%, compared to 51% in rifampin-sensitive strains. Level III
Critical Decision Point

Rifampin susceptibility is arguably the most important medical data point for any staphylococcal PJI considered for DAIR. A "resistant" result should prompt serious discussion about shifting to a two-stage revision, as the likelihood of implant salvage with DAIR is minimal. Level III

VI. DAIR in Fracture-Related Infection

Critical Differences from PJI

While the underlying biofilm principle is identical, several factors limit direct translation of PJI protocols to FRI: Level III

  • State of the bone: FRI occurs in unhealed fractures; the implant must provide stability for bone healing
  • Implant nature: Fracture fixation hardware is often temporary vs. permanent arthroplasty components
  • Soft tissue injury: Often severely compromised, especially in high-energy/open fractures
  • Microbiology: S. aureus is more frequent in FRI (37.4%) vs. PJI (27.9%) Level III
The Composite Endpoint: A Higher Clinical Hurdle

For FRI, success requires both infection eradication AND fracture consolidation—a dual requirement that represents a significantly higher hurdle than PJI. DAIR can fail in two ways: failure to control infection, or successful infection suppression but failure of fracture healing. Level IV

VIII. Emerging Frontiers & Unresolved Controversies

Repeated DAIR

While some studies identify repeat DAIR as a predictor of ultimate failure, Level III recent evidence suggests it may still avoid implant removal in 77.8% of cases. Level IV The decision requires careful consideration of clinical status, pathogen, and reasons for initial failure.

Biofilm Disruption Strategies

  • Chemical adjuncts: 0.05% chlorhexidine gluconate shown superior to saline in reducing colony counts Level III
  • Novel anti-biofilm agents: PLG0206 (peptide antibiotic) in clinical trials with potent anti-biofilm activity against staphylococci Not from source
  • Enzymatic disruption: DNase I (targeting eDNA), quorum-sensing inhibitors, persister cell-targeting therapies (experimental) Not from source

Diagnostic Advances

  • Synovial fluid calprotectin: Point-of-care test with sensitivity 98.1%, specificity 95.7% Level III
  • Molecular methods: PCR and next-generation sequencing (NGS) offer culture-independent detection with faster turnaround Not from source

IX. Evidence-Based Algorithm for DAIR Decision-Making

1Initial Suspicion

Patient presents with signs/symptoms of acute infection around an orthopedic implant (pain, swelling, erythema, drainage, fever).

2Diagnostic Workup
  • PJI: Apply 2018 MSIS criteria → serum ESR, CRP, D-dimer; joint aspiration for WBC, PMN%, alpha-defensin, culture
  • FRI: Apply international consensus criteria → evaluate for confirmatory/suggestive signs; deep tissue cultures
3DAIR Candidacy – "Four Pillars"
  • Timing: Acute infection? PJI: symptoms <3 weeks, surgery <30 days; FRI: early/delayed window (<10 weeks)
  • Implant stability: Well-fixed, no loosening; for FRI: adequate fixation for healing
  • Soft tissues: Healthy, amenable to closure; absence of chronic sinus
  • Host: Immunocompetent, medically optimized
4Pathogen-Specific Consideration

For staphylococci: Isolate rifampin susceptibility. If resistant → DAIR success probability is extremely low → strongly consider two-stage revision.

5Decision Point
  • Proceed DAIR: All Four Pillars met, favorable pathogen
  • Implant removal/revision: Chronic infection, loose implant, compromised soft tissues, severe immunocompromise, rifampin-resistant staphylococcus
6Post-DAIR Management

Prolonged pathogen-specific antibiotics (3–6 months for PJI; until union for FRI), managed by MDT. Close clinical and laboratory follow-up.
