Iatrogenic Axillary Nerve Injury in Proximal Humeral IMN

Evidence-Based Review High Subclinical Incidence Prevention-Focused

1. Introduction: Neurovascular Safety in Humeral Nailing

Proximal humeral fractures (PHFs) represent a significant and growing challenge in orthopedic trauma, driven by an aging population. PHFs account for 4–6% of all adult fractures and rank as the third most common osteoporotic fracture in the elderly, following hip and distal radius fractures. Level III–IV

Incidence rates range from 60.1 to 90.8 cases per 100,000 person-years, with a marked predilection for elderly females sustaining low-energy falls. As the prevalence rises, so does the complexity of management, necessitating stable fixation with minimal soft tissue morbidity.

💡 Why Intramedullary Nailing?

Historically, displaced 2- and 3-part fractures were treated with ORIF using locking plates. While plating provides robust fixation, it requires extensive soft tissue dissection, associated with compromised vascularity and increased risk of avascular necrosis (AVN). Level III

Intramedullary nailing (IMN) re-emerges as a biomechanically advantageous alternative: load-sharing implants closer to the mechanical axis, superior resistance to varus/valgus forces, and percutaneous insertion that preserves periosteal blood supply. Level IV

However, the percutaneous nature of IMN introduces a formidable risk: the potential for "blind" iatrogenic injury to the axillary nerve. Unlike open plating where the axillary nerve can be visually identified, proximal locking screw insertion relies on anatomical landmarks and fluoroscopic guidance. The axillary nerve winds horizontally around the surgical neck—directly in the trajectory of lateral-to-medial locking screws. Consequences range from neurapraxia to complete neurotmesis, potentially resulting in deltoid paralysis and loss of shoulder abduction. Level IV

2. Surgical Anatomy of the Axillary Nerve

2.1 Origin and Course Through the Quadrilateral Space

The axillary nerve is a mixed motor and sensory nerve from the posterior cord of the brachial plexus (C5–C6). It courses anterior to the subscapularis, posterior to the axillary artery, then dives inferiorly and posteriorly to exit through the quadrilateral space: Level V (Anatomical)

  • Superior: Teres minor
  • Inferior: Teres major
  • Medial: Long head of triceps
  • Lateral: Surgical neck of the humerus

Within this space, the nerve is intimately associated with the posterior circumflex humeral artery (PCHA), often lying just 10–25 mm from the inferior glenoid rim. This proximity places it at risk during both the initial trauma (traction from subluxation/dislocation) and surgical fixation.

Upon exiting, the nerve bifurcates into two critical branches:

  1. Posterior Branch: Motor to teres minor and posterior deltoid; gives rise to the superior lateral brachial cutaneous nerve (sensory to the "regimental badge" area).
  2. Anterior Branch (highest surgical risk): Winds horizontally around the surgical neck, deep to the deltoid, providing motor innervation to the middle and anterior deltoid heads—the primary drivers of shoulder abduction. Its horizontal course places it directly perpendicular to lateral proximal locking screws.

2.2 The "Safe Zone" and Anthropometric Variability

⚠️ The "5 cm Rule" Is Unreliable

Traditional teaching cites a "safe zone" of 5 cm distal to the acromion. However, rigorous cadaveric studies show this is an oversimplification: in 20% of specimens, the nerve lies less than 5 cm from the acromion. A locking screw at this classic mark would directly transect the nerve in 1 of 5 patients. Level V

Correlation with Arm Length

A positive linear correlation exists between upper arm length and nerve position: for every 1 cm increase in arm length, the nerve shifts distally by 0.1–0.23 cm. Regression formulas (e.g., Y = 0.104x + C) have been derived, but intraoperative calculation is impractical compared to the principle of staying proximal. Level V

Distance from Humeral Head (Fluoroscopic Reference)

The cranial tip of the humeral head is a more reliable osseous reference during fluoroscopy. The anterior branch crosses the humerus 36–58 mm distal to the cranial tip of the humeral head. Main trunk: 48–58 mm; proximal motor branches to anterior deltoid: as high as 36 mm. This defines a "Danger Zone" of ~3.5–6 cm distal to the head. Level V

2.3 Medial Relations and Neurovascular Risk

Secondary risk exists medially: if a drill bit over-penetrates the medial cortex, it enters the axilla where the main axillary nerve trunk and PCHA reside. The nerve lies 9–28 mm from the medial anatomical neck (mean 20 mm). Protrusion of screws up to 5 mm beyond the medial cortex is generally safe if the screw is >30 mm distal to the neck. Screws penetrating >20–30 mm risk impaling neurovascular structures. Level V

🔴 Bent Nails: Critical Risk Data

Zimmer Sirus: In cadaveric analysis, the mean distance from oblique locking bolt to the axillary nerve was only 1.0 mm (SD=2). The nerve was damaged in 4 out of 5 specimens during bolt insertion.

Stryker T2 PHN: Mean distance of 2.7 mm—within the margin of error for surgical placement.

Straight nails (e.g., Synthes MultiLoc, S&N Trigen, Aesculap Targon) use a medial entry point at the apex of the humeral head or the supraspinatus junction. By shifting the entry medially, locking screws are elevated away from the surgical neck, demonstrating significantly larger safety margins. Level V

3.2 Risk of Medial Support Screws

Medial support ("calcar") screws are biomechanically sound for preventing varus collapse but introduce new risk vectors. A 2024 anatomical study found that in 6.9% of specimens, the axillary nerve came into direct contact with the screw trajectory. These screws are inserted from a more distal starting point (2–4 cm below standard proximal screws), bringing instrumentation dangerously close to the anterior axillary nerve. Level V

4. Epidemiology: Clinical vs. Subclinical Injury

4.1 Clinical Incidence

In large retrospective series, the rate of symptomatic axillary nerve palsy is 2–6%—generally lower than ORIF (6.8–10.8%), as nailing avoids extensive soft tissue retraction. Most clinical palsies are transient neurapraxias resolving within 3–6 months. Level III–IV

4.2 Subclinical Incidence: The "Silent Epidemic"

⚡ Subclinical Injury Rate: 58–67%

Prospective EMG studies reveal denervation rates of 58% to 67%—more than half of all patients sustain some degree of axonal insult. This is clinically "silent" due to deltoid functional reserve, supraspinatus compensation for abduction initiation, and postoperative pain masking. These silent injuries likely contribute to unexplained pain, muscle fatigue, atrophy, and lower Constant/DASH scores. Level III

5. Mechanisms of Injury

5.1 Traction and Stretch Injury (Neurapraxia)

The most common mechanism. During fracture reduction—particularly "joy-sticking" or applying strong traction—the tethered nerve is stretched. Stretching a nerve by just 15% of its resting length interrupts intraneural microcirculation, leading to ischemia. Hyper-abduction also tightens the nerve against the surgical neck. Level V (Animal)

5.2 Direct Laceration and Drilling (Neurotmesis)

The most severe injury. A drill bit or locking screw advanced percutaneously can catch the horizontally-oriented nerve and avulse or transect it. Oblique screws in bent nails are the primary culprit. Level V

5.3 Compression and Crush Injury

Aggressive insertion of tissue protection sleeves or trocars against the humerus can crush the nerve. Blind placement of Hohmann retractors around the surgical neck in mini-open approaches can impinge the nerve. Level V

5.4 Thermal Injury

High-speed drilling generates friction heat. Even without direct contact, proximity transfers sufficient thermal energy to cause coagulation necrosis of the perineurium or thermal neurapraxia. Level V (Biomechanical)

6. Surgical Prevention Techniques

6.1 The Abduction Paradox

📐 Arm Position Critically Affects Safe Zone

While abduction facilitates fluoroscopy, it decreases the safe zone:

  • Neutral adduction (0–30°): Acromion-to-nerve distance ≈ 58–61 mm (maximal)
  • 90° abduction: Distance decreases to ≈ 52 mm
  • Abduction >45° brings the nerve closer to the joint and locking screw trajectories

Recommendation: Proximal locking should be performed with the arm in neutral adduction or slight abduction (<30°). Adjust the C-arm, not the arm. Level V

6.2 The "Three-Stitch" Tissue Protection Technique

1
Precise Stab Incision
Longitudinal 1 cm incision through skin and fascia at the targeted locking hole site.
2
Blunt Dissection
No sharp instruments deep to fascia. Use mosquito hemostat or blunt obturator to spread deltoid fibers longitudinally down to bone—pushing nerve fibers aside rather than cutting them.
3
Tissue Protection Sleeve
Advance drill guide through the dissected track until firmly seated against lateral cortex. The sleeve acts as a physical shield—the drill cannot wind up the nerve while inside it.
4
"Woodpecker" Drilling
Pulsed advance-withdraw-advance technique. Minimizes heat generation and allows tactile verification of cortices, preventing medial over-penetration into the neurovascular bundle.

This standardized technique is advocated for all percutaneous screw insertions. Level IV

6.3 Key Intraoperative Maneuvers

  • "Tug Test": In mini-open approaches, insert finger deep to deltoid to palpate the anterior branch. Gentle tug confirms location. Level V
  • Avoid Blind Retraction: Never place retractors blindly around the surgical neck—visualize the nerve.
  • Shoulder Extension for Nail Entry: Extend the shoulder (elbow posterior to torso) to translate the humeral head anteriorly, facilitating correct medial entry without damaging the rotator cuff. Level V

7. Management of Postoperative Nerve Palsy

7.1 Immediate Assessment

In recovery, perform a focused neurovascular exam: test "regimental badge" sensation, palpate deltoid contraction, and assess radial pulse (note: presence does not rule out axillary artery injury due to collateral circulation—any expanding hematoma raises suspicion with OR 109 for brachial plexus association). Level IV

7.3 Surgical Salvage Options

  1. Neurolysis: If the nerve is intact but encased in scar/callus.
  2. Nerve Grafting: Autologous sural nerve grafts; outcomes diminish with graft length and age.
  3. Nerve Transfer (Radial-to-Axillary) — Gold Standard: A branch of the radial nerve (long/medial head of triceps) is transferred to the anterior axillary nerve branch. Advantages: short regeneration distance, robust motor donor, minimal triceps functional deficit. Studies report 79–85% achieving MRC grade 4/5 strength. Effective even in elderly patients. Level IV

9. Conclusion

✅ Key Takeaways
  1. Clinical palsy rates are 2–6%, but subclinical EMG-detected injury exceeds 50%.
  2. Straight nails with medial entry points maximize nerve-to-screw distance (10–14 mm) vs. bent nails (1–3 mm).
  3. The "5 cm rule" from acromion is unreliable—20% of nerves lie closer.
  4. Proximal locking in <30° abduction and the "Three-Stitch" technique are non-negotiable standards.
  5. When injury occurs, EMG by 3 months is critical; nerve transfer before 6–12 months has the best outcomes.
  6. A major evidence gap exists: no RCTs comparing nail designs with nerve injury as a primary endpoint.
