Management of Humeral Shaft Fracture Nonunion

A Comprehensive Review of Evidence-Based Algorithms, Surgical Techniques & Biological Augmentation

1. Introduction

The management of humeral shaft fractures constitutes a significant portion of orthopaedic trauma workload, representing approximately 1% to 5% of all adult fractures Ref 1. While the humerus is historically regarded as a bone with high healing potential due to its rich vascular envelope provided by the surrounding triceps and brachialis musculature, the progression to nonunion remains a persistent and debilitating complication.

Although non-operative management via functional bracing (the Sarmiento method) has yielded high union rates in historical cohorts, contemporary systematic reviews indicate that nonunion rates following conservative management may be significantly higher than previously thought, ranging from 17.6% to as high as 33% in specific patient populations Level I · Ref 3. Operative fixation of acute fractures is not a panacea; nonunion rates following primary surgical intervention—whether by plate osteosynthesis or intramedullary nailing—range between 5% and 15% Level IV · Ref 4.

Defining Nonunion in the Humerus The U.S. FDA defines nonunion as a fracture that is at least 9 months old with no radiographic signs of progression for 3 months Ref 7. However, many surgeons consider a fracture a "recalcitrant nonunion" if it fails to heal after the first surgical attempt or shows fracture site mobility and lack of bridging callus at 4 to 6 months post-injury Ref 8.

The burden of humeral nonunion extends beyond radiographic persistence of a fracture line. It manifests as profound functional deficit—pain, instability, and inability to perform activities of daily living requiring a stable fulcrum for the upper extremity lever arm. The economic impact is substantial, driven by direct healthcare costs, prolonged loss of productivity, and management of associated complications such as joint stiffness and complex regional pain syndrome.

2. Etiology, Pathophysiology & Classification

Understanding the failure mechanism is the prerequisite for successful revision. Nonunion is almost invariably the result of a specific failure in the biological or mechanical environment of the fracture.

2.1 The Biological and Mechanical Dichotomy

Biological Risk Factors

  • Vascular Compromise: The nutrient artery of the humerus typically enters the canal at the middle third of the diaphysis. Fractures or surgical approaches that disrupt this supply create an avascular environment prone to atrophic nonunion Ref 4.
  • Patient Comorbidities: Smoking is a potent antagonist to bone healing, causing vasoconstriction and direct cellular toxicity to osteoblasts. Diabetes mellitus, metabolic bone disease, and malnutrition also blunt the reparative response Ref 10.
  • Infection: Occult infection is a leading cause of "aseptic" failure. Biofilm-forming organisms, particularly Staphylococcus aureus and Cutibacterium acnes, can exist in a quiescent state Ref 11.

Mechanical Risk Factors

  • Instability: According to Perren's strain theory, excessive motion (strain >10%) leads to fibrous tissue rather than bone formation Ref 12.
  • Distraction: A fracture gap greater than 5 mm is a strong predictor of nonunion Ref 4.

2.2 Weber & Cech Morphological Classification

This classification directly informs treatment strategy by categorizing nonunions based on vascularity and osteogenic potential.

Weber & Cech Nonunion Classification
Type Radiographic Appearance Pathophysiology Treatment Implication
Hypertrophic "Elephant's foot" — abundant callus Viable biology, unstable mechanics Rigid fixation alone sufficient; graft unnecessary Ref 10
Atrophic "Pencil tip" — bone resorption, no callus Biological failure — avascular, inert bone ends Stability + decortication + bone graft required Ref 2
Oligotrophic Minimal callus Moderate instability + suboptimal biology Plating + bone graft recommended Ref 2

2.3 Septic vs. Aseptic Differentiation

The most critical branch point in the treatment algorithm is distinguishing between septic and aseptic nonunion Ref 11.

  • Aseptic Nonunion: Absence of clinical signs of infection, normal inflammatory markers, negative cultures.
  • Septic Nonunion: May be obvious (purulence, sinus tract) or occult. Preoperative evaluation must include ESR and CRP. If elevated, aspiration or MRI is warranted. Intraoperative tissue sampling for culture is mandatory during all revision surgeries.

3. The Diamond Concept

Introduced by Giannoudis et al., the Diamond Concept is the prevailing intellectual framework for treating complex nonunions, moving beyond the "stability vs. biology" duality to a five-pillar model Ref 7.

🧬

1. Osteogenic Cells

MSCs and osteoprogenitor cells. Corrected with autologous bone graft (ICBG) or RIA harvest Ref 13.

🏗️

2. Osteoconductive Scaffolds

Structural trellis for vascular ingrowth. Allografts, cancellous chips, synthetic ceramics, or Masquelet membrane Ref 7.

💊

3. Growth Factors

Osteoinductive signals (BMP-2, BMP-7, VEGF). Masquelet membrane secretes these naturally; exogenous BMP-7 used in humeral nonunions Ref 9.

⚙️

4. Mechanical Stability

Prerequisite for all biological pillars. Revision plating (absolute stability) or exchange nailing (relative stability) Ref 12.

🩸

5. Vascularity

Blood supply delivers cells and factors. Corrected with decortication ("shingling") to reach bleeding "paprika" bone; soft tissue flaps if envelope is scarred Ref 9.

Clinical Application — Checklist Approach For an atrophic humeral nonunion: Is it unstable? → Plate. Is it avascular? → Decortication. Is it cell-deficient? → Autograft. Is there a gap? → Scaffold. Studies applying this concept report union rates of roughly 80% in recalcitrant cohorts Ref 9.

4. Revision Plating vs. Exchange Nailing

The choice of fixation for aseptic humeral shaft nonunion typically lies between revision ORIF with plating and exchange intramedullary nailing. The current evidence strongly favors plating for most parameters.

4.1 Revision Plating with Bone Grafting

Widely considered the "gold standard" for aseptic nonunions, particularly atrophic or oligotrophic types Ref 8.

  • Technique: Open approach, radical excision of fibrous tissue, recanalization of medullary canal, decortication, fixation with 4.5 mm LC-DCP, autologous bone graft.
  • Union Rate: Exceeds 95–98% Level I · Ref 10. Meta-analysis: augmentative plating 100% vs exchange nailing 89.6% Level I · Ref 14.
  • Time to Union: ~5.4 months vs ~7.4 months for nailing Ref 14.
  • Functional Recovery: Shoulder function scores superior (rotator cuff sparing). Significantly better Constant-Murley and DASH scores Ref 15.
  • Complications: Transient radial nerve palsy ~10–12% (vs ~0% for nailing) Ref 16. Higher infection risk (RR 1.56) Ref 18.

4.2 Exchange Nailing

  • Technique: Remove indwelling nail, ream canal for biological stimulation and larger diameter, insert larger nail for interference fit.
  • Union Rate: 60–100% depending on series Ref 19. Key failure mode: persistent rotational instability Ref 20.
  • Complications: Shoulder impingement from proximal nail protrusion; iatrogenic fracture from reaming sclerotic canal.

5. Double Plating for Recalcitrant Cases

A single 4.5 mm broad compression plate may be insufficient in high mechanical demand or poor bone quality. Dual plating increases the area moment of inertia against torsional and bending forces Ref 4.

5.1 Indications

  1. Osteopenia/Osteoporosis: "Soft" bone compromises screw pullout strength; dual plates distribute load Ref 4.
  2. Distal/Proximal Nonunions: Limited real estate for screw purchase; sandwich or 90–90 configuration creates rigid girder construction.
  3. Recalcitrant Nonunion: Patients who failed single plate revision.
  4. Comminution/Defects: Implant must bear entire load; dual plates share this burden.

5.2 Biomechanical Configurations

  • 90-Degree (Orthogonal): One plate posteriorly + one laterally provides best restoration of compressive and torsional stiffness Ref 23 · Biomechanical.
  • Face-to-Face (Sandwich): Anterior + posterior plates — biomechanically sound but technically demanding.

5.3 Clinical Outcomes

Systematic review found no significant difference in union rates between single and double plating for routine fractures Level I · Ref 24. However, for nonunions, double plating showed superior early functional outcomes (MEPS, Constant Score) at 3 months, allowing immediate aggressive rehabilitation Ref 22. Double plating does not significantly increase nonunion or infection rates with careful soft tissue handling Ref 24.

6. Management of Bone Defects: The Masquelet Technique

Segmental bone defects exceeding the critical size (typically >3–4 cm or >2× the diaphyseal diameter) cannot spontaneously bridge Ref 25.

6.1 Critical Defect Size Decision

  • < 3–4 cm: Acute shortening viable. The humerus tolerates 3–5 cm of shortening with minimal functional impairment Ref 26.
  • > 4 cm: Reconstruction required — Masquelet technique, vascularized fibular graft (VFG), or bone transport.
  • > 10 cm: VFG or modified Masquelet (VFG inside induced membrane) preferred Ref 27.

6.2 The Two-Stage Protocol

Stage 1 — Induction (Week 0)
Radical debridement of infected/necrotic bone → PMMA antibiotic cement spacer (overlapping bone ends) → Rigid fixation (plate or external fixator). Over 4–8 weeks, a vascularized pseudo-synovial membrane forms, secreting VEGF and BMP-2 Ref 7.
↓
Stage 2 — Reconstruction (Week 6–8)
Membrane incised, spacer removed → "Biological chamber" filled with autologous cancellous bone graft (ICBG or RIA) → Membrane sutured closed over graft to prevent resorption and provide vascularity.

6.3 Efficacy

  • 18 patients (50% humeral): 100% union at median 6 months Level IV · Ref 28.
  • Upper limb systematic review: ~90% union, average defect 4.5 cm Level I · Ref 26.
  • vs. Vascularized Fibula: Similar union outcomes, but Masquelet is associated with shorter operative time, less blood loss, and shorter hospital stays Level III · Ref 29.

10. Conclusion

The management of humeral shaft nonunion has evolved from a purely mechanical pursuit to a sophisticated bio-engineering challenge. The evidence supports revision plating with autologous bone grafting as the gold standard for the majority of aseptic nonunions, offering superior union reliability and functional outcomes compared to exchange nailing.

For complex cases involving bone loss, the Masquelet technique offers a reproducible and effective salvage option, leveraging the body's own foreign body reaction to create a vascular bed for healing. By adhering to the Diamond Concept—systematically addressing cells, scaffolds, factors, stability, and vascularity—surgeons can reliably treat this debilitating condition and restore function to the upper extremity.
