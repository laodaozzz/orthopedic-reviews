1. Introduction: The Evolution of Surgical Intervention

Plantar fasciitis — increasingly referred to as plantar fasciosis to accurately reflect its histopathological nature of myxoid degeneration and collagen necrosis rather than acute inflammation — represents one of the most ubiquitous pathologies in foot and ankle surgery. E: N/S

Approximately 90% of patients respond to conservative modalities including eccentric stretching, orthotic offloading, corticosteroid injections, and extracorporeal shockwave therapy. E: N/S For the recalcitrant subset — patients enduring chronic, disabling heel pain impairing quality of life and activities of daily living — surgical intervention becomes a necessary consideration.

Historical Trajectory

Early open plantar fasciotomy, as described by DuVries, involved extensive medial incisions, complete fascial release, and often calcaneal exostosis excision. While effective, open procedures were associated with significant morbidity: prolonged recovery, hypertrophic scarring, medial calcaneal nerve entrapment, and arch instability. E: N/S

Paradigm Shift The advent of Endoscopic Plantar Fasciotomy (EPF) in the early 1990s — first described by Barrett and Day — utilized a two-portal system (medial and lateral) for direct visualization and controlled release, promising reduced postoperative pain, rapid weight-bearing, and improved visualization compared to open techniques.[7,8]

Contemporary evolution has produced two main variants: the Combined Medial-Lateral Portal (Barrett) technique and the Dual Medial Portal (Chow) technique. This review provides an exhaustive, evidence-based comparison of these approaches.

2. Surgical Anatomy & Biomechanical Considerations

A nuanced understanding of the plantar foot's complex anatomy is the prerequisite for safe endoscopic intervention. The procedure occurs in a confined physiological space where millimeter-level precision determines the difference between successful release and iatrogenic neurovascular injury.

2.1 The Plantar Aponeurosis

The plantar fascia is a complex, multilayered aponeurosis consisting of three distinct structural components:

Table 1 — Structural Components of the Plantar Aponeurosis
BandOriginInsertionClinical Significance
Central Band (thickest) Medial tubercle of calcaneus Bases of proximal phalanges via flexor tendon sheaths Primary target for EPF release; often thickened >4 mm on MRI with degenerative changes
Lateral Band Lateral aspect of calcaneus Base of 5th metatarsal Critical for lateral column stability; must be preserved to prevent lateral column instability (LCI)
Medial Band (thinnest) Medial calcaneal region Covers abductor hallucis Clinically less significant structurally; anatomical landmark for neurovascular bundle

2.2 The "Safe Zone" and Neurovascular Anatomy

The endoscopic channel is created in a tissue plane: deep (dorsal) to subcutaneous fat and superficial (plantar) to the flexor digitorum brevis (FDB) muscle belly and the plantar fascia itself.

⚠ Critical Danger Structures

Medial Calcaneal Nerve (MCN): Branches from the posterior tibial nerve; courses superficially over the medial heel. Improper medial portal placement risks transection → painful neuroma formation.[22,26]

Baxter's Nerve (First Branch of LPN): Runs immediately dorsal (superior) to the medial origin of the plantar fascia. Injury causes ADM atrophy and recalcitrant neurogenic heel pain — often worse than the original condition.[16,18]

2.3 Anatomical Landmarks for Portal Localization

Medial Portal:

  • Palpate the medial tubercle of the calcaneus (origin of central band)
  • Vertical reference: 1–3 mm distal to the calcaneal tubercle
  • Horizontal reference: approximately 17 mm dorsal from the plantar skin surface (non-weight bearing)[19,20]
  • The intersection ensures the cannula enters just plantar to the fascial band

Lateral Portal (Barrett technique):

  • Created via "inside-out" technique using trans-illumination/tenting
  • Ensures the lateral portal is in the exact transverse plane as the medial portal, preventing skewing

3. Biomechanics of Release: Partial vs. Complete

The central biomechanical dilemma of plantar fasciotomy is achieving sufficient release to relieve pain while maintaining ligamentous continuity to support the longitudinal arch. The plantar fascia acts via the windlass mechanism (Hicks) to elevate the arch and lock the midtarsal joints during propulsion. E: N/S

3.1 Consequences of Complete Release

  • Arch Collapse: Cadaveric studies show complete division reduces arch stiffness by ~25% and causes significant vertical settling[22]
  • Lateral Column Shift: Mechanical loads transfer laterally → calcaneocuboid joint strain → new lateral midfoot pain[15]
  • Metatarsal Loading: Altered forefoot loading patterns → increased central metatarsal pressure → potential stress fractures or metatarsalgia
⚠ Lateral Column Instability (LCI) — Critical Threshold Brugh et al. found patients with persistent lateral column pain had 60.6% of the fascia released, while those without pain had only 48.7% released. E: III[15]
→ Release must NOT exceed 50% of the fascial width.

3.2 The Optimal Extent of Release

✓ Evidence-Based Recommendation Partial fasciotomy confined to the medial 1/3 to 1/2 of the plantar fascia. This addresses the primary pathology while preserving the lateral band's "tie-rod" function, protecting the calcaneocuboid joint.E: II[3,15]

4. Comparative Review of Surgical Techniques

4.1 Combined Medial-Lateral Portal (Barrett Technique)

The Barrett technique utilizes a through-and-through tunnel to facilitate instrument passage and fluid management.[7,8,20]

Key Steps:

  1. Patient supine; thigh tourniquet for bloodless field
  2. Medial portal established per anatomical landmarks (Section 2.3); blunt dissection plantar to fascia
  3. Blunt obturator advanced laterally hugging plantar surface of fascia
  4. Lateral portal created over palpable obturator tip (inside-out)
  5. Slotted cannula placed from lateral to medial
  6. Arthroscope (4.0 mm, 30°) from lateral; hooked/triangle knife from medial
  7. Cotton swabs for debridement; fascia visualized as white fibrous band
  8. Release medial-to-lateral under dorsiflexion; stop at ~50% width; confirm full-thickness via FDB visualization

4.2 Dual Medial Portal (Chow/Modified Technique)

The dual medial portal approach prioritizes efficiency and minimizes lateral trauma.[6,9,10]

Key Steps:

  1. Similar setup; medial incision made
  2. Slotted cannula system (e.g., ECTRA II) introduced from medial side
  3. Lateral stab incision may anchor cannula tip, but primary instrumentation is medial-dominant
  4. Endoscope and retrograde knife work within cannula assembly from medial side
  5. Probe/ruler inserted medially to measure total fascial width
  6. Triangle knife marks lateral limit (50%); retrograde knife cuts lateral-to-medial
  7. Dorsiflexion maintained to facilitate fiber separation

5. Management of the Calcaneal Spur

The inferior calcaneal exostosis (heel spur) is now recognized as an adaptive traction osteophyte secondary to chronic fascial stress — not the primary pain generator. Modern consensus: routine resection during EPF is not recommended.[5,28]

Indications for Resection

  • Large, palpable exostosis causing distinct pressure point or deformity through the heel pad
  • Point tenderness maximally located directly over the bony prominence (not the fascial band)
  • Revision cases where fascial release alone failed and the spur is implicated in ongoing irritation

Endoscopic Resection Techniques

Three-Portal Technique (Plantar) Third portal on plantar heel for improved triangulation[31,32] Cadaveric validated: closest distance to LPN ≈15.3 mm; to Baxter's nerve ≈10.0 mm
Spur Resection Without Fascial Release Resects spur + decompresses calcaneal marrow edema while preserving arch mechanics[28,29] Controversial; less common. Preserves fascial biomechanics.

6. Intraoperative Safety: Protecting Baxter's Nerve

⚠ Baxter's Nerve — #1 Structure at Risk Injury results in recalcitrant neurogenic heel pain often worse than the original fasciitis. The nerve runs dorsal to the fascia, in the plane between abductor hallucis and quadratus plantae/FDB interface.[16,18]

Guidelines for Nerve Protection

  1. The "Plantar-to-Fascia" Rule: Maintain the instrument plane strictly plantar (inferior) to the fascia at all times. The trocar/obturator must slide along the smooth undersurface of the fascia. If it passes easily but feels "deep," it may be in the dorsal compartment with the nerve.
  2. Visual Landmarks:
    • "Pearl" Sign: Proximal plantar fascia appears as a pearl-shaped soft tissue structure under endoscopy — helps orient depth.
    • FDB Endpoint: Cutting through white fibrous fascia until reddish/dark muscular fibers of FDB are seen confirms full-thickness release. STOP — do not dissect into the muscle.
  3. Retrograde Cutting Technique: The blade engages the dorsal surface of fascia and pulls it plantar-wards, away from the nerve. Avoid "push" knives or dorsal-directed cutting.
  4. Ultrasound Pre-mapping (Optional): Using landmarks (inferior tip of medial malleolus → posterior tip of calcaneus) to map the nerve's course preoperatively. Valuable in anomalous anatomy or revision cases.[33,34]

8. Conclusion

Endoscopic Plantar Fasciotomy has redefined the surgical standard for recalcitrant plantar fasciitis. The comparative analysis reveals that while the Barrett technique offers through-and-through verification safety, the Chow technique provides statistically significant advantages in operative efficiency (~26 vs. ~46 minutes) E: III[6] and reduced lateral morbidity without compromising clinical outcomes.

✓ Three Pillars of EPF Success
  1. Release Extent: Partial release (medial 1/3 to 1/2) — never exceed 50% to prevent LCI
  2. Spur Management: Resect only with distinct clinical indications; use hooded burrs + fluoroscopy
  3. Nerve Safety: Strict plantar-to-fascia plane; use FDB visualization as endpoint
