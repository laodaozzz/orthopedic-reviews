Differential Diagnosis of Chronic Radiocapitellar Instability

An Interactive Educational Summary: Monteggia Fracture vs. Congenital Radial Head Dislocation in Children

1. The Diagnostic Dilemma: Trauma vs. Dysplasia

Differentiating between a chronic, neglected Monteggia fracture-dislocation and a Congenital Radial Head Dislocation (CRHD) is a critical challenge in pediatric orthopedics. While both present with a dislocated radial head, their etiologies, natural histories, and treatments are diametrically opposed.

Chronic Monteggia is a sequela of missed trauma, requiring reconstructive surgery (ulnar osteotomy, open reduction) to restore anatomy and function.III In contrast, CRHD is a developmental dysplasia where surgical reduction is often contraindicated due to the high risk of stiffness and arthritis.III

Clinical Imperative: Misdiagnosing a congenital dislocation as traumatic and attempting surgical reduction can lead to catastrophic outcomes. Accurate radiographic interpretation is the safeguard against this error.

2. Pathomechanics & Radiographic Morphology

The morphology of the radial head is the single most discriminatory factor.III

2.1 The Radial Head: "Concave vs. Dome"

Feature Chronic Monteggia Fracture (Early <3yr) Chronic Monteggia Fracture (Late >3yr) Congenital Radial Head Dislocation (CRHD)
Articular Surface Concave or Flat (retains traumatic "memory") Flattened, Irregular, or "Mushroomed" Convex, Dome-shaped, Oval (pathognomonic)
Radial Notch (Ulna) Normal depth Shallow (filled in), Increased Angle (mean 45.5° vs 29.7° normal)IV Hypoplastic / Absent from birth
Head Size/Neck Normal Hypertrophic / Broad Dysplastic / Variable / Often elongated, tapered neck

Table 1: Morphological comparison of the radial head and ulnar notch.

Key Insight: A dome-shaped, convex radial head in a child is essentially pathognomonic for congenital etiology, distinguishing it from the traumatic head which is concave (early) or irregularly flattened (late).III

2.2 The Capitellum: The "Anvil"

  • In CRHD: The capitellum is frequently hypoplastic, absent, or severely flattened due to a lack of developmental stimulation from the radial head.III
  • In Monteggia: The capitellum is usually normal or only slightly flattened, as it developed normally prior to the injury.

Imaging Pitfall: The "pseudo-defect" of the capitellum is a normal posterior-lateral groove on MRI that can be mistaken for a traumatic lesion. True dysplasia affects the anterior load-bearing surface.V

2.3 The Ulna: The "Silent" Culprit

The ulna often holds the definitive answer.

  • The Ulnar Bow Sign: On a true lateral radiograph, a straight line along the dorsal ulnar border should deviate <1mm. A deviation of >1mm is suspicious, and >4mm is diagnostic of plastic deformation from a Monteggia fracture.III
  • In CRHD: The ulna is generally straight but may be hypoplastic, resulting in a negative ulnar variance and an increased radius/ulna length ratio.III
  • Sclerosis: Focal sclerosis within the ulnar medullary canal can indicate a healed, missed fracture (Monteggia).

4. Treatment Implications

The correct diagnosis dictates the entire treatment algorithm.

  • For Chronic Monteggia: Surgical reconstruction is indicated. The goal is anatomical restoration via ulnar osteotomy (to correct bow and length) and open reduction of the radial head.III
  • For CRHD: Surgical reduction is contraindicated. The dysplastic joint surfaces are incompatible. Management is observation (most patients remain functional) or radial head excision after skeletal maturity if painful.III
