Advances in Cervical Endoscopic Spinal Surgery

Evidence-Based Interactive Summary

1. Introduction: The Paradigm Shift

The surgical management of cervical spine pathology is undergoing a profound transformation, characterized by a shift from extensive reconstruction to targeted preservation. For nearly half a century, Anterior Cervical Discectomy and Fusion (ACDF) has been the gold standard for cervical radiculopathy and myelopathy. Its dominance rests on reliable neural decompression and arthrodesis-provided stability. However, the biomechanical cost of fusion—loss of a motion segment and stress transfer to adjacent levels—has driven the search for motion-preserving alternatives. Level I

The emergence of Cervical Endoscopic Spinal Surgery (CESS) represents the culmination of this search. Unlike open microscopy, which requires significant soft tissue retraction, endoscopic surgery places the optical source directly at the pathology site. This "inside-out" visualization allows decompression with minimal damage to stabilizing structures—the anterior longitudinal ligament (ALL), uncinate processes, and facet joint capsules.

Scope of This Report

This document analyzes the two dominant endoscopic modalities challenging ACDF: Anterior Cervical Endoscopic Discectomy (AECD) (pioneered by Sebastian Ruetten) and Posterior Cervical Unilateral Biportal Endoscopy (UBE) (advanced by D.H. Heo and C.K. Park). It synthesizes seminal papers, systematic reviews, and technical reports covering techniques, outcomes, learning curves, and safety profiles.

2. Anterior Cervical Endoscopic Discectomy (AECD)

The anterior route provides direct access to the uncinate process, central disc, and anterior osteophytes—the most common offenders in cervical radiculopathy. Traditional open approaches, however, require significant retraction of visceral (esophagus/trachea) and vascular (carotid/jugular) compartments, leading to dysphagia and hematoma. AECD utilizes this anatomical interval via a percutaneous corridor, mitigating approach-related trauma while maintaining direct anterior access.

2.1 The Ruetten Philosophy: Selective Discectomy

The standardization of Full-Endoscopic Anterior Cervical Discectomy (FACD) is largely attributed to Sebastian Ruetten. His technique centers on "selective discectomy"—only pathological tissue compressing the nerve is removed, preserving disc integrity and the motion segment. This contrasts with the "complete discectomy" required for ACDF. Level I

2.1.1 The "Safe Working Zone"

AECD feasibility relies on the precise identification of the safe working zone—defined medially by the visceral compartment (trachea/esophagus) and laterally by the vascular compartment (carotid artery/jugular vein). Ruetten emphasizes a tactile, rather than purely radiographic, identification of this interval.

The surgeon stands contralateral to the pathology and uses two fingers to deeply palpate the interval between the carotid pulse and the trachea. This "two-finger technique" serves two functions: confirming vascular anatomy tactilely and physically displacing visceral structures medially, creating a protected corridor for needle insertion. This is the primary safety mechanism against esophageal perforation.

2.1.2 Step-by-Step Surgical Technique

  1. Percutaneous Access & Discography: An 18-gauge spinal needle is inserted under fluoroscopic guidance. The trajectory enters centrally or slightly contralaterally to reach the symptomatic uncinate region. Intraoperative discography with radiopaque contrast and indigo carmine (blue dye) stains degenerated nucleus pulposus blue, while healthy annulus remains white—guiding selective tissue removal.
  2. Dilation and Anchoring: A guidewire replaces the needle; a 3–5 mm skin incision is made. Sequential blunt dilators separate prevertebral fascia and longus colli muscles. A specialized working sheath (outer diameter 3.8–6.9 mm) is anchored into the anterior annulus, creating a sealed working environment. Level II
  3. Endoscopic Decompression: A high-definition uniportal endoscope is introduced through the sheath with continuous saline irrigation for visualization and hemostasis.
    • Soft Disc Removal: Blue dye-guided removal of stained nucleus pulposus using micro-forceps and flexible graspers, proceeding from center to PLL.
    • Osteophytectomy (Hard Disc): A high-speed endoscopic diamond-tip burr resects posterior osteophytes and the uncinate process (foraminoplasty) under direct vision.
    • Annuloplasty: Bipolar radiofrequency probe ablates annular fissures and ensures hemostasis, potentially reducing recurrence risk. Level III

2.2 Clinical Outcomes: AECD vs. ACDF

2.2.1 Neurological Recovery and Pain Metrics

In a landmark prospective, randomized controlled study, Ruetten et al. compared AECD with ACDF. Level I

  • Pain Reduction: Both groups showed significant reduction in arm and neck pain (VAS) postoperatively (P < 0.001), maintained at 2-year follow-up.
  • Success Rates: At 2 years, 85.9% of the endoscopic group were pain-free; an additional 10.1% reported only occasional pain—statistically equivalent to ACDF.
  • Systematic Review Verification: A systematic review of over 1,300 articles confirmed that for single-level soft disc herniation, AECD outcomes (VAS, NDI, Macnab criteria) are statistically indistinguishable from ACDF. Level I

2.2.2 Perioperative Advantages

  • Operative Efficiency: Mean AECD operative time ~50–65 min vs. ACDF ~80–105 min (once the learning curve is overcome).
  • Hospitalization: AECD is inherently outpatient (<24 h stay) vs. 1–4 days for ACDF.
  • Motion Preservation: AECD retains the motion segment, theoretically reducing Adjacent Segment Disease (ASD) incidence. Level III

2.3 Major Complications of the Anterior Approach

2.3.1 Visceral and Vascular Injuries

Esophageal perforation is a rare but catastrophic risk during needle insertion. Ruetten emphasizes tactile confirmation of the trachea and carotid over fluoroscopy alone. The use of blunt dilators (rather than cutting instruments) significantly mitigates this risk.

2.3.2 Neurological and Recurrence Risks

  • Recurrent Herniation: ~3–7% (comparable to lumbar microdiscectomy). Thorough thermal annuloplasty and fragment removal reduce this risk. Level I
  • Spinal Cord Injury: Risk from increased intrathecal pressure (if irrigation outflow is obstructed) or inadvertent instrument advancement. Mitigated by safety stop design and fluoroscopic depth checks. Level II

3. Posterior Cervical Unilateral Biportal Endoscopy (UBE)

While the anterior approach excels for central pathologies, the posterior approach is preferred for foraminal stenosis, lateral disc herniations, and ossification of the posterior longitudinal ligament (OPLL). UBE (Biportal Endoscopic Spinal Surgery / BESS) utilizes two independent portals—a viewing portal and a working portal—mimicking the triangulation ergonomics of knee arthroscopy and open microsurgery, offering unparalleled instrument freedom. Level II

3.1 Technical Innovations: Heo & Park Methodologies

3.1.1 Patient Positioning & Water Management

The patient is placed prone with the neck flexed to open interlaminar spaces. The head is secured (Mayfield clamp or taped) with eye protection. Continuous saline irrigation is essential for visualization and hemostasis.

⚠ Critical Safety Parameter: Hydrostatic Pressure

Inflow pressure must be maintained below 30 mmHg with unimpeded outflow. Excessive pressure can cause neck swelling (airway compromise) or increased intracranial pressure. Not specified in source

3.1.2 Portal Placement & The "V-Point"

Correct portal placement is the cornerstone of UBE. Landmarks are identified fluoroscopically relative to the pedicles.

  • The "V-Point": Formed by the intersection of the inferior margin of the upper lamina, the superior margin of the lower lamina, and the medial aspect of the facet joint. This is the safe starting point for drilling. Level III
  • Scope Portal: 1–2 cm cranial to the disc space (over cranial pedicle).
  • Working Portal: 1–2 cm caudal to the disc space (over caudal pedicle).
  • Distance: Portals spaced ~2–3 cm apart; triangulation angle of 30–45°.

3.1.3 Advanced Techniques

  • Sliding Technique (Heo et al.): For multi-level pathology (e.g., C5–6 and C6–7), the surgeon pivots instruments subcutaneously after treating the first level, "sliding" caudally under muscle fascia to decompress the second level without new incisions. Level III
  • Posterior Cervical Inclinatory Foraminotomy (PCIF) (Song & Lee): Instead of resecting the dorsal facet (risk: instability if >50% resected), the drill is angled to remove only the medial aspect of the superior articular process (SAP) and osteophytes. This "dome-shaped decompression" preserves biomechanical stability. Level III

Level II
  • Initial Phase (First 10–30 cases): Longer operative times; higher risk of dural tears as surgeons adapt to "floating" instruments and triangulation mechanics.
  • Proficiency Phase (30–50+ cases): Operative times plateau; complication rates stabilize.
  • Mastery (≥54 cases): Surgeons can consistently handle complications and complex variations efficiently (Heo et al.).
  • Comparative Ease: UBE's learning curve is "gentler" than uniportal endoscopy for surgeons with open microsurgery or knee arthroscopy training—the separation of hands allows familiar instrument handling.
Training Recommendation

Experts universally recommend mastering UBE in the lumbar spine first—where the thecal sac is robust—before attempting cervical cases. Procedural competency in lumbar UBE is considered a prerequisite.

3.3 Safety Profiles and Complications

  • Dural Tears: Incidence 2.9–6.2%. UBE's separate working portal allows introduction of suturing instruments for primary closure underwater. Level II
  • C5 Palsy: ~2–3% (similar to open laminoplasty), attributed to the "tethering effect" as the cord shifts backward post-decompression.
  • Neck Swelling / Airway Compromise: Extravasation of fluid into retropharyngeal space. Managed by limiting pump pressure (<30 mmHg), widely spaced portals, and efficient operative times.
  • Postoperative Headaches: From increased intrathecal pressure; transient, managed conservatively.

4.1 Clinical Efficacy Summary

  • AECD vs. ACDF: Ruetten's RCTs confirm equivalent pain relief (~90% success) for soft disc herniation, with faster recovery and no graft-related complications. Level I
  • UBE vs. ACDF: An RCT found no significant difference in VAS/NDI/mJOA at 1 year, but UBE had significantly shorter hospital stays (3.5 vs. 5.7 days) and trend toward lower complication rates. Level I
  • UBE vs. Uniportal PE: Comparable clinical outcomes; UBE has slightly higher intraoperative blood loss but offers broader decompression and is easier to learn. Level I

4.2 The Future: Hybrid Technique

Recent innovations, such as the Hybrid Technique (HT) described by Yan et al. (referencing Heo's biportal principles), combine benefits of both uniportal and biportal systems. By utilizing a uniportal endoscope within a UBE setup, surgeons create a dual-channel environment with the protection of a docked sleeve (uniportal) and the freedom of a separate working instrument (biportal). Level III

6. Conclusion

Cervical Endoscopic Spinal Surgery has matured from a niche experimental field into a robust, evidence-based discipline challenging the historical dominance of fusion. Anterior AECD offers a definitive, motion-preserving solution for central disc pathology, virtually eliminating dysphagia and reducing ASD risk. Posterior Cervical UBE provides a versatile platform for foraminal stenosis and lateral herniations, replicating open microsurgery safety with a fraction of soft tissue trauma.

These techniques demand rigorous adherence to technical protocols—the "safe zone" palpation in AECD and hydrostatic pressure management in UBE—but offer superior perioperative metrics. As training programs standardize the learning curve and instrumentation continues to evolve, endoscopic techniques are poised to become the new gold standard for patients seeking effective decompression without the lifelong biomechanical penalty of fusion.
