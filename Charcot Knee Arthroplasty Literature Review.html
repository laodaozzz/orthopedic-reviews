Introduction to Neuropathic Arthropathy

Neuropathic (Charcot) arthropathy is a progressive, destructive inflammatory process affecting the joint, stemming from profound sensory, motor, and autonomic neuropathy. Originally linked to tabes dorsalis from neurosyphilis, the primary etiology is now poorly controlled diabetes mellitus, with other causes including syringomyelia, leprosy, chronic alcoholism, and hereditary neuropathies like Charcot-Marie-Tooth (CMT) disease. Level IV

While most common in the foot and ankle, its manifestation in the knee presents a unique reconstructive challenge due to massive bone fragmentation, capsuloligamentous laxity, and multiplanar instability. Historically, TKA was contraindicated due to high failure rates, but modern constrained prostheses and porous metal fixation have revolutionized treatment algorithms. Level I (Systematic Review)

Pathophysiology & Diagnostic Challenges

The disease arises from a synergistic combination of theories:

  • Neurotraumatic Theory: Loss of pain perception and proprioception leads to unchecked, repetitive microtrauma. Level V
  • Neurovascular Theory: Autonomic dysfunction causes hyperemia and arteriovenous shunting, upregulating osteoclastic activity and bone resorption. Level V
  • Inflammatory Cascade: Upregulation of IL-1, TNF-alpha, and the RANK/RANKL/OPG pathway accelerates bone destruction. Level V
Clinical Hallmark: A profound dissociation between reported pain and the extent of structural destruction visible on imaging. Average diagnosis delay is approximately 50.5 months.

Presentation includes unilateral swelling, warmth, massive effusions, and erythema. Joint aspiration is mandatory to rule out infection (negative cultures) and pseudogout (absence of CPPD crystals). Level IV

Eichenholtz Classification & Surgical Timing

The Eichenholtz staging system remains critical for guiding management and determining the timing for surgical intervention. Expert Consensus

Table 1: The Eichenholtz Classification of Charcot Arthropathy
Stage Phase Clinical Findings Radiographic Findings
0 Prodromal Swelling, warmth, erythema. Stable joint. Normal X-ray. MRI shows soft tissue/edema.
I Development Worsening symptoms, onset of laxity. Osteopenia, fragmentation, debris, subluxation.
II Coalescence Decreased warmth/swelling, resolving erythema. Debris absorption, sclerosis, fragment consolidation.
III Reconstruction Stable but deformed joint. No inflammation. Deformity consolidated, severe arthrosis.
Critical Surgical Timing: Total knee arthroplasty (TKA) is strictly contraindicated during the acute inflammatory phases (Stages 0 & I). Intervention is exclusively reserved for patients in the coalescence (Stage II) or reconstruction (Stage III) phases when bone quality is sufficient for fixation.

Implant Selection: The Constraint Debate

Due to global ligamentous incompetence, standard CR or PS implants are contraindicated. The debate centers on Constrained Condylar Knee (CCK) vs. Rotating Hinge Knee (RHK) prostheses. Level II (Meta-analysis)

Table 2: Comparison of Prosthetic Constraint in Charcot Knee Arthroplasty
Constraint Type 10-Yr Survivorship 15-Yr Revision Risk Primary Indications Functional Outcome
Constrained Condylar (CCK) ~83.8% 11.3% Moderate laxity, intact extensor, contained bone loss. Slightly superior (closer to native kinematics).
Rotating Hinge (RHK) ~81.3% 13.6% Global instability, AORI Type 3 defects, CMT disease, recurvatum. Excellent salvage; profound pain relief.

Management of Massive Bone Defects

Profound, uncontained bone loss is hallmark. The Anderson Orthopaedic Research Institute (AORI) classification guides reconstruction. Charcot knees often present as AORI Type 2B or 3. Level IV

Modern Fixation Strategies

  • Structural Allografts: Poor incorporation in neuropathic bone; high resorption and loosening rates. Reserved for extreme cases. Level IV
  • Metaphyseal Sleeves: Effective but require intact cortical rim; risk of iatrogenic fracture. Level IV
  • Trabecular Metal Cones: Current gold standard. 75-80% porosity, elastic modulus mimics bone. Provide immediate stability and excellent osseointegration (>94%). 10-yr survivorship free of aseptic revision: ~97%. Level III (Cohort Study)
  • 3D-Printed Custom Implants: For extreme, atypical defects. Perfect fit reduces need for bone preparation. Early results show excellent stability. Level IV

Kinematic Restoration & Extensor Mechanism

Accurate joint line (JL) restoration is critical but difficult due to obliterated landmarks. Elevation ≥5mm leads to poor functional outcomes (KSS, WOMAC). Level III

Referencing Techniques

  1. Fibular Head: Traditionally 1.5-2.0 cm proximal, but highly variable and now considered unreliable.
  2. Epicondylar Ratio: Distance from lateral epicondyle to JL / Transepicondylar width ≈ 0.28 (28%). Level IV
  3. Adductor Tubercle Formula: JL Distance (mm) = 6.40 + (Femoral Width [mm] × 0.49), measured from apex of adductor tubercle. Level III (MRI Study)

Managing Patellar Maltracking

Chronic dislocation is common. Management requires a stepwise approach:

  • Exposure: Subvastus approach preserves VMO, enabling faster straight leg raise. Tibial Tubercle Osteotomy (TTO) for stiff, deformed knees.
  • Realignment: Extensive lateral retinacular release. Medial plication or VMO advancement. Medial Patellofemoral Ligament (MPFL) reconstruction for incompetent medial structures.
  • Extensor Rupture: Requires massive augmentation (allograft or synthetic mesh) and prolonged immobilization.

Synthesis: Long-Term Outcomes & Complications

Contemporary evidence (2016-2026) shows improved outcomes with modern techniques, but complication rates remain high due to the underlying neuropathy. Level I (Systematic Review)

Key Conclusion: Disease etiology does not significantly affect outcomes, but temporal stage at surgery is the ultimate determinant of success. Operations prior to Eichenholtz Stage II/III are destined to fail.

Conclusion & Protocol-Defining Principles

The management of Charcot knee is a frontier in complex arthroplasty, requiring abandon of routine primary principles. The evidence mandates:

  1. Strict Surgical Timing: Intervene only in Eichenholtz Stage II or III.
  2. Mandatory Constraint: Unconstrained implants have no place. CCK for moderate cases, RHK for global instability/defects.
  3. Biologic Fixation Over Allograft: Trabecular metal cones/sleeves are the gold standard for metaphyseal defects.
  4. Kinematic Precision: Restore joint line using mathematical formulas; aggressively manage maltracking via TTO, releases, and reconstructions.

While aseptic survivorship is now ~88% at 10 years, the systemic neuropathy ensures high overall complication rates, particularly periprosthetic joint infection (PJI). Success relies on impeccable surgical technique, rigorous patient selection, multidisciplinary optimization, and lifelong surveillance.
