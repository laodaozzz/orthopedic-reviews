📋 Executive Summary

The half-decade spanning 2020 to 2025 witnessed a fundamental restructuring of the epistemological and clinical frameworks governing musculoskeletal infection. This period was characterized not merely by incremental updates to antibiotic regimens but by a radical redefinition of core disease states.

Key Paradigm Shift: The historical, monolithic concept of "osteomyelitis" has been effectively dismantled in orthopedic trauma literature, replaced by the precise, consensus-driven ontology of "Fracture-Related Infection" (FRI). Simultaneously, PJI management has moved from an era of "expert opinion" to one of rigorous algorithmic precision, driven by competing guidelines from MSIS and EBJIS.

Transatlantic Divergence in Diagnostic Philosophy

A distinct transatlantic divergence in diagnostic philosophy emerged during this period:

  • North American guidelines (AAOS, MSIS) continue to prioritize specificity to safeguard against unnecessary revision surgery, influenced by CDC and insurance surveillance demands. Level III
  • European guidelines (EBJIS, Pro-Implant Foundation) have embraced a high-sensitivity paradigm, validating aggressive treatment of "culture-negative" or "low-grade" infections that fall below American thresholds. Level III

📚 Part I: The Modern Canon — Authoritative Textbooks

In a field advancing as rapidly as musculoskeletal infection, standard reference texts stabilize the oscillation of clinical trials into a coherent pathophysiological narrative. Between 2020 and 2025, updated editions of the field's most venerable texts solidified the shift toward biofilm-centric management and interdisciplinary care.

1. Bone and Joint Infections (Zimmerli, 2nd Ed., 2021)

Editor: Werner Zimmerli · Publisher: Wiley-Blackwell · ISBN: 978-1-119-72065-2 Level I

Widely regarded as the father of modern implant-associated infection management, Zimmerli curated this edition to address the "post-genomic" era of diagnostics while retaining rigorous bedside management focus.

The Biofilm Paradigm

The 2021 edition significantly expands the section on biofilm physiology, moving beyond simple glycocalyx descriptions to discuss metabolic dormancy of "persister cells" — a subpopulation of bacteria within the biofilm that are phenotypically tolerant (rather than genetically resistant) to antibiotics. This pathophysiological grounding explains why 6 weeks of IV therapy often fails in the presence of retained hardware.

Critical Insight: Biofilm-active antibiotics (Rifampin for Staphylococci, Fluoroquinolones for Gram-negatives) are only effective after rigorous surgical debridement of the biomass. The text provides evidence-based DAIR limits: symptom duration <3–4 weeks. Level II

Sonication as Standard of Care

New chapters detail the logistics of sonication — using low-frequency ultrasound to dislodge biofilm from explanted hardware for culture. The text establishes sonication as standard of care for culture-negative PJI in Europe, reflecting strong influence of Swiss and German schools. Level II

2. OKU: Musculoskeletal Infection 2 (AAOS, 2024)

Editor: Montri D. Wongworawat · Publisher: AAOS/Wolters Kluwer · ISBN: 978-1-97-520242-2 Level I

This text synthesizes the explosion of literature between 2018 and the post-pandemic era, representing the consensus of the American orthopedic establishment. It is the primary resource for US board certification and recertification.

Host Optimization & Risk Stratification

A dominant theme is the shift from "infection treatment" to "infection prevention" through host optimization:

  • Glycemic Control: HbA1c thresholds (<7.5% or 8.0%) alone are insufficient; the text advocates for fructosamine testing and tighter perioperative glucose monitoring.
  • Obesity & Malnutrition: Updated protocols for nutritional screening (albumin, transferrin, prealbumin); discussion of denying surgery to BMI >40 patients based on registry data showing exponential infection risks.

The Biomarker Revolution

The 2024 edition provides an evidence-based appraisal of Alpha-defensin. While early industry-sponsored studies promised near-perfect sensitivity, independent 2020–2023 literature suggests it is not a "magic bullet" and should be interpreted within the MSIS criteria context. The text also introduces D-Dimer for PJI diagnosis, noting its utility in ruling out infection in patients with high baseline inflammation (e.g., rheumatoid arthritis). Level II

Formal Adoption of FRI

Critically, OKU 2 formally adopts the term "Fracture-Related Infection" (FRI), integrating 2018/2020 international consensus definitions and signaling the end of "post-traumatic osteomyelitis" as a diagnostic label in American orthopedic discourse.

3. Principles of Orthopedic Infection Management (AO Trauma)

Editors: Stephen L. Kates, Olivier Borens · Publisher: Thieme / AO Foundation Practice Standard

This foundational text for AO Trauma courses focuses on the "hardware" of infection management, providing illustrated, step-by-step guides for the Masquelet Technique (two-stage management of large infected bone defects) and Spacer Fabrication (high-dose antibiotic thresholds: >4g per 40g PMMA bag for elution vs. low-dose prophylaxis).

4. Oxford Handbook of Trauma and Orthopaedics (2nd Ed., 2025)

Released in late 2025, this edition aligns with the UK's "Get It Right First Time" (GIRFT) initiative, which centralized PJI care into specialized "Revision Hubs." It serves as a concise repository for the 2023 SANJO guidelines and provides specific antibiotic dosing compliant with the British National Formulary (BNF).

5. Musculoskeletal Infections (ID Clinics of North America, 2025)

This specialized volume moves beyond standard management to address "salvage" scenarios, including phage therapy for pan-resistant organisms and the transformative role of Dalbavancin/Oritavancin (half-lives allowing weekly dosing), revolutionizing OPAT by eliminating the need for permanent central venous access.

⚔️ Part II: The Definition Wars — Periprosthetic Joint Infection

The period between 2020 and 2025 was dominated by a philosophical and practical conflict regarding PJI definition. The clinician must navigate two distinct frameworks: the specificity-focused American criteria (MSIS) and the sensitivity-focused European criteria (EBJIS).

1. The MSIS / AAOS Framework (2018/2021)

Philosophy: Specificity is paramount — a "Rule-In" approach to avoid unnecessary revision surgery. Level II

MSIS Scoring System: A weighted score of ≥6 is definitive for infection. Major criteria (sinus tract, 2 positive cultures with same organism) are definitive. Minor criteria include elevated CRP (>10 mg/L) or D-Dimer (>860 ng/mL): 2 pts; ESR (>30 mm/hr): 1 pt; Synovial WBC (>3,000 cells/µL): 3 pts; Synovial PMN% (>80%): 1 pt; Alpha-defensin (>1.0 S/CO): 3 pts; Leukocyte Esterase (++ or higher): 3 pts; Single positive culture: 2 pts.

2020–2025 Status: This remains the standard for US CDC and NHSN surveillance. Required for ICD-10 coding and quality reporting in American hospitals. Critics argue the strict 3,000 WBCs/µL threshold misses "low-grade" infections caused by indolent organisms like Cutibacterium acnes.

2. The EBJIS Definition (2021)

Philosophy: Sensitivity is paramount — the cost of a "false negative" (leaving an infected implant in place) outweighs the risk of a false positive. Level II

Three-Tier Structure:

  • Infection Unlikely: Clear alternative cause, normal biomarkers.
  • Infection Likely (Critical Innovation): Includes cases with single positive culture, elevated biomarkers but negative cultures, or radiological loosening without mechanical cause. Recommendation: Treat as infected.
  • Infection Confirmed: Sinus tract, microbial growth, or visible pus.
Clinical Impact: A 2023 survey of MSIS and EBJIS members revealed a sharp divide: 80% of European centers adopted EBJIS definitions to capture culture-negative cases, while North American centers remain largely adherent to MSIS criteria. Level III

🦴 Part III: Fracture-Related Infection — A New Ontology

One of the most significant achievements of this period was the successful decoupling of trauma-associated infections from the imprecise term "osteomyelitis," driven by recognition that infected fracture fixation devices behave fundamentally differently from hematogenous osteomyelitis. Level II

The 2020 FRI Consensus Definition

The AO Foundation and EBJIS finalized the FRI consensus definition in 2020, creating a binary diagnostic path based on "Confirmatory" signs (diagnostic) and "Suggestive" signs (trigger further workup).

Confirmatory Criteria (Diagnostic of FRI)

  • Fistula/Sinus: Sinus tract or wound breakdown communicating with bone or implant.
  • Purulence: Purulent drainage from wound or presence of pus during surgery.
  • Microbiology: Phenotypically indistinguishable pathogens from ≥2 separate deep tissue specimens. Superficial swabs are explicitly excluded.
  • Histopathology: Microorganisms in deep tissue with >5 neutrophils per high-power field. This is a major addition in the 2020 update.

Suggestive Criteria (Trigger Investigation)

  • Clinical: Redness, fever, localized warmth, new onset pain.
  • Radiological: Osteolysis, implant loosening, sequestration, failure of union progression.
  • Microbiological: Single positive culture from deep tissue sample.
  • Biomarkers: Elevated CRP, ESR, or WBC.
Why "Osteomyelitis" Is Obsolete in Trauma: For any infection involving trauma hardware (plates, nails, screws), the term FRI is now mandatory in high-impact journals. This reflects the unique pathophysiology where biofilm on hardware is the primary driver of persistence, necessitating implant removal or exchange — unlike native osteomyelitis where antibiotics alone might suffice. Level II

The literature from 2020–2025 increasingly restricts "osteomyelitis" to specific scenarios: hematogenous osteomyelitis (children/adults vertebral), diabetic foot osteomyelitis, and Cierny-Mader classifications for chronic native bone infection.

📜 Part IV: Clinical Practice Guidelines — Prevention & Prophylaxis

The 2020–2025 period saw the release of several high-impact guidelines that directly altered clinical practice.

1. AAOS Dental Prophylaxis Update (2024) — Major Reversal

Endorsing Bodies: IDSA, MSIS, AAHKS Level I

For decades, millions of doses of prophylactic antibiotics were prescribed annually based on the belief that bacteremia from dental procedures could seed a prosthetic joint. The 2024 guideline, based on systematic review of 2020–2023 literature, found no strong evidence to support this causal link. Daily activities like brushing teeth and chewing food cause transient bacteremia of similar magnitude.

Recommendation: Discontinue routine antibiotic prophylaxis for patients with total joint replacements undergoing dental procedures — regardless of time since surgery. This applies even within 2 years. Shared decision-making only for severely immunocompromised patients; the default is now "No Prophylaxis."

2. ESCMID/EUCIC Preoperative Decolonization (2024)

Target: MDR Gram-Positive Bacteria · Published in Clinical Microbiology and Infection Level I

While the AAOS relaxed dental guidelines, ESCMID tightened preoperative protocols:

  • Mandate: Universal screening or targeted decolonization for S. aureus (MSSA and MRSA) prior to orthopedic surgery involving implants.
  • Protocol: Nasal Mupirocin (2× daily for 5 days) + Chlorhexidine body wash.
  • Evidence: Meta-analyses show significant SSI rate reduction (RR ~0.50) when carriers are decolonized.

3. IDSA Pediatric Osteomyelitis Guidelines (2021)

Topic: Acute Hematogenous Osteomyelitis (AHO) Level I

Codified the shift toward early oral therapy. Building on the OVIVA trial, the guideline suggests that for uncomplicated AHO, short IV induction (2–4 days) followed by high-dose oral therapy is non-inferior to prolonged IV courses. This has radically changed patient experience, allowing earlier discharge and fewer complications.

🔬 Part V: Therapeutic Controversies & Consensus

1. Antibiotic Duration: The 6 vs. 12 Weeks Debate

The DATIPO Trial (2021)

Design: Randomized, open-label, non-inferiority trial. Question: Can PJI be treated with 6 weeks instead of 12? Level I

Result: Failure to show non-inferiority. Higher failure rate in the 6-week arm for PJI. Implication: Unlike native osteomyelitis (where 6 weeks is standard), PJI with retained implant or complex exchanges requires longer durations. The "shorter is better" mantra of general ID stewardship hit a wall due to implant-associated biofilm recalcitrance.

The SOLARIO Trial (2024/2025)

Topic: Short or Long Antibiotic Regimes in Orthopaedics. Level II

Preliminary discussions suggest a nuanced outcome: true "short course" (7 days) works for removed implants, but retained hardware still demands prolonged suppression. The trial pushes toward "tailored duration" based on surgical margin and host status rather than fixed calendars.

2. The Rifampin Conundrum

Rifampin has been the cornerstone of staphylococcal PJI treatment since the 1990s. Recent evidence introduces nuance:

  • VA Study (2023): Clear benefit of adjunctive Rifampin in Diabetic Foot Osteomyelitis, reducing amputation risks. Level II
  • PJI Context: Emerging 2023/2024 data questions the magnitude of benefit in PJI, suggesting rigorous debridement (DAIR) is the primary driver of success.
  • Consensus: Both IDSA and EBJIS continue to recommend Rifampin for S. aureus PJI with implant retention (DAIR), provided the wound is dry and the patient can tolerate the drug. It remains the only agent with proven in vivo efficacy against staphylococcal biofilm persisters. Level II

3. SANJO Guidelines: Native Septic Arthritis (2023)

SANJO = Septic Arthritis in Native Joints · Released by EBJIS Level II

Key Recommendation: Emergency Lavage. Septic arthritis is classified as a surgical emergency comparable to compartment syndrome. Joint lavage (arthroscopic or open) is mandated within 24 hours of diagnosis. Antibiotic duration is guided by biomarker normalization (CRP) — stop therapy 2 weeks after normalization and clinical symptom resolution.

🔭 Part VI: Advanced Diagnostics & Future Directions

The limitations of culture-based diagnostics (sensitivity ~70–80%) have driven adoption of advanced technologies.

1. Sonication & the Pro-Implant Algorithm

The Pro-Implant Foundation, led by Andrej Trampuz, standardized sonication for explanted hardware. Low-frequency ultrasound disrupts the biofilm, releasing bacteria into fluid for culture. Level II

A 2023 study demonstrated that using the Pro-Implant algorithm (mandating sonication and specific antibiotic cocktails) significantly improved eradication rates in PJI. While standard in German-speaking Europe, sonication remains logistically difficult in the US due to the need for specialized sonic baths and lack of specific CPT reimbursement codes.

2. Host Immunity & the ORS Consensus (2023)

The ORS International Consensus Meeting (2023) focused on host immunity — recognizing that "clearing the bacteria" is only half the battle. Level III

  • The Shift: Defective host immunity (T-cell exhaustion) contributes to recurrence.
  • Future Therapy: Potential for immunotherapy (checkpoint inhibitors) to reverse T-cell exhaustion in chronic osteomyelitis — a concept borrowed from oncology, currently in pre-clinical trials.

3. Long-Acting Lipoglycopeptides

Dalbavancin and Oritavancin, with half-lives allowing weekly dosing, have revolutionized OPAT for osteomyelitis by eliminating the need for permanent central venous access, thereby reducing line-associated complications. Level II

4. Phage Therapy

Compassionate use of bacteriophages for pan-resistant organisms (Pseudomonas, Acinetobacter) is discussed in the 2025 ID Clinics volume. Logistical hurdles of custom phage compounding are being addressed through the establishment of "Phage Banks" at centers like Mayo Clinic and Yale. Level IV
