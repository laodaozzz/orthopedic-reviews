Pediatric versus Adult Ankle Injury

Ligamentous Injury, Physeal Pathomechanics & Clinical Management — Evidence-Based Educational Summary

1. Introduction

The ankle joint is a critical nexus of biomechanical transmission and one of the most frequent sites of pathology in both pediatric and adult populations. While clinical presentations—pain, edema, and functional loss—appear similar, the underlying pathomechanics, diagnostic imperatives, and management strategies diverge profoundly between the skeletally immature and mature ankle.

In the adult, the ankle is a mature construct where ligamentous tensile strength is generally inferior to osseous integrity, predisposing to ligamentous sprains. In the pediatric and adolescent ankle, the presence of the physis (growth plate) creates a distinct biomechanical hierarchy in which the physis often represents the point of mechanical failure, fundamentally altering injury patterns.

The burden of ankle injuries in the pediatric population is substantial. Ankle trauma accounts for a significant proportion of pediatric emergency department visits and orthopedic consultations, driven largely by increasing participation in organized, high-demand sports. Evidence Level V [1,2] The incidence of ankle sprains has been estimated at 2.15 per 1,000 person-years in the general population, with a distinct peak in the adolescent cohort aged 15–19 years. Evidence Level II [3] Recent systematic reviews and consensus statements have illuminated a concerning prevalence of long-term sequelae, including chronic ankle instability (CAI) and premature growth arrest, challenging the historical laissez-faire approach. Evidence Level I [5]

Scope of This Review: This report synthesizes data from systematic reviews, clinical trials, and consensus statements to examine the pathomechanical inversion of failure points, diagnostic rules (e.g., Ottawa Ankle Rules) in developing skeletons, the evolving paradigm of functional rehabilitation versus immobilization, and the divergent spectrum of complications from physeal bars to chronic instability.

2. Pathomechanical Divergence: Physis versus Ligament

The cornerstone of understanding pediatric ankle trauma is recognition that the pediatric skeleton is not merely a smaller version of the adult skeleton—it is a biologically distinct entity with unique material properties and failure mechanisms.

2.1 The Biomechanical Hierarchy: The "Weak Link" Phenomenon

In the skeletally mature adult, the lateral ligament complex (ATFL, CFL, PTFL) possesses tensile strength generally lower than the cortical bone to which it attaches. Under inversion stress, energy dissipates through rupture of ligamentous fibers—a sprain. The bone typically remains intact unless force is sufficient for avulsion or malleolar fracture. Evidence Level V [1]

In the skeletally immature patient, this hierarchy is inverted. The physis, particularly the hypertrophic zone, represents a plane of relative structural weakness. Biomechanical studies suggest the physis may be two to five times weaker than associated ligamentous structures. Evidence Level II (biomechanical) [8] Therefore, the same inversion mechanism producing a grade III ATFL rupture in an adult is more likely to propagate a fracture through the distal fibular physis in a child, resulting in a Salter-Harris Type I (SH I) fracture. Evidence Level V [9]

This "weak link" phenomenon has historically led to the axiom that "children do not get sprains; they get fractures." However, this binary distinction has been challenged by MRI-based systematic reviews:

  • SH I fractures were diagnosed in 0%–57.5% of cases across various series. Evidence Level I [11]
  • ATFL injuries were identified in 3.2%–80% of symptomatic pediatric ankles. Evidence Level I [11]

This discrepancy confirms that while the physis is mechanically vulnerable, ligaments are not immune to failure, and concomitant injuries to both structures can occur, particularly as the child approaches skeletal maturity. [11,12]

The Salter-Harris classification remains the standard system for describing physeal injuries:

The "mimicry" between SH I fractures and sprains carries real prognostic implications: a missed SH I fracture carries a small but non-zero risk of premature physeal closure—a complication nonexistent in adult sprains. Conversely, over-diagnosis of SH fractures based solely on the "weak link" dogma may lead to unnecessary immobilization. [11]

2.3 Transitional Pathomechanics: The Asymmetric Closure

A unique pathomechanical event occurs during adolescence (typically ages 12–15 in girls, slightly later in boys) known as the "transitional period" (~18 months). During this window, the distal tibial physis undergoes physiological closure in a predictable, asymmetric sequence:

  1. Central aspect fuses first
  2. Anteromedial aspect fuses second
  3. Posteromedial aspect fuses third
  4. Anterolateral aspect fuses last [16]

This asymmetry creates a temporary state where the medial distal tibia is solid bone capable of transmitting high loads, while the anterolateral portion remains an open physis vulnerable to shear and avulsion forces. This predisposes adolescents to two fracture patterns with no adult equivalent:

Juvenile Tillaux Fracture (SH III): External rotation force tensions the AITFL, which avulses a rectangular block of the anterolateral epiphysis because the AITFL is stronger than the remaining open anterolateral physis. Evidence Level V [18]

Triplane Fracture (SH IV): Higher energy propagates the fracture through three anatomic planes—transverse (open physis), sagittal (epiphysis), and coronal (posterior metaphysis)—creating a complex lesion difficult to visualize on standard radiographs. Evidence Level V [16]

Misdiagnosis of these transitional fractures as simple sprains can result in articular step-off, incongruity, and rapid development of post-traumatic arthritis. [1]

2.4 Medial Ankle Mechanics: Deltoid Ligament vs. Medial Malleolus

The deltoid ligament is a robust, multifascicular structure mechanically stronger than the lateral complex. In children, the deltoid ligament's strength relative to the medial malleolar physis is so great that isolated medial ligament injuries are exceptionally rare. An eversion force in a child will almost invariably shear the medial malleolus (SH I or II) before the deltoid ligament ruptures. Evidence Level V [23] The diagnosis of a "medial ankle sprain" in a skeletally immature patient should be viewed with extreme skepticism.

3. Diagnostic Challenges and Algorithms

3.1 Validity of the Ottawa Ankle Rules (OAR) in Pediatrics

The OAR dictate that ankle x-rays are indicated only if there is pain in the malleolar zone AND bone tenderness at the posterior edge/tip of either malleolus OR inability to bear weight both immediately and in the ED. [7]

Table 2. Diagnostic Performance of Ottawa Ankle Rules: Pediatric vs. Adult
MetricPediatric PopulationAdult Population
Sensitivity98.5%–100% [26]High (comparable)

Key limitationTenderness over ossification centers, physes, and apophyses (e.g., Sever's disease) causes false positivesFewer anatomic mimics

Sensitivity data: Evidence Level I [26]   Specificity data: Evidence Level II [27]

Low Risk Ankle Rule (LRAR): Developed specifically for pediatric populations, the LRAR states that if a child has a low-risk examination (tenderness limited to the distal fibula below the joint line) and can bear weight, radiographs may be unnecessary. Studies suggest the LRAR improves specificity without compromising sensitivity. Evidence Level I [7]

3.2 Identifying Transitional Fractures: The Radiographic Blind Spots

  • Radiographic signs: On AP view, the vertical fracture line of a Tillaux fracture may be subtle (slight articular surface irregularity). The lateral view reveals the metaphyseal spike of a Triplane fracture. The combination of SH III appearance on AP and SH II appearance on lateral is the hallmark of a Triplane injury. [16]
  • The "Mercedes-Benz" sign: On axial CT scans, Triplane fracture lines converge to resemble the Mercedes-Benz logo. [21]
  • Critical imaging red flags:
    • Any adolescent (12–15 years) with an external rotation mechanism should trigger immediate suspicion for a transitional fracture. [19]
    • Tenderness localized to the anterior joint line (AITFL insertion) is a red flag for Tillaux fracture—distinct from the inferior/lateral tenderness of an ATFL sprain. [18]
    • CT scanning is the gold standard for surgical planning: displacement >2 mm is the standard threshold for intervention. Evidence Level V [16,17]

Syndesmotic injuries become increasingly relevant in the adolescent athlete. Diagnostic accuracy of physical exam maneuvers in children is less robustly validated than in adults:

Given diagnostic uncertainty, MRI is the preferred modality to evaluate AITFL, PITFL, and interosseous membrane integrity when standard x-rays are negative but clinical suspicion remains. [34]

4. Management Guidelines

The management of pediatric ankle injuries is undergoing a significant paradigm shift. Traditional dogma of "immobilize everything" to protect the physis is being challenged by high-quality evidence supporting functional rehabilitation and early mobilization. Evidence Level I [37,40]

4.1 Conservative Immobilization vs. Functional Rehabilitation

For decades, the standard of care was a short-leg cast for 3–6 weeks. However, recent evidence suggests this may be unnecessary and potentially detrimental.

Key RCT — The "Non-Restraint" Trial (2024): A landmark randomized clinical trial compared a "restraint" group (bandaging/immobilization) vs. a "non-restraint" group (no external support, early mobilization) in children with mild ankle sprains. Results demonstrated that non-restraint was non-inferior to bandaging regarding functional recovery and pain management. Evidence Level I (RCT) [40]

Current consensus guidelines now emphasize a phased, functional approach:

  • Phase I (Acute): Protection and pain control. Paradigm evolving from "RICE" to "PEACE & LOVE" (Protection, Elevation, Avoid anti-inflammatories, Compression, Education & Load, Optimism, Vascularisation, Exercise). Key shift: "Rest" → "Optimal Loading." [40]
  • Phase II (Subacute): ROM restoration and isometric strengthening. Dorsiflexion recovery is critical. [41]
  • Phase III (Advanced): Proprioceptive training (balance boards, single-leg stance) and dynamic strengthening (heel raises, plyometrics)—crucial for preventing recurrent instability. [42]

4.3 Syndesmosis Fixation: The Pediatric Debate

When syndesmotic instability is identified in an adolescent, stabilization is indicated. A dramatic shift in technique preference has occurred:

  • Screw fixation: Historical gold standard; requires second surgery for removal before full weight-bearing. [44]
  • Suture button (e.g., TightRope): Allows physiologic micromotion; eliminates routine removal surgery.
POSNA Survey Results: 76% of pediatric orthopedists now prefer suture-button fixation over screws, driven by elimination of second surgery and extrapolation of superior functional outcomes from adult literature. Evidence Level V (survey) [44]

5. Complications: Growth Arrest vs. Chronic Instability

5.1 Premature Growth Arrest

Physeal arrest is the most specific and feared complication of pediatric ankle trauma. Risk varies dramatically by fracture type:

Table 5. Risk of Growth Arrest by Fracture Location
LocationFracture TypesIncidence of Growth ArrestRisk Factors
Distal FibulaSH I, SH II<1% [10]Exceedingly rare; good prognosis
Distal TibiaSH III, IV, V15%–40% [15]High-energy injuries; significant initial displacement [6]

Distal fibula data: Evidence Level IV–V [10]   Distal tibia data: Evidence Level III [15]

Mechanism: Trauma damages the germinal (resting) zone of the physis, forming a bony bar (bridge) that tethers growth. The distal tibia contributes approximately 40% of tibial longitudinal growth (3–4 mm/year). [1] Partial (peripheral) bars cause angular deformity (varus/valgus) while the uninjured physis continues to grow. [46,47]

Management of Physeal Bars:

  • Bar <50% of physis: Physeal bar excision (epiphysiolysis) with fat or cement interposition to restore growth. [46]
  • Bar ≥50% or near maturity: Epiphysiodesis (completing arrest) of affected and/or contralateral limb ± corrective osteotomy for angular deformities. [46]

5.2 Chronic Ankle Instability (CAI)

While growth arrest is the classic pediatric complication, CAI is the "silent epidemic." Contrary to the belief that children "bounce back":

  • Up to 71% of children with a history of ankle sprain report perceived instability
  • 47% demonstrate mechanical instability Evidence Level I [5,48]

These rates are comparable to or even higher than those in adults (~40%). [3]

Pathomechanics of Pediatric CAI: Driven by mechanical laxity (pathologic ligament elongation) and sensorimotor deficits. The developing neuromuscular system may be less robust in compensating for proprioceptive loss. Generalized ligamentous laxity and high-demand sports amplify risk. [5]

Assessment — The ROAST Consensus: The International Ankle Consortium recommends the Rehabilitation-Oriented ASsessmenT framework: Evidence Level V (consensus) [49,51,52]

  1. Mechanical stability: Anterior Drawer and Talar Tilt tests
  2. Perceived instability: Validated questionnaires (e.g., FAAM)
  3. Functional performance: Hop tests and balance assessments

Management of CAI:

  • Conservative (first-line): Aggressive functional rehabilitation focusing on peroneal strengthening and proprioceptive retraining; bracing during high-risk activities. [42]
  • Surgical: For mechanical instability refractory to rehab, the Modified Broström-Gould procedure is the gold standard. Long-term studies (5–10+ years) demonstrate good to excellent outcomes. Evidence Level III [53,54] However, recurrence rates can reach 27%, particularly in athletes or those with generalized laxity. [54] Periosteal flap augmentation from the distal fibula may mitigate recurrence in revision cases. [56–58]

8. Key Clinical Takeaways

  1. Challenge the "Sprain" Dogma: While the physis is the weak link, ligamentous injuries are common. Maintain high index of suspicion for SH I injuries that mimic sprains.
  2. Vigilance for Transitional Fractures: In the 12–15 age group, an external rotation injury is a Tillaux or Triplane fracture until proven otherwise. Anterior joint line tenderness is the critical exam finding. CT scanning is mandatory for surgical planning.
  3. Refined Diagnostics: Use OAR to rule out fractures but understand their poor specificity. Incorporate the LRAR to reduce unnecessary radiation.
  4. Embrace Functional Recovery: Move away from routine casting for mild injuries. The "non-restraint" and early mobilization approach is safe, effective, and may mitigate chronic instability development.
  5. Monitor for Growth and Stability: Follow-up must extend beyond fracture union to monitor for growth arrest (tibial fractures) and CAI development. Pediatric chronic instability is real, prevalent, and requires aggressive rehabilitation or surgical stabilization.
