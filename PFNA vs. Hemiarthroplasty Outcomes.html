PFNA vs Hemiarthroplasty for Elderly Intertrochanteric Fractures: Systematic Review & Meta-Analysis

Evidence-Based Educational Summary

1. Introduction: The Demographic & Clinical Imperative

The global orthopedic community faces a "silver tsunami"—a rapid increase in the geriatric population and a concurrent rise in fragility fractures. Intertrochanteric femoral fractures (IFF) account for nearly half of all hip fractures in the elderly and carry one-year mortality rates of 15–30%.1 These injuries represent systemic insults to patients with diminished physiological reserves, frequently complicated by osteoporosis, sarcopenia, and cardiovascular or metabolic comorbidities.

The primary objective of surgical intervention has evolved beyond fracture union to the rapid restoration of mobility, aiming to mitigate lethal complications of recumbency—hypostatic pneumonia, deep vein thrombosis, and pressure ulcers.

1.1 The Therapeutic Debate

Management of unstable intertrochanteric fractures (AO/OTA 31-A2 and A3) remains intensely debated. Historically, the Dynamic Hip Screw (DHS) was standard but suffered high failure rates in unstable patterns.3 This led to a paradigm shift toward intramedullary devices, principally the Proximal Femoral Nail Antirotation (PFNA), which compacts cancellous bone via its helical blade to enhance anchorage in osteoporotic femoral heads.

Conversely, Hemiarthroplasty (HA) bypasses biological uncertainty by replacing the femoral head entirely. It promises immediate full weight-bearing but at the cost of increased surgical invasiveness, hemodynamic instability, and implant-specific complications including dislocation and periprosthetic infection.1

🎯 Scope of This Review Synthesis of data from retrospective cohorts, randomized trials, and meta-analyses to compare perioperative safety (blood loss, infection, cerebrovascular events), long-term functional recovery, and mortality between PFNA and HA for elderly intertrochanteric fractures.

2. Pathophysiology & Biomechanics

2.1 PFNA: The Biological Approach

PFNA is a minimally invasive, "biological" fixation strategy—an intramedullary load-sharing device allowing controlled impaction.3

  • Mechanism: The helical blade is hammered into the femoral head, compacting trabecular bone to create a denser bone-implant interface resisting rotation and varus collapse (cut-out).3
  • Physiological impact: Closed reduction preserves the fracture hematoma—a potent source of osteoinductive factors. The soft tissue envelope and periosteal blood supply remain largely intact, minimizing the "second hit" of surgical trauma and reducing systemic inflammatory response syndrome (SIRS).5

2.2 Hemiarthroplasty: The Reconstructive Approach

HA is a reconstructive procedure that sacrifices the native joint for stability.4

  • Mechanism: Resection of the femoral head and neck, removal of fracture fragments, and insertion of a metal stem (cemented or press-fit) articulating with the native acetabulum via a bipolar head.
  • Physiological impact: The open procedure requires significant dissection, broaching or reaming of the femoral canal, and in cemented cases, pressurization with polymethylmethacrylate (PMMA). This disrupts intramedullary vasculature and exposes patients to embolization risks and greater hemodynamic shifts.9
⚠️ Key Divergence The fundamental biomechanical differences—closed biological fixation vs. open reconstructive arthroplasty—drive the stark differences in perioperative blood loss, infection risk, and functional recovery timelines detailed below.

3. Hemodynamic Analysis: Visible, Hidden & Systemic Blood Loss

In the geriatric population, blood loss is a critical determinant of postoperative morbidity. Elderly patients often present with preoperative anemia, reduced cardiac output, and limited compensatory mechanisms for hypovolemia.

3.1 Intraoperative (Visible) Blood Loss

The literature is unequivocal: PFNA is associated with significantly lower visible intraoperative blood loss compared to HA.5,11

  • One study documented mean blood loss of 120.00 ± 58.00 mL for PFNA vs 195.00 ± 85.00 mL for InterTAN (p = 0.001).5
  • Another study comparing PFNA to bipolar HA: 151.3 ± 30.5 mL vs 281.8 ± 50.7 mL (p < 0.001).11

3.2 Hidden Blood Loss (HBL)

"Hidden" or "occult" blood loss—blood extravasating into soft tissue or retained within the medullary canal—constitutes a massive portion of total hemoglobin drop.9

  • Magnitude in HA: Mean HBL = 465.9 ± 58.3 mL, accounting for 81.4% of total estimated blood loss (571.1 mL).9
  • Drivers: Canal preparation (bleeding cancellous bone), dead space after femoral head excision, and activated local fibrinolytic pathways promoting continued oozing.
  • Risk factors for high HBL: Low preoperative albumin (malnutrition marker), higher ASA classification, and receipt of blood transfusions.9

3.3 Transfusion Requirements

HA patients are roughly 1.5–2× more likely to require blood products. In one study, 68.9% of HA patients required intraoperative transfusion vs 47.1% of PFNA patients.5 Allogeneic transfusion is an independent risk factor for postoperative infection, TRALI, and volume overload—particularly hazardous in patients with congestive heart failure.

4. Infectious Complications: A Dichotomy of Risk

4.1 Superficial Surgical Site Infection (SSI)

HA is associated with higher superficial SSI rates due to larger incisions (10–15 cm) and greater soft tissue retraction, creating dead space for seroma formation.14

  • Infection rate: 10% in HA vs 5% in PFNA in one study;14 25% vs 0% in another.7
  • PFNA's "stab" incisions preserve skin flap vascularity, reducing necrosis and dehiscence risk.

4.2 Deep Infection & Periprosthetic Joint Infection (PJI)

Deep infections are graver threats, driven by biofilm formation on large foreign bodies.15

  • In HA: The prosthesis and cement-bone interface provide ideal substrate for Staphylococcus biofilm. Deep infection rate: ~4.9%.15
  • In PFNA: Generally lower deep infection rates (<1%). An infected nail can often be managed by removal and conversion to antibiotic rod, potentially preserving the joint.
  • Risk factors for HA deep infection: Surgeon inexperience, operative time >90 min, postoperative hematoma.15
🚨 Consequence Asymmetry An infected PFNA nail can be removed with potential joint preservation. A deep PJI in HA often requires prosthesis removal, resulting in a Girdlestone resection arthroplasty—a flail hip with significant shortening, instability, and permanent disability.

4.3 Pulmonary Infection (Pneumonia)

Pneumonia rates are comparable between groups despite HA's earlier mobilization. One cohort reported 6.0% for HA vs 3.8% for PFNA.6 This paradox may reflect higher surgical stress, greater pain, and increased opioid requirements in HA suppressing respiratory drive in the immediate postoperative period.

5. Cerebrovascular Events & Neurological Outcomes

5.1 Incidence of Stroke

Absolutestroke incidence is relatively low and similar between groups (~1–2%).5 However, approximately 56–63% of patients have pre-existing cerebrovascular disease, making any perioperative hemodynamic insult consequential.

5.2 Mechanisms of Cerebrovascular Injury

  • Bone Cement Implantation Syndrome (BCIS) in HA: Cement pressurization can force fat/marrow emboli into the venous circulation. With a patent foramen ovale, these can reach the cerebral circulation causing embolic stroke. Acrylic monomer can cause vasodilation and hypotension, leading to "watershed" strokes.7
  • Intramedullary pressurization in PFNA: Reaming generates high intramedullary pressure with fat embolism risk, though generally less severe than BCIS.
  • Hypotension and anemia: HA's significantly higher blood loss and longer anesthesia time increase intraoperative hypotension duration, causing silent cerebral ischemia in geriatric brains with compromised autoregulation.11

5.3 Postoperative Delirium

PFNA is consistently faster (53–79 min vs 77–115 min for HA), and shorter anesthesia exposure is neuroprotective.11 The minimally invasive nature of PFNA also reduces opioid requirements, further lowering delirium risk—though anterior thigh pain from the nail tip is a specific PFNA complaint.17

6. Functional Recovery: The Short-Term vs Long-Term Trade-off

6.1 Weight-Bearing & Mobilization

HA advantage: Immediate, full weight-bearing (prosthesis-based stability). Patients mobilize 2–3 days post-surgery vs nearly a week or more for PFNA.1

PFNA limitation: Surgeons often restrict weight-bearing for 4–6 weeks until radiographic callus appears, especially in severe osteoporosis. This protects fixation but promotes muscle atrophy.

6.2 Harris Hip Scores (HHS)

  • Early (1–3 months): HA patients demonstrate superior HHS (p < 0.05).1 Immediate removal of painful fragments and stable articulation drive this advantage.
  • Long-term (6–12 months): As fracture heals in the PFNA group, the gap closes. Meta-analyses consistently report no significant difference in HHS at 1 year.5
✅ Clinical Implication If life expectancy is limited (<1 year), the short-term functional superiority of HA is compelling. For patients with longer life expectancy, PFNA offers equivalent function without long-term prosthesis risks.

7. Mortality Analysis: The Survival Paradox

7.1 One-Year Mortality

Most studies and meta-analyses report no statistically significant difference in 1-year mortality (10–23% depending on cohort health).5 This suggests that HA's benefits of early mobilization may counterbalance increased surgical stress.

7.2 Mid-Term Divergence (2-Year Mortality)

Some longitudinal data shows a divergence beyond year one: HA 21.4% vs PFNA 10.3% (p = 0.028).20 This may reflect the long-term systemic burden of the more invasive procedure, or "confounding by indication"—surgeons may select HA for patients with poorer bone quality who are inherently more likely to die.

7.3 The "Super-Elderly" (Octogenarians and Nonagenarians)

  • In patients >85 years, some evidence suggests HA may offer lower all-cause mortality because fixation failure (requiring reoperation and bed rest) is poorly tolerated.22
  • Conversely, other studies in super-elderly patients emphasize PFNA's reduced surgical time and blood loss lead to higher survival rates compared to more invasive surgeries.23

8. Fracture-Specific Indications: Stable vs Unstable Patterns

8.1 Stable Fractures (AO 31-A1)

PFNA is the undisputed gold standard. Intact posteromedial cortex resists collapse once reduced. Hemiarthroplasty for stable fractures is widely considered overtreatment—exposing patients to unnecessary infection and dislocation risks without added benefit.24

8.2 Unstable Fractures (AO 31-A2 and A3)

This category represents the core of the controversy.

  • PFNA indication: Remains the standard for most unstable fractures. The intramedullary design supports the lateral wall and resists bending moments. However, cut-out risk increases in severe osteoporosis.
  • HA indication (specific patterns):8
    1. Severe comminution where anatomical reduction is impossible
    2. Reverse obliquity fractures mechanically unfavorable for sliding devices
    3. Failed fixation (salvage for cut-out or non-union)
    4. Severe osteoporosis (Singh index <3) precluding screw anchorage

11. Conclusion & Clinical Recommendations

11.1 Synthesis of Key Findings

  • Safety Profile → PFNA is superior: Significantly reduced operative time, minimal visible and hidden blood loss, and lower infection risk. Preserves native joint and bone stock.5,9,11
  • Short-Term Function → HA is superior: Immediate stability not reliant on healing, earlier weight-bearing, and better pain relief in the first 3 months.1
  • Complications are distinct: PFNA → mechanical failure (cut-out, varus collapse); HA → biological and prosthetic failure (infection, dislocation, loosening).

11.2 Recommendations for Clinical Practice

  1. Primary Choice — PFNA: Should remain the default for both stable and unstable intertrochanteric fractures, particularly for patients with significant anemia, coagulopathy, or high operative risk (ASA 3–4), to minimize physiological insult.
  2. Role of Hemiarthroplasty — Reserve for specific indications:
    • Inability to achieve stable reduction (shattered lateral wall)
    • Severe osteoporosis (Singh index 1–2) precluding screw anchorage
    • Concomitant severe hip osteoarthritis
    • Limited life expectancy (<2 years) requiring immediate, unrestricted mobility
  3. Perioperative management when HA is chosen: Anticipate significant hidden blood loss. Preoperative hemoglobin optimization and aggressive intraoperative fluid resuscitation are mandatory to mitigate hypotension and subsequent cerebrovascular events.9
📋 Bottom Line PFNA offers a safer physiological profile for the fragile geriatric patient, while Hemiarthroplasty ensures mobility in fractures that defy reconstruction. The choice must be individualized, weighing the biological cost of surgery against the mechanical demands of the fracture pattern, patient comorbidities, and life expectancy.
