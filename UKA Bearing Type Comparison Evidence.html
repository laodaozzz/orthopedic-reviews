Mobile-Bearing vs. Fixed-Bearing Unicompartmental Knee Arthroplasty

A Comprehensive Evidence Synthesis from Registries and Meta-Analyses

1. Design Rationale & Biomechanical Considerations

Unicompartmental knee arthroplasty (UKA) is a bone- and ligament-sparing surgical procedure for end-stage, single-compartment osteoarthritis of the knee. Level III As an alternative to total knee arthroplasty (TKA), UKA offers faster recovery, fewer major complications, and superior functional outcomes attributable to preservation of native knee kinematics guided by the cruciate ligaments. Level III Two distinct design philosophies exist for the tibial bearing surface: fixed-bearing (FB) and mobile-bearing (MB). Level IV

Fixed-Bearing Paradigm

The FB design features a polyethylene (PE) insert securely locked to a metallic tibial baseplate, prioritizing mechanical simplicity and inherent stability and eliminating the risk of bearing dislocation. Level IV

Biomechanical Challenge

Traditional FB designs incorporate a relatively flat tibial PE surface articulating with a curved femoral component, creating a non-conforming contact interface. This geometry mismatch leads to point or line loading, concentrating stress on the polyethylene ($P = F/A$), historically accelerating wear and creep. Level IV

🔬 Material Science Breakthrough The development of highly cross-linked ultra-high molecular weight polyethylene (UHMWPE) has dramatically improved wear resistance, fundamentally challenging the historical disadvantage of FB designs and potentially neutralizing what was once its primary biomechanical flaw. Level IV

Kinematic Performance

In-vivo studies demonstrate FB UKAs successfully restore knee motion close to normal, largely attributed to preservation of both the anterior and posterior cruciate ligaments, which remain the primary guides of knee kinematics. Level IV

Mobile-Bearing Concept

In MB UKA, the PE insert is not fixed to the tibial tray but designed to rotate and translate anteroposteriorly on a highly polished cobalt-chromium baseplate, specifically to address high contact stress and wear in early FB designs. Level IV

Core Advantages

  • Highly conforming "curved-on-curved" articulation dramatically increases contact area, reducing peak contact stresses
  • Decoupled motion — bearing rotates with the femur, theoretically dissipating torsional forces from the fixation interface, reducing risk of aseptic loosening

Key Risks

  • Bearing dislocation — the signature, unique failure mode; PE insert displaces from the tibial tray Level III
  • Technical demands — highly dependent on precise soft-tissue balancing and component alignment
  • Steeper learning curve — stability relies on ligament tension, not implant constraint

2. Long-Term Implant Survivorship

2.1 Meta-Analyses: General Equivalence in Medial UKA

A substantial body of meta-analytic evidence consistently fails to demonstrate superiority of one bearing design in medial UKA: Level II

  • A 2022 meta-analysis (25 studies, 4,696 patients) found no statistically significant difference in overall revision rate (p = 0.2). Level II
  • Pooled Time Incidence Rates: 0.96 revisions/100 component-years (MB) vs. 0.81 (FB) — not significant (p = 0.3). Level II
⚠️ Important Limitations Meta-analyses are constrained by relatively short follow-up periods, significant heterogeneity in implant models (historical and contemporary), diverse surgical techniques, and different patient populations. These limitations may mask clinically important long-term differences only apparent in longitudinal registry datasets.

UK National Joint Registry (NJR) — Market Shift

The 21st Annual Report (2024 data) reveals a dramatic shift: while MB options dominated until ~2015, fixed-bearing implants now account for 69% of all UKA procedures. Best-performing brands are FB designs with revision rates as low as 5.03% at 10 years. Level III

Australian Orthopaedic Association (AOANJRR)

Analysis of >50,000 medial UKAs: At 15 years, FB modular UKA CPR = 16% vs. MB = 23%, hazard ratio of 1.5 (p<0.001) — a 50% higher risk of revision for MB design. Level III

2.3 Lateral Compartment: Mobile-Bearing Inferiority

🚨 Unequivocal Evidence Against MB in Lateral UKA For lateral UKA, mobile-bearing revision rates are dramatically higher: PTIR 2.20 vs 0.72 per 100 component-years (p<0.01) — approximately four-fold higher risk of revision. Level II

The biomechanical rationale: the lateral compartment exhibits greater femoral rollback and inherent laxity, placing greater demands on soft tissues for stability. The MB design, relying heavily on soft-tissue tension, is at much higher intrinsic risk of instability and dislocation in this environment. Level IV

3. Etiology of Failure: Distinct Revision Profiles

MB and FB UKAs do not simply fail at different rates — they fail for fundamentally different reasons and on different timelines. This distinction reflects a core trade-off between early technique-related failure (MB) and late material failure (FB). Level III

4.2 Range of Motion & In-Vivo Kinematics

Despite MB's more anatomic design, ROM is largely comparable: 1–122° (MB) vs. 1–120° (FB), p = 0.34. Level III The dominant factor is preservation of cruciate ligaments, which provides sufficiently powerful kinematic guidance to overshadow subtle design differences. Level IV

✅ Clinical Implication The choice between MB and FB is not about achieving a better functional result — both designs are excellent for surviving implants. The decision is almost entirely one of risk mitigation and implant longevity, choosing the design that minimizes the lifetime probability of revision.

5.2 Patient Selection Factors

  • Age: Younger age (<70 yr) is an independent risk factor for revision regardless of bearing type, driven by higher demands and longer life expectancy. Level III
  • BMI: Obese patients (BMI ≥30) may have higher revision risk at long-term follow-up (88.1% vs. 98.6% 10-year survival, p = 0.012). Level IV However, some MB-specific studies found BMI did not influence survival. Level IV
  • Activity/Ethnicity: Asian patient populations have 3.7× higher odds of bearing dislocation, potentially attributable to lifestyle factors and floor-based cultural activities. Level II
⚠️ Dynamic Patient Selection Selection criteria should be stratified based on the chosen implant's risk profile. A young, active patient not engaging in high-flexion activities might suit MB; an older, sedentary patient is an excellent FB candidate where dislocation risk is nil and wear is less of a concern over their remaining lifespan.

6. Synthesis, Conclusions & Recommendations

6.1 Reconciling Theory with Clinical Reality

The theoretical biomechanical advantages of MB UKA have not consistently translated into superior long-term survivorship. Simultaneously, the primary historical disadvantage of FB — high contact stress — has been largely mitigated by highly cross-linked UHMWPE. Modern FB modular implants demonstrate equivalent or superior survivorship in the largest, longest-term registries. Level III

6.3 Future Directions

  • Robotics & Navigation: May reduce variability in surgical execution, potentially making MB designs safer for a wider range of surgeons. (Not yet proven in long-term registry studies)
  • Material Evolution: Antioxidant-infused polyethylene variants continue to improve FB long-term performance. MB design modifications aim to reduce dislocation risk.
  • Centralization of Care: Data strongly suggests that centralizing UKA to high-volume surgeons/specialized centers is the single most effective strategy for improving overall outcomes, regardless of implant choice. Level III
