Surgical Drainage in Modern Orthopedic Practice

An Evidence-Based Comparative Review and Clinical Guideline — Interactive Educational Summary

1. Foundational Principles of Surgical Drainage

The use of surgical drains in orthopedics is rooted in 19th-century surgical principles, encapsulated by Lawson Tait's maxim: "when in doubt, drain." The primary objective has been to evacuate postoperative fluid collections—hematoma and seroma—that theoretically increase wound tension, impair healing, and serve as a culture medium for bacteria, thereby increasing the risk of surgical site infection (SSI). Source narrative

1.1 Dead Space Obliteration

A core tenet is the concept of "dead space"—a potential cavity between tissue planes created during dissection. Drains are intended to obliterate this space by removing accumulating fluid and promoting tissue re-approximation, essential for optimal healing after extensive soft-tissue elevation or reconstruction. Source narrative

1.2 Historical Counterpoint

As Halsted countered over a century ago: "no drainage at all is better than the ignorant employment of it." Modern advances in hemostasis, aseptic technique, and perioperative care necessitate a critical re-evaluation of routine drain use. The central debate has evolved from how best to drain to whether to drain at all. Source narrative

Evolution of the Debate The question is no longer which drain system is safer—closed systems are demonstrably superior—but whether the risks of placing any foreign body into a clean surgical wound can be justified by demonstrable clinical benefit.

2. Classification and Mechanics of Surgical Drains

Surgical drains are categorized into two fundamental types based on mechanism of action and design. Source narrative

2.1 Open (Passive) Systems — Penrose Drain

The Penrose drain is a soft, flexible, open-ended tube (latex, silastic, or polypropylene). It does not function through its lumen; fluid is drawn to an external dressing via gravity, pressure differentials, and capillary action (wicking) along its external surface. Efficiency is proportional to surface area. It must be placed in the most gravity-dependent part of the wound. Typically used for superficial spaces or viscous/infected fluid (e.g., abscesses). Source §1.1.1, Ref 6–7

2.2 Closed-Suction (Active) Systems

These systems use active negative pressure to pull fluid through perforated or channeled tubing into a sealed reservoir, creating a unidirectional, sealed flow path that mitigates retrograde contamination. Source §1.1.2, Ref 6

Jackson-Pratt (JP) Drain

The most common surgical drain: flexible tube connected to a compressible bulb reservoir (100–400 mL). Squeezing air from the bulb creates negative pressure. Ref 8

Hemovac Drain

Larger collapsible cylindrical container (up to 500 mL) with an internal spring mechanism. Higher capacity suits procedures with anticipated significant drainage, such as total joint arthroplasty (TJA). Ref 8

Other Variants

  • Blake drains: Channeled tubing rather than perforations for reduced occlusion.
  • Pigtail drains: Locking coil at the distal tip for self-retention within cavities or abscesses.
Ref 6

2.3 Materials and Biocompatibility

Drains are manufactured from latex, rubber, polypropylene, PVC, silastic, and silicone. Latex and rubber provoke significant inflammatory and fibrous response, leading to dense drain tracts. Silicone, silastic, and PVC are more biologically inert. An ideal drain should be smooth (prevent fibrin adherence), firm enough to resist kinking but not cause pressure necrosis, and made from non-electrolytic, non-carcinogenic material. Ref 6–7, 9

4. Evidence in Total Joint Arthroplasty (TJA)

The central controversy is not drain type but whether drains should be used at all. A substantial body of high-level evidence—including RCTs, systematic reviews, and meta-analyses—has scrutinized prophylactic closed-suction drainage in TJA and spinal fusion. Level I — Systematic Reviews / Meta-analyses

4.1 The Case Against Routine Drainage

Multiple Cochrane reviews and other systematic reviews consistently conclude there is insufficient evidence to support routine use of closed-suction drainage in orthopedic surgery, and TJA specifically. Level I — Cochrane Review (Parker et al., 2007; Ref 17)

A 2025 expert consensus statement in the Journal of Arthroplasty explicitly recommends against the use of surgical drains in routine primary TKA and THA, supported by an overwhelming majority (83.52%) of voting experts. Level II — High-level expert consensus (Ref 24)

This recommendation extends to revision TJA; recent systematic reviews find no demonstrable benefit from drain placement in revision cases. Level II — Systematic Review (2024; Ref 25)

4.2 Impact on Key Clinical Outcomes

Infection & Hematoma

Pooled data from thousands of patients demonstrates no statistically significant difference in superficial wound infections, deep periprosthetic joint infections, or hematoma formation between drained and undrained groups. Level I — Multiple meta-analyses (Ref 17, 19, 31)

Blood Loss & Transfusion

Closed-suction drains are strongly associated with increased total postoperative blood loss and a significantly higher requirement for allogeneic blood transfusions. This is attributed to the drain's interference with the natural tamponade effect. Transfusion carries its own risks (immunologic reaction, fluid overload, disease transmission). Level I — Meta-analyses (Ref 17, 19)

Wound Healing, Ecchymosis & Dressings

The only consistently identified benefit is less postoperative bruising and fewer dressing reinforcements. Level II — RCTs (Ref 2, 19)

Functional Outcomes & Hospital Stay

No significant difference in postoperative pain, ROM, or functional recovery. Several studies link drain presence to longer length of hospital stay (LOS). Level II — RCTs and retrospective studies (Ref 2, 30)

4.3 Special Considerations

Shoulder Arthroplasty (TSA): A prospective RCT found no significant differences in transfusion requirements or hospital stay with drain use. Average cost increase of $7,423 associated with drain use. Authors concluded drains are safe but unnecessary and costly. Level II — Prospective RCT (Ref 29)

Role of Tranexamic Acid (TXA): TXA has been proven to significantly reduce postoperative blood loss and transfusion needs. Studies comparing drains to TXA found TXA is superior, suggesting this pharmacological intervention may have rendered drains obsolete for managing bleeding. Level I — Comparative studies (Ref 19)

5. Evidence in Spinal Fusion Surgery

The debate is equally contentious in spine surgery. The theoretical risk of epidural hematoma (EDH) causing neurological compression has historically driven high prophylactic drain placement rates. A global survey indicates that the majority of spine surgeons continue to use drains routinely after lumbar surgery, despite lack of evidence. Ref 36

5.1 Systematic Review Findings

Multiple systematic reviews and meta-analyses conclude that for both adult and pediatric patients undergoing posterior spinal fusion, there is no demonstrable benefit to using surgical drains in reducing SSI or EDH. Level I — Meta-analyses (Ref 37–39)

5.2 Epidural Hematoma (EDH)

Meta-analyses pooling thousands of patients found no statistically significant difference in postoperative EDH rates between drained and undrained groups. Level I — Meta-analysis (Ref 37)

A 2025 meta-analysis noted a lower incidence of postoperative epidural hematoma (PEH) in single-segment surgeries with drainage, but this must be weighed against other findings. Level I — Meta-analysis (2025; Ref 39)

5.3 Surgical Site Infection (SSI)

Evidence consistently shows no significant difference in superficial or deep SSI rates. Some sources raise the concern that drains may paradoxically increase infection risk by providing a conduit for microbial entry. Level II (Ref 27, 36)

5.4 Length of Stay and Recovery

Drain use is associated with a significantly longer LOS — one meta-analysis quantified this as an average of 1.36 additional hospital days for the drain group. Omitting a drain facilitates faster postoperative ambulation. In multi-segment procedures, drain use correlates with a higher risk of transfusion. Level I — Meta-analyses (Ref 37, 39, 40)

6. Operative Technique & Postoperative Management

When a drain is deemed necessary, meticulous technique and diligent management are critical. Each step—from placement to removal—presents opportunities for error. Source §4

6.1 Placement Principles

  • Separate stab incision: The drain must exit through a dedicated stab incision, not through the primary surgical wound, to preserve wound closure integrity. Ref 7
  • Subcutaneous tunneling: A hemostat or trocar creates a tunnel from the surgical bed to the exterior. A small stab incision is made over the instrument tip. Ref 43
  • Perforation positioning: The perforated section must lie within the deepest, most dependent part of the wound. No perforations should be within the subcutaneous tunnel or outside the skin. Ref 9, 44
  • Securing: A non-absorbable suture (2-0 or 3-0 silk) using a "Roman Garter" hitch holds the drain firmly without causing pressure ischemia at the skin entry point. Ref 9, 44–45

6.2 Postoperative Care

  • Patency: Keep tubing straight, free of kinks, clots, and compression. Position below wound level. Ref 9, 46
  • Suction maintenance: Empty reservoir when ⅓–½ full; re-compress to re-establish vacuum. Ref 8
  • Clearing occlusions: Gentle "stripping" or "milking" of tubing to dislodge small clots. Ref 6, 11
  • Dressing protocol: Open drains require frequent absorbent dressing changes. Closed drains need a simple split-gauze dressing. All handling requires aseptic technique. Ref 6–7, 9
  • Documentation: Record volume and character (sanguineous → serosanguineous → serous) at regular intervals. Sudden return to bloody drainage or purulent fluid should be reported immediately. Ref 8, 11, 14

7. Evidence-Based Guidelines for Drain Removal

7.1 The Critical Window

Approximately 70–91% of total drainage occurs within the first 24 hours. After 24–48 hours, drainage slows to a negligible amount. Conversely, the risk of bacterial contamination increases with indwelling time — drain tip cultures show marked contamination increases after 24–48 hours. Level II — Clinical studies (Ref 47)

7.2 Removal Criteria: Fixed Time vs. Volume-Based

Fixed time protocols (24, 48, or 72 hours) are arbitrary and fail to account for patient variability. Volume-based removal (removal after output decreases to a predetermined threshold) is more patient-centered and evidence-supported. Ref 10, 50

7.3 Optimal Threshold

A large 2024 retrospective study compared a strict criterion (<50 mL/24h) with a more lenient criterion (<100 mL/24h) in short-level lumbar fusion patients. Level II — Retrospective study (Ref 51)

  • The lenient (<100 mL) group had drains removed significantly earlier.
  • Patients ambulated sooner, had shorter postoperative LOS (median 5 vs 6 days), and fewer overall perioperative complications (5.19% vs 10.39%).
  • Earlier removal was not associated with any increase in symptomatic hematoma or SSI.
Recommended Evidence-Based Removal Protocol
  1. Removal guided by drainage volume, not fixed time.
  2. Threshold: <100 mL over 24 hours. Level II
  3. Typically met within 24–48 hours for routine TJA and short-level fusion. Ref 15

7.4 Aseptic Removal Technique

  1. Explain procedure; administer analgesia if needed. Ref 14
  2. Release suction from reservoir. Clean skin with antiseptic. Ref 14
  3. Cut and remove securing suture. Gently rotate tubing to free from tissue. Ref 14
  4. Instruct patient to take a slow, deep breath; apply counter-pressure; remove with a single smooth motion. Ref 14
  5. Apply sterile occlusive dressing. Inspect drain for integrity — confirm all fragments are present. Ref 14
  6. Document removal, integrity, and exit site appearance. Ref 14

8.1 Retained Drain — A "Never Event"

Unintentional retention of a surgical drain is a serious, entirely preventable medical error classified as a "retained surgical item." It can lead to chronic pain, infection, and often requires reoperation. Ref 58–60

Prevention Checklist
  1. Glide Test: Before final closure, gently push/pull drain to confirm it glides freely (not caught by suture). Ref 58
  2. Document Perforations: Record number of holes placed within wound in operative report. Ref 58
  3. Inspect at Removal: Count perforations, reconcile with operative note, confirm integrity. Ref 14, 58

8.2 Increased Blood Loss — A Predictable Consequence

Increased postoperative blood loss and higher transfusion need are not a "complication" but a known and predictable consequence of drain use, particularly in TJA. Level I

  • Pharmacologic hemostasis: Routine TXA use dramatically reduces bleeding, often making drainage unnecessary. Ref 19
  • Drain clamping: Some protocols advocate temporary clamping for initial hours; an evidence-based nursing protocol suggested clamping for 1 hour if >600 mL in 6 hours. Evidence is not universally established. Ref 52
  • Restrictive transfusion protocols: Adhere to evidence-based triggers to avoid unnecessary blood products.

9. Clinical Recommendations & Decision Framework

1
Default Position: NO DRAIN for routine, primary, uncomplicated orthopedic procedures. High-Level Evidence
Applies to: Primary TKA, THA, TSA, and short-level (1–2 level) posterior lumbar fusion.
2
Consider drain ONLY in high-risk scenarios: Conditional / Expert Opinion
• Revision arthroplasty (extensive dissection, scar tissue, poor soft tissue envelope)
• Coagulopathy or therapeutic anticoagulation
• Uncontrolled intraoperative bleeding
• Extensive multi-level spinal fusion
3
If drain is necessary → CLOSED-SUCTION system only. High-Level Evidence
Open (Penrose) drains have unacceptably high infection risk and are not supported for clean orthopedic surgery. Ref 5
4
Best practices if drain used:
• Place through separate stab incision; perform glide test before closure
• Maintain closed sterile system; ensure patency and suction
• Document volume and character at regular intervals
• Remove based on volume: <100 mL/24h threshold Ref 51
• Aim for removal within 48 hours; inspect drain for integrity at removal

Future Directions

  • Modern perioperative context: RCTs evaluating drains within multimodal protocols (TXA, local infiltrative analgesia, rapid recovery pathways).
  • High-risk populations: Prospective trials needed in high-energy trauma, complex spinal deformity, and orthopedic oncology.
  • Bridging the evidence-practice gap: Education and quality improvement research to overcome cultural and dogmatic barriers to evidence adoption. Ref 36
