1. Introduction: The Biomechanical and Medico-Legal Landscape

In orthopedic surgery, patient positioning is not merely a preparatory logistical step but a critical intraoperative intervention with profound physiological and biomechanical implications. The requirement for specific anatomical exposure—whether for complex spinal deformity correction, total hip arthroplasty, or shoulder reconstruction—often necessitates placing the anesthetized patient in non-physiologic postures for extended durations. While essential for surgical access, these positions subject the body to abnormal force vectors, including shear, compression, and traction, which threaten the integrity of the peripheral nervous system and the integumentary barrier.[1]

The clinical stakes are exceptionally high. Perioperative peripheral nerve injuries (PPNI) and intraoperative pressure injuries (IPI) remain leading causes of postoperative morbidity and litigation. Data from the American Society of Anesthesiologists (ASA) Closed Claims Project reveals that nerve injuries account for a significant proportion of anesthesia-related malpractice claims, with ulnar neuropathies (28%) and brachial plexus injuries (20%) dominating the landscape.[1] Furthermore, the doctrine of res ipsa loquitur ("the thing speaks for itself") is frequently applied in legal proceedings regarding positioning injuries, predicated on the assumption that such complications are preventable deviations from the standard of care.[3]

Scope of This Review This report provides an evidence-based review of the biomechanics governing positioning-related injuries in orthopedic surgery. It synthesizes the most recent guidelines from the Association of periOperative Registered Nurses (AORN) (2024–2025 updates) and Practice Advisories from the ASA, offering a granular analysis of risk mitigation strategies for the prone and lateral decubitus positions.[8, 25]
Evidence Level Assignment: The source document provides guideline-level evidence (AORN, ASA) and epidemiological data (ASA Closed Claims). Where specific study designs are described, evidence levels are assigned accordingly. Where the source does not specify, "not specified in source" is noted.

2. Fundamental Pathophysiology of Positioning Injury

To effectively mitigate risk, the perioperative team must possess a nuanced understanding of the cellular and gross mechanics of injury. General anesthesia abolishes the protective somatosensory reflexes that normally prompt a conscious individual to reposition when tissue ischemia or joint strain occurs. Consequently, the burden of protection falls entirely on the strategic application of biomechanical principles by the surgical team.

2.1 The Biomechanics of Peripheral Nerve Injury (PPNI)

Peripheral nerves are complex, vascularized structures that rely on intrinsic elasticity and extraneural gliding to function. Injury in the perioperative setting is rarely the result of transection; rather, it stems from the mechanical disruption of the nerve's microenvironment.[2]

2.1.1 Ischemia and Microvascular Compromise Level II–III Evidence

The primary driver of PPNI is ischemia. Peripheral nerves are nourished by the vasa nervorum, a delicate network of intrinsic blood vessels in the epineurium and endoneurium. These vessels lack autoregulation, making nerve perfusion directly dependent on the gradient between mean arterial pressure (MAP) and intraneural pressure.[2]

Critical Ischemic Thresholds
  • External pressure >30–40 mmHg disrupts capillary perfusion pressure, initiating ischemia.
  • 10–30 minutes of acute ischemia → reversible conduction block (neurapraxia).
  • >3–4 hours of prolonged ischemia → endoneurial edema, blood-nerve barrier disruption, and axonal degeneration (axonotmesis).[2]

Venous Congestion: In positions like the prone position or steep Trendelenburg, increased venous pressure can impair localized drainage from the nerve, causing secondary congestion and increasing intraneural pressure from the inside out, further compromising perfusion.[5]

2.1.2 The Mechanics of Stretch and Traction Level III Evidence

Nerves have a finite capacity for elongation. Under physiologic conditions, the undulating course of nerve fibers (the Bands of Fontana) allows for some stretching. However, biomechanical studies demonstrate that elongation of a peripheral nerve by 5% to 15% of its resting length can obliterate intraneural blood flow.[4]

The stress-strain curve shows that beyond the elastic limit (approximately 10–15% elongation), the structural integrity of the perineurium is compromised. In orthopedic surgery, traction is often intentional—such as distracting the glenohumeral joint for arthroscopy or correcting a kyphotic deformity—placing nerves like the brachial plexus or sciatic nerve at high risk of stretch-induced ischemia.[4]

2.1.3 The "Double Crush" Phenomenon Level III Evidence

A critical theoretical framework for understanding PPNI is the "Double Crush" syndrome. This hypothesis posits that a nerve compromised at one level (e.g., a proximal cervical radiculopathy or metabolic compromise from diabetes) becomes hypersensitive to mechanical stress at a second, distal site (e.g., the cubital tunnel).[7]

Clinical Implication – Double Crush A patient with asymptomatic cervical stenosis may develop a profound ulnar neuropathy from a degree of elbow flexion that would be benign in a healthy individual. This underscores the necessity of rigorous preoperative assessment protocols emphasized in the 2024 AORN guidelines, which mandate evaluating patients for pre-existing neuropathies and mobility limitations.[8]

2.2 Pathophysiology of Intraoperative Pressure Ulcers (IOPU)

Intraoperative pressure injuries differ mechanistically from chronic decubitus ulcers. They are characterized by high-intensity loading over a shorter duration (hours), often compounded by shear forces.[9]

Deep Tissue Injury (DTI): In the OR, pressure injury often propagates from the "bone out." Muscle tissue is more metabolically active and sensitive to ischemia than the overlying skin. Prolonged compression of muscle against a bony prominence (e.g., the sacrum or heel) by a rigid surface (OR table mattress) causes deep necrosis while the skin initially remains intact. This manifests postoperatively as a severe DTI, often described as a "purple pressure ulcer."[9]

Shear Mechanics: Shear force occurs when the skin remains stationary (fixed by friction against the sheets) while the underlying skeletal structures move. This distorts the angulation of perforating blood vessels, leading to thrombotic occlusion and tissue death. The AORN 2024 Guidelines for Safe Patient Handling and Movement (SPHM) specifically target shear reduction during lateral transfers as a primary prevention strategy.[11]

3. The Prone Position: Systemic Physiology & Equipment Biomechanics

The prone position is indispensable for surgical access to the posterior spine, cranium, and gluteal region. However, turning a patient 180° from the supine position introduces massive physiological alterations affecting every organ system, necessitating specialized equipment like the Wilson Frame or Jackson Table.[5]

3.1 Hemodynamic and Respiratory Alterations

Cardiovascular Impact: Upon proning, compression of the abdomen can transmit pressure to the inferior vena cava (IVC), obstructing venous return to the right atrium. This leads to a reduction in stroke volume and cardiac index.[5] Furthermore, obstruction of the IVC forces venous return through the valveless epidural venous plexus (Batson's plexus), resulting in engorgement of surgical site veins and significantly increased intraoperative bleeding during spinal procedures.[13]

Respiratory Mechanics: Paradoxically, prone positioning can improve oxygenation in patients with ARDS by recruiting dorsal lung segments and reducing V/Q mismatch. However, in the surgical setting, restriction of abdominal wall excursion by improper bolsters increases intrathoracic pressure, reducing functional residual capacity (FRC) and increasing peak airway pressures.[12]

The choice of positioning device is the single most significant variable in preventing prone complications.[16]

3.3 Postoperative Visual Loss (POVL) ASA Registry Data

POVL is the most feared complication of prone spine surgery, with an incidence of 0.013% to 0.2%, rising significantly in complex deformity cases.[20] The ASA POVL Registry has provided critical insights, identifying Ischemic Optic Neuropathy (ION) and Central Retinal Artery Occlusion (CRAO) as distinct entities with distinct prevention strategies.

3.3.1 Ischemic Optic Neuropathy (ION)

Posterior Ischemic Optic Neuropathy (PION) accounts for 89% of POVL cases in lumbar spine surgery. It is an infarction of the retrobulbar optic nerve caused by a failure of oxygen delivery.[20]

Pathophysiology: PION is likely multifactorial, involving a "perfect storm" of decreased arterial perfusion pressure (hypotension), decreased oxygen-carrying capacity (anemia/blood loss), and increased resistance to blood flow (venous congestion). Prone positioning increases orbital venous pressure, which decreases the effective perfusion gradient across the optic nerve head.[14]

Independent Risk Factors (ASA Registry):[23]

  • Male sex
  • Obesity
  • Wilson frame use
  • Prolonged duration (>6 hours)
  • Substantial blood loss (>1 L)
Prevention of ION (ASA Practice Advisory)[25]
  • Maintain MAP at or near baseline in high-risk patients.
  • Avoid Wilson frame in obese patients.
  • Position the head neutral or above the level of the heart to facilitate venous drainage.

3.3.2 Central Retinal Artery Occlusion (CRAO)

Unlike the systemic etiology of ION, CRAO is almost exclusively a result of direct external pressure on the globe.[20]

Mechanism: Improper head positioning (e.g., resting the eye on a horseshoe headrest) or shifting of the patient during surgery compresses the globe. This raises intraocular pressure above systolic arterial pressure, halting retinal perfusion.

Prevention: The prevention of CRAO is strictly mechanical. The use of a 3-pin head holder (Mayfield) completely eliminates facial pressure and is the gold standard for cervical cases. For other cases, foam pillows with mirror cutouts allow continuous visualization of the eyes. AORN and ASA guidelines mandate periodic checks (every 20 minutes) to verify the eyes are free of pressure.[27]

3.4 Brachial Plexus Injury in Prone Positioning

The Swimmer's Position Level III Evidence

Used to visualize the cervicothoracic junction or in ICU proning for ARDS, this position involves abducting one arm and rotating the head toward it.[29]

Mechanism of Injury: The plexus is tethered between the cervical vertebrae and the axillary fascia. Excessive neck rotation combined with arm abduction places extreme tensile strain on the plexus roots. Furthermore, abduction >90° forces the humeral head into the axilla, compressing the neurovascular bundle.[29]

Prevention – Swimmer's Position[31]
  • Arm abduction <90° whenever possible.
  • Head must be rotated toward the abducted arm to relieve tension.
  • For prolonged cases (e.g., ICU ventilation), alternate arm position every 2 hours.

4. Lateral Decubitus Positioning: Asymmetry & "Down" Side Risks

The lateral decubitus position is standard for hip arthroplasty, thoracic surgery, and shoulder arthroscopy. It creates a significant disparity in physiological load, with the dependent ("down") side bearing the weight and the non-dependent ("up") side subjected to surgical traction.[33]

4.1 The "Axillary Roll": A Biomechanical Misnomer AORN Guideline

Common Error Placing a roll inside the axilla is a critical biomechanical error. This turns the roll into a fulcrum, directly compressing the neurovascular bundle against the humeral head, causing brachial plexus palsy and vascular occlusion.[33]

Correct Application: The device functions as a chest roll or thoracic roll. It must be placed caudad (distal) to the axilla, typically at the level of the 7th to 9th ribs. Its biomechanical purpose is to lift the thorax off the mattress, suspending the shoulder girdle so the humeral head exerts no pressure on the axillary structures. Verification is confirmed by sliding a hand easily between the patient's axilla and the mattress.[33]

4.2 Peg Board Injuries in Lateral Hip Surgery

To maintain rigid lateral stability during total hip arthroplasty (THA), surgeons often use peg boards (e.g., MorphBoard). While effective, these rigid fixators introduce high-pressure points and nerve compression risks.[35]

Pressure Ulcers: The pegs are rigid cylinders. If not heavily padded with gel overlays, the interface pressure between the peg and the patient's skin (often at the pubic symphysis or sacrum) far exceeds capillary closing pressure, leading to geometrical pressure ulcers mirroring the peg shape.[35]

Nerve Compression Risks:[36]

  • Anterior Pegs: Can compress the femoral nerve or the Lateral Femoral Cutaneous Nerve (LFCN) if placed directly over the femoral triangle or ASIS.
  • Posterior Pegs: Pegs placed too deeply into the gluteal mass can impinge on the sciatic nerve.

Prevention Protocol: Apply a gel pad over the pegs and ensure anterior pegs are positioned at the pubis (bone) rather than the soft tissue of the groin.[38]

4.3 Peroneal Nerve Vulnerability Level III Evidence

The common peroneal nerve is the most frequently injured nerve in the lower extremity during lateral positioning due to its superficial course around the fibular head.[39]

Dependent Leg Risk: The lateral aspect of the down knee rests directly on the hard OR table. Without specific intervention, the weight of the leg compresses the peroneal nerve against the fibular neck, leading to foot drop.[39]

Prevention (AORN Guidelines):[41]

  • Place a soft cushion, gel pad, or "donuts" under the lateral aspect of the dependent knee.
  • Flex the dependent leg slightly to relax the sciatic/peroneal tract.
  • Support the upper leg with pillows or a specialized leg holder to prevent adduction.[42]

5. Specific Peripheral Nerve Injury Profiles & Prevention

5.1 The Ulnar Nerve: The Ubiquitous Threat Level II Evidence

Ulnar neuropathy at the elbow (UNE) accounts for approximately one-third of all anesthesia-related nerve injury claims. Its high frequency is attributed to the superficial anatomy of the cubital tunnel.[1]

Anatomy: The ulnar nerve passes posterior to the medial epicondyle in the cubital tunnel, covered only by the arcuate ligament (Osborne's band). It has minimal adipose padding.

Mechanism of Injury:[2]

  • Direct Compression: In the supine position, if the arm is pronated (palms down) and tucked, the medial epicondyle rotates against the mattress, directly crushing the nerve.
  • Traction/Ischemia: Flexion of the elbow >90° tightens the arcuate ligament, reducing the cross-sectional area of the cubital tunnel and increasing intraneural pressure. MRI studies show the nerve elongates by up to 18% during hyperflexion.[23]
Ulnar Nerve Protection Protocol
  • Forearm Position: Studies using computerized pressure sensing mats confirm that supination (palms up) minimizes pressure in the cubital tunnel (~2 mmHg), whereas pronation increases it significantly (~95 mmHg).[43]
  • Elbow Flexion: Maintain <90° whenever possible to prevent the "hammock effect" of nerve stretching.[44]
  • Padding: Use convoluted foam or gel at the elbow.
  • Fixation: Do not over-tighten draw sheets across the elbow (tourniquet effect).[43]

5.2 The Brachial Plexus: Traction and Impingement Level III Evidence

The brachial plexus is susceptible to traction due to its fixation points at the cervical vertebrae and the axillary fascia.[45]

Mechanism:

  • Abduction >90° levers the humerus against the acromion and stretches the plexus under the coracoid process.
  • Lateral head rotation away from the abducted arm increases traction.
  • Shoulder braces in Trendelenburg positioning can crush the plexus against the first rib.[45]

Prevention:

  • Strictly limit abduction to <90°.
  • Avoid shoulder braces; use non-slip mattresses or vacuum bean bags instead.[46]
  • Maintain neutral cervical alignment whenever possible.

5.3 The Peroneal Nerve: Superficial Compression

The peroneal nerve is uniquely vulnerable due to its superficial location at the fibular head. It is at risk in both lateral decubitus (direct table compression of the dependent leg) and lithotomy (lateral knee against stirrup post) positions.[39]

Prevention: "Float" the fibular head using gel pads or foam donuts. Ensure no rigid equipment contacts the lateral knee.[39]

5.4 Lower Extremity Nerves in Prone Position (LFCN & Femoral)

LFCN (Meralgia Paresthetica): The LFCN is compressed by the hip bolsters of the Jackson table. The nerve exits medial to the ASIS. If bolsters are positioned too medially (on the groin) rather than laterally (on the ASIS/iliac crest), the nerve is crushed.[47]

Femoral Nerve (Stretch): Excessive hip extension on the Jackson table stretches the femoral nerve. While extension aids in lumbar lordosis restoration, extension beyond 15–30° can cause femoral palsy. Biomechanical studies show the femoral nerve moves posteriorly and tightens with extension.[49]

6. AORN Guidelines 2024–2025: Key Updates & Strategic Shifts AORN Guideline

The AORN has released significant updates for 2024, with projected 2025/2026 refinements, shifting the paradigm from simple "positioning" to a broader Safe Patient Handling and Movement (SPHM) framework. This holistic approach recognizes that injury often begins before the incision, during the transfer itself.[8]

6.1 Safe Patient Handling and Movement (SPHM) Updates

The 2024 guidelines emphasize that manual lifting is insufficient for modern patient populations (increasing BMI).[11]

  • Mechanical Lifts: Strong recommendation for overhead-mounted lifts (ceiling lifts) over floor-based lifts to reduce torque and shear forces during lateral transfers. This protects both the patient (shear injury) and the staff (musculoskeletal injury).[8]
  • Staffing Fluidity: Previous rigid rules (e.g., "always use 4 people") have been replaced with a requirement for "adequate staff to maintain body alignment," acknowledging that patient condition and BMI dictate the resource need.
  • Air-Assisted Devices: Use of air-assisted lateral transfer devices (e.g., HoverMatt) is strongly encouraged to eliminate friction and shear during transfer to the OR bed.[51]

6.2 Preoperative Assessment and Briefing

A critical administrative control introduced is the mandatory Preoperative Mobility and Positioning Assessment.[8]

  • The Assessment: The team must assess the patient's baseline Range of Motion (ROM) and pre-existing neuropathies before anesthesia. This identifies patients with "Double Crush" potential (e.g., limited shoulder abduction due to adhesive capsulitis).
  • The Briefing: Positioning considerations must be discussed during the Time Out/Briefing. Conflicts (e.g., "The patient cannot pronate the left arm") must be resolved, and equipment availability verified before induction.[52]

6.3 Prophylactic Technology: Silicone Foam Dressings Level II Evidence (Meta-Analysis)

The guidelines now support the prophylactic use of multi-layer silicone foam dressings to prevent pressure injuries.[53]

Evidence: Meta-analyses demonstrate that applying these dressings to high-risk areas (sacrum in supine; chin, cheekbones, and iliac crests in prone) significantly reduces the incidence of pressure ulcers. These dressings work by absorbing shear forces and redistributing pressure at the skin interface.[53]

Protocol: In prone spine surgery, applying silicone foam to the face (zygoma/chin) and chest reduces the rate of facial pressure ulcers, a common complication of the prone pillow.[54]

  • Padding: Use convoluted foam or gel at the elbow.
  • Fixation: Do not over-tighten draw sheets across the elbow (tourniquet effect).[43]

8. Conclusion

The prevention of positioning-related injuries in orthopedic surgery requires a paradigm shift from viewing positioning as a static preparatory step to viewing it as a dynamic, physiological intervention. The potential for catastrophic outcomes—from blindness (POVL) to permanent nerve palsy—demands vigilance.

The evidence presented in this review supports three foundational pillars of safety:

  1. Biomechanical Precision: Adherence to strict angular limits (e.g., <90° abduction) and correct equipment usage (e.g., thoracic vs. axillary roll) is non-negotiable.[3, 29]
  2. Individualized Assessment: The AORN 2024 focus on preoperative mobility assessment acknowledges that patient-specific factors (e.g., diabetic neuropathy, limited ROM) dictate the safety margin for positioning.[8]
  3. Proactive Technology: The integration of prophylactic silicone dressings, air-assisted transfer devices, and pressure-distributing tables (Jackson) represents the modern standard of care.[51, 53]

By rigorously applying these protocols, the perioperative team can effectively shield the patient from the "second insult" of surgery, ensuring that the positioning does not cause more harm than the pathology being treated.
