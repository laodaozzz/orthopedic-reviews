1. Biomechanical Rationale

The surgical management of complex ankle fractures involving the posterior malleolus has evolved significantly, transitioning from a historical focus on mere syndesmotic stabilization toward an absolute requirement for precise articular reconstruction. The posterior malleolus—classically referred to as the Volkmann fragment—is not merely a static structural buttress; it is a highly dynamic, weight-bearing component of the distal tibia that serves as the primary osseous anchor for the posterior inferior tibiofibular ligament (PITFL). LOE not specified [1]

This complex anatomical relationship establishes the Volkmann fragment as the keystone of the distal tibiofibular syndesmosis and the posterior tibial plafond. Contemporary orthopaedic traumatology recognizes that the posterior tibial plafond bears a substantial proportion of axial and shear loads during the stance phase of gait. Failure to achieve anatomical reduction of the posterior tibial plafond results in altered joint contact stresses, which inexorably leads to the rapid onset of post-traumatic osteoarthritis (PTOA). LOE not specified [4]

🔑 Key Concept: The ATFL as a Surgical Gateway The biomechanical rationale for utilizing aggressive surgical exposures—specifically the ATFL transection or release combined with fibular rotation—is deeply rooted in the necessity to overcome the severe anatomical visual obstructions inherent to the lateral and posterolateral ankle approaches.

The Lateral Ligamentous Complex & Syndesmotic Interdependence

The distal tibiofibular articulation is stabilized by a sophisticated interplay between two ligamentous complexes. The syndesmosis comprises the anterior inferior tibiofibular ligament (AITFL), the PITFL, the interosseous ligament (IOL), and the inferior transverse ligament. Concurrently, the lateral collateral ligament complex—consisting of the ATFL, calcaneofibular ligament (CFL), and posterior talofibular ligament (PTFL)—tethers the distal fibula to the talus and calcaneus. LOE not specified [1]

The ATFL, originating from the anterior tubercle of the distal fibula and inserting onto the talar body, acts as the primary restraint against anterior displacement and internal rotation of the talus. Blood supply to this region, primarily derived from the anterior artery of the lateral malleolus and the anterior branch of the peroneal artery, is delicate and easily compromised during extensive dissection. [13]

Biomechanical Basis for Controlled Fibular Mobilization

Under normal physiological conditions, the ankle syndesmosis permits approximately 4.3° of external fibular rotation in the axial plane and 3.3 mm of fibular translation in the sagittal plane during maximum dorsiflexion. LOE not specified [14]

Rigorous finite element analyses and cadaveric testing demonstrate that isolated disruption of the syndesmotic ligaments (AITFL, IOL, PITFL) does not immediately precipitate gross lateral ankle instability; the intact ATFL and CFL continue to restrict excessive talar excursion and fibular rotation. LOE not specified [12]

⚠️ Critical Biomechanical Finding It is only upon the additional sequential transection of the ATFL that the lateral ankle becomes profoundly unstable in both varus and anterior drawer conditions (tested at 50–80 N direct force for anterior drawer, and 1.7 N·m torque for talar tilt). Subsequent CFL/PTFL transection exponentially exacerbates this instability. [12]

By understanding this mechanism, the surgical technique leverages controlled instability. By deliberately executing an ATFL transection—and potentially releasing the AITFL if it has not already avulsed—the surgeon uncouples the distal fibula from its anterior talar restraints, allowing it to be externally rotated and mobilized posteriorly, hinging on the robust, heavily vascularized posterior soft tissues (PTFL and remaining PITFL fibers). LOE not specified [9]

This near-total, perpendicular visual access permits evacuation of intra-articular haematomas, debridement of chondral debris, and meticulous direct reduction of intercalary "die-punch" fragments. Critically, it ensures interfragmentary lag screws and posterior anti-glide plates can be applied along optimal biomechanical trajectories—perfectly orthogonal to primary fracture planes—without fibular obstruction. LOE not specified [9]

🔴 Non-Negotiable Requirement: ATFL Repair The release of the ATFL eliminates the ankle's primary defence against anterior talar subluxation. If the posterior malleolus fracture is perfectly reduced but the fibula is rotated back without concurrent rigid anatomical ATFL repair, cyclical loading during post-operative weight-bearing will subject the reconstruction to abnormal, unconstrained shear forces. This promotes subfibular ossicles, chronic anterolateral impingement, and advanced articular degradation. LOE not specified [9, 12]

2. Incisura Fibularis Morphometry

Understanding the incisura fibularis (fibular notch) morphology is essential for anatomical syndesmotic reduction. The incisura is a specialized concave facet on the lateral aspect of the distal tibia, designed to accommodate the distal fibula and allow complex triplanar ankle kinematics. LOE not specified [6, 7]

Table 2. Incisura Fibularis Morphological Classification [8]
Morphological Type Description Population Prevalence
C-shape (Concave) Depth > 4 mm; well-defined concavity 66.5%
I-shape (Shallow) Minimal depth; flat to gently curved 18.3%
r-shape (Asymmetric) Irregular, asymmetric facet 15.2%

These morphological variations, combined with significant sex-based differences in posterior tibiofibular depth and incisura retroversion, dictate the precise native seating of the fibula. When a high-energy posterior malleolus fracture occurs, the fracture line frequently propagates through or directly adjacent to the incisura fibularis. If the surgeon cannot directly visualize the incisura fibularis, the distal fibula cannot be anatomically reduced into its native footprint, leading to syndesmotic malreduction, chronic lateral pain, and restricted dorsiflexion. [7, 8]

3. Strict Indications

The ATFL transection/fibular rotation technique is an aggressive, highly invasive surgical manoeuvre. It is not indicated for routine management of standard trimalleolar fractures or simple posterior malleolar avulsions. The historical "Volkmann dogma"—that only fractures > 25–33% of the articular surface required ORIF—has been upended by modern CT-based classification systems. LOE not specified [3, 22]

📋 Modern Philosophy The contemporary surgical philosophy prioritizes the morphological characteristics of the fracture, the presence of articular comminution, and the involvement of the incisura fibularis over sheer percentage of involved articular surface. [4, 22]

Classification Systems Referenced

Indications are most accurately described utilizing the Haraguchi and Bartoníček CT-based classification systems. LOE not specified [22]

Approach Variations Based on Fibular Status

  • Transfibular Approach (TFA): Used when a displaced/comminuted Weber B or C fibular fracture exists in the same axial plane as the posterior fracture. The traumatic fracture line serves as a natural osteotomy. LOE not specified [19]
  • Fibular Notch Approach / Fibular Window / Fibular Door: Used when the fibula is intact or the fracture is too proximal (e.g., Maisonneuve variant). Requires a formal osteotomy. LOE not specified [9]
🚫 Contraindications
  • Severe peripheral vascular disease or poorly controlled diabetes mellitus
  • Heavily contused and blistered lateral soft tissue envelopes (risk of full-thickness tissue necrosis and deep infection)
  • Highly comminuted fibular fractures obliterating structural integrity (precludes fibular door technique) [19]

4. Surgical Technique Overview

Positioning: Patient prone, lateral decubitus, or highly bumped supine position to allow posterolateral access. LOE not specified [23]

Incision: Longitudinal or gently curved 10 cm incision over the posterolateral fibula, extending distally toward the base of the fifth metatarsal or peroneal tubercle. Dissection proceeds through the internervous plane between the sural nerve (posteriorly) and superficial peroneal nerve (anteriorly). [19, 27]

Key Surgical Steps

  1. ATFL Transection: The ATFL is sharply transected near its fibular footprint, leaving a sufficient cuff of robust tissue for later repair. [9]
  2. Fibular Osteotomy (if indicated): Trapezoidal or oblique osteotomy mimicking a Weber B fracture line, extending laterally and superiorly from the joint line to exit the lateral cortex 2–3 cm proximally. [16]
  3. Fibular Rotation: Using a K-wire or Schantz pin as a "joystick," the fibula is externally rotated and rolled backward laterally, hinging on preserved posterior soft tissues (PITFL, PTFL). [9]
  4. Joint Preparation: Rigorous irrigation to remove haematomas; intercalary fragments manipulated from the metaphyseal void, reduced to the talar dome, and temporarily stabilized with subchondral K-wires. [3]
  5. Volkmann Fragment Fixation: Derotated, keyed into metaphyseal fracture lines, compressed with large reduction clamps. Fixation with anterior-to-posterior or posterior-to-anterior lag screws + posterior anti-glide plate. [16, 33]
  6. Fibular Derotation & Syndesmotic Stabilization: Fibula rotated back into reconstructed incisura; osteotomy/fracture plated; syndesmosis stabilized if necessary. [4]
  7. Mandatory ATFL Repair: Anatomical repair using suture anchors (e.g., 1.4 mm all-soft anchors) with modified Broström-Gould technique. Ankle held in ~20° plantar flexion and ~15° internal rotation during tensioning. LOE not specified [50]
✅ Biomechanical Validation of ATFL Repair Cadaveric studies demonstrate no statistically significant difference in failure torque, failure angle, or construct stiffness between 1.4 mm all-soft suture anchors and traditional modified Broström-Gould repair using 2-0 FiberWire through bone tunnels. In both scenarios, primary failure mode is at the tissue level (sutures pull through ATFL substance), not knot failure or anchor pullout. LOE not specified [50]

5. Evidence-Based Medicine Summary

⚠️ Evidence Gap Disclosure Due to the high-energy mechanisms, extreme morphological heterogeneity, and ethical barriers precluding randomization of severe articular trauma, no large-scale prospective RCTs (Level I) or high-quality prospective comparative studies (Level II) exist evaluating the fibular mobilization technique. The evidence base relies on meticulously conducted Level III retrospective comparative cohorts and Level IV retrospective case series. [37]

Primary Outcome Metric

The American Orthopaedic Foot and Ankle Society (AOFAS) Ankle-Hindfoot Score (validated 100-point scale evaluating pain, function, and alignment) is the primary clinical outcome metric utilized across the majority of studies.

Key Study Findings

Liu et al. (2021) LOE IV [9] — Retrospective case series of 22 patients with complex distal tibial plafond fractures involving the fibular notch. Mean AOFAS score: 88.8 at final follow-up. Achieved 100% satisfactory restoration of all fractures and syndesmosis. Mild PTOA in only 18% (4/22). No significant increase in iatrogenic injuries.

Gonzalez et al. (2016) LOE III/IV [19] — Multi-institutional retrospective cohort. Mean AOFAS score: 87.7 at 12-month follow-up. Confirmed excellent articular congruity and successful intra-articular reduction across all cases.

Lateral Trans-malleolar Approach Study LOE III/IV [19] — 16 patients with severe posterior malleolus fractures (AO/OTA types 44B, 44C; Haraguchi types 1 and 2). Mean AOFAS: 93.0 ± 5.2. Highlighted risk of syndesmotic malreduction in highly comminuted fibular fractures.

Comparative Cohort: TFA vs. PA LOE III [19] — 85 patients. Articular step-off ≥ 1 mm incidence: TFA = 8.7% vs. PA = 35.5%. The multivariable analysis identified articular step-off ≥ 1 mm as an independent risk factor for post-traumatic arthritis and lower AOFAS scores.

Long-term TFFR vs. Posteromedial Comparative Study LOE III [36] — Functional outcomes (FAOS, FAAM, SF-36) demonstrated complete non-inferiority. TFFR group exhibited a statistically significantly shorter surgical duration (p < 0.001), attributed to the single-incision transfibular corridor's logistical efficiency.

7. Complications & Mitigation Strategies

7.1 Chronic Lateral Ankle Instability (CLAI)

The most catastrophic complication specific to ATFL transection is failure of the severed ATFL to achieve robust biological healing, culminating in chronic lateral ankle instability. The ATFL is inherently the most vulnerable of the lateral collateral ligaments, bearing the highest strain during physiological loading. LOE not specified [1]

An ankle with combined syndesmotic trauma and an incompetent ATFL exhibits profound mechanical instability, completely losing its primary restraint against anterior talar translation and failing under varus stress and anterior drawer testing. LOE not specified [12]

Clinical manifestations: Persistent "giving way" sensation on uneven terrain, recurrent anterolateral pain, symptomatic recurrent sprains, positive anterior drawer test (> 2–3 mm talar subluxation vs. contralateral limb), and occasionally positive talar tilt test. LOE not specified [20]

Proprioceptive component: Sharp transection disrupts the dense network of mechanoreceptors within the ligament, resulting in delayed peroneal muscle activation reflex during sudden inversion stresses, perpetuating the instability cycle. LOE not specified [20]

7.2 ATFL Repair: Current Standards

Simple, unsupported end-to-end suturing is vastly insufficient. Contemporary standards rely on suture anchor technology and the modified Broström-Gould procedure: LOE not specified [21, 50]

  • 1–2 small-diameter suture anchors (1.4 mm all-soft anchors, e.g., JuggerKnot or Twin-fix)
  • High-tensile sutures (2-0 FiberWire or equivalent) passed through residual ATFL tissue
  • Tensioning in ~20° plantar flexion and ~15° internal rotation
  • Gould modification: Mobilization and advancement of inferior extensor retinaculum (IER) over primary repair if native tissue is compromised [50]
✅ Biomechanical Outcome of Modern ATFL Repair When the ATFL is rigidly repaired using suture anchor techniques, extensive kinematic tracking confirms that normal ankle mechanics, translation, and rotational laxity are almost entirely restored to their native, uninjured parameters. LOE not specified [49, 50]

7.3 Syndesmotic Malreduction

Despite expansive visualization, achieving precise syndesmotic reduction remains highly complex. In cases of highly comminuted fibular fractures or osteoporotic bone, the anatomical "keying" effect of the incisura is lost. Surgeons must rigorously employ intraoperative 3D fluoroscopy or immediate postoperative CT to verify perfect rotational and translational alignment. LOE not specified [19, 24]

7.4 Wound and Soft Tissue Complications

Extensive exposure stresses the thin, poorly vascularized lateral soft tissue envelope. Reported complications include delayed wound healing, superficial skin edge necrosis, and localized infections—though these routinely resolved with targeted antibiotic therapy and aggressive local wound care, rarely necessitating deep hardware removal. LOE not specified [36]
