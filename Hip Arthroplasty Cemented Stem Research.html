Cemented Femoral Fixation in Total Hip Arthroplasty

EBM Interactive Summary Registry Data Synthesis

1. Introduction

Total hip arthroplasty (THA) is among the most transformative surgical interventions in modern orthopaedic medicine, restoring mobility and eradicating pain for millions of patients globally. At the biomechanical core of this success lies the refinement of femoral component fixation. The integration of self-curing polymethylmethacrylate (PMMA) bone cement, pioneered by Sir John Charnley in the early 1960s following early experiments by Haboush, Thompson, and McKee, established the foundation of the low-friction arthroplasty.4

For decades, cemented fixation was the undisputed gold standard. However, the 1980s saw the emergence of the "cement disease" hypothesis, which erroneously attributed late-term implant failures to the chemical toxicity of cement itself, rather than recognizing the true culprit: particulate wear debris triggering macrophage-mediated osteolysis.6 This misconception drove a paradigm shift toward cementless, porous-coated implant designs.6

Today, cementless fixation accounts for approximately 90% of primary elective THA procedures in North America.6 Despite this prevalence, cemented fixation remains structurally critical and undeniably superior in specific patient cohorts—particularly the aging demographic, the osteoporotic population, and patients with compromised proximal femoral morphology.1

The transition from rudimentary first-generation finger-packing methods to the highly sophisticated fourth-generation cementing technique has dramatically revolutionized long-term survivorship of cemented constructs.3 This report synthesizes high-level evidence-based medicine criteria, multi-decade registry data from the Swedish Hip Arthroplasty Register (SHAR) and the United Kingdom National Joint Registry (NJR), and intricate biomechanical analysis to delineate the precise indications, risks, and technical requirements for optimal outcomes.7

2. Indications for Cemented Femoral Fixation

The decision to use cemented versus cementless fixation must be a highly individualized clinical determination based on a complex matrix of patient-specific variables: chronological age, systemic inflammatory conditions, bone mineral density measurements, and radiographic morphological characteristics of the proximal femur.2

2.1 Bone Morphology & the Dorr Classification

The architectural integrity and geometric shape of the proximal femur are the primary anatomical determinants for fixation strategy selection. The Dorr classification system stratifies the proximal femur into three morphotypes based on cortical thickness and the geometric relationship between the metaphyseal flare and the diaphyseal isthmus.6

The Dorr ratio is calculated by comparing the inner canal diameter at the midportion of the lesser trochanter to the inner canal diameter 10 cm distal to that landmark.6

Table 1 — Dorr Classification of Proximal Femoral Morphology6
Dorr TypeMorphological CharacteristicsStructural ImplicationsSuggested Fixation
Type A Narrow medullary canal, extremely thick cortices, classic "champagne flute" appearance. Ratio < 0.5. High cortical density provides excellent resistance to hoop stresses. Abundant cancellous bone facilitates optimal biological ingrowth. Cementless (Press-fit)
Type B Mildly widened medullary canal, discernible thinning of the posterior cortex, moderate metaphyseal flare. Ratio 0.5–0.75. Transitional bone quality. Adequate structural integrity for press-fit stability, though careful broaching is required. Cementless or Cemented
Type C "Stovepipe" geometry, extensively widened canal, severe cortical thinning, depleted cancellous reserve. Ratio > 0.75. Severely compromised structural integrity. Extremely poor resistance to radial expansion forces during uncemented broaching. Cemented
Key Biomechanical Principle: In Dorr Type C femurs, the medullary canal is extensively widened and structural cancellous bone is severely depleted. Cementless stems rely on a high-friction "scratch fit" against healthy bone for primary stability.8 Attempting this in a Dorr Type C femur generates massive hoop stresses that frequently exceed the ultimate tensile strength of the compromised cortical shell, leading directly to intraoperative periprosthetic fractures.10

PMMA acts as a volumetric, load-distributing grout—not a biological adhesive.22 It bridges the volumetric mismatch between the rigid metallic implant and the expansive endosteal surface, creating a custom-molded mantle that distributes physiological loads evenly.11 By eliminating the need for an interference fit, cemented fixation neutralizes the extreme hoop stresses associated with uncemented impaction. The presence of a Dorr Type C femur is universally recognized as a definitive biomechanical indication for cemented fixation.6

2.2 Osteoporosis & Bone Mineral Density Deficits

Osteoporosis profoundly alters both the structural integrity and the biological healing capacity of the proximal femur. Epidemiological estimates suggest that 22% of women and 7% of men over age 50 in Europe suffer from clinical osteoporosis, with prevalence rates soaring to 40–60% among women over 65.1

The biological mechanisms governing cementless fixation dictate that only healthy, vascularized, actively dividing bone tissue can successfully proliferate into the porous coating of a cementless prosthesis.8 In osteoporotic patients, osteoblastic recruitment and metabolic activity are significantly impaired.20 This deficit substantially complicates osseointegration, increasing the risk of:

  • Delayed biological fixation and fibrous tissue encapsulation
  • Early aseptic loosening
  • Symptomatic micromotion (debilitating thigh pain)
  • Catastrophic implant subsidence1

Cemented fixation provides immediate, maximal mechanical interlock upon exothermic polymerization of the PMMA, effectively bypassing the biological requirement for osteoblastic bone ingrowth.5

Registry Evidence: Data from the AOANJRR, NJR, and EPRD demonstrate that in patients over 75, cementless THA is independently associated with increased risk of all-cause revision. At the 15-year benchmark, cumulative failure risks are lower for fully cemented constructs (NJR: 4.95%; AOANJRR: 5.1%) compared to fully cementless constructs (NJR: 6.98%; AOANJRR: 5.9%).20 Level II Evidence

2.3 Inflammatory Arthritis & Rheumatoid Disease

Rheumatoid arthritis (RA) introduces complex variables: chronic synovial inflammation, severe periarticular osteopenia, and aggressive pharmacological management including DMARDs, biological agents, JAK inhibitors, and long-term glucocorticoids.24,26

A fifteen-year longitudinal analysis in patients aged ≥55 with RA demonstrated that overall survival of cementless THA was 80%, statistically comparable to cemented cohorts (79–86%) when revisions for any reason were the endpoint.25

Hybrid Fixation Advantage: In RA populations, hybrid fixation (uncemented acetabular shell + fully cemented femoral stem) is linked to significantly lower risk of major revision vs. fully uncemented constructs (HR 0.56, 95% CI 0.42–0.74; p < 0.001).14 Level II Evidence

Patients receiving cemented femoral fixation demonstrate superior functional recovery: Harris Hip Scores are statistically superior at 6 months (P = 0.03) and 12 months (P = 0.002) postoperatively.30 This is likely attributable to immediate alleviation of micromotion and elimination of early weight-bearing restrictions.

Current EBM criteria emphasize that the presence of inflammatory arthropathy inherently elevates infection, dislocation, and periprosthetic fracture risk.18 In instances of questionable bone quality, the EBM consensus leans heavily toward hybrid or fully cemented constructs.23

2.4 Periprosthetic Fracture Risk in the Elderly

Periprosthetic femoral fractures (PFFs) represent a catastrophic complication associated with prolonged hospitalizations, complex revision surgeries, substantial morbidity, and alarmingly high mortality rates.33

Level I & II Evidence: Rigorous meta-analyses and massive propensity-matched registry cohorts consistently identify cementless femoral fixation as a primary independent risk factor for both intraoperative and early postoperative periprosthetic fractures in the elderly.19

A comprehensive systematic review demonstrated a distinct trend toward lower fracture rates in cemented groups (Risk Ratio = 0.46; 95% CI: 0.14–1.49), though with substantial heterogeneity (I² = 93.1%) in isolated RCTs.34

However, real-world registry data paints an unequivocal picture:

  • Swedish Arthroplasty Register (n > 150,000): Cementless fixation associated with OR 2.40 for PFF requiring revision (95% CI 1.92–2.99, P < 0.001).36 Level II Evidence
  • Pooled systematic reviews: Uncemented THA groups exhibit OR = 2.95 for PFF (95% CI 1.62–5.36, p = 0.0004).37 Level I Evidence
  • Females receiving cementless fixation: OR 3.70 for postoperative PFF (95% CI 2.94–4.76, P < 0.001).40 Level II Evidence

Multivariate analyses confirm cemented fixation significantly reduces PFF odds at: 30 days (OR 0.350, p < 0.0001), 90 days (OR 0.544, p < 0.0001), and 180 days (OR 0.573, p = 0.002) postoperatively.29

Clinical Guideline: Cemented femoral fixation should be strongly considered, if not routinely utilized, for patients aged ≥70 (particularly women), patients with documented fragility fractures, and those undergoing arthroplasty for displaced femoral neck fractures.11

3. Implant Biomechanics

Long-term survivorship of a cemented THA is inextricably linked to the complex biomechanical interaction at the interfaces between the metallic prosthesis, the PMMA cement, and the host endosteum. PMMA possesses no inherent adhesive properties to bone or metal;22 it functions as a viscoelastic grout that forms a rigid mechanical interlock by penetrating microscopic interstitial spaces of cancellous trabecular bone during pressurization.22

Engineers divide cemented femoral stems into two fundamentally opposed biomechanical design philosophies:4

3.1 Taper-Slip (Force-Closed) Philosophy

Predicated on the concept of controlled, continuous micro-subsidence.7 Implants utilizing this design—most notably the Exeter stem (Stryker) and the Charnley C-stem (DePuy)—feature a collarless geometry combined with a highly polished surface finish completely devoid of macroscopic texturing, grit-blasting, or precoating.7

The stem geometry is a double-taper or triple-taper, narrowing progressively both distally and medially. Under cyclical axial loading during gait, the polished tapered wedge is designed to undergo microscopic subsidence within the cement mantle over the lifetime of the patient.7

Critical Biomechanical Advantage: As the stem subsides, its wedge shape converts destructive vertical axial loads into radial compressive forces (hoop stresses) directed outward into the cement and cortical bone.7 Since PMMA is exceptionally strong in compression but weak in tension/shear, this keeps the cement in a constant state of radial compression—protecting it from fatigue fracture.

The highly polished surface ensures the stem glides smoothly against the inner cement envelope without abrading it, generating no particulate PMMA debris at the stem-cement interface.7 RSA studies demonstrate that while taper-slip stems undergo controlled subsidence within the cement, there is virtually zero motion at the cement-bone interface.7

3.2 Composite-Beam (Shape-Closed) Philosophy

Aims to create a unified, immovable construct comprising stem, cement, and bone.22 Implants—such as the Charnley Elite, Spectron EF, CPT stem, and Müller straight stem—feature a medial collar and matte, roughened, or grit-blasted surface finish.7

The shape-closed stem is designed never to subside.22 However, maintaining a flawless micro-bond across the entire surface area under cyclical loading is virtually impossible. When the rigid bond inevitably fails (debonding), the roughened surface acts as an aggressive rasp, severely abrading the PMMA mantle and generating vast quantities of particulate cement debris.7

Consequence: Particulate debris triggers aggressive macrophage-mediated inflammatory cascade → massive periprosthetic osteolysis → early aseptic loosening.45 Aseptic loosening accounts for 75% of late revisions in matte composite-beam stems vs. only 20% in polished taper-slip stems.

RSA studies indicate that when composite-beam stems eventually subside, they pull the entire cement mantle down with them, destroying osseointegration at both interfaces and leading to gross macroscopic loosening.7

4. Technical Nuances

The clinical longevity of a cemented femoral stem is inextricably linked to the meticulous execution of the cementation process. Inadequate cementation leads to early catastrophic failure regardless of implant design.11 Successive iterations from rudimentary first-generation techniques have culminated in the fourth-generation cementing technique—linked to a 20% absolute reduction in overall revision risk compared to earlier generations.41

4.1 The Fourth-Generation Cementing Protocol

Table 3 — Fourth-Generation Cementing Technique: Standardized Protocol3,11
Surgical PhaseStandardized Procedural StepsBiomechanical Rationale
Bone Bed Preparation High-pressure pulsatile lavage (with brush attachment) using warmed saline without additives. Evacuates marrow, free fat, and cancellous debris. Exposes trabecular micro-architecture for deep cement interdigitation. Reduces embolic material.11
Hemostasis & Drying Vigorous brushing and packing the entire canal with epinephrine-soaked sponges. Intense local vasoconstriction ensures a dry, blood-free environment. Blood inclusions compromise PMMA tensile strength and prevent cement penetration.11
Canal Restriction Placement of an intramedullary cement restrictor 1–2 cm distal to planned stem tip. Prevents distal cement migration. Occlusion is the prerequisite for generating high intramedullary pressures during injection.11
Cement Preparation Vacuum mixing and centrifugation at controlled ambient temperatures. Eliminates trapped air; drastically reduces macro- and micro-porosity. Exponentially increases fatigue life and fracture resistance.3
Cement Delivery Retrograde filling with mechanized cement gun, injecting slowly "bottom to top." Prevents air entrapment, blood mixing, and lamination within the cement column. Creates a single, continuous, homogeneous mass.11
Pressurization Proximal cement pressurizer (silicone seal) to occlude canal, forcing cement distally under sustained pressure. Drives viscous curing cement deep into cancellous trabeculae, creating a profound 3D micro-interlock. Most critical step for long-term fixation.3
Stem Insertion Pre-fitted proximal and distal centralizers. Strict avoidance of heavy mallet impaction. Guarantees perfect central positioning within the canal axis, producing a uniform, symmetrical cement mantle. Prevents cortical contact.3

4.2 Cement Mantle Thickness

Extensive empirical evidence and finite element analyses mandate a continuous, uniform cement mantle measuring 2.0–3.0 mm circumferentially around the stem.38 Particular emphasis must be placed on the proximal medial region (Gruen zones 8 and 9).51

Deficient Mantle (< 2.0 mm): Exponentially increased focal shear strains → rapid fatigue micro-cracking → widespread fragmentation → macroscopic failure → osteolytic particle intrusion → aseptic loosening.50,51
Excessive Mantle (> 5.0 mm): Immense exothermic heat generation → thermal necrosis of host bone. Inadequate physiological stress transfer → stress shielding → disuse osteopenia.49

To achieve optimal 2–3 mm mantles, cemented preparation utilizes intentionally oversized broaches—unlike cementless preparation where the broach matches the final implant size. This deliberate over-reaming creates a reproducibly larger medullary envelope.38

4.3 Bone Cement Implantation Syndrome (BCIS)

BCIS is a potentially fatal perioperative complication characterized by rapid onset of severe hypoxemia, profound hypotension, cardiac arrhythmias, increased pulmonary vascular resistance, and cardiovascular collapse.52

The pathophysiology is multifactorial: high intramedullary pressures during cement pressurization and stem insertion force a massive volume of marrow, fat droplets, air, and toxic unpolymerized PMMA monomer into the venous sinusoids.41 This embolic shower travels to the pulmonary vasculature, causing mechanical capillary occlusion and triggering release of vasoactive mediators (histamine, serotonin).54

Table 4 — BCIS Severity Grading55
GradeClinical CriteriaPhysiological Impact
Grade 1 Moderate hypoxia (SpO₂ < 94%) OR moderate hypotension (SBP drop > 20%) Initial embolic shower reaching the pulmonary bed. Transient hemodynamic instability.
Grade 2 Severe hypoxia (SpO₂ < 88%) OR severe hypotension (SBP drop > 40%) OR unexpected loss of consciousness Massive embolic event with significant right ventricular strain. Requires immediate pharmacological intervention.
Grade 3 Complete cardiovascular collapse requiring CPR Catastrophic pulmonary outflow occlusion or severe toxic monomer reaction. High intraoperative mortality risk.

Clinical incidence ranges from 15.4% to 46.7%, with most cases presenting as Grade 1.54 Mortality is exponentially higher in trauma scenarios (e.g., displaced femoral neck fractures) compared to elective arthroplasty.54

Risk factors: Age > 60, ASA Grade 3–4, pre-existing cardiopulmonary disease, hypovolemia, primary lung cancer or pulmonary metastases.54

Prevention strategies:

  • Anesthesia: Preemptive FiO₂ increase to 100%, aggressive fluid resuscitation, prophylactic vasopressors, ondansetron (serotonin antagonist to block pulmonary vasoconstriction).56,59
  • Surgery: Fourth-generation techniques—copious pulsatile lavage, meticulous canal brushing, suction catheter venting prior to retrograde filling—to reduce peak intramedullary pressures and embolic load.59

5. Long-Term Registry Outcomes

The SHAR and NJR represent the largest, most comprehensive joint replacement databases globally, capturing millions of procedures with completeness rates exceeding 94%.7

5.1 Polished Taper-Slip vs. Matte Composite-Beam at 10–20 Years

Short- and mid-term evaluations often fail to capture the disparate failure mechanisms between the two groups.45 However, as constructs enter the 10–20 year horizon, survival curves radically diverge.7

  • At 10–15 years (SHAR + NJR): composite-beam stems show 1.7% absolute increased revision rate vs. taper-slip stems at 1.3% (p < 0.001).7
  • Aseptic loosening accounts for 75% of late revisions in matte composite-beam stems vs. 20% in polished taper-slip stems.45
  • Independent 11-year follow-up: 100% survivorship for polished finishes vs. 88% for matte finishes (p = 0.02) with aseptic loosening as endpoint.66 Level II Evidence (Prospective RCT)

6. Conclusion

The contemporary application of cemented femoral stems in THA is sustained by rigorous biomechanical logic, modern physiological understanding, and definitively validated by the most comprehensive orthopaedic registries globally.

Cemented stems remain indispensable and mathematically superior in patients characterized by:

  • Compromised bone density (osteoporosis/osteopenia)
  • Advanced chronological age (≥ 70 years)
  • Severe systemic inflammatory arthropathies
  • Diminished proximal femoral morphology (Dorr Type C)

Most notably, cemented stems in elderly demographics drastically reduce the catastrophic incidence of periprosthetic femoral fractures—an intervention supported by the highest levels of EBM.

Optimal outcomes require correct implant philosophy selection and flawless surgical technique execution. Multi-decade data from SHAR and NJR prove that highly polished, collarless, taper-slip designs vastly outperform matte, collared, composite-beam stems in averting long-term aseptic loosening. When implanted using meticulous fourth-generation cementing techniques, resulting constructs routinely achieve survival rates exceeding 99% at two decades.

Orthopaedic practitioners must synthesize these evidence-based principles while remaining vigilant regarding the systemic perioperative risks of BCIS to reliably execute what remains one of the most durably successful procedures in the history of surgical science.
