I. The Paradigm Shift: From Static Targets to Dynamic, Patient-Specific Stability

1.1 Dislocation as a Leading Cause of Early THA Revision

Total hip arthroplasty (THA) is widely regarded as one of the most successful and cost-effective operations in modern medicine. Despite this, postoperative dislocation remains a "troublesome complication" Level I and is consistently cited as one of the leading causes of early revision surgery, driving substantial patient morbidity, dissatisfaction, and significant social, health, and economic costs. [1]

Key Epidemiological Data
  • Incidence after primary THA: 1–3% [3]
  • 70% of dislocations occur within the first month [3]
  • 75–90% are posterior dislocations [3]

1.2 The Obsolescence of the Lewinnek "Safe Zone"

For decades, surgical strategy centered on the Lewinnek "safe zone" — first described in 1978 — proposing that acetabular component placement within inclination 40° ± 10° and anteversion 15° ± 10° minimized instability risk. [5]

Contemporary evidence has rendered this static target "historical" [6] and "obsolete". [7] A pivotal 2015 study analyzing 206 dislocated THAs found that the majority (58%) had both components positioned within the Lewinnek zone. [5] A review of 11 studies found only two demonstrating a statistically significant reduction in dislocation rates. [8]

Critical Finding

The failure of the Lewinnek zone is not a minor discrepancy but a profound conceptual error: a static, implant-centric model that completely ignores the dynamic, patient-specific biomechanics of the hip-spine relationship. [9] The fact that 58% of dislocations occur despite adherence proves that any universal, static zone is an invalid model for a dynamic biological system.

1.3 The New Imperative: The Hip-Spine Relationship and the "Functional Safe Zone"

The most important contemporary finding in dislocation prevention is the shift to preoperative assessment of spinopelvic mobility. [1] The pelvis is the crucial biomechanical link between the lumbar spine and the hip joints. Dynamic changes in pelvic tilt — most notably posterior pelvic tilt during sitting — "significantly influence the functional orientation of the acetabulum." [11]

An acetabular cup in a "safe" position while standing can become functionally retroverted (risking posterior dislocation) or over-anteverted (risking anterior impingement) when the patient sits. [11]

This gives rise to the "functional safe zone" [13]: a patient-specific, dynamic model accounting for individual spinopelvic motion to prevent impingement in all functional positions. [14] This mandates "bespoke THA surgery" [6] where stability is engineered preoperatively.

Why Prior Lumbar Spinal Fusion Is Critical

A fused spine is the iatrogenic creation of the most severe "stiff spine" pathology. This stiffness prevents the body from distributing motion between spine and hips, forcing all compensatory motion onto the hip joints [29], dramatically increasing impingement and instability risk. Any patient with LSF is, by definition, a high-risk spinopelvic patient requiring mandatory functional safe zone planning.

2.2 Essential Spinopelvic Parameters and Dynamic Imaging Protocol

For high-risk patients, a "clinical and radiographic assessment of the lumbar spine is mandatory." [1] Key parameters include:

  • Pelvic Incidence (PI): Fixed, patient-specific anatomical angle [30]
  • Pelvic Tilt (PT): Positional angle; increases (posterior tilt) from standing to sitting [31]
  • Sacral Slope (SS): Positional angle; decreases as person sits [31]
  • Biomechanical relationship: PI = PT + SS (constant). As PT increases, SS must decrease correspondingly. [30]
Imaging Protocol

Lateral spinopelvic radiographs (L1 to proximal femur) in both standing and sitting positions. [33] Sitting evaluation is "essential" to accurately predict posterior instability risk. [32]

2.3 A Clinical Framework: Classifying Spinopelvic Mobility

Patients are stratified along two axes:

  1. Sagittal Balance: PI − LL mismatch. <10° = balanced. [10]
  2. Spinopelvic Mobility: Change in Sacral Slope from standing to sitting (ΔSS). <10° = "stiff" spine. [29]

III. Surgical Approach: Comparative Analysis of Dislocation Risk

3.1 The Posterior Approach (PA): Modern Soft Tissue Repair

The PA has been "historically associated with a higher rate of hip dislocation" [37] (meta-analytic rate: 3.23% [20]). However, this risk is linked to violation of the posterior capsule and short external rotators, not the approach itself.

Modern techniques emphasize "enhanced posterior soft tissue repair". [19] A 2025 systematic scoping review concluded that "preserving or repairing the short external rotator muscles significantly contributed to improved hip joint stability." [38]

Repair Technique Matters
  • Tendon-to-bone (TB) repair: most effective; dislocation rate 0.98% [38]
  • Tendon-to-tendon (TT) repair: rate 1.62% [38]
  • Capsule repair is more effective than rotator repair alone [39]
  • With enhanced repair, PA dislocation rate becomes "close to [the] anterior approach" [3]

3.2 The Direct Anterior Approach (DAA)

The DAA has "gained momentum" as a muscle-sparing approach that preserves the native posterior soft tissue envelope. [41]

3.3 A Nuanced Clinical Decision

The critical variable for posterior stability is not the "approach" but the functional integrity of the posterior capsule and external rotators at the end of the procedure. The DAA achieves this by avoidance; the modern PA achieves it by repair. The RR of 0.22 [42] likely compares against a mix of repaired and unrepaired PAs, overstating the DAA benefit relative to a meticulously repaired PA.

Trade-offs to Consider
  • DAA has a described "learning curve" [43] and is associated with a significantly higher rate of periprosthetic femoral fractures [44]
  • PA offers greater exposure for complex reconstructions [44]
  • DAA may offer better early functional recovery (e.g., at 6 weeks), but no significant difference in patient-reported outcomes at 12 weeks or 1 year [45]
  • For high-risk spinopelvic patients (Group 1B), DAA may be superior as it intrinsically protects at-risk posterior structures and is more amenable to intraoperative fluoroscopy [43]

IV. Implant Selection for Intrinsic and Extrinsic Stability

4.1 Large Femoral Head (LFH) Diameter

The most fundamental strategy: increase femoral head diameter (e.g., ≥36 mm or 40 mm). [35] Evidence "strongly suggests" that larger heads reduce dislocation by increasing jump distance and reducing component-to-component impingement. [48] This must be balanced against the "negative long-term impact" of thinner polyethylene liners with potentially higher volumetric wear. [48]

4.2 Dual Mobility (DM) Constructs

DM constructs feature a small femoral head articulating within a larger polyethylene liner, which in turn articulates within the metal shell — creating a large effective head size with maximal jump distance.

DM in High-Risk Cohorts

  • Patients with Lumbar Spinal Fusion: DM constructs are increasingly used to "optimize hip stability" [50]. One 2020 study of high-risk LSF patients with DM reported zero dislocations at mean 3-year follow-up. [50]
  • Neuromuscular/Cognitive Impairment: DM "significantly reduce[s] dislocation rates" — only 2 dislocations in 588 patients (0.34%) [53], vs. up to 14% with standard THA in cerebral palsy. [24]

4.3 Comparative Effectiveness: DM vs. LFH

  • Evidence favoring DM: 2022 meta-analysis found DM reduced all-cause revision (OR 0.70) and dislocation not requiring revision (OR 0.29) vs. LFH. [56]
  • Conflicting evidence: 2023 interim analysis of a multicenter RCT in revision THA found no significant difference (DM 2.6% vs. LFH 4.3%; P=0.67). [57] However, this was underpowered (lower-than-anticipated dislocation rates) and limited to revision THA — insufficient to overturn broader consensus. [57]
Clinical Recommendation

While LFH is likely sufficient for a low-risk patient [48], the evidence strongly supports DM as the superior implant for high-risk patients (e.g., LSF, neuromuscular disorders). [50]

4.4 DM-Specific Complications

DM introduces the unique complication of intraprosthetic dislocation (IPD) — dissociation of the small head from the polyethylene liner. [24] Risk is low: 0.07–1.17% in primary THA. [59] Clinical decision balances known high risk of standard dislocation in high-risk patients against the low but real risk of IPD.

V. Intraoperative Execution: Enabling Technologies

5.1 Computer-Assisted Navigation (CAS)

CAS offers "potential for more accurate placement of acetabular components." [60] Multiple meta-analyses confirm improved accuracy and reduced "outliers." [61]

However, evidence for clinical benefit (reduced dislocation) has been "mixed." [6]

  • Negative/Neutral: Several meta-analyses [61] and an RCT [62] found no strong correlation between improved accuracy and reduced dislocation.
  • Positive: A 2019 JBJS Medicare study found navigated procedures had lower dislocation rates (1.00% vs. 1.70%; adjusted HR 0.69; p<0.001). [63]
Limitation of Intraoperative Fluoroscopy

While useful for inclination, fluoroscopy is not reliable for assessing anteversion, with measurements differing from postoperative CT by 4.5°–5.3° on average. [72]

5.2 Robotic-Assisted THA (rTHA)

rTHA demonstrates "a significant improvement in acetabular component alignment" [64] and precision. [65] The key advantage is full integration of the preoperative spinopelvic plan:

  1. Preoperative: CT scan loaded into robotic planning software [66]
  2. Planning: "Functional spino-pelvic assessment" combining supine CT with upright/seated EOS scans; visualize dynamic effects of component positioning [67, 68]
  3. Intraoperative: Robot assists execution with sub-millimetric and sub-degree precision [66]

VI. Re-Evaluating Postoperative Management: Against Routine Hip Precautions

6.1 The Consensus: Hip Precautions Are Unnecessary

For decades, postoperative "hip precautions" (avoiding flexion >90°, adduction, internal rotation) were a cornerstone of dislocation prevention. The most recent evidence from 2023–2024 conclusively refutes this practice. [75]

6.2 Shifting Focus: From Restriction to Fall Prevention

The rationale for abandoning precautions is twofold: they do not reduce dislocation rates [37] and may slow recovery and functional return. [37] Resources are "better directed towards evidence-based strategies to reduce falls" [76], given that falls were associated with 24% of dislocations in one study.

The Ultimate Validation

The evidence against precautions [75] is the final proof that dislocation prevention has been successfully transformed from a postoperative, patient-compliance-based problem to a preoperative, engineering-based problem solved by the surgeon. A hip that is (1) planned using dynamic spinopelvic models, (2) executed with DAA or meticulously repaired PA, and (3) implanted with LFH or DM, is intrinsically stable from the moment the patient leaves the OR.

VII. Evidence-Based Algorithm for Dislocation Prevention

7.1 Proposed Algorithm

Step 1: Universal Risk Stratification (All Patients)

Screen every patient preoperatively using the High-Risk Checklist (Table 2). Pay special attention to: Prior Lumbar Spinal Fusion (LSF), prior hip surgery, and neuromuscular/cognitive disorders. [3]

Step 2A: Low-Risk Patient Pathway

  • Pre-op: Standard templating
  • Approach: Surgeon preference. If PA: meticulous enhanced posterior repair (tendon-to-bone preferred) [19]
  • Implant: Large Femoral Head (LFH, ≥36 mm) [48]
  • Intra-op: Conventional, CAS, or rTHA acceptable [63]
  • Post-op: No hip precautions. Focus on fall prevention. [37]

Step 2B: High-Risk Patient Pathway (≥1 factor from Table 2)

  • Pre-op: Mandatory spinopelvic assessment — standing + sitting lateral radiographs [1]
  • Planning: Classify spinopelvic mobility (Table 1). Create patient-specific component target. [36]
  • Implant: Dual Mobility (DM) construct — strong evidence for LSF and neuromuscular cohorts [50]
  • Approach: Strongly consider DAA to protect posterior envelope [42]. If PA: meticulous enhanced repair is non-negotiable. [38]
  • Intra-op: Strongly consider rTHA for precision execution of non-standard plan [67]
  • Post-op: No hip precautions. Aggressive fall prevention. [37]

7.2 Final Conclusion

This synthesis confirms a complete paradigm shift. Stability is now a solvable, data-driven, engineering problem achieved through:

  1. Preoperative Analytics: Understanding individual spinopelvic kinematics
  2. Meticulous Execution: Respecting (DAA) or repairing (PA) the soft tissue envelope
  3. Bespoke Implantation: Selecting high-stability implants (DM) for high-risk patients
  4. Technological Integration: Using rTHA to execute the complex biomechanical plan

The "era of bespoke THA surgery" [6] is no longer a future concept — the evidence-based framework for its implementation is clear.
