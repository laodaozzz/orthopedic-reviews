1. Introduction & Pathophysiological Framework

The posterior hip and deep gluteal space (DGS) have historically represented a "no man's land" in orthopaedic surgery due to the complex convergence of neurovascular structures, deep muscle layers, and the lack of distinct topographical landmarks compared to the anterior hip or knee Level IV. Pathologies within this region—specifically ischiogluteal bursitis and proximal hamstring cysts—are increasingly recognized as sources of recalcitrant posterior hip pain, often grouped under the umbrella of Deep Gluteal Syndrome (DGS) or proximal hamstring tendinopathy.

While open surgical excision has been the traditional gold standard, it is associated with significant soft tissue dissection, large incisions, and prolonged recovery times Level IV. The advent of high-definition arthroscopy and specialized endoscopic instrumentation has facilitated a shift toward minimally invasive management, allowing surgeons to address these pathologies with reduced morbidity and improved visualization of critical structures such as the sciatic nerve Level IV.

Key Principle: Endoscopic approaches to the DGS combine the benefits of minimal tissue disruption with enhanced neurovascular visualization, addressing both the mechanical and neuropathic components of posterior hip pain.

2. Anatomy of the Deep Gluteal Space

The deep gluteal space is a distinct anatomical compartment bounded:

  • Posteriorly: Gluteus maximus muscle
  • Anteriorly: Posterior hip capsule and proximal femur
  • Superiorly: Sciatic notch
  • Inferiorly: Hamstring origin at the ischial tuberosity Level IV

This space contains the sciatic nerve, the posterior femoral cutaneous nerve (PFCN), the inferior gluteal artery, and the complex interplay of the "deep six" external rotators.

The Ischial Tuberosity Complex

The ischial tuberosity serves as the primary anchor for the posterior kinetic chain. It is the origin of the proximal hamstring complex, comprising:

  • Semitendinosus
  • Long head of the biceps femoris (conjoint tendon)
  • Semimembranosus Level IV

The ischiogluteal bursa lies superficial to this tendon complex but deep to the gluteus maximus muscle. In a physiologic state, this bursa acts as a friction-reducing interface during hip flexion and extension. However, mechanical overload—often seen in sedentary individuals ("weaver's bottom") or endurance athletes—can precipitate pathological changes.

3. Pathogenesis: Bursitis & Hamstring Cysts

3.1 Ischiogluteal Bursitis

Ischiogluteal bursitis manifests as localized inflammation of the synovial lining within the bursal sac. The etiology is typically multifactorial, involving repetitive compressive forces (prolonged sitting on hard surfaces) or shear forces (cycling, running) Level IV.

Chronic irritation leads to:

  • Synovial hypertrophy
  • Fluid accumulation
  • Formation of thickened, fibrotic walls

Histologically, excised bursal tissue often reveals chronic inflammatory changes including granulation tissue, neovascularization (capillary proliferation), and fibrinoid necrosis Level IV. In severe, chronic cases, the bursa may undergo cystic degeneration or calcification, becoming a space-occupying lesion that can mechanically irritate the adjacent sciatic nerve, mimicking radicular symptoms.

3.2 Proximal Hamstring Cysts

Proximal hamstring cysts are distinct from pure bursitis, although they often coexist. These cysts are frequently ganglionic in nature or result from intrasubstance delamination of the hamstring tendon (tendinopathy) Level IV.

Unlike a simple bursa, a hamstring cyst often possesses a "stalk" or pedicle that communicates directly with a tear in the tendon or the tendon-bone interface. The formation is driven by a valve-mechanism where synovial fluid is pumped from the tendon sheath into the cyst cavity during muscle contraction, but cannot return. This leads to progressive enlargement. Large cysts can exert mass effect in the subgluteal space, compressing the sciatic nerve against the external rotators or the ischium, leading to secondary sciatica Level IV.

Clinical Pearl: Failure to address the communicating stalk is a primary cause of cyst recurrence. Simple aspiration without stalk management is associated with high recurrence rates.

4. Preoperative Clinical & Radiographic Evaluation

A rigorous preoperative workup is essential to distinguish between isolated bursitis, cystic tendinopathy, and primary sciatic nerve entrapment, as the surgical strategy differs for each Expert opinion – not graded in source.

4.1 Clinical Presentation

Patients typically present with deep, aching buttock pain localized to the ischial tuberosity. The pain is often exacerbated by sitting (the "chair sign") and may radiate down the posterior thigh, mimicking lumbar radiculopathy.

4.2 Physical Examination Maneuvers

Test Technique Clinical Significance Evidence

Active Piriformis Test Lateral decubitus; examiner resists abduction and external rotation Reproduction of pain indicates deep gluteal nerve irritation Level IV
Seated Piriformis Stretch Passive flexion, adduction, and internal rotation while seated Compresses sciatic nerve against ischium; reproduces symptoms Level IV
Long Stride Walking Test Assess pain during extension phase of long stride Suggests ischiofemoral impingement or hamstring pathology Level IV

4.3 Advanced Imaging

Magnetic Resonance Imaging (MRI) is the gold standard for preoperative planning Level IV. Key imaging features include:

  • T2-Weighted / STIR Sequences: Fluid-sensitive sequences essential. Bursitis appears as a hyperintense, fluid-filled sac superficial to the tendon. Cysts appear as well-defined, loculated fluid collections, often with a visible stalk Level IV.
  • Tendon Integrity Assessment: "Feathery" edema within the muscle indicates Grade I strain; fluid signal disrupting tendon continuity indicates tear. Retraction of the tendon requires distinct surgical planning (repair vs. decompression) Level IV.
  • Sciatic Nerve Course: Axial views critical for mapping nerve position relative to cyst. In chronic cases, the nerve may appear swollen or show increased T2 signal (neuritis) Level IV.

5. Surgical Indications & Decision Making

Endoscopic intervention is indicated when conservative measures—including activity modification, physical therapy, and ultrasound-guided corticosteroid injections—have failed to provide relief after 3 to 6 months Consensus – not graded in source.

Indications for Endoscopic Resection:
  1. Recalcitrant Ischiogluteal Bursitis: Persistent pain and inability to sit despite non-operative management
  2. Symptomatic Proximal Hamstring Cysts: Cysts causing mass effect, pain, or nerve compression
  3. Deep Gluteal Syndrome: Sciatic nerve entrapment secondary to fibrotic bands or cystic compression
  4. Partial Hamstring Tears: Tears with cystic degeneration requiring debridement and possible suture anchor repair
Contraindications:
  • Infection: Active local or systemic infection
  • Massive Retraction: Chronic complete hamstring avulsions with retraction >5 cm may require open or hybrid "endoscopic-to-open" approach
  • Medical Comorbidities: Contraindications to general anesthesia

6. Patient Positioning & Operating Room Setup

Precise patient positioning is the cornerstone of safe endoscopic ischial surgery. The deep gluteal space requires positioning that uses gravity and joint angles to expand the working volume and protect neurovascular structures Level IV.

6.1 Anesthesia and Monitoring

General anesthesia with endotracheal intubation is mandatory. Complete muscle relaxation is required to allow distension of the subgluteal space. Hypotensive anesthesia (mean arterial pressure ~60–70 mmHg) helps minimize bleeding, which is critical given the vascularity of the gluteal region Level IV.

6.2 The Prone Position

The patient is placed in the prone position on a radiolucent table. This position allows the gluteus maximus to fall anteriorly, creating a potential space between the muscle and the deep posterior hip structures. All bony prominences must be meticulously padded Level IV.

6.3 Limb Manipulation & Joint Angles

Table 2: Optimal Limb Positioning Parameters for Endoscopic Ischial Surgery
Parameter Recommended Value Rationale
Hip Angle 0°–10° Extension Relaxes gluteus maximus for trocar insertion
Knee Flexion 45°–90° Relaxes hamstring tension and reduces sciatic nerve tension for mobilization
Leg Abduction ~45° Draws sciatic nerve laterally, widening the "safe zone" between ischium and nerve
Table Tilt 15° contralateral Places ischial tuberosity in horizontal plane, simplifying triangulation

6.4 Fluoroscopy Setup

The C-arm fluoroscope is brought from the contralateral side, arced over the patient's back, centered over the ischial tuberosity to provide an en face (AP) view. Radiographic confirmation is performed before incision to verify landmark mapping Level IV.

7. Portal Placement & Anatomical Landmarks

7.1 Anatomical Landmarks

Before prepping, the surgeon outlines palpable landmarks:

  1. Ischial Tuberosity (IT): Most prominent bony landmark
  2. Gluteal Fold: Horizontal skin crease at inferior aspect of buttock
  3. Lateral Border of the Ischium: Critical boundary — dissection lateral to this line risks sciatic nerve injury Level IV

7.2 Standard Two-Portal Technique

Table 3: Standard Portal Configuration for Endoscopic Ischial Surgery
Portal Location (Relative to IT) Primary Function Safety Profile
Posteromedial (PM) 2 cm medial to lateral border of IT Viewing (arthroscope) / Inflow ✅ Safe Zone — Furthest from sciatic nerve; established first
Posterolateral (PL) 2–4 cm lateral to lateral border of IT Working (shaver / RF / anchors) ⚠️ Danger Zone — Closer to sciatic nerve; requires direct vision or fluoroscopy

7.3 Modified "Midcentral" Technique

An alternative portal configuration has been described to optimize suture management and visualization for repairs Level IV:

  • Midcentral Portal: Established first, directly centered over the ischium using fluoroscopy — serves as primary viewing portal
  • Accessory Portals: Medial and lateral portals created 4 cm from the central portal, creating wider triangulation angle for complex suture passing
  • Advantage: Utilizes the bony backstop of the ischium for safety during initial access, preventing "plunging" into deep tissues

7.4 Fluid Management

⚠️ Critical Safety Parameter: Unlike joint arthroscopy, this is an extra-articular procedure. Fluid pump pressure must be kept low: 30–40 mmHg. High pressures can cause massive fluid accumulation in the thigh and retroperitoneum, potentially leading to compartment syndrome or abdominal fluid overload. Use of valved cannulas is recommended Level IV.

8. Step-by-Step Endoscopic Procedure

1 Phase 1: Access and Space Creation

  1. Infiltration: 20–30 mL saline injected into the subgluteal space via spinal needle before trocar insertion to pre-dilate the space
  2. Blunt Dissection: Trocar used to gently sweep the posterior aspect of the ischium, separating the gluteus maximus from the underlying bursa and tendon
  3. Initial Visualization: A 70° arthroscope is preferred as it allows for a "look down" view onto the hamstring footprint or a "look up" view at the gluteus maximus undersurface Level IV

2 Phase 2: Identification of the "Lighthouse"

Disorientation is a common challenge in the DGS. The "Lighthouse" refers to the proximal hamstring tendon origin (specifically the conjoint tendon of semitendinosus and biceps femoris) on the lateral aspect of the ischial tuberosity. The surgeon directs the shaver medially to clear tissue until the white, glistening fibers of the tendon are visualized against the bone. This establishes the medial boundary of dissection Level IV.

3 Phase 3: Ischial Bursectomy

  1. Debridement: Full-radius shaver (4.0 mm or 5.5 mm) introduced through the PL portal
  2. Resection Pattern: Proceeds from medial to lateral
  3. Completeness: The bursa must be excised completely to expose the hamstring tendon and gluteus maximus fascia. Radiofrequency (RF) probes used for hemostasis Level IV

4 Phase 4: Sciatic Nerve Identification & Protection

The most technically demanding step. The sciatic nerve lies lateral to the hamstring origin and is typically covered by a layer of fatty connective tissue known as the "veil" Level IV.

  • Exposure: Blunt probe or switching stick used to dissect laterally from hamstring origin. Shaver blade must be turned away from lateral soft tissues
  • Visual Cues: Distinct striated appearance (fascicles) and longitudinal blood vessels (vasa nervorum)
  • Neurolysis: If indicated, fibrous bands tethering the nerve are released. The nerve must be mobilized until it glides freely during passive knee flexion/extension Level IV

5 Phase 5: Cyst Resection & Stalk Management

  1. Cyst Localization: Guided by preoperative MRI; may be superficial (within bursa) or deep (emanating from tendon)
  2. Excision: Cyst wall resected with shaver. Simple aspiration is associated with high recurrence Level IV
  3. Stalk Management: The "stalk" or communicating channel must be traced to its origin and thoroughly debrided or ligated. Failure to address the stalk is a primary cause of recurrence
  4. Tendon Inspection: The base of the stalk often reveals a tear or delamination. If a significant tear is present, repair is required to seal the fluid pathway

6 Phase 6: Concomitant Hamstring Repair (If Required)

  1. Footprint Preparation: Decortication of ischial tuberosity with a burr to create a bleeding bed for healing
  2. Anchor Placement: Suture anchors (4.5 mm or 5.5 mm PEEK or biocomposite) placed into ischium
  3. Suture Passing: Retrograde suture passers or penetrators; horizontal mattress configuration standard
  4. Knot Tying: Sutures tied (or knotless anchors used) to compress tendon against bone, eliminating potential space for cyst recurrence Level IV

9. Key Anatomical Relationships & Hazard Management

A three-dimensional understanding of the anatomy is vital to minimize complications Expert opinion.

Table 4: Critical Neurovascular Structures & Hazard Management Strategies
Structure Location Hazard Avoidance Strategy
Sciatic Nerve Lateral to ischial tuberosity; exits via greater sciatic notch Most vulnerable structure; injury causes lower extremity deficits Abduction (45°) + ER maximizes safe zone; never pass instruments blindly laterally; establish PL portal under direct vision
Posterior Femoral Cutaneous Nerve (PFCN) Medial to or posterior to sciatic nerve; within subcutaneous fat Injury causes numbness of posterior thigh and perineum Avoid aggressive shaving of superficial "roof" (gluteus maximus undersurface)
Inferior Gluteal Artery Exits pelvis with sciatic nerve Profuse bleeding; may retract into pelvis Careful RF hemostasis; awareness during neurolysis near sciatic notch
Quadratus Femoris / Ascending Vessels Deep to sciatic nerve near superior border Deep dissection can encroach on vessels Dissection remains superficial to external rotators unless specific pathology addressed
The Dynamic "Safe Zone": The distance between the ischium and the sciatic nerve changes with leg position. Abduction (45°) and external rotation maximize this distance, moving the nerve laterally and creating a wider safe zone for instrumentation. This is a critical principle for safe portal placement.

Rehabilitation is strictly dictated by the surgical procedure performed. A simple excision allows rapid mobilization, whereas tendon repair requires prolonged protection for biological healing Protocol – not graded.
