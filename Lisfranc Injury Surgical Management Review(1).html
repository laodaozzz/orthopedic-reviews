Surgical Management of Lisfranc Joint Injuries

An Evidence-Based Analysis of Approaches, Reduction, and Fixation

Introduction: The Challenge of the Lisfranc Joint Complex

Injuries to the tarsometatarsal (TMT) joint complex, commonly known as Lisfranc injuries, represent a challenging spectrum of trauma for the orthopaedic surgeon. Level I [1] These injuries range from subtle, low-energy sprains to high-energy, comminuted fracture-dislocations. Level I

The critical nature of this injury is underscored by its high rate of misdiagnosis; it is estimated that up to 20% of Lisfranc injuries are unnoticed or diagnosed late, particularly low-energy variants mistaken for simple midfoot sprains. Level II Failure to achieve and maintain anatomic reduction leads to devastating long-term sequelae, including progressive midfoot instability, arch collapse, forefoot abduction, chronic pain, and debilitating post-traumatic osteoarthritis (PTA). Level I

Key Clinical Pearl: Given the poor outcomes of missed or malreduced injuries, operative intervention is indicated for any displaced or unstable injury pattern. Level I

The optimal surgical strategy remains debated, centered on three primary controversies: 1) the choice of surgical approach, 2) the optimal primary fixation (open reduction and internal fixation vs. primary arthrodesis), and 3) the ideal fixation hardware (transarticular screws vs. dorsal plates vs. flexible suture button fixation). Level I

Surgical Approaches: Principles of Dorsal Exposure

Achieving anatomic reduction necessitates an open approach to visualize the key articulations. Level IV While various approaches have been described, dorsal incisions are standard.

The Standard Two-Incision Dorsal Approach

The most common technique for adequate exposure of the medial and middle columns is the standard two-incision dorsal approach. Level IV This technique involves:

  1. Dorsomedial Incision: Centered over the first TMT joint, placed in the interval between the EHL and EDL tendons. The critical structure to identify and protect is the deep peroneal neurovascular bundle (dorsalis pedis artery and deep peroneal nerve). Level IV
  2. Dorsolateral Incision: A second incision over the lateral border of the third TMT joint. This provides access to the second (lateral aspect), third, and fourth TMT joints. Level IV

Surgical Note: A critical consideration is the preservation of the soft tissue bridge between the two incisions, which must be at least 4 cm to 5 cm wide to minimize the risk of skin necrosis or slough. Level IV

The Modified Single-Incision Dorsal Approach

In response to wound complication rates, a modified single-incision technique has been developed. Level IV This technique, described by Philpott et al., utilizes a single, long longitudinal incision centered over the second metatarsal, developing subcutaneous "windows" to access different columns. Level IV

Key Study Finding: A 2021 cadaveric study demonstrated that this modified single-incision approach provided equivalent joint surface preparation for arthrodesis of the first three TMT joints compared to the standard two-incision technique, confirming its viability. Level V [12]

Reduction Maneuvers: The Paramount Goal

The single most important factor determining a successful long-term outcome is the quality of the anatomic reduction. Level III Functional results are most dependent on the restoration of the TMT anatomy, not the specific fixation implant used. Level III

Sequence of Reduction

A stepwise approach, as advocated by AO principles, is essential. The reduction must build upon a stable base, focusing on the keystone architecture of the midfoot. Level IV

1 Restore the Medial Column: First, the relationship between the cuneiforms and the navicular is restored.

2 Reduce the Keystone: The normal anatomical relationship between the second metatarsal (M2) base and the middle cuneiform (C2) is restored. This step is fundamental.

3 Reduce the Lisfranc Ligament: The relationship between the M2 base and the medial cuneiform (C1) is reduced, closing the space of the disrupted Lisfranc ligament.

4 Sequential Fixation: The remaining TMT joints are then addressed in sequence: first, third, fourth, and fifth.

Critical Technique: Provisional fixation with Kirschner wires (K-wires) is a critical and indispensable step. Level IV Surgeons will first temporarily stabilize the reduced joints with K-wires, allowing for fluoroscopic verification of the reduction and foot stability before securing the reconstruction with definitive plates or screws.

The Central Debate: ORIF vs. Primary Arthrodesis

The choice between joint-sparing ORIF and joint-sacrificing Primary Arthrodesis (PA) has been the most contentious topic in Lisfranc surgery. Level I A critical finding is that purely ligamentous Lisfranc injuries have worse functional outcomes when treated with ORIF compared to injuries with associated fractures. Level II

Considerations for the Athletic Population

A 2025 systematic review focusing on return-to-sport (RTS) found no significant difference in the likelihood of RTS between athletes treated with ORIF versus PA. Level II RTS times were comparable, ranging from 8–30 weeks for ORIF and 19.7–28.5 weeks for PA. Level II

Fixation Hardware Principles

Once the reduction is achieved and the decision to perform ORIF or PA is made, the surgeon must select the appropriate hardware.

Dorsal Bridge Plates vs. Transarticular Screws

Biomechanical studies comparing dorsal bridge plates (DBP) to transarticular screws (TAS) have found that both constructs provide comparable fixation stability. Level V However, clinical data strongly favors dorsal plating. A 2020 meta-analysis found that DBP fixation resulted in statistically superior AOFAS functional scores compared to TAS. Level II

Dorsal Bridge Plates

Span TMT joints without penetrating articular surface. Associated with lower reoperation rate and may lead to less post-traumatic arthritis. Level II

Transarticular Screws

Traditional rigid fixation. Requires violating articular cartilage. Associated with higher rates of hardware removal. Level III

Flexible Fixation: Suture Button Solution

A major advancement has been the adoption of flexible fixation, such as suture button devices, for purely ligamentous injuries. Level II

Evidence-Based Treatment Algorithm

The current evidence allows for a stratified treatment algorithm based on injury pattern and patient goals.

1 Stable, Non-displaced (Nunley/Vertullo Stage I): Non-operative treatment with 6–8 weeks of non-weightbearing in a cast or boot. Level I

2 Unstable, Purely Ligamentous (Nunley/Vertullo Stage II):

  • Athlete/High-Demand: Flexible (Suture Button) Fixation. Rationale: Excellent short-term RTS rates (100%), 0% HWR. Level II
  • General Population/Definitive: Primary Arthrodesis (PA). Rationale: Prophylactically eliminates risk of PTA (2.8% vs 17.3% for ORIF). Level I
  • Traditional/Compromise: ORIF with Dorsal Bridge Plates. Rationale: Joint-sparing, but carries high likelihood of requiring HWR. Level II

3 Unstable, Bony Fracture-Dislocation (Non-comminuted): ORIF with Dorsal Bridge Plates. Rationale: The joint is salvageable. Articular-sparing plates provide superior functional outcomes. Level II

4 Unstable, Severe Comminution / High-Energy: Primary Arthrodesis (PA). Rationale: The articular surfaces are non-salvageable. PA provides definitive, stable fixation. Level I

Emerging and Novel Surgical Techniques

Minimally Invasive and Arthroscopic-Assisted Reduction

The most valuable application for arthroscopy is in delayed-presentation or "missed" Lisfranc injuries. Level III Arthroscopic-assisted reduction allows visualization, debridement of scar tissue, and confirmation of anatomic reduction, followed by percutaneous fixation. Level III

Novel Implants: Biointegrative Screws

A 2024 biomechanical study introduced "biointegrative screws" designed to provide rigid fixation while being bio-integrative, thus eliminating the need for hardware removal. Level V Initial cadaveric study found that metal screws, flexible fixation, and biointegrative screws all showed comparable effectiveness in correcting diastasis intra-operatively. Level V

Evidence Limitation: High-quality randomized controlled trials are needed to compare long-term (10-year) outcomes of suture button fixation, primary arthrodesis directly against suture button fixation for purely ligamentous injuries, and the clinical efficacy of novel biointegrative screws.
