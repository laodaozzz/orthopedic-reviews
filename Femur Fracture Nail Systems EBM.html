Comprehensive Summary for Intermediate Residents

Introduction

Subtrochanteric femur fractures, defined from the lesser trochanter to ~5 cm distally, represent a major challenge in orthopedic traumatology. They account for 10-34% of proximal femoral fractures with a bimodal distribution: low-energy injuries in osteoporotic geriatric bone vs. high-energy trauma in young adults (ages 20-40) from MVCs, falls, or ballistic injuries 1.

Key Biomechanical Principle: The subtrochanteric region experiences the skeleton's highest mechanical stress, with medial calcar compression up to 8.3 MPa and lateral cortex tensile forces ~6.2 MPa 2. Powerful deforming muscles (iliopsoas, glutei, adductors) create characteristic deformity: shortening, varus angulation, and rotational malalignment.

Surgical intervention is universally advocated, with intramedullary nailing as the gold standard due to superior biological preservation and load-sharing biomechanics compared to plating 3,5. The primary debate centers on cephalomedullary nail design: standard Reconstruction Intramedullary Nails (RIN) with dual parallel screws vs. newer Integrated Dual-Screw (IDS) systems like the InterTAN™.

Section 1: InterTAN IDS vs. Reconstruction Nail (RIN)

Fracture Classification & Anatomical Context

Validated classification systems guide implant selection:

  • Seinsheimer Classification: Categorizes by fragment geometry and comminution. Type V fractures (subtrochanteric with intertrochanteric extension) require long cephalomedullary nails for multi-regional fixation 14.
  • Russell-Taylor Classification: Evaluates piriformis fossa integrity. Type II fractures (piriformis involvement) contraindicate piriformis-entry nails, mandating modern trochanteric-entry devices 3.

Biomechanical Stability: The "Z-Effect" & IDS Solution

Standard reconstruction nails use two independent, parallel screws: a larger inferior lag screw and smaller superior anti-rotation screw. This creates vulnerability to the "Z-effect"—unequal sliding causing superior screw cut-through and inferior screw back-out, leading to varus collapse 18.

Z-Effect Incidence: Retrospective analyses indicate Z-effect occurs in 5.7-21% of cases with independent parallel screw systems 18.

The InterTAN's Integrated Dual-Screw (IDS) technology features an 11mm lag screw and 7mm compression screw interlocking via worm-gear mechanism. This converts rotational torque into active linear compression, creating a rigid 15.5mm composite construct that eliminates independent migration and the Z-effect 9,18.

Nonunion Rates & Geometric Mismatch

Intramedullary fixation reduces nonunion risk by 77% vs. plating 2. Level I RCTs show no significant difference in ultimate union rates between InterTAN and RINs, but InterTAN often achieves faster union due to maintained compression 6,29.

Critical Consideration in Young Adults: The InterTAN's trapezoidal proximal profile (15.25×16.25mm) and 15.5mm composite dual-screw create geometric mismatch with dense, narrow young adult femurs. This risks:
  • Lateral wall blowout if thickness < 20.5mm 34
  • Anterior cortex impingement due to femoral bow mismatch 32,36
Meticulous reaming is required to accommodate the rigid implant.

Section 2: Hardware Removal Complications & Protocols

Young adults frequently request implant removal due to hardware prominence, ITB irritation, or occupational requirements 11. However, extraction creates massive stress risers.

Physics of Stress Risers

The InterTAN's integrated dual-screw removal leaves a 15.5mm oval void in the lateral cortex. When defect diameter exceeds 30% of bone diameter, stress concentration increases by >60% 13. The lateral cortex normally withstands highest tensile forces (~6.2 MPa) 2, making this void perilous.

Iatrogenic Refracture Rates

Retrospective reviews report refracture rates of 3-14.5% after cephalomedullary nail removal 11. A Level III study found 1.1% refracture rate for InterTAN vs. 6.8% for PFNA-II at 9 months post-removal 38.

Extraction-Specific Complications

The worm-gear mechanism can undergo "cold welding" or osseointegration, making independent extraction impossible 39. Compression screw fatigue failure may require destructive trephine reaming, massively expanding the cortical defect. In extreme cases, this necessitates conversion to Total Hip Arthroplasty 39,40.

EBM-Supported Post-Removal Protocols

Prophylactic Bone Grafting: Grade IA recommendation to fill the 15.5mm void at time of extraction using:

  • Autologous iliac crest cancellous bone (osteogenic/inductive/conductive)
  • Allograft + osteoinductive agents (rhBMP-7, Demineralized Bone Matrix) 1,44

Conclusion

The InterTAN's IDS mechanism provides superior biomechanical stability, eliminates the Z-effect, and promotes primary bone healing in complex subtrochanteric fractures. However, its geometric mismatch with young adult femurs demands meticulous surgical technique. Hardware removal creates massive stress risers requiring prophylactic bone grafting and strict protected weight-bearing protocols spanning 12 weeks. Only through this disciplined approach can young adults safely return to high-impact function.

Evidence Synthesis: This summary integrates Level I-IV evidence from orthopedic literature. Key findings include:
  • InterTAN eliminates Z-effect (0% vs 5.7-21%) 18
  • 1.1% refracture rate after InterTAN removal vs 6.8% for PFNA-II 38
  • 12-week rehabilitation protocol with prophylactic bone grafting is mandatory post-removal
