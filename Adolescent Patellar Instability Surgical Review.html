1. Introduction: The Clinical Paradigm

The management of patellofemoral pathology in the adolescent population represents one of the most nuanced and evolving challenges in modern orthopaedic surgery. This demographic presents a unique intersection of high physical demand, ligamentous laxity, and skeletal immaturity, complicating the application of surgical algorithms designed for adult cohorts.

The clinical triad of Excessive Lateral Pressure Syndrome (ELPS), patellar tilt, and mild lateral subluxation with associated chondral damage creates a biomechanical paradox that has historically divided surgical opinion.[1, 7, 25]

Historical Context
For decades, the lateral retinaculum was viewed as the primary culprit in anterior knee pain and maltracking, leading to widespread adoption of the Isolated Arthroscopic Lateral Retinacular Release (ILR). However, long-term outcome data has revealed that while lateral release effectively reduces static pressure, it fails to address dynamic instability — often exacerbating the condition by removing a secondary passive restraint.[19, 36]

In response to the high failure rates of isolated release, the focus shifted toward the Medial Patellofemoral Ligament (MPFL). Reconstruction of this ligament has become the gold standard for stabilizing the patella. Yet, a subset of patients presents with both medial incompetence (instability) and lateral tightness (tilt/compression), giving rise to the Combined Procedure: MPFL reconstruction with simultaneous lateral release to "balance" tracking and unload the lateral facet.[3, 5, 6]

This report evaluates the indications, biomechanics, outcomes, and failure rates of Isolated Lateral Release versus Lateral Release combined with MPFL Reconstruction in the adolescent patient, synthesizing evidence on physeal-sparing techniques, radiological decision-making, and cartilage lesion management.

2. Pathophysiology & Biomechanics

2.1 The Spectrum of Patellofemoral Dysplasia: ELPS vs. Instability

While often co-existing, Excessive Lateral Pressure Syndrome and Patellar Instability are distinct biomechanical entities. ELPS is fundamentally a problem of compression, whereas instability is a problem of containment.[1, 7]

Excessive Lateral Pressure Syndrome (ELPS)

Defined by adaptive shortening of the lateral retinaculum. This fibrous band tethers the lateral facet of the patella into the lateral trochlear ridge, creating a "vector of tilt" that dramatically reduces the contact surface area, concentrating joint reaction forces onto a small strip of lateral cartilage. Over time, this focal overload leads to chondromalacia, fissuring, and eventually osteoarthritis. Crucially, in pure ELPS, the patella is stable — held tight against the femur.[1, 7, 25]

Patellar Instability

Involves failure of the medial restraints to keep the patella engaged in the trochlea during early flexion (0–30°). The primary restraint is the MPFL, which provides 50–60% of total restraining force against lateral displacement. In adolescents with "mild lateral subluxation," the MPFL is often elongated or attenuated rather than frankly ruptured.[3]

Mixed Pathology
The patient profile in question — presenting with both ELPS and mild subluxation — suffers from a "mixed" pathology: lateral tightness tilts the patella (causing compression and chondral damage), while medial laxity allows it to subluxate (causing shear damage and instability).

2.2 Biomechanical Vectors and the "Unloading" Paradox

The theoretical goal of adding a lateral release to an MPFL reconstruction is to "unload" the lateral compartment while "stabilizing" the medial side. Finite Element Analysis (FEA) has been instrumental in quantifying these effects.[5]

  • Cutting the lateral retinaculum significantly reduces Contact Pressure (CP) on the lateral facet — by approximately 39% at 90° flexion compared to the intact knee.[5] Level V
  • However, lateral release increases lateral patellar displacement by roughly 20% at flexion angles >20°.[6] Level V
⚡ The Biomechanical Paradox
Lateral Release Unloads → Good for chondromalacia (pain relief).
Lateral Release Destabilizes → Bad for subluxation (recurrence risk).

In a combined procedure, the reconstructed MPFL graft must work harder to overcome the loss of lateral restraint. If the graft is not perfectly isometric or is tensioned incorrectly, the lack of lateral counterbalance can lead to graft elongation or failure.[5, 6]

2.3 Skeletal Maturity and the Physis

The distal femoral physis accounts for approximately 70% of longitudinal femoral growth. Traditional adult MPFL reconstruction techniques involving transverse femoral tunnels risk crossing the growth plate, potentially causing angular deformities (genu valgum) or limb length discrepancies.[8, 10, 11]

Surgical techniques for adolescents must be categorized as:

  • Physeal-Sparing — avoiding the physis entirely
  • Physeal-Respecting — using guidance to stay safely epiphyseal

3. Preoperative Diagnostic Parameters

The decision to perform an isolated release versus a combined reconstruction is strictly dictated by anatomical parameters. A "one-size-fits-all" approach leads to predictably high failure rates.[12, 13, 14]

3.1 Tibial Tuberosity–Trochlear Groove (TT-TG) Distance

The TT-TG distance is the standard metric for assessing lateralization of the extensor mechanism.

Adolescent nuance: TT-TG distance increases with age and skeletal size. A percentile-based growth chart may be more appropriate than absolute cutoffs in younger patients.[12, 13]

3.2 Trochlear Dysplasia (Dejour Classification)

Trochlear dysplasia is the most consistent anatomical risk factor for recurrent instability.[14, 15, 16]

Critical Point
In high-grade dysplasia (Type B or D), the lateral retinaculum often acts as a secondary restraint. Isolated Lateral Release is strictly contraindicated because it removes one of the few remaining soft tissue restraints, precipitating catastrophic instability.[14, 15]

3.3 Patellar Tilt and Height

Patellar Tilt

Measured on axial MRI with the quadriceps relaxed. A tilt angle of >20° is indicative of ELPS. Key distinction:[17]

  • Fixed Tilt: Patella is angled but centered → suggests tight lateral structures
  • Dynamic Tilt: Patella tilts as it subluxates → suggests medial incompetence

If the tilt is rigid and fixed (>20°), a lateral release may be necessary as an adjunct to MPFLR. If dynamic, MPFLR alone usually corrects it.[17]

Patella Alta (Caton-Deschamps Index)

A Caton-Deschamps index >1.2 defines patella alta. The high-riding patella does not engage the trochlea until deep flexion, leaving it dependent on soft tissues for stability. Lateral release does not correct patella alta. Severe alta (>1.4) often requires distalization procedures.[14]

4. Isolated Arthroscopic Lateral Retinacular Release (ILR)

4.1 Procedure

Involves arthroscopic or open sectioning of the lateral retinaculum, usually from the superior pole of the patella to the tibial tubercle. The goal is to eliminate the lateral tether, reduce the Q-angle dynamically, and decrease contact pressure on the lateral facet.[1, 2, 7, 24]

4.2 Outcomes and Failure Analysis

Despite its theoretical benefits for compression, the evidence for ILR in the setting of instability is uniformly poor.

🚨 Landmark Database Study
Patients undergoing Isolated Lateral Release were 4.5 times more likely to require revision surgery within 5 years compared to MPFL reconstruction (OR 4.5, 95% CI 3.0–7.3, p < 0.0001). The 5-year revision rate for ILR was 7.4% versus 1.8% for MPFLR.[19] Level III

Mechanism of Failure

In an adolescent with mild subluxation, the lateral retinaculum provides passive tension that holds the patella against the femur. Cutting this structure creates a "floating patella":[20, 21, 22]

  • Lateral Instability: Paradoxically worsened — reduced force seating the patella in the groove
  • Medial Instability: In over-release, the patella can dislocate medially — a disabling complication

Restricted Indications

Current consensus restricts ILR to patients with:[23, 24, 25]

  1. Pure Lateral Patellar Compression Syndrome (LPCS): Confirmed tilt, lateral facet pain, and NO history of dislocation or objective instability
  2. Negative Apprehension Sign: No medial laxity
⛔ Contraindication
For the patient profile described (mild lateral subluxation), Isolated Lateral Release is contraindicated.

5. Medial Patellofemoral Ligament Reconstruction (MPFLR)

5.1 The Gold Standard for Instability

MPFL reconstruction has supplanted lateral release and non-anatomic reefing procedures as the treatment of choice, restoring the primary restraint to lateral translation.[3, 32, 33]

5.2 Physeal-Sparing Techniques in Adolescents

Table 3: Physeal-Sparing MPFL Reconstruction Techniques
TechniqueDescriptionProsCons
A. Epiphyseal Socket (Schöttle)[10] Femoral attachment distal to physis; shallow socket (15–20 mm) reamed under fluoroscopy Anatomic; strong fixation; <1% growth arrest Requires precise fluoroscopic guidance

C. Soft Tissue Slings / Suture Anchors[30, 31] Graft sutured to periosteum or MCL fibers at femoral site Equivalent clinical outcomes; lower patellar fracture risk Potentially less rigid fixation

5.3 Outcomes of Isolated MPFLR

  • Recurrence: Consistently below 5%; one study of 34 adolescents showed 0% re-dislocation at 5 years.[32] Level IV
  • Functional Scores: Significant improvements in Kujala (mean increase ~30–40 points), Lysholm, and Tegner scores.[33]
  • Cartilage Protection: By preventing lateral excursion, MPFLR reduces shear stress on the cartilage.[33, 39]

6. The Combined Procedure: Lateral Release + MPFL Reconstruction

6.1 Rationale for Combination

The rationale is synergistic: MPFLR stabilizes the medial side (treats subluxation), while lateral release relieves the lateral tether (treats ELPS/tilt). Proponents argue that a tight lateral retinaculum will act as a constant antagonist to the MPFL graft.[5, 6]

6.2 Evidence Against Routine Combination

Randomized Controlled Trial (Level II Evidence)
A RCT compared isolated MPFLR vs. Combined MPFLR+LRR:[34] Level II
  • No significant difference in IKDC scores (78.1 vs 80.7, p=0.309)
  • No significant difference in postoperative patellar tilt (20.9° vs 17.3°, p=0.097)
  • Conclusion: MPFLR alone is sufficient to correct tilt in the vast majority of cases
Systematic Review (Level I Evidence)
A systematic review of >2,000 knees concluded there is no evidence that adding a lateral release impacts positively on recurrence or revision rates. It introduces the risk of medial instability if the release is too aggressive.[35] Level I

Additional Complications of the Combined Procedure

  • Increased postoperative swelling and hemarthrosis
  • Higher risk of arthrofibrosis (bleeding from lateral genicular vessels)
  • Delayed quadriceps recovery due to inhibition[19]

6.3 Indications for the Combined Approach

The combination is reserved for specific situations:[26, 37]

  1. Irreducible Intraoperative Tilt: After MPFL graft fixation, if the patella cannot be manually everted to neutral or "hangs up" on the lateral condyle
  2. Severe Fixed Deformity: Rare cases of longstanding ELPS with fibrotic, contracted retinaculum
  3. Cartilage Unloading (Specific): Deep, full-thickness lateral facet ulcers where unloading is the primary goal

7. Management of Chondral Pathology

7.1 Location Matters

  • Lateral Facet Lesions: Typical of ELPS (compression). Lateral release reduces pressure; MPFLR also reduces shear forces during subluxation.[38]
  • Medial Facet Lesions: Typical of dislocation (impact). Lateral release has minimal effect. MPFLR is critical to prevent recurrent impact.[39]

7.2 Surgical Strategy

Principle: Stabilization First
The primary treatment for instability-associated chondral damage is stabilization. If the patella continues to subluxate, no cartilage procedure will survive the shear forces. Therefore, Isolated MPFLR is the baseline requirement.[38, 39]
  • Adjuvant Lateral Release: If the lesion is on the lateral facet and there is significant tilt, a conservative release (lengthening rather than complete cutting) may be added
  • Cartilage Restoration: For focal defects >1–2 cm², concurrent procedures (osteochondral autograft/allograft, MACI) can be performed alongside MPFLR. For diffuse chondromalacia, stabilization and rehabilitation are the mainstays.[38, 39]

⚠ Adolescent Caveat
The rapid return after ILR (3–4 months) often masks underlying instability, leading to early recurrence. Adolescents undergoing MPFLR often require 9–12 months to regain neuromuscular control and confidence compared to adults.[4, 41, 42]

10. Conclusion & Surgical Guidelines

Based on comprehensive evidence review, the following conclusions are drawn for the adolescent patient with ELPS, patellar tilt, mild lateral subluxation, and chondral damage:

1. Abandonment of Isolated Release
Isolated Arthroscopic Lateral Retinacular Release is contraindicated for this patient profile. It addresses ELPS/Tilt but disastrously fails to control subluxation (4.5× increase in revision rates). Reserve strictly for pure compression syndromes with zero instability.[19, 20, 23]
2. Primacy of MPFL Reconstruction
Physeal-sparing MPFL reconstruction is the definitive treatment. It addresses root cause (medial incompetence) and provides stability to halt chondral damage progression. For open physes, use epiphyseal sockets (Schöttle) or soft-tissue slings.[8, 10, 28, 30]
3. Selective Use of Combination
Routine addition of lateral release to MPFLR is not supported by evidence. Reserve for irreducible intraoperative tilt or severe rigid retinacular contracture.[34, 35]
4. Diagnostic Imperative
Preoperative measurement of TT-TG distance and Trochlear Dysplasia (Dejour type) is non-negotiable. TT-TG >15–20 mm or high-grade dysplasia = high-risk for soft-tissue-only surgery. Counsel families on potential need for bony realignment upon skeletal maturity.[12, 13, 14]

Final Recommendation

For the adolescent patient described, the optimal evidence-based intervention is an Isolated Physeal-Sparing Medial Patellofemoral Ligament Reconstruction. This approach maximizes stability, minimizes iatrogenic complications, and offers the highest probability of a durable return to active living.
