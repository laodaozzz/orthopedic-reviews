Total Ankle Arthroplasty in Rheumatoid Arthritis

Evidence-Based Educational Summary

1. Executive Summary & Historical Context

The management of end-stage ankle arthritis in rheumatoid arthritis (RA) remains one of the most complex challenges in modern orthopaedics. Despite the fact that a substantial proportion of RA patients eventually develop symptomatic ankle involvement, the ankle was historically referred to as the "forgotten joint" in RA research. ILevel I – Registry / prospective cohort data

The pathophysiology involves a relentless sequela of synovial proliferation, cartilage degradation, and distinctive periarticular erosions that destabilize the talocrural joint. When superimposed upon systemic osteoporosis and chronic corticosteroid exposure, surgical reconstruction becomes a high-stakes endeavor requiring meticulous planning.

Historically, ankle arthrodesis (fusion) was the gold standard. The rationale was mechanical reliability—fusion provided a stable, plantigrade foot in patients with poor bone stock. However, the functional cost is high: loss of ankle motion places supraphysiologic stress on adjacent subtalar and talonavicular joints, often already ravaged by the same inflammatory process. Long-term follow-up reveals a cascade of adjacent segment disease, leading to progressive disability. IIILevel III – Retrospective comparative study

Total ankle arthroplasty (TAA) emerged as a function-preserving alternative. Early first-generation implants (1970s–1980s) were catastrophic—massive bone resection, cement reliance, and aseptic loosening rates exceeding what is reported in modern series within five years. It was not until the advent of third-generation, porous-coated, bone-sparing implants that TAA became viable for this population. IVLevel IV – Historical case series / expert review

Contemporary data suggest that properly selected RA patients can achieve survivorship and functional outcomes comparable to—and in some metrics superior to—patients with primary osteoarthritis (OA). However, this success is contingent upon sophisticated perioperative medical management regarding DMARDs and glucocorticoids, and surgical strategies to mitigate infection and osteoporotic subsidence risks.

Key Insight
Modern registry data indicate that RA is not an independent risk factor for revision when accounting for other variables, provided patients are medically optimized and appropriate surgical techniques are employed.

2. Pathophysiology of the Rheumatoid Ankle

2.1 The Osteoporotic Substrate

Bone quality in RA is compromised by two synergistic forces: the disease itself and its treatment. Systemic inflammation driven by TNF-α and IL-6 upregulates the RANKL pathway, stimulating osteoclast differentiation while suppressing osteoblast-mediated bone formation. This results in generalized osteopenia and focal juxta-articular osteoporosis, creating a "soft" bony bed for implant fixation. ILevel I – Systematic review of pathophysiology

This is exacerbated by chronic glucocorticoid therapy—the leading cause of secondary osteoporosis. Corticosteroids induce rapid apoptosis of osteocytes and osteoblasts, leading to profound uncoupling of bone remodeling. In long-term steroid therapy, fracture rates are significantly elevated and the risk of nontraumatic osteonecrosis—particularly of the talus—is markedly increased. IILevel II – Prospective cohort study

For the TAA surgeon, this presents a critical biomechanical challenge: the talar component must resist substantial compressive and shear forces during gait, yet it relies on a talar dome that may be structurally hollow or necrotic.

2.2 The Compromised Soft Tissue Envelope

The anterior ankle incision is notoriously thin—often less than 2 mm over the extensor tendons—supplied by a precarious angiosome. In RA patients, this envelope is frequently atrophic: chronic steroid use inhibits collagen synthesis and fibroblast proliferation, leading to dermal atrophy and capillary fragility. RA-associated vasculitis further impairs microvascular perfusion. IIILevel III – Anatomical and clinical studies

Clinical Warning
The combination of thin skin, suppressed immunity, and a superficially located implant creates a "perfect storm" for wound complications. The RA cohort consistently demonstrates higher risk of major wound complications and deep infection compared to OA cohorts.

3. Preoperative Evaluation

3.1 Inflammatory Markers: ESR & CRP

In the general orthopaedic population, elevated preoperative ESR and CRP are traditional red flags for latent infection. An increased preoperative CRP in hip and knee arthroplasty has been linked to higher probability of periprosthetic joint infection (PJI). However, applying these thresholds to RA patients is fraught with difficulty due to the "noise" of systemic inflammation. IILevel II – Prospective diagnostic study

Research indicates that RA patients often have baseline elevations in these markers that do not necessarily correlate with surgical site infection risk in the same linear fashion as in OA patients. A study involving TKA patients found no statistically significant link between modest preoperative CRP/ESR elevations and postoperative wound complications or PJI, suggesting that routine cancellation of surgery based solely on these markers (absent clinical signs of infection) may be unwarranted. IILevel II – Prospective cohort with statistical analysis

Important Nuance
The key distinction is between controlled systemic inflammation and uncontrolled flare. Composite disease activity scores (DAS28) provide more granular assessment than raw lab values.

3.2 Disease Activity Score (DAS28)

The DAS28 integrates tender and swollen joint counts, acute phase reactants, and patient global assessment:

  • DAS28 < 2.6: Remission ILevel I – Validated composite score
  • DAS28 2.6–3.2: Low disease activity
  • DAS28 3.2–5.1: Moderate disease activity
  • DAS28 > 5.1: High disease activity — extrapolation from orthopaedic literature suggests impaired wound healing and need for higher perioperative steroid doses. IIILevel III – Extrapolated from broader literature

3.3 Active vs. Quiescent Disease

Recent evidence has challenged the dogma that active inflammatory disease is an absolute contraindication to TAA. A pivotal study comparing outcomes in patients with active inflammatory arthritis versus quiescent disease found no significant difference in implant survivorship at intermediate follow-up. Similarly, a systematic review found that while RA patients generally have lower absolute functional scores (likely due to polyarticular involvement), the magnitude of improvement from baseline is often equal to or greater than that in OA patients. IILevel II – Systematic review and meta-analysis

4. Perioperative Pharmacological Management

The complexity of modern rheumatologic pharmacotherapy requires the orthopaedic surgeon to manage medications outside their traditional expertise. The 2022 ACR/AAHKS guidelines provide the most authoritative framework, balancing infection risk from immunosuppression against disease flare from withdrawal. ILevel I – Guideline based on systematic review

4.1 Traditional DMARDs

The most significant shift is the permissive stance toward non-biologic DMARDs. Historically, agents like Methotrexate were stopped weeks before surgery. Current consensus: the risk of flare upon discontinuation outweighs the negligible reduction in infection risk.

Current Recommendation (ACR/AAHKS 2022)
CONTINUE through the perioperative period: Methotrexate, Leflunomide, Hydroxychloroquine, Sulfasalazine, and Apremilast. Patients arriving on the morning of surgery having taken their weekly Methotrexate dose should proceed.

4.2 Biologic Agents & Targeted Synthetic DMARDs

These require timing surgery to correspond with the nadir of the drug's serum concentration:

  • TNF Inhibitors (Adalimumab, Etanercept, Infliximab, Golimumab): Withhold one dosing cycle prior to surgery. Schedule surgery when the next dose is due.
  • IL-17 / IL-12/23 Inhibitors (Secukinumab, Ustekinumab): Withhold one dosing cycle.
  • JAK Inhibitors (Tofacitinib, Baricitinib, Upadacitinib): Withhold ≥ 3 days prior to surgery. ⚠FDA black box warning for VTE; heightened vigilance for DVT prophylaxis
  • Rituximab (B-cell depleter): Plan surgery 4–6 months after last infusion, or in the last month of the dosing cycle.

4.3 SLE-Specific Medications

  • Non-Severe SLE: Withhold Mycophenolate Mofetil (MMF), Azathioprine, Cyclosporine, Tacrolimus for 1 week before surgery.
  • Severe SLE: Continue these medications — risk of organ damage (lupus nephritis flare) outweighs infection risk.

4.4 Restarting Medications

Restart biologic and targeted synthetic DMARDs approximately 14 days postoperatively, contingent upon:

  1. Evidence of wound healing (dry, approximated edges)
  2. Removal of sutures or staples
  3. Absence of clinical signs of infection (erythema, drainage)

4.5 Perioperative Glucocorticoid Management

The HPA axis is generally considered suppressed if a patient has taken ≥ 5 mg/day of prednisone for ≥ 3 weeks or has a Cushingoid appearance. Current recommendations:

  • Continue daily maintenance dose on the day of surgery
  • For TAA (moderate stress): Administer 25 mg Hydrocortisone IV at induction, followed by 100 mg Hydrocortisone (divided) over 24 hours, then rapid taper to home dose
  • For minor procedures: No stress dose; continue home dose only

5. Surgical Strategies in the Osteoporotic Patient

5.1 Implant Selection

Modern three-component mobile-bearing designs (e.g., STAR) and fixed-bearing designs (e.g., Salto Talaris, Infinity) rely on press-fit fixation. However, in osteoporotic bone, standard press-fit may be insufficient. For these patients, stemmed implants are increasingly favored: IVLevel IV – Expert opinion / case series

  • INBONE II System: Features a modular intramedullary tibial stem bypassing metaphyseal bone to engage stronger diaphyseal cortical bone—robust option for severe deformity or compromised bone quality.
  • INFINITY System: Lower-profile implant; may have higher early revision rates in challenging bone.
  • INVISION System: Revision-specific design with wider talar components to bridge defects—salvage option for severe osteonecrosis or subsidence.

5.2 Biocementation

For "eggshell" bone, biocementation uses injectable, resorbable bone graft substitute (calcium sulfate/phosphate composite) at the bone-implant interface. This provides immediate compressive strength (grout-like) that resorbs over time and is replaced by host bone—unlike PMMA, which is permanent. A case series of 9 ankles in bone-fragile patients showed stable fixation without PMMA-associated complications. IVLevel IV – Case series

5.3 Osteoporosis and Reoperation Rates

Surprisingly, a 2025 study analyzing 270 osteoporotic TAA patients found no statistically significant difference in reoperation (5.9% vs. 5.6%) or periprosthetic fracture (6.3% vs. 4.1%) at 3 years compared to non-osteoporotic controls. This may reflect selection bias or the effectiveness of modern stemmed implants. IIILevel III – Retrospective cohort study

Interpretation
A diagnosis of osteoporosis alone should not exclude a patient from TAA, provided specific surgical adaptations (stemmed implants, biocementation) are employed.

6. Wound Management & Soft Tissue Complications

6.1 Technical Prevention Strategies

  1. Full-Thickness Flaps: Raise the fascia and extensor retinaculum with the skin flap to preserve the subdermal plexus.
  2. No Retraction Injury: Self-retaining retractors used with extreme caution or released periodically to prevent pressure necrosis.
  3. Extensor Retinaculum Repair: Meticulous closure provides a vascularized bed over the implant—if the skin dehisces, this deep layer protects the prosthesis.

6.2 Advanced Wound Care Modalities

  • Incisional Negative Pressure Wound Therapy (iNPWT): Prophylactic NPWT (e.g., PICO, Prevena) over closed incisions in high-risk patients significantly decreases wound complications. ILevel I – Systematic review
  • "Aperture Pad" Dressing: Modified Sir Robert Jones compressive dressing with a cutout allowing pressure relief over the incision while compressing surrounding tissue—shown to reduce wound complications threefold. IIILevel III – Comparative cohort study
  • Tranexamic Acid (TXA): Reduces postoperative hematoma formation, lowering infection rate (9% vs. 22% in comparative series). ILevel I – Systematic review

7. Complications: Periprosthetic Joint Infection

7.1 The Diagnostic Dilemma in RA

PJI diagnosis in RA patients is notoriously difficult due to the "Seronegative Infection" phenomenon: IIILevel III – Diagnostic accuracy studies

  • False Positives: Baseline systemic inflammation keeps ESR/CRP chronically elevated.
  • False Negatives: Potent immunosuppression (e.g., Tocilizumab, an IL-6 inhibitor) can completely blunt the CRP response, masking active infection. Up to 4% of PJI patients may present with normal markers.

7.2 Synovial Fluid Analysis

Due to unreliability of serum markers, synovial fluid analysis is the cornerstone of diagnosis: IILevel II – Diagnostic accuracy study

  • MSIS Thresholds: WBC > 3,000/µL, PMN > 80% — however, RA patients with sterile flare (pseudosepsis) can also present with high counts.
  • Alpha-Defensin: Less affected by systemic inflammatory disease and antibiotics; superior diagnostic performance in RA patients compared to traditional cell counts.
Critical Recommendation
In an RA patient with a painful TAA and elevated markers, aspiration is mandatory. Relying on ESR/CRP alone is insufficient for clinical decision-making.

8. Outcomes & Survivorship

  • Survivorship: Modern registry data indicates RA is not an independent risk factor for revision. 10-year survivorship is comparable to OA cohorts. ILevel I – Registry data with 8-year mean follow-up
  • Function: While RA patients may not achieve high-impact sports function, their reported satisfaction is often higher—a paradox explained by the massive relative improvement in global quality of life when ankle pain is relieved in the context of polyarticular disease. IILevel II – Systematic review
  • Perioperative Complications: No significant differences between RA and OA groups when modern protocols are followed. ILevel I – Systematic review and meta-analysis

Key Clinical Takeaways

  1. Do Not Fear the Markers: Elevated preoperative ESR/CRP are not absolute contraindications but require differentiation between active local infection and systemic disease activity. Aspiration is the arbiter.
  2. Follow the Cycle: Time surgery to the nadir of biologic drug levels. Continue Methotrexate. Do not stress-dose steroids unless the patient is truly HPA-suppressed.
  3. Stem the Tide: In severe osteoporosis, favor stemmed or modular implants (INBONE/INVISION) over standard low-profile designs to prevent subsidence.
  4. Protect the Envelope: Use prophylactic negative pressure wound therapy and aperture dressings. The soft tissue is the primary mode of failure in the early postoperative period.
