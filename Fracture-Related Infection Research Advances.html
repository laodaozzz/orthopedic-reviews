Advances in Fracture-Related Infection (FRI): 2020–2025

Interactive Evidence-Based Educational Summary • Source: Comprehensive Research Report (2020–2025)

1. Introduction: Burden & Paradigm Shift

Fracture-related infection (FRI) remains one of the most formidable complications in orthopedic trauma surgery, representing a dual pathology of deep bone infection and compromised fracture stability.[1] Incidence ranges from 1–2% for closed fractures to ≥30% in complex open fractures with severe soft tissue compromise.[2]

The clinical consequences are profound: revision surgeries, prolonged hospitalization, high-dose antimicrobial exposure, and in recalcitrant cases, permanent impairment or amputation.[2] Recent data indicates that treatment of FRI generates healthcare costs approximately 6.5 times higher than non-infected fractures.[6]

The period 2020–2025 has marked a distinct epoch: a transition from extrapolated dogma (borrowed from prosthetic joint infection literature) to evidence-based precision specific to FRI. This shift was necessitated by FRI's unique pathophysiology—fracture healing challenges, implant-bone interfaces, and often compromised vascularity.[5]

Scope of This Report: Synthesizes data from high-impact systematic reviews, RCTs, and international consensus guidelines across four pivotal domains: (1) validation of the consensus definition; (2) diagnostic biomarkers and molecular sequencing; (3) single-stage revision with local antibiotic carriers; and (4) re-evaluation of systemic antibiotic duration and route.

2. Standardization of Diagnosis: EBJIS/AO Consensus Definition

Historically, the literature was clouded by heterogeneous terminology ("post-traumatic osteomyelitis," "infected nonunion," "osteitis") with disparate diagnostic criteria, rendering meta-analyses nearly impossible.[7] The FRI Consensus Definition—developed by the AO Foundation, EBJIS, OTA, and PRO-Implant Foundation—represents the foundational achievement of the current era.[5]

2.1 Confirmatory vs. Suggestive Criteria

The definition establishes a hierarchical algorithm acknowledging the clinical spectrum of FRI.[9]

Confirmatory Criteria (Any Single Sign = Diagnostic for FRI)

  • Fistula, Sinus, or Wound Breakdown communicating with the fracture/implant — pathognomonic.[3]
  • Purulent Drainage from wound or during surgical exploration.[4]
  • Microbiological Confirmation: Phenotypically indistinguishable pathogens in ≥2 separate deep tissue/implant specimens (including sonication fluid).[9]
  • Histopathological Evidence: Microorganisms or >5 neutrophils per high-power field in deep tissue samples.[4]

Suggestive Criteria (Require Further Investigation)

  • Clinical signs: erythema, swelling, increased local temperature, fever, new-onset/increasing pain.[2]
  • Radiological signs: bone lysis, implant loosening, sequestration, nonunion, periosteal reaction.[2]
  • Single positive culture from one deep tissue sample.[9]
  • Elevated inflammatory markers (CRP, ESR, WBC).[2]
  • Persistent wound drainage beyond initial postoperative days.[2]

2.2 Validation & Diagnostic Performance

A multicenter, multinational retrospective cohort study (637 patients, 480 with FRI) validated the consensus definition with exceptional statistical performance.[10] Level II

Key Finding: ≥1 confirmatory sign yielded sensitivity 97.5%, specificity 100%, AUROC 0.99 (p<0.001). This 100% specificity provides surgeons confidence that meeting a confirmatory criterion is an absolute mandate for infection management.[10]

Among patients without confirmatory signs (the "gray zone"), clinical suggestive signs such as fever, wound drainage, local warmth, and redness maintained specificities of ≥95%.[10]

Criterion / Combination Sensitivity Specificity AUROC Interpretation

2.3 Impact on Research Homogeneity

The consensus definition resolved the "Tower of Babel" problem in orthopedic infection research. By standardizing inclusion criteria, it now enables data aggregation across centers and countries, as evident in systematic reviews published in 2024–2025.[2][5]

3. Diagnostic Frontiers: Biomarkers & Molecular Methods

3.1 Evolution of Serum Biomarkers

Limitations of the Classic Triad (CRP, ESR, WBC)

While useful for monitoring trends, these markers are insufficiently accurate for diagnosing late or chronic FRI in isolation. They are non-specific, often elevated from trauma, surgery, or other inflammatory conditions.[3][11]

Procalcitonin (PCT): Disappointing Results

A comprehensive meta-analysis (621 patients) demonstrated that serum PCT has poor diagnostic accuracy for PJI/FRI: pooled sensitivity of only 44.1%, AUROC 0.76, positive likelihood ratio 2.27, negative likelihood ratio 0.71.[12] Level I

Current Recommendation: Guidelines now recommend against routine use of PCT as a primary screening tool for FRI.[13]

Calprotectin: The Emerging Candidate

Calprotectin (S100A8/S100A9 heterodimer) is released directly by activated neutrophils at infection sites, offering a more immediate reflection of local inflammation than CRP (which is a lagging liver-synthesized indicator).[15]

  • Optimal cut-off: 3.58 mg/L → sensitivity 75%, specificity 66% on postoperative day 3.[13]
  • Rapid kinetics: rises quickly with infection, falls rapidly with successful treatment.[15]
  • May be particularly valuable for monitoring therapeutic response and early detection of relapse.[15]

3.2 Advanced Microbiological Methods

Sonication

Low-frequency ultrasound disrupts biofilm on explanted hardware, dislodging bacteria for culture. Sensitivity ranges 65–95%, specificity 50–97%.[9] The EBJIS/AO guidelines emphasize that sonication should be used in conjunction with deep tissue sampling for optimum accuracy.[9]

Next-Generation Sequencing (NGS)

Metagenomic NGS (mNGS) represents the most significant technological leap in the last five years. Unlike PCR (which requires specific primers), mNGS sequences all DNA/RNA in a sample for unbiased pathogen identification.[16] Level II

  • Pre-treated patients: NGS detects DNA of non-viable/inhibited bacteria — a "molecular footprint" of infection.[16]
  • Polymicrobial detection: Comprehensive profiling including strict anaerobes and fastidious organisms.[16]
  • Resistance profiling: Detects AMR genes, enabling tailored therapy even when organisms cannot be cultured.[16]
  • AUROC typically exceeds 0.80 in systematic reviews (2023–2024).[18]
Clinical Indications for NGS:
  1. Culture-negative FRI with strong clinical/radiological suspicion.[21]
  2. Anatomically constrained sites or inconclusive prior cultures.[22]
  3. "Rule-out" infection in complex nonunion before synthesis.[21]
Note on NGS limitations: NGS is not yet a universal replacement for culture due to high costs, complexity of data interpretation, and potential detection of environmental contaminants/skin flora (e.g., Cutibacterium acnes).[19]

4. Surgical Management: Bioactive Reconstruction

Surgical management has evolved from purely mechanical debridement to a "bio-active" approach characterized by local antibiotic carriers and increasing viability of single-stage revision protocols.

4.1 Local Antibiotic Carriers

Systemic antibiotics alone are often insufficient to eradicate biofilm infections in the avascular fracture environment. Local delivery systems achieve tissue concentrations 10–100× higher than MIC—concentrations that would be toxic systemically.[24]

Efficacy

A landmark multinational study (2022) demonstrated that Antibiotic-Loaded Carriers (ALCs) reduced FRI recurrence risk (HR: 0.55), with even more pronounced effect in chronic FRI (onset >10 weeks, HR: 0.21).[24] Level II

Biodegradable vs. Non-Biodegradable Carriers

  • PMMA (non-biodegradable): Effective elution but requires second surgery for removal; risk of secondary biofilm if left in situ.[25]
  • Biodegradable carriers (e.g., Cerament G, Stimulan): Sustained antibiotic elution, no removal surgery needed, act as bone void fillers with osteoconductive scaffold properties.[26]

Cost-Effectiveness

A single-stage protocol using Cerament G was estimated to reduce healthcare costs by ~$27,943 per patient (US) compared to standard multi-stage protocols with PMMA, through avoidance of second surgeries, reduced LOS, and lower reinfection/amputation rates.[28] Level III

4.2 Single-Stage vs. Two-Stage Revision

A systematic review published in Journal of Bone and Joint Infection (2025) provided comprehensive comparison.[31] Level I

4.3 Implant Retention (DAIR)

DAIR remains first-line for acute FRI, but recent evidence underscores strict patient selection:[34]

  • Success rates diminish significantly if symptoms persist >4 weeks.[34]
  • Failure rate: 21.4% for DAIR vs. 12.5% for implant exchange and 10.3% for external fixation conversion.[35] Level II
  • Tobacco smoking is a potent predictor of failure (HR ~3.1).[35]
  • Surgeons should have a low threshold for implant exchange when the implant is unstable or infection is beyond the acute window.[35]

5. Systemic Antibiotics: The Post-OVIVA Era

5.1 The OVIVA Trial: "Oral is the New IV"

The OVIVA trial (Oral Versus Intravenous Antibiotics) was a landmark multicenter RCT comparing oral vs. IV therapy for bone and joint infections.[6] Level I

  • Non-inferiority demonstrated: Oral therapy is non-inferior to IV during the initial 6 weeks.[6]
  • A cohort study implementing OVIVA protocol for FRI specifically found treatment failure of only 13% in the oral group—comparable to IV.[36]
  • Oral switch significantly reduces catheter-related complications (line sepsis, thrombosis, mechanical failure).[36]
Standard of Care (Post-OVIVA): Short IV induction (5–7 days post-debridement) → switch to highly bioavailable oral antibiotics (fluoroquinolones, rifampicin, clindamycin, doxycycline, linezolid) once susceptibility confirmed. Bioavailability and bone penetration—not route—determine success.[34]

5.2 Duration of Therapy: 6-Week Standard

A 2024 RCT compared 6, 12, and 24 weeks of therapy and found no statistically significant difference in recurrence rates at one year (p=0.98 between 6 and 12 weeks).[38] Level II

  • Prolonged courses were associated with higher adverse events and microbiome disruption without improved cure rates.[38]
  • Emerging guidelines recommend 6 weeks for FRI treated with implant retention or one-stage exchange (after thorough debridement).[34]
  • Short course (2 weeks): May suffice for "clean" segmental resection with all infected bone removed.[34]
  • Extended (12 weeks): May be considered in high-risk hosts or complex cases with retained hardware, though evidence remains weak.[34]

Scenario Recommended Duration Rationale

Implant retention (DAIR) / One-stage exchange 6 weeks Standard; RCT shows no benefit of longer courses

6. Landmark Studies (2020–2025)

Source limitation: The provided report references multiple studies but does not consistently list formal trial registrations. The studies below are extracted from the source document's text and references. Evidence gap: Only 6 key studies are formally described in sufficient detail to summarize as "landmark."
1. Validation of FRI Consensus Definition (Multicenter Cohort)
637 patients • Multinational retrospective cohort • Ref [10]

Validated the EBJIS/AO diagnostic framework with 97.5% sensitivity and 100% specificity for ≥1 confirmatory sign. AUROC 0.99. Established the definition as a robust clinical tool.

2. OVIVA Trial (Oral vs. IV Antibiotics)
Multicenter RCT • Bone and joint infections • Ref [6]

Demonstrated non-inferiority of oral antibiotics to IV therapy during the first 6 weeks of treatment for bone and joint infections. Paradigm-shifting for systemic antibiotic administration.

3. Multinational ALC Efficacy Study
Published in Antibiotics (2022) • Ref [24]

Showed Antibiotic-Loaded Carriers reduced FRI recurrence risk (HR 0.55 overall, 0.21 for chronic FRI). Provided robust evidence for routine integration of ALCs.

4. Single- vs. Two-Stage Revision Systematic Review
Published in JBJI (2025) • Ref [31]

Comprehensive comparison showing single-stage revision is non-inferior: bone healing 80% vs 77%, infection eradication 87% vs 81%. Supports shift toward single-stage protocols in selected patients.

5. Antibiotic Duration Comparison (6 vs. 12 vs. 24 weeks)
Published 2024 • Ref [38]

Found no statistically significant difference in recurrence rates (p=0.98 between 6 and 12 weeks). Prolonged courses increased adverse events without improving cure rates.

6. Procalcitonin Meta-Analysis
621 patients • Meta-analysis • Ref [12]

Demonstrated poor diagnostic accuracy of serum PCT for PJI/FRI (sensitivity 44.1%, AUROC 0.76). Led to recommendations against routine PCT use for FRI screening.

7. Conclusion & Future Outlook

FRI management has matured significantly 2020–2025, moving from anecdotal practices to a structured, evidence-based discipline anchored by the EBJIS/AO Consensus Definition.

Key Advances Summary

  1. Diagnosis: 100% specificity via consensus confirmatory criteria. NGS as a powerful tool for culture-negative and polymicrobial infections.[10][16]
  2. Surgical Strategy: Biodegradable local antibiotic carriers (Cerament G, Stimulan) reduce recurrence and enable single-stage revision. Non-inferior to two-stage exchange with superior cost-effectiveness.[24][26][28][31]
  3. Systemic Therapy: OVIVA trial validated early oral switch. Duration rationalized to 6 weeks.[6][38]

Future Directions

Beyond 2025, "precision FRI management" will likely involve routine molecular diagnostics profiling the resistome, single-stage reconstruction with customized antibiotic-eluting scaffolds, and emerging therapies such as bacteriophages and immunomodulatory treatments targeting T-cell dysfunction.[17]

Ultimate Goal: Restore form and function while minimizing the physical, psychological, and economic toll of infection.
