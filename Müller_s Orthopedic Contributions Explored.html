1. Introduction: The Paradigm Shift in Twentieth-Century Orthopedics

Maurice Edmond Müller (1918–2009) fundamentally reshaped musculoskeletal care. Before his ascendancy, fracture treatment was dominated by the conservative school, characterized by prolonged traction, bed rest, and plaster casting. This approach accepted "fracture disease"—joint stiffness, muscle atrophy, and venous thrombosis—as the necessary price of union. Müller instigated a revolution prioritizing anatomical reduction, rigid internal fixation, and early functional mobilization. His influence encompassed implant manufacturing, global surgical education, systematic trauma classification, and rigorous outcomes documentation. II

The Economic Burden (Pre-AO Era)

In 1945, the Swiss Accident Insurance (SUVA) reported that 70% of femoral fractures resulted in permanent invalidity pensions. The prevailing biological dogma held that metal implants inhibited callus formation and introduced infection risks. Müller saw "fracture disease" not as a biological inevitability, but as a mechanical failure of the treatment, not the fracture. III

1.1 The Intellectual Catalyst: Robert Danis

The pivotal moment occurred in March 1950 when Müller visited Robert Danis in Brussels. Danis's text Théorie et Pratique de l'Ostéosynthèse (1949) argued for immediate mobilization through rigid fixation and compression to achieve primary bone healing without callus. Müller observed Danis's patients walking days after surgery—a sight impossible in Swiss clinics. This encounter provided the intellectual seed for the AO. III

1.2 The Strategic Visionary

What separated Müller from other innovators was his recognition that surgical technique alone was insufficient. He understood that a revolution required a system: standardized instrumentation, dedicated research, a teaching mechanism, and a documentation center to prove efficacy. This systemic approach would become the hallmark of Müller's career. IV

2. The Foundation of a New Order: The AO Principles

On November 6, 1958, Müller convened a meeting at the Hotel Elite in Biel, Switzerland, bringing together a "core group" of Swiss surgeons dissatisfied with current outcomes. This group, eventually expanding to 13 surgeons ("The Thirteen Friends"), established the Arbeitsgemeinschaft für Osteosynthesefragen (AO) not merely as a society, but as a vertically integrated system. 2.1 The Original 1958 AO Principles

The initial clinical philosophy was radical and uncompromising, advocating for absolute mechanical stability:

  1. Anatomical Reduction: Perfect restoration of bone shape, particularly articular congruity.
  2. Rigid Internal Fixation: Absolute stability using compression techniques (lag screws and compression plates) to abolish interfragmentary motion.
  3. Preservation of Blood Supply: Atraumatic surgical technique.
  4. Early Active Mobilization: Immediate movement to prevent "fracture disease."

Note: Clinical experience over subsequent decades revealed that excessive stripping for perfect reduction could lead to necrosis. The AO philosophy later evolved toward "biological fixation" for diaphyseal fractures, prioritizing soft tissue envelope preservation over absolute stability, while Müller's original principles remain the gold standard for intra-articular fractures. II

3. The Language of Trauma: The Comprehensive Classification

Müller's Comprehensive Classification of Fractures (CCF) provided the universal taxonomy for skeletal trauma. Before this, fracture description was a chaotic mix of eponyms and vague terms, making comparison impossible. III

3.1 The Alphanumeric Structure

Published in its definitive form in 1987 and later adopted by the Orthopaedic Trauma Association (OTA), the system assigns a five-character alphanumeric code to every long bone injury. This structure is the global standard for orthopedic research. II

Fractures are typed A, B, or C based on severity and joint involvement. This creates a hierarchy of severity where A is least severe and C is most severe.

4. Revolutionizing the Hip: Innovations in Arthroplasty

Müller's contributions to elective hip surgery are equally foundational. Following John Charnley's work, he sought to improve implant design and handling of complex bone deficits. III

4.1 The Straight Stem: A Biomechanical Schism

In 1977, Müller introduced the "Original Müller Straight Stem" (the "Setzholz" or dibble). This created two distinct biomechanical schools in cemented hip arthroplasty:

4.2 The Acetabular Reinforcement Ring (ARR)

Also introduced in 1977, the ARR was a revolutionary device for salvage hip surgery, addressing cavitary and segmental acetabular defects in revision surgery or congenital dysplasia. It transforms the acetabulum into a load-bearing structure by transferring forces through screws into the ilium. Long-term studies confirm its efficacy. II

5. The Science of Verification: The AO Documentation Center

Müller realized that surgical innovation without data is merely experimentation. In 1958, he established the AO Documentation Center in Davos, mandating that every surgeon using AO implants complete a detailed fracture report. III

From Punch Cards to Global Registries

The center utilized IBM punch cards to track every case. This was the world's first large-scale, prospective orthopedic registry. It allowed rapid identification of "bad" implants and transformed the AO from a group of enthusiasts into a scientific body. This foresight predated the modern "Evidence-Based Medicine" movement by decades. The current International Documentation and Evaluation System (IDES) is its direct descendant. IV

6. Philanthropy and Legacy

Müller channeled his commercial success (founding Protek AG in 1967) back into the field. In 1974, he established the Maurice E. Müller Foundation, directing royalties to fund education and research, endowing chairs at Harvard, McGill, Toronto, and universities across Europe. IV

In a monumental act of cultural philanthropy, Müller and his wife donated 70 million Swiss francs for the construction of the Zentrum Paul Klee in Bern, designed by Renzo Piano.

7. Conclusion

Maurice E. Müller's impact is total. He combined the manual dexterity of a master surgeon, the classificatory mind of a taxonomist, the strategic vision of an industrialist, and the rigorous conscience of a scientist. He found orthopedics a craft of traction, plaster, and resignation; he left it a discipline of titanium, classification, and data-driven hope. As the AO Foundation noted, his "unceasing quest for the best way to treat problems of the musculoskeletal system" forever changed patient care. IV
