1. Introduction: The Evolution of Surgical Intervention

Plantar fasciitis—more accurately termed plantar fasciosis due to its degenerative rather than inflammatory nature—represents one of the most common causes of chronic heel pain. Approximately 90–95% of patients achieve symptom resolution through conservative modalities (eccentric stretching, orthotics, NSAIDs, extracorporeal shockwave therapy) within 12 months. II

A recalcitrant subset of 5–10% requires surgical intervention, typically considered only after 6–12 months of failed non-operative therapy. II

⚠ Historical Context
Open plantar fasciotomy, while effective, required substantial dissection of the plantar heel pad, leading to prolonged recovery, painful hypertrophic scarring, wound dehiscence, infection, and iatrogenic nerve entrapment. The introduction of Endoscopic Plantar Fascia Release (EPFR) in the early 1990s marked a pivotal shift—minimizing soft tissue trauma, preserving the plantar fat pad, and accelerating return to weight-bearing. III

EPFR success rates are reported between 70–90%, yet the procedure is not without technical nuance. Ongoing debates include optimal portal placement, necessity of calcaneal spur resection, and the precise extent of fascial release required to prevent lateral column destabilization. II

2. Pathomechanics & Anatomical Considerations

The plantar fascia is a complex aponeurosis consisting of three distinct bands: the medial, central, and lateral bands. The central band is the thickest and most clinically significant, originating from the medial process of the calcaneal tuberosity and extending distally to the proximal phalanges. Not specified in source

2.1 The Windlass Mechanism

During the propulsive phase of gait, dorsiflexion of the hallux winds the plantar fascia around the head of the first metatarsal. This shortens the calcaneus-to-metatarsal distance, elevates the medial longitudinal arch, and locks the midtarsal joints—converting the foot from a flexible shock absorber to a rigid lever essential for efficient push-off. IV

Clinical Implication
Surgical release of the fascia inevitably disrupts the windlass mechanism. The therapeutic goal is to release enough tension to alleviate enthesopathic pain while maintaining sufficient continuity to preserve arch stability and prevent pathological load transfer to the lateral column.

2.2 Neural Anatomy: The Zone of Danger

The safety of EPFR depends on avoiding iatrogenic injury to critical neurovascular structures. The posterior tibial nerve bifurcates into the medial and lateral plantar nerves within or just distal to the tarsal tunnel. III

  • Medial Calcaneal Nerve (MCN): Provides sensation to the medial heel. Its variable course makes it the structure most at risk during medial portal placement.
  • Lateral Plantar Nerve (LPN): Courses deep to the abductor hallucis, lateral to the medial portal. Provides motor innervation to lateral intrinsic muscles.
  • Baxter's Nerve (First branch of LPN): Of paramount importance—courses between the abductor hallucis and quadratus plantae before turning laterally. Compression of this nerve accounts for up to 20% of heel pain cases. Cadaveric studies have measured the distance from the third plantar portal to LPN at 15.21 mm and to Baxter's nerve at 10.46 mm. III

2.3 Calcaneal Spur

Recent anatomical and histological studies challenge the traditional "traction theory." The spur is located deep to the fascia, within the flexor digitorum brevis origin, with vertically oriented trabeculae—suggesting formation from vertical compression forces rather than longitudinal traction. This "vertical stress" theory supports spur resection as an independent procedure. III

3. Surgical Approaches: Comparative Analysis

3.1 Conventional Combined Medial-Lateral Portal Approach

The standard technique employs a "through-and-through" dual portal configuration. An obturator and slotted cannula assembly is passed from medial to lateral, deep to the plantar fascia. The endoscope is typically introduced laterally while the retrograde cutting knife is introduced medially. Not specified in source

Advantages: Triangulation, superior fluid management, bilateral depth confirmation.

Limitations: Risk to sural nerve branches via lateral portal, second incision with potential lateral scar pain. Not specified in source

3.2 Medial Dual-Portal (Modified) Approach

Utilizes two portals on the medial aspect only, avoiding the lateral side of the foot entirely. II

✦ Pivotal 2025 Comparative Study Findings
  • Operative time: Medial dual = 25.96 ± 1.52 min vs. Medial-lateral = 45.59 ± 2.38 min (P < 0.05)
  • Clinical outcomes at 12 months: VAS ~0.26–0.27; AOFAS ~92–93 — statistically indistinguishable
  • Safety: No significant difference in complications; medial-only theoretically eliminates lateral portal neuritis risk
II — Comparative study (Prospective cohort)

3.3 The Third Plantar Portal

A novel adjunct for difficult calcaneal spur resections, providing a perpendicular angle of attack using a burr. Cadaveric studies confirm safety: LPN distance 15.3 ± 1.47 mm, Baxter's nerve distance 10.46 mm. III

4. Intraoperative Landmarks & Visualization Methods

4.1 Surface Anatomy — Stryker EPF Measurement Protocol

A widely validated technique involving specific triangulation: Not specified in source

  1. Palpate the medial calcaneal tubercle or exostosis.
  2. Make a vertical skin marking 1–3 mm distal to the tubercle.
  3. Measure 17 mm dorsal from the plantar skin surface (non-weight bearing).
  4. The intersection marks the medial portal incision point.

This 17 mm measurement is critical—sufficiently superior to the weight-bearing surface to avoid painful scarring, yet inferior enough to clear the neurovascular bundle. Not specified in source

4.2 Fluoroscopic Localization

  • Lateral Projection: True lateral view of the calcaneus; metallic instrument overlies the spur tip. Not specified in source
  • Axial "Magneto View": Beam parallel to the floor for assessing spur width and medially/laterally projecting prominences. Not specified in source
  • Paper Clip Technique: Metallic marker taped over the proposed incision site with static lateral X-ray for pre-operative planning when live fluoroscopy is limited. Not specified in source

4.3 Endoscopic Verification

The "Plane Finder" blunt obturator is used to palpate the medial aspect of the fascia. The surgeon feels for a "drop-off" as the instrument passes over the medial edge, confirming correct plane positioning without radiation. Endoscopically, the plantar fascia appears as a white, fibrous band with underlying flexor digitorum brevis appearing reddish-brown—the transition zone indicates the depth of release. 5. Partial vs. Complete Release: The 50% Rule

5.1 Biomechanical Consequences

Finite element analysis and clinical studies consistently demonstrate that releasing the plantar fascia increases strain on the lateral column and remaining plantar ligaments. Complete sectioning leads to loss of the windlass locking mechanism, resulting in hypermobility at the calcaneocuboid and talonavicular joints—the precursor to Lateral Column Instability (LCI). III

5.2 The Evidence-Based 50% Threshold

🔒 Critical Threshold

A study analyzing 47 patients found that those who developed lateral column pain had a mean release of 60.6% of the fascia, whereas those without pain had a mean release of 48.7% (P = .019). II

Consensus recommendation: Release a maximum of 50% of the plantar fascia width.

5.3 Anatomical Target & Intraoperative Control

  • Focus release exclusively on the medial (central) band.
  • The lateral band must be preserved intact as a "check rein" against arch collapse.
  • Many endoscopic systems feature slotted cannulas with calibrated markings (double band = start of medial fascia; single band = limit of intended cut). Not specified in source
  • Stepwise release: medial-to-lateral; halt upon visualizing lateral band fibers or reaching the pre-measured distance.

6. Calcaneal Spur: Resection, Evidence & Techniques

6.1 Etiology Debate

Calcaneal spurs are present in 50–75% of plantar fasciitis patients, yet their role in pain generation remains debated. The vertical stress theory—supported by histological evidence showing spur location dorsal to the fascia within the FDB origin with vertically oriented trabeculae—challenges the necessity of fascial release to address the spur. III

6.2 Spur Resection Without Fascial Release

✦ Key Finding: Endoscopic Calcaneal Spur Resection (CSR) Without Plantar Fascia Release (PFR)

A 2022 retrospective study of 47 patients demonstrated:

  • VAS scores: 79.6 ± 12.9 mm → 5.3 ± 7.3 mm postoperatively
  • JSSF score: 54.0 → 97.5
  • Return to full weight-bearing: Average 4.4 days (significantly faster than release protocols)

This suggests that in patients with large spurs (>2 mm) and focal tenderness, the spur itself acts as a mechanical pressure source ("stone in the shoe"), and removing it relieves pain without compromising the windlass mechanism. III

6.3 Combined Release and Resection

Combined procedures yield good long-term results but are associated with higher complication rates (up to 20%), including flatfoot, scar pain, and nerve injury. Not specified in source

Recommendation: Spur resection should be reserved for cases with radiographic evidence of a significant spur (>2 mm) correlating with focal palpable tenderness, or used as a standalone procedure where arch stability preservation is prioritized. III

7.2 Mandatory Safety Maneuvers

  1. Blunt Dissection Only: Use a mosquito hemostat to "push and spread" subcutaneous tissue to the fascia. Never plunge with a scalpel or sharp trocar. This allows nerves to slide out of the instrument's path. Not specified in source
  2. Instrument Stability: Once the cannula is inserted, avoid excessive levering or rotation. The cannula acts as a shield, but aggressive manipulation can compress nerves against the rigid calcaneus. Not specified in source
  3. Deep Plane Visualization: For suspected Baxter's neuritis, a specific release of the deep abductor fascia (neurolysis) can be performed under direct endoscopic vision. Not specified in source
  4. Adherence to the 17 mm Rule: Positions the portal superior to the nerve's course within the "safe zone." Not specified in source

8.2 Managing Lateral Column Instability (LCI)

If a patient develops lateral foot pain postoperatively:

  • Diagnosis: Clinical tenderness at the calcaneocuboid joint or cuboid.
  • Conservative Treatment: Custom orthotic with lateral forefoot post or "balance pad"; return to CAM boot for 4–6 weeks. Not specified in source
  • Surgical (rare): Lateral column stabilization (calcaneocuboid fusion) or lateral ligament reconstruction. II

10. Summary of Key Recommendations

✦ Evidence-Based Practice Guidelines
  • Technique: The Medial Dual-Portal approach is the modern recommendation for efficiency and safety. II
  • Landmarks: Utilize the Stryker 17 mm dorsal / 1–3 mm distal rule or fluoroscopic confirmation. Not specified in source
  • Release Extent: Strictly limit to the medial 33–50% to prevent lateral column instability. II
  • Spur Management: Resect only if large (>2 mm) and focal. Consider standalone spur resection when spur is the primary pressure generator. III
  • Safety: Maintain a 10 mm safety margin to Baxter's nerve; blunt dissection exclusively for portal creation. III
