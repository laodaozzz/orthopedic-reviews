1. Evolution of Templating Modalities: From Acetate to Artificial Intelligence

The architectural foundation of successful adult joint reconstruction lies not merely in surgical execution, but in the meticulous, systematic precision of preoperative planning. As THA and TKA undergo a philosophical transition—from uniform mechanical targets toward personalized, kinematically and functionally driven paradigms—the requisite depth of preoperative templating has expanded exponentially. Mastering component sizing, alignment philosophy, spinopelvic kinematics, and biomechanical restoration is now a mandatory prerequisite for clinical excellence and the mitigation of catastrophic failure.

Historically, the gold standard involved manual superimposition of two-dimensional (2D) acetate templates over printed analogue radiographs. The primary limitation was the fixed magnification factor of the acetate cutouts, which often failed to correspond with the variable magnification inherent to plain radiography—typically ranging from 110% to 120% depending on tube-patient-film distance. The ubiquitous adoption of PACS has subsequently established 2D digital templating and, more recently, 3D CT-based templating as contemporary standards of care.

1.1 Accuracy & Inter-Observer Reliability of Digital Software

Digital templating platforms (e.g., TraumaCad, OrthoView) allow surgeons to dynamically scale digital templates to the exact radiographic magnification of the patient.[1] This scaling necessitates the use of a radiopaque calibration marker—typically a 25 mm metallic sphere—positioned at the exact coronal plane of the pathology (between the patient's legs at the greater trochanter level for THA).

🔑 Key Finding

Digital templating software is as accurate, if not superior, to traditional acetate templating across all measured parameters.[2]

Table 1: Comparative Accuracy of Templating Modalities
Modality Type THA Cup (±1 Size) THA Stem (±1 Size) TKA (±1 Size) Primary Limitation
Traditional Acetate 2D Manual 67–90% 80–82% 91% Fixed magnification; no dynamic scaling
TraumaCad / OrthoView 2D Digital 88–91% 85–94% 93–95% Vulnerable to positioning & marker errors
3D CT / Robotic 3D Digital 96–100% 96–100% 96–100% High cost, radiation, planning time

Level II Level III Diagnostic studies and retrospective cohorts consistently support 2D digital templating over acetate, noting equivalent or superior accuracy, higher reproducibility, and superior workflow efficiency.[2]

Inter- and Intra-observer Reliability

Digital templating demonstrates "substantial" to "almost perfect" agreement. Intraclass correlation coefficients (ICC) routinely exceed 0.84 for acetabular cup and femoral stem sizing, validating reproducibility across surgeons of varying experience.[3]

1.2 The Ascendancy of 3D CT & Artificial Intelligence

While 2D digital templating is robust, it remains highly vulnerable to projectional artifacts from positioning errors (anterior/posterior pelvic tilt, external rotation contractures). The contemporary evolution involves 3D CT-based templating and AI integration.

📊 3D CT Superiority Data

In robot-assisted TKA, 3D templating predicts exact component sizes in up to 93.1% of cases, compared to just 28.7% for 2D planning.[10] For THA, AI-assisted 3D models yield an odds ratio of 3.85 for predicting exact cup size and 3.28 for exact femoral stem size.[12]

Level III Systematic reviews and meta-analyses strongly support 3D CT templating and AI-assisted planning as the most accurate modalities, significantly reducing operative outliers.[10]

2. Total Hip Arthroplasty (THA) Templating Mechanics

The fundamental objective of THA templating is the precise, patient-specific restoration of hip biomechanics—specifically targeting the Center of Rotation (COR), Femoral Offset (FO), and Leg Length Discrepancy (LLD)—while achieving stable, durable osseointegration within the confines of the patient's proximal femoral geometry.

2.1 Dorr Classification of Proximal Femoral Geometry

Successful biologic integration of a cementless femoral stem depends heavily on matching implant design to the patient's specific endosteal morphology. The Dorr classification categorizes proximal femoral bone quality based on cortical thickness and the canal flare index.[14] The classification is determined by the ratio of the medullary canal diameter 10 cm distal to the lesser trochanter's midportion divided by the inner canal diameter at the midportion.[15]

Table 2: Dorr Classification of Proximal Femoral Geometry
Type Canal/Calcar Ratio Morphology Biomechanical Implications Fixation
A (Champagne Flute) < 0.50 Thick cortices; narrow diaphysis, wide metaphysis High risk of calcar fracture during broaching Uncemented (tapered wedge)
B (Normal) 0.50–0.75 Mild cortical thinning; moderate diaphyseal widening Excellent for standard uncemented fixation Uncemented (standard)
C (Stovepipe) > 0.75 Severe cortical thinning; wide, indistinct canal High risk of subsidence & periprosthetic fracture Cemented (polished taper slip)
⚠️ Clinical Pearl

Identifying the Dorr type dictates the fundamental choice of stem design. A standard fully porous-coated cylindrical stem templated to fill the metaphysis of a Type A femur will inevitably fracture the narrow diaphysis. Conversely, a cementless tapered wedge stem in a Type C "stovepipe" femur carries significantly higher risk of postoperative subsidence. Cemented fixation remains the gold standard for Type C morphology.[19]

Level III Level IV Stem subsidence and periprosthetic fracture rates are significantly higher in Dorr Type B and C femurs treated with uncemented stems.[19]

2.2 Algorithms for Restoring COR, Femoral Offset & Leg Length Discrepancy

A failure to optimize these parameters results in instability, abductor fatigue, altered gait, and early polyethylene wear. Restoration involves a highly systematic triangulation of the acetabulum and femur.

Correcting Leg Length Discrepancy (LLD)

LLD is assessed by drawing a horizontal reference line connecting the inferior aspects of the bilateral ischial tuberosities (or the inferior margins of the radiographic teardrops). Vertical perpendicular lines are dropped to the most prominent superior aspect of the lesser trochanters bilaterally.[22] The difference quantifies radiographic LLD, which must be correlated with clinical examination to exclude apparent LLD from fixed flexion or adduction contractures.

Establishing the Center of Rotation (COR)

The native COR is identified by referencing the unaffected contralateral hip. The templated acetabular COR is established by placing the cup template at approximately 40° to 45° of radiographic inclination.[22] The medial border must approximate Kohler's line and sit adjacent to the radiographic teardrop, while the inferior border rests at the obturator foramen level.

🎯 Biomechanical Rationale for Medialization

Anatomical medialization of the cup to the true acetabular floor decreases joint reactive forces by shortening the body-weight lever arm, significantly reducing the force required by the hip abductor musculature to maintain a level pelvis during single-leg stance.[22]

Restoring Femoral Offset (FO)

Femoral offset is the perpendicular distance from the true COR of the femoral head to the central longitudinal axis of the femoral shaft. Failure to restore adequate FO results in abductor laxity, Trendelenburg gait, increased joint reactive forces, and higher propensity for impingement-driven dislocation.[23]

FO is modulated by selecting high-offset stem options (altering the neck-shaft angle, e.g., from standard 132° to high-offset 127°) or by utilizing longer modular femoral heads. However, increasing head length simultaneously increases leg length, necessitating careful geometric balance.

Level III Anatomic restoration of COR and FO to within 5 mm of native anatomy significantly reduces polyethylene wear rates and improves postoperative Harris Hip Scores.[26]

2.3 The Lewinnek Safe Zone & Spinopelvic Kinematics

In 1978, Lewinnek proposed a universal "safe zone" for acetabular cup placement: 40° ± 10° of radiographic inclination and 15° ± 10° of anteversion, suggesting this minimized dislocation risk.

🚫 The Lewinnek Safe Zone Is Obsolete as a Universal Target

Extensive modern analyses demonstrate that the vast majority of contemporary dislocating THAs (58% to 86%) occur in patients whose cups were placed perfectly within the Lewinnek safe zone.[29] The safe zone's primary failing is its static nature—it assumes the pelvis is a rigidly fixed osseous block.

Dynamic Spinopelvic Kinematics

During the transition from standing to sitting, the normal pelvis tilts posteriorly by 10° to 20°. This posterior tilt functionally opens the acetabulum, increasing functional anteversion and inclination to accommodate extreme femoral flexion and prevent anterior impingement.

Patients with spinal stiffness (advanced lumbar spondylosis, prior multi-level lumbar fusions, or severe PI-LL mismatch) lack the ability to recruit compensatory posterior pelvic tilt. If a surgeon places a cup at 15° anteversion in such a patient, the cup will not open upon sitting, leading to anterior impingement and posterior dislocation.[32]

✅ Contemporary Recommendation

Surgeons must abandon one-size-fits-all metrics and adopt a "Functional Safe Zone", customized via 3D planning to account for the patient's Combined Sagittal Index (CSI) and specific spinopelvic mobility.[29]

Level III Dynamic biomechanical studies consistently demonstrate the Lewinnek safe zone does not reliably prevent dislocation. High-level evidence mandates personalized, spinopelvic-adjusted cup orientation.[29]

2.4 Actionable THA Templating Workflows

2D Digital THA Planning Workflow

1
Image CalibrationEnsure the AP pelvis radiograph is calibrated using a 25 mm radiopaque marker at the greater trochanter level to account for magnification.[1]
2
Establish LLD BaselineDraw the inter-teardrop or inter-tuberosity horizontal line. Measure perpendicular drops to the lesser trochanters bilaterally to quantify preoperative radiographic LLD.[22]
3
Acetabular Sizing & PositioningPosition cup template at 40°–45° inclination. Medialize to the teardrop lateral border. Set inferior border at the inferior teardrop level. Mark the newly established COR.[22]
4
Femoral Stem Sizing & FixationAssess Dorr classification (A/B/C) to guide cemented vs. cementless stem selection. Overlay femoral template, filling metaphyseal-diaphyseal junction per implant philosophy.
5
Neck Resection & Biomechanical RestorationAlign femoral head center with acetabular COR. Adjust offset options (standard vs. high-offset) to restore FO without altering LLD. Determine neck resection level from the lesser trochanter.[22]

3D CT & Robotic THA Planning Workflow

1
Spinopelvic Kinematic AssessmentObtain sitting and standing lateral spinopelvic radiographs to calculate pelvic tilt change, assess lumbar flexibility, and determine PI-LL mismatch.
2
3D Segmentation & Coordinate FramingSegment hemipelvis and proximal femur from CT. Establish the Anterior Pelvic Plane (APP) as the spatial coordinate reference frame.[28]
3
Virtual Acetabular ImplantationPosition virtual cup accounting for native version and reaming depth. Apply functional adjustments (e.g., increasing anteversion to 25° for stiff-spine patients).
4
Virtual Femoral ImplantationMaximize endosteal contact (Canal Fill Ratio). Select modular neck options to optimize combined anteversion (target: 25°–40°).
5
Dynamic Impingement SimulationRun automated virtual ROM simulation to detect bone-on-bone or component-on-component impingement in deep flexion and internal rotation.

3. Total Knee Arthroplasty (TKA) Templating & Alignment Philosophy

While THA templating focuses on spatial coordinate restoration and volumetric sizing, TKA templating addresses angular alignment, soft-tissue gap balancing, and the restoration of dynamic, multi-axial kinematics. The orthopedic community is currently undergoing a profound philosophical schism, shifting away from traditional Mechanical Alignment (MA) toward customized Kinematic Alignment (KA) and Functional Alignment (FA).

3.1 Mechanical Alignment vs. Kinematic Alignment vs. Functional Alignment

Mechanical Alignment (MA)

For over three decades, MA served as the unquestioned gold standard. MA dictates the final Hip-Knee-Ankle (HKA) mechanical axis must be perfectly neutral (0° ± 3°). Distal femoral and proximal tibial resections are executed strictly perpendicular (90°) to their respective mechanical axes.[35] The femoral component is set at 5°–7° valgus relative to the anatomical axis, and the tibial component at 0° to the mechanical axis. Because this creates a rigid, non-anatomic construct, the surrounding soft tissues must frequently be released or "pie-crusted" to equalize gaps.

⚠️ Biomechanical Flaw of MA

3D anatomic mapping reveals that only approximately 2% to 15% of the native, non-arthritic population possesses a perfectly neutral 180-degree HKA axis.[36] Thus, MA structurally alters the native anatomy and ligamentous tension of ~85% of patients. This is hypothesized as a primary reason why up to 20% of patients remain chronically dissatisfied post-TKA.[36]

Kinematic Alignment (KA)

KA is a modern "ligament-sparing," joint-resurfacing philosophy that abandons the pursuit of a neutral mechanical axis. KA aims to precisely restore the patient's pre-arthritic constitutional limb alignment, joint line obliquity, and native knee kinematics.[35] The femoral component is aligned co-linearly to the cylindrical axis of the posterior femoral condyles—the true flexion-extension axis. Bone resections equal the exact implant thickness, and native joint line obliquity is restored, inherently balancing ligamentous tension.

Restricted KA (rKA) & Functional Alignment (FA)

rKA establishes safe boundaries: native joint line is restored unless anatomy falls outside predetermined safe zones (e.g., tibial cut max 5° varus, overall HKA within ± 3° of neutral).[40] FA represents the ultimate hybrid: 3D robotic planning captures native anatomy (like KA) but prioritizes real-time soft-tissue gap tensioning, allowing millimetric bone resection adjustments to achieve perfect collateral ligament tension without releases.[41]

Strategy Femoral Resection Tibial Resection Soft-Tissue Strategy Limb Alignment Goal
Mechanical (MA) Perpendicular to mechanical axis Perpendicular to mechanical axis Mandatory releases to balance gaps Rigid Neutral HKA (0°)
Kinematic (KA) Match pre-arthritic anatomy Match native joint line obliquity Ligament sparing (no releases) Constitutional patient alignment
Functional (FA) Robotic adjustment for tension Robotic adjustment for tension Tension-guided; minimal releases Phenotype-specific, tension-balanced
✅ Outcomes Summary

Level I Systematic reviews and meta-analyses of RCTs demonstrate that KA and rKA yield comparable or slightly superior short-to-mid-term functional outcomes (Oxford Knee Score, KSS, WOMAC) without increasing revision rates compared to MA.[42,43]

3.2 Critical Analysis of Transverse Plane Landmarks for Femoral Rotation

Setting proper rotational alignment of the femoral component is paramount for TKA success. Malrotation induces patellofemoral maltracking, anterior knee pain, flexion instability, and premature asymmetric wear. Three primary bony landmarks serve as surrogates for the flexion-extension axis:

1. Surgical Transepicondylar Axis (sTEA)

The sTEA connects the central sulcus of the medial epicondyle to the most prominent point of the lateral epicondyle. It is widely considered the "gold standard" because it most closely approximates the true flexion-extension axis.[48]

The Flaw: The medial sulcus is frequently obscured by soft tissue or scar, and is completely absent in up to 50% of severely osteoarthritic knees, leading to substantial inter-observer error.[49]

2. Posterior Condylar Axis (PCA)

The PCA connects the posterior-most points of the medial and lateral condyles. The classic "PCA + 3° ER" rule attempts to approximate the sTEA. However, a CT study of 2,128 femora revealed immense intersubject variability: the PCA is significantly more internally rotated in females (2.64°) vs. males (2.18°), and highly unpredictable in valgus knees.[50]

3. Whiteside's Line (AP Axis)

Whiteside's line runs from the deepest part of the trochlear groove to the apex of the intercondylar notch. However, its relationship to the sTEA is highly variable (mean error 1.90° ± 1.38°), and it is vulnerable to distortion in trochlear dysplasia or severe patellofemoral arthritis.[50]

🔑 Evidence-Based Recommendation

Because no single transverse landmark guarantees perfect rotation due to vast anatomic heterogeneity, the most robust algorithm dictates: (1) use 3D CT preoperative templating to identify the sTEA; (2) intraoperatively cross-reference multiple axes—PCA adjusted for patient-specific angles, validated against Whiteside's line; (3) ultimately confirm via dynamic soft-tissue gap balancing.[51]

Level II Level III Large-scale CT analyses conclude the sTEA is the true kinematic target, but the universal "PCA + 3°" rule is morphologically flawed. Patient-specific rotational adjustments are mandated.[50]

3.3 Actionable TKA Templating Workflows

2D Digital TKA Planning Workflow

Level III 2D TKA templating predicts component size accurately in ~50–80% of cases, heavily influenced by magnification variability.[54]

1
Mechanical Axis IdentificationOn a full-length weight-bearing AP radiograph, draw the femoral mechanical axis (femoral head center → knee center) and tibial mechanical axis (ankle talus → knee center). Measure overall HKA varus/valgus deformity.[35]
2
Femoral Sizing & ResectionSuperimpose femoral template on lateral radiograph. Size to match AP dimension, preventing anterior cortical notching or posterior overstuffing. Set distal femoral valgus angle (usually 5°–7°).[35]
3
Tibial Sizing & ResectionSuperimpose tibial template on AP view for maximal cortical coverage without overhang. Plan proximal cut perpendicular to mechanical axis (for MA). Plan posterior tibial slope on lateral view (e.g., 3°–5° for CR designs).[35]

3D KA Robotic Planning Workflow

Level II Level III 3D CT robotic planning for KA achieves 96–100% sizing accuracy and significantly reduces angular outliers.[9]

1
Image SegmentationObtain CT from hip to ankle. Robotic software generates 3D bone model, auto-identifying hip center, knee center, ankle center, and the sTEA.[40]
2
Cartilage Wear EstimationInput virtual cartilage thickness (typically 2 mm) onto bare, sclerotic bone identified on weight-bearing radiographs. Bone resections must equal implant thickness minus cartilage wear.
3
Resection TemplatingAlign virtual femoral component co-linearly to the cylindrical axis of the posterior condyles. Tibial cut mimics native joint line obliquity and posterior slope.[39]
4
Boundary Assessment (rKA)If native anatomy is extreme, implement restricted KA: adjust plan to ensure final HKA does not exceed ± 3° of neutral, protecting against excessive shear stress.[40]
5
Intraoperative Dynamic BalancingRobotic system uses optical tracking and gap assessment. Full ROM evaluated; millimetric adjustments ensure perfect kinematic balance before any saw engages bone.[55]
