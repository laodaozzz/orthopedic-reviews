Periprosthetic Fractures: Comprehensive Clinical Review

Surgical Strategies, Clinical Pearls & Avoidance of Pitfalls — Evidence-Based Interactive Summary

1. The Growing Challenge of Periprosthetic Fractures

Periprosthetic fractures (PPFs) represent one of the most formidable challenges in modern orthopedic surgery. Their incidence has escalated dramatically due to increasing volumes of primary and revision total joint arthroplasty (TJA), expanding indications in younger patients, and extended longevity of aging populations living with prosthetic joints. LOE I–II [1, 3, 5]

1.1 Epidemiology & Incidence

  • Periprosthetic knee fractures: estimated ~300,000 events/year in the US [5]
  • Risk after primary TKA: 0.3%–2.5%; after revision TKA: up to 38% [5]
  • Risk after revision THA: 4.4× higher than after primary THA LOE II [4]
  • 2016–2021 trends: surgically treated periprosthetic hip fractures rose by 38%, knee fractures by 73% [1]
  • Projection: total PPFs expected to increase by 212% between 2016 and 2032 [1]
  • One-year mortality for femoral PPFs: ~11%, substantially higher than elective arthroplasty LOE II [2]

1.3 Implant- & Surgery-Related Risk Factors

  • Stress shielding: rigid intramedullary stems bypass proximal bone, causing adaptive resorption and cortical thinning [13]
  • Osteolysis: inflammatory response to wear particles → cavitary defects, compromised bone-implant interface [5]
  • Stress riser at stem tip: transition from rigid implant to flexible bone creates focal high-stress zone [13]
  • Anterior femoral cortical notch (TKA): reduces torsional strength by up to 30%, increasing supracondylar fracture risk LOE III [17]
  • Revision arthroplasty: one of the most significant risk factors; higher intraoperative fracture risk and more vulnerable postoperative bone [4]

2. Preoperative Evaluation & Strategic Planning

Successful PPF management is predicated on meticulous preoperative evaluation. A significant proportion of "fixation failures" are in reality diagnostic failures — most commonly, failure to recognize a loose implant or an occult periprosthetic joint infection (PJI) before surgery. LOE II [4, 20]

2.1 Systematic Patient Assessment

  • History: Inquire about pre-injury pain (start-up thigh pain → femoral loosening; groin pain → acetabular loosening), progressive functional decline, instability, and atraumatic fracture (suspicion for loosening/infection) [4, 21]
  • Physical examination: neurovascular status, soft-tissue envelope, previous incisions [9]
  • Medical comorbidities: anticoagulants, immunosuppressants, pre-injury ambulatory status [9]

2.2 Diagnostic Imaging

  • Standard protocol: Scaled AP pelvis + full-length AP & lateral of the entire bone housing the prosthesis — non-negotiable [9]
  • Comparison with prior radiographs: most sensitive method for detecting loosening signs (subsidence, alignment change, radiolucent lines) [20]
  • Limitation: up to 20% of loose femoral stems are not identified on plain radiographs alone [21]
  • CT: invaluable for complex fracture patterns, bone loss quantification, bone-implant interface assessment [13]
  • Acetabular fractures: require CT + Judet views (iliac & obturator oblique) [9]
🔑 Clinical Pearl: The Crucial Triumvirate Every decision in PPF management hinges on three interrelated factors: (1) fracture pattern/location, (2) implant stability, and (3) bone quality. A displaced fracture around a well-fixed implant in good bone = trauma problem (ORIF). A fracture around a loose implant = arthroplasty problem (revision). [20]

2.3 Excluding Periprosthetic Joint Infection (PJI)

A high index of suspicion for PJI must be maintained in all cases, particularly with history of prolonged wound drainage, late-onset pain, or atraumatic fracture. LOE II [4]

  • ESR/CRP: limited utility in acute fracture — false-positive rates of 43% (CRP) and 31% (ESR) [9]
  • Definitive step: joint aspiration prior to surgery → cell count with differential, gram stain, aerobic & anaerobic cultures [20]
  • Alpha-defensin assay: can further improve diagnostic accuracy [20]
  • If infection confirmed → two-stage protocol: implant removal, antibiotic spacer, systemic antibiotics, delayed reimplantation [9]
⚠️ Pitfall: Missed Infection Performing single-stage ORIF or revision in the setting of unrecognized PJI is doomed to failure and subjects the patient to significant morbidity. Always rule out infection before definitive surgery. [9, 20]

3. Management of Periprosthetic Hip Fractures

Management is guided almost universally by the Vancouver classification, which is widely adopted due to its simplicity and strong correlation with treatment strategies and clinical outcomes. LOE I–II [25, 27]

3.1 The Vancouver Classification

Table 1: Vancouver Classification & Treatment Algorithm for Periprosthetic Hip Fractures
TypeDescriptionPrimary Treatment
AG / AL Greater or lesser trochanter fracture Nonoperative if <2 cm displacement; ORIF if >2 cm displaced (restore abductors)
B1 Fracture around a well-fixed, stable stem ORIF with locking plate + cerclage cables/wires; stem retained
B2 Fracture around a loose stem; adequate proximal bone stock Revision arthroplasty: long diaphyseal-engaging revision stem bypassing fracture
B3 Fracture around a loose stem; severe proximal bone loss Revision with augmentation: long stem + structural allograft / impaction grafting; ± proximal femoral replacement
C Fracture well distal to stem tip ORIF: long plate overlapping existing stem tip by ≥2 cortical diameters

Source: Duncan & Masri, as cited in [26, 27]

3.2 Stable Femoral Component: Vancouver B1 & C

When the femoral component is stable, the goal is anatomical reduction and durable fixation while preserving the well-integrated implant. LOE II [16]

⚠️ Critical Decision Point: The B1 Dilemma ORIF for supposedly "stable" B1 fractures has, in some series, a higher failure rate than revision for "loose" B2 fractures. This strongly suggests many B1 fractures are misclassified, with an unrecognized loose stem. A surgeon must treat the patient's history and clinical context, not just the radiograph. If pre-injury thigh pain is present, be prepared to convert to long-stem revision with all equipment available. [21]

Principles of ORIF

  • Locking compression plates: angular stability in osteoporotic bone; allow unicortical screws proximally around the stem [25]
  • Cerclage cables/wires: indispensable for spiral/comminuted fractures; apply and tension cables for provisional reduction BEFORE inserting screws [22, 30]
  • Plate length: must extend well distal to fracture AND overlap stem tip proximally by ≥2 cortical diameters to avoid new stress riser [9]
  • Screw strategy: Unicortical locking screws proximally (avoiding stem); bicortical screws distally [25]

3.3 Loose Femoral Component: Vancouver B2 & B3

Loose stem diagnosis fundamentally changes treatment from fracture fixation to implant revision. The loose stem is the pathology; the fracture is its sequela. LOE II [25]

  • Revision stem: long, cementless, tapered, fluted titanium stem — achieves press-fit diaphyseal fixation with rotational stability [25]
  • Length: must extend past the most distal fracture line by ≥2 femoral shaft diameters (~7 cm) [22]
  • Trend: growing preference for non-modular (monoblock) stems over modular designs due to concerns about fretting, corrosion, and fatigue failure at modular junctions [25]
  • B3 management: cortical strut allografts, impaction grafting, or proximal femoral replacement for extreme bone loss [26, 29]

3.4 Acetabular & Trochanteric Fractures

Trochanteric (Type A): Undisplaced (<2 cm) → nonoperative with protected weight-bearing. Displaced (>2 cm) greater trochanter → ORIF with claw plate/tension-band wiring to restore abductor mechanism. [26]

Acetabular fractures: Far less common but exceptionally complex. Stable cup → protected weight-bearing ± supplemental screws. Unstable cup/displaced fracture → revision surgery with ORIF + revision acetabular component; guided by Paprosky/AAOS classification for bone defects. [22, 23]

4. Management of Periprosthetic Knee Fractures

Supracondylar femur fractures are most common (0.3%–2.5% after primary TKA, up to 38% after revision). Management is guided by the Rorabeck & Taylor classification. LOE II [5, 7]

4.1 Classification (Rorabeck & Taylor)

Table 2: Rorabeck & Taylor Classification & Treatment Algorithm
TypeDescriptionPrimary Treatment
Type I Undisplaced fracture, stable prosthesis Nonoperative (rarely — high risk of secondary displacement); reserved for medically frail patients
Type II Displaced (>5 mm or >5° angulation), stable prosthesis ORIF with locking plate OR retrograde IMN (depends on TKA design)
Type III Any fracture with a loose prosthesis Revision TKA: long-stemmed femoral component; constrained (hinged) if ligamentous instability or bone loss

🔑 Clinical Pearl: Know the Implant The surgeon who implanted a "closed-box" TKA component effectively eliminates retrograde nailing as a future fixation option. Expert arthroplasty practice involves "future-proofing" the joint against potential complications — this is especially relevant in osteoporotic patients at high risk for PPF. [6]

4.3 Loose Prosthesis: Rorabeck Type III

Mandates revision arthroplasty. A long-stemmed femoral component bypasses the fracture. If collateral ligaments are compromised or bone loss is massive → constrained condylar knee (CCK) or rotating-hinge prosthesis. For unreconstructible bone loss → distal femoral replacement (megaprosthesis) or allograft-prosthetic composite (APC). LOE III–IV [17, 18, 33]

4.4 Tibial & Patellar Fractures

Less common. Tibial fractures classified by Felix system (Type I–IV by anatomic location); often associated with loose components and malalignment [17]. Patellar fractures classified by Goldberg system based on implant stability, displacement, and extensor mechanism integrity [18].

5. Management of Periprosthetic Shoulder Fractures

Less common than hip/knee PPFs but rising in incidence. Presents unique challenges due to rotator cuff/deltoid importance and distinct biomechanics of anatomic TSA vs. reverse RSA. LOE III [3, 12]

5.1 Classification

  • Wright & Cofield: Type A (centered near stem tip, extends proximally), Type B (extends distally), Type C (entirely distal) [13]
  • Kirchhoff classification (modern): integrates prosthesis type (TSA vs. RSA), rotator cuff status, fracture location/pattern, and implant stability [12]
🔑 Critical Distinction: TSA vs. RSA Biomechanics RSA medializes/distalizes the center of rotation, increasing deltoid tension and placing new stresses on the acromion and scapular spine. Consequently, RSA is associated with unique stress fractures of the acromion and scapular spine rarely seen after anatomic TSA. A surgeon evaluating post-RSA pain must maintain high suspicion for these scapular injuries. [38, 40]

5.2 Surgical Strategies

Conservative management with coaptation splint → functional brace may be considered for medically unfit patients with stable, minimally displaced fractures. However, high risk of nonunion, malunion, and prolonged stiffness due to intramedullary stem disrupting endosteal blood supply. Generally reserved for patients where surgical risks are prohibitive. [13]

ORIF with long plate + screws + cerclage cables:

  • Key pearl: Place the plate eccentrically (anterior or posterior) on the humeral shaft — this creates a trajectory allowing locking screws to pass on either side of the stem, achieving bicortical purchase for a much stronger construct [13]
  • Augmentation: cortical strut allograft on opposite cortex + cerclage cables for poor bone quality or significant comminution [13]

Revision arthroplasty for any fracture with a loose implant:

  • Long-stemmed revision prosthesis bypassing most distal fracture extent by ≥2 cortical diameters [13]
  • Poor bone stock: long-stemmed prosthesis + APC for proximal bone reconstruction [13]
  • Good bone stock: conversion to a shorter stem (tip proximal to fracture) can be advantageous — removes the stress riser and facilitates standard ORIF [13]

6. Advanced Techniques, Pearls & Pitfalls

Successful PPF management requires the surgeon to operate as a "hybrid" specialist, seamlessly integrating trauma principles (fracture healing biology, mechanical stability) with arthroplasty principles (bone stock management, implant design, complex revisions). LOE II [4]

6.1 The Role of Structural Allograft

  • Indications: severe segmental/cavitary bone loss (Vancouver B3), poor bone quality, part of allograft-prosthetic composite (APC) [34]
  • Cortical onlay strut grafts: contour to host femur; place anteriorly/medially for maximal mechanical benefit; secure with cerclage cables [25]
  • "Sandwiched" strut technique: dual struts (anterior + posterior) with cable fixation to compress native bone against existing stem — alternative to high-risk long-stem revision in low-demand patients [43]
  • APC technique: resect compromised bone → size-matched allograft segment → cement revision component into allograft → implant composite with plate/screws/cables; restores bone stock and provides soft-tissue attachment points [34]
  • Risks: increased deep infection rates, longer time to union — use cautiously and selectively [41]

6.2 Curated Surgical Pearls

🔑 General Principle: Bypass the Problem Any fixation plate or revision stem must bypass the fracture and/or the tip of an existing implant by at least two cortical diameters. Failure to do so is the most common cause of iatrogenic subsequent fracture. [9]
  • Respect biology: Use minimally invasive plating techniques (MIPO/LISS) to preserve periosteal blood supply — critical when an endosteal implant has already compromised medullary flow [17]
  • Reduction first: Always use cerclage cables to achieve reduction BEFORE inserting screws; prevents loss of reduction when cables are tensioned [22]
  • Hybrid fixation: Use plates accepting both locking and non-locking screws; unicortical locking proximally, bicortical distally [30]
  • Shoulder bicortical technique: Place plate eccentrically → angle screws to miss the stem → achieve bicortical purchase [13]
  • Shoulder stem nuance: For good proximal bone stock, convert to a shorter stem to remove the stress riser from the fracture site and facilitate easier ORIF [13]

6.3 Common Pitfalls to Avoid

⚠️ Pitfall 1: The "B1 in Disguise" Misdiagnosing a loose stem as stable and proceeding with ORIF. Over-reliance on radiographs and failure to heed clinical history (pre-injury pain) inevitably leads to nonunion and fixation failure. [4]
⚠️ Pitfall 2: Missed Infection Failing to rule out low-grade PJI leads to certain failure and commits the patient to multiple future surgeries. [9]
⚠️ Pitfall 3: Creating a Stress Riser Plate too short (ends near stem tip) or revision stem too short (ends near fracture line) → new fracture at the junction of rigid construct and unsupported bone. [9]
⚠️ Pitfall 4: Devascularization Overly aggressive open approaches with extensive periosteal stripping → avascular nonunion. Rationale for minimally invasive techniques. [19]
⚠️ Pitfall 5: Component Damage During retrograde nailing of knee PPFs, risk of scuffing or catastrophically damaging the polyethylene tibial insert. [6]

7. Postoperative Management & Complications

7.1 Rehabilitation Principles

The overarching goal is early patient mobilization. Prolonged bed rest in elderly patients leads to thromboembolic disease, pneumonia, pressure sores, and severe deconditioning. Surgical constructs should aim to tolerate immediate or early weight-bearing whenever possible. LOE II [20, 45]

  • Hip: Weight-bearing as tolerated with walker/crutches after stable ORIF or long-stem revision. Strict hip precautions for posterior approaches. Hip brace for several weeks. Full recovery: several months [45, 46, 47]
  • Knee: Initial brace immobilization → hinged brace for controlled ROM. Weight-bearing depends on fixation stability: some constructs allow early WB; complex/osteoporotic fractures may require 6+ weeks non-weight-bearing [17, 48]
  • Shoulder: Four-phase protocol:
    1. Weeks 1–3: Sling immobilization; gentle passive ROM only
    2. Weeks 4–6: Active-assisted ROM, gravity-eliminated
    3. Weeks 7–12: Full active ROM, gentle isometric strengthening
    4. Week 13+: Progressive strengthening, functional return
    [49]
