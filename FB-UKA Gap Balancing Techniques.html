1. Introduction to the Fixed-Bearing Paradigm in UKA

Unicompartmental knee arthroplasty (UKA) is a highly specialized, bone-preserving resurfacing procedure designed for isolated single-compartment osteoarthritis. Unlike total knee arthroplasty (TKA), UKA preserves the anterior and posterior cruciate ligaments and maintains native soft-tissue proprioception.II [1]

Within UKA, implant designs are bifurcated into mobile-bearing (MB) and fixed-bearing (FB) systems. Understanding the biomechanical distinctions between these two paradigms is fundamental to mastering FB-UKA surgical technique.

Mobile-Bearing vs. Fixed-Bearing: A Fundamental Distinction

Mobile-bearing systems (e.g., Oxford UKA) employ a highly congruent, round-on-round articulation that minimizes polyethylene wear by maximizing contact area. However, this design mandates exact symmetric balancing of the flexion and extension gap — if the flexion gap is even 1–2 mm looser than the extension gap, the dynamic distraction forces can cause the bearing to spin out or dislocate.III [3]

Conversely, fixed-bearing systems utilize a round-on-flat or slightly conforming biconcave geometry. Because the polyethylene insert is locked into the metal tibial baseplate, bearing dislocation is mechanically impossible.III [3] This imparts a profound advantage: the FB design inherently tolerates the knee's physiologic asymmetric laxity, including:

  • The screw-home mechanism (tibial internal rotation in terminal extension)
  • Physiologic internal rotation of the tibia during flexion
  • Natural femoral rollback during deep flexionII [7]

Long-term survivorship analyses and meta-analyses demonstrate that FB-UKA yields superior profiles in specific cohorts — particularly lateral compartment UKA (where native joint laxity in flexion is greater) and in complex patient populations where isolated aseptic loosening rather than bearing dislocation is the primary failure mode.II [9]

While the kinematic forgiveness of FB-UKA does not absolve the surgeon from precise gap balancing, the implant is highly sensitive to edge-loading, accelerated localized polyethylene wear, and aseptic loosening if the mechanical environment is compromised by excessive coronal malalignment or sagittal slope mismatch.III [13]

2. Flexion-Extension Gap Balancing in Fixed-Bearing Systems

2.1 The Absolute Mandate of Soft-Tissue Preservation

The foundational principle of UKA is that the procedure relies entirely on measured bone resection and calculated gap filling to restore limb alignment; soft-tissue releases are universally contraindicated.II [5]

In TKA, surgeons frequently utilize MCL pie-crusting or posterior capsular releases. Applying this philosophy to FB-UKA is a critical technical error. Releasing the deep or superficial MCL to correct varus deformity leads to:

  • Medial compartment overstuffing — a thicker poly insert fills the artificial space
  • Pathological tension transfer to the lateral compartment — causing lift-off, attenuation, and contralateral degenerationIII [17]
  • ACL fatigue and failure — altered collateral tension changes vector forces on the cruciate pivot

The non-negotiable goal of FB-UKA is to restore the natural tension of the retained ligaments, returning the knee to its pre-arthritic kinematic state through precise osteotomy alone. Zero soft-tissue releases.II [18]

2.2 Physiologic Asymmetric Laxity vs. Symmetric Gaps

A critical distinction exists in FB vs. MB gap philosophy. MB systems demand symmetrical flexion-extension gaps to trap the bearing. However, the native knee does not possess identical gaps. The knee exhibits physiologic laxity that varies dynamically throughout the range of motion:II [20]

  • In deep flexion, the lateral compartment demonstrates significantly greater laxity than the medial
  • The medial compartment remains relatively isometric from extension through mid-flexion

FB-UKA systems uniquely accommodate this. Therefore, surgeons must not create perfectly tight, symmetrical gaps, but rather deliberately under-correct and preserve a controlled degree of native laxity.

Table 2 — Gap Balancing Targets by Compartment
Parameter Medial FB-UKA Lateral FB-UKA
Target Laxity ~2 mm symmetric laxity in both extension and flexion gaps Intentionally asymmetric — flexion gap looser than extension
Coronal Alignment Goal Slight undercorrection: 2°–4° residual varus Restore native lateral alignment profile
Consequence of Overstuffing Edge-loading, contralateral degeneration, ACL fatigue Restricted femoral rollback, lateral patellofemoral impingement
Key Risk of Overcorrection Valgus overcorrection → lateral compartment disease progression Tight lateral flexion → impingement

Medial FB-UKA: Achieve slight undercorrection (2°–4° varus) with ≥2 mm symmetric laxity in both gaps to prevent overstuffing and edge-loading.II [1]

Lateral FB-UKA: Perfectly balanced gaps are pathological. The native lateral compartment's normal disparity must be recreated by leaving the lateral flexion gap physiologically looser than the extension gap.II [20]

2.3 Intraoperative Assessment Techniques

Mechanical Spacer Blocks and Tensors

Following proximal tibial resection, gap checkers of incremental thickness are introduced into the joint space. Critical technical nuances include:

  • Extension gap measured at 5°–10° of flexion (not true 0° terminal extension). Measuring at terminal extension engages the intact posterior capsule, artificially tightening the space and leading to underestimation — which causes erroneous distal femoral resection and mid-flexion instability.II [1]
  • Tactile confirmation: Thicker checker = impossible to force; exact-match = mild resistance; thinner = visible toggle/lift-off under stress

Robotic-Arm Assisted & Sensor-Guided Dynamic Balancing

Modern robotic protocols utilize dynamic virtual gap balancing. After mapping the patient's anatomy, the surgeon takes the knee through continuous range of motion while the robotic software live-models predicted flexion and extension gaps before any bone cut.II [25]

Real-time intraoperative pressure sensors provide objective, quantifiable load data in Newtons (N) across the compartment.

Sensor-based data: Experienced surgeons consistently achieve an optimal mean difference between flexion and extension contact forces of ~83 N. Novice surgeons utilizing manual techniques frequently leave mismatches exceeding 200–300 N, correlating with early pain, stiffness, and delayed rehabilitation.III [26]

Robotic-assisted UKA provides significantly more consistent intraoperative medial compartment pressures and superior soft-tissue balancing compared to conventional jig-based techniques, directly translating to improved early functional rehabilitation and lower rates of aseptic loosening.II [29]

3. Resection Sequence & Gap Mismatch Algorithm

FB-UKA surgeons typically utilize a hybrid "measured resection" approach blended with gap-balancing principles.II [19] Unlike TKA, the foundational step in nearly all FB-UKA systems is the proximal tibial resection. The tibial cut is the baseline from which all subsequent geometry is derived — it establishes coronal orientation and sagittal posterior tibial slope.

Typically, 2–3 mm of healthy bone is resected from the deepest part of the arthritic defect, creating a flat horizontal plateau.II [35] A standard preferred flexion gap is 10–11 mm, accommodating an 8–9 mm composite tibial component thickness while leaving the mandatory 2 mm of physiologic laxity.II [21]

The Core Algorithmic Principle

When a mismatch is identified, the surgeon must execute a precise, mathematically driven algorithm:

  • Tibial cut → influences both flexion and extension gaps simultaneously and equally
  • Distal femoral cut → strictly and exclusively alters the extension gap
  • Posterior femoral condylar cut → strictly and exclusively alters the flexion gap

Clinical Scenario Extension Gap Flexion Gap Primary Intervention Secondary Intervention
1. Symmetric Tightness Tight (<8 mm) Tight (<8 mm) ↑ Tibial horizontal resection depth uniformly Remove posterior/medial osteophytes
2. Extension Tightness Tight (<8 mm) Optimal (10–11 mm) Remove posterior femoral osteophytes Resect additional 1–2 mm distal femur
3. Flexion Tightness Optimal (10–11 mm) Tight (<8 mm) ↑ Posterior Tibial Slope (PTS) Downsize femoral component (↓ posterior offset)
4. Loose Flexion Optimal (10–11 mm) Loose (>12 mm) ↓ Posterior Tibial Slope (PTS) Upsize femoral component (↑ posterior offset)

Scenario Analyses

Scenario 1: Symmetrically Tight Knee

The most efficient solution is to lower the tibial joint line by resecting more proximal tibia. Dropping the horizontal cut by exactly 2 mm opens both the flexion and extension spaces equally by 2 mm while preserving native femoral kinematics and posterior slope.II [37]

Scenario 2: Extension Tightness with Optimal Flexion

The absolute first step — before any further bone resection — is to thoroughly identify and debride posterior femoral osteophytes. These tent the posterior capsule, artificially tightening the extension space. Removal often resolves up to 2 mm of tightness without altering bone geometry.II [37]

If the knee remains tight: resect an additional 1–2 mm from the distal femur. Because the distal femur rotates away from the tibia at 90° flexion, this has zero impact on the flexion gap.II [21]

Aggressive distal resection can shift the femoral component proximally, causing the anterior implant edge to overlap the native cartilage contour, inducing severe patellofemoral impingement and anterior knee pain.II [22]

Scenario 3: Flexion Tightness with Optimal Extension

This poses a greater biomechanical challenge. The surgeon cannot simply resect more posterior femoral condyle indiscriminately — this alters the center of rotation and degrades femoral rollback mechanics.

The primary, EBM-supported intervention is to increase the posterior tibial slope (PTS). Altering the cutting block angle from 3° to 7° removes a specific posterior wedge while maintaining anterior cortex height.II [37]

If PTS is maximized (at the safe limit of 7°), the secondary intervention is to downsize the femoral component, mathematically decreasing posterior condylar offset and increasing flexion gap volume.II [36]

PTS increase directly and safely mitigates a tight flexion gap. Patient-specific anatomical slope tailoring significantly improves varus/valgus and rotational laxity without PCL or posterior capsular releases.II [41]

When femoral downsizing is used for flexion tightness, care must be taken to avoid mid-flexion mismatch. Excessive reduction of posterior condylar offset leads to mid-flexion instability and paradoxical anterior slide.II [43]

4. Posterior Tibial Slope (PTS): The Primary Sagittal Kinematic Driver

The PTS is unequivocally the primary driver of sagittal plane kinematics in FB-UKA. In the native knee, PTS varies widely (0°–15°), and medial and lateral compartments often demonstrate different baseline slopes within the same knee.II [39]

4.1 Kinematics and Cruciate Ligament Tension

Table 4 — PTS Biomechanical Effects on Cruciate Ligaments and Joint Kinematics
PTS Scenario Biomechanical Effect Clinical Consequence
Excessive PTS (>7°) Femur drives down the slope → anterior tibial translation ACL attenuation, chronic pain, eventual ACL rupture; aseptic looseningII [44]
Optimal PTS (3°–7°) Balanced sagittal resting position; physiologic cruciate tension Maximal flexion, normal kinematics, long-term implant survivorship
Inadequate/Reverse PTS (<3°) Restricted posterior flexion space; femoral impingement on PE insert Restricted maximal flexion, posterior tibiofemoral subluxation riskII [45]

In vivo fluoroscopic analyses and finite element models demonstrate that each 1° incremental increase in PTS contributes to measurable, linear increases in anterior tibial translation and overall flexion gap volume.II [46]

4.2 The 7° Threshold Debate

The orthopaedic literature has engaged in significant debate regarding the absolute upper safe limit for PTS in UKA.

The Landmark Hernigou Study

In a paradigm-defining study with mean 16-year follow-up, Hernigou and colleagues established a strict upper limit. Knees with postoperative PTS >7° exhibited a direct linear relationship with late-onset ACL rupture, anterior translation instability, and catastrophic aseptic loosening. This rule remains absolute even if the patient's native slope exceeded 7°.II [44]

Modern Re-evaluation

More recent analyses using modern FB designs (with different PE conformity and highly cross-linked polyethylene) have prompted re-evaluation. Plancher and colleagues demonstrated that while PTS >7° was correlated with higher KOOS pain subscores, it did not universally result in objective functional decline, catastrophic implant failure, or higher revision rates — even in ACL-deficient cohorts selected for FB-UKA.II [52]

Consensus recommendation: Despite modern nuances, the standard of care remains highly conservative. Meticulously target a postoperative PTS of 3°–7°. Match the patient's native slope only if it falls within this biomechanically safe window.II [55]

4.3 Risk of Proximal Tibial Subsidence

Trabecular bone density of the anterior proximal tibia decreases exponentially as resection moves distal to the subchondral surface.II [45]

Combining aggressive PTS (>10°) with a deep horizontal cut drastically thins dense anterior cortical bone, exposing weak cancellous bone. Because FB implants rely on surface-area load distribution (unlike MB rolling load-sharing), this cortical weakening dramatically elevates the risk of anterior tibial subsidence — representing 3.6–10.4% of all UKA failures.II [5]

Subsidence in FB-UKA immediately alters the joint line, destroys collateral ligament tension, induces massive wear, and frequently requires complex revision to a stemmed TKA.II [5] Preservation of anterior bone stock is a paramount constraint.

5. Joint Line Restoration & CPAK Phenotypes

Unlike mobile-bearing implants that self-align, a fixed-bearing implant acts as a rigid mechanical spacer that dictates strict kinetic pathways based on physical seating in coronal and sagittal planes.II [58]

5.1 The CPAK Classification and Constitutional Alignment

The Coronal Plane Alignment of the Knee (CPAK) classification system categorizes knees by two independent variables:

  • Arithmetic Hip-Knee-Ankle angle (aHKA)
  • Joint Line Obliquity (JLO)

CPAK classification data from source references [60] and [21].

The CPAK system mandates restoring the individual patient's pre-arthritic constitutional phenotype, challenging the historical dogma that every knee must achieve 0° neutral mechanical alignment.

Forcing neutral alignment in a naturally varus CPAK knee (e.g., Type I: varus aHKA with apex distal JLO) is destructive — it unnaturally elevates the medial joint line, overstuffs the compartment, stretches the MCL, and generates massive shear forces leading to abrasive PE wear and delamination.II [61]

Kinematic alignment superiority: At mean 10-year follow-up, patients whose FB-UKA alignment was kinematically matched to their constitutional aHKA (within 3° of native phenotype) achieved statistically superior return-to-sport metrics, higher KOOS scores, and greater implant longevity compared to mechanically neutral cohorts.II [18]

Undercorrection advantage: An intentional minor undercorrection leaving the knee in 2°–4° of varus provides significantly better survivorship, superior functional scores, and more natural kinematics than neutral alignment in medial FB-UKA.II [21]

5.2 Joint Line Height and Patellofemoral Kinematics

Artificial Elevation of the Joint Line

Elevation — typically from inadequate tibial resection combined with a thick poly insert — forces the patella to ride lower (patella baja effect). During deep flexion, the patella impinges against the anterior metallic flange or transitional zone, causing severe anterior knee pain — a leading cause of patient dissatisfaction and early revision.II [8]

Depression of the Joint Line

Depression from excessive tibial resection induces profound mid-flexion instability (30°–60°). Collateral ligaments anchored to precise epicondylar points become slack in mid-arc. Inserting a thicker poly to stabilize mid-flexion causes disastrous overstuffing in terminal extension.II [31]

Exact replication of both the pre-arthritic height and specific obliquity of the joint line is an absolute prerequisite for normal postoperative kinematics. This is particularly well achieved with robotic-assisted systems.II [31]

2. Plancher et al. — Modern PTS Outcomes in FB-UKA

Contemporary FB designs · Evidence Level: II (Comparative cohort) · [52], [53]

Demonstrated that while PTS >7° correlated with higher KOOS pain subscores, it did not universally result in objective functional decline, implant failure, or higher revision rates — even in ACL-deficient cohorts carefully selected for FB-UKA.

3. Kinematic Alignment in FB-UKA — 10-Year Outcomes

Mean 10-year follow-up · Evidence Level: II (Therapeutic study) · [18], [61]

Patients whose FB-UKA alignment was kinematically matched to their constitutional aHKA (within 3° of native phenotype) achieved statistically superior return-to-sport metrics, higher KOOS scores, and greater overall implant longevity vs. mechanically neutral cohorts.

4. CPAK Classification — Coronal Plane Alignment of the Knee

Classification system · Evidence Level: III (Expert opinion / Consensus) · [60]

Introduced a nuanced framework categorizing knees by arithmetic HKA and Joint Line Obliquity, mandating restoration of individual pre-arthritic constitutional phenotypes rather than forcing arbitrary neutral alignment.

5. Robotic-Assisted vs. Conventional UKA — Pressure Sensor Study

Comparative cadaveric/clinical study · Evidence Level: II (Comparative) · [29]

Robotic arm-assisted UKA achieves significantly more consistent intraoperative medial compartment pressures and superior soft-tissue balance compared to conventional jig-based techniques, with improved early functional outcomes.

6. Sensor & Machine Learning-Based Gap Balancing Assessment

Cadaveric surgical training study · Evidence Level: III · [26]

Expert surgeons achieve optimal mean flexion-extension contact force difference of ~83 N. Novice surgeons using manual techniques leave mismatches of 200–300 N, correlating with postoperative pain and stiffness.

7. Lateral FB-UKA Series (246 Cases)

Case series · Evidence Level: IV · [9]

Demonstrated superior survivorship of fixed-bearing designs in lateral compartment UKA, where native joint laxity in flexion is significantly greater than medial compartment.

8. Tibial Subsidence in FB-UKA

Multiple sources · Evidence Level: II–IV · [5], [45], [55]

Anterior tibial subsidence represents 3.6–10.4% of all UKA failures. Large reductions in postoperative PTS risk anterior collapse. Bone density decreases exponentially with deeper resections, particularly under the anterior baseplate edge.
