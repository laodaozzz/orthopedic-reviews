Adolescent Transitional Ankle Fractures: Tillaux & Triplane

Comprehensive Review Evidence-Based Interactive

🦴 Pathoanatomy of the Transitional Ankle

A predictable vulnerability during the 18-month physeal closure window

Adolescent transitional ankle fractures — specifically the Tillaux and triplane patterns — are injuries unique to a specific developmental window. Their existence is dictated entirely by the predictable, yet asymmetric, nature of physeal (growth plate) closure in the distal tibia. Understanding this "predictable vulnerability" is the foundational prerequisite for accurate diagnosis and effective surgical management. LOE: III

The 18-Month Window: Asymmetric Physeal Closure

Unlike the abrupt cessation of growth seen in other bones, the distal tibial physis undergoes a protracted closure process lasting approximately 18 to 20 months. During this phase, the physis is a biomechanical composite, with areas of fused, strong bone coexisting alongside areas of open, weak hyaline cartilage. LOE: III

Ossification occurs in a specific, well-documented sequence:

1. Central — first to begin closure
2. Anteromedial
3. Posteromedial
4. Lateral & Anterolateral — last to close (the "culprit" zone)

This sequence leaves the anterolateral aspect of the physis as the final, weakest point of the articular construct during the last stage of skeletal maturation. This specific area of vulnerability is precisely what predisposes the adolescent ankle to these unique transitional fracture patterns. LOE: III

The "Culprit" Ligament: AITFL and Mechanism of Injury

The most common mechanism of injury for both Tillaux and triplane fractures is a supination-external rotation (SER) force applied to the foot. In the skeletally immature patient, the ligaments of the ankle are biomechanically stronger and more robust than the open, cartilaginous physis. LOE: III

The SER force places significant tension on the stout Anterior Inferior Tibiofibular Ligament (AITFL). This ligament originates from the anterolateral distal tibial epiphysis (the tubercle of Chaput) and inserts onto the anterior fibula (the Wagstaffe tubercle). In an adult, this force would result in a syndesmotic sprain; in the adolescent, the AITFL does not tear — instead, its tensile strength avulses its bony attachment, propagating a fracture through the underlying, unfused anterolateral physis. LOE: III

Fracture Signatures: Tillaux vs. Triplane

The same SER mechanism, acting on the same AITFL attachment, can produce two distinct fracture patterns. The specific fracture that results is a direct proxy for the exact stage of physeal closure at the time of injury.

Tillaux Fracture — Salter-Harris Type III Avulsion An isolated avulsion of the anterolateral distal tibial epiphysis. The fracture propagates horizontally through the open anterolateral physis and exits vertically into the ankle joint. It is defined by the lack of a coronal-plane metaphyseal component. Studies suggest Tillaux fractures occur later in the transitional period, when the posterior physis has already fused. LOE: III
Triplane Fracture — Complex SH Type IV Variant A multiplanar injury with components in all three anatomic planes: (1) Sagittal — vertical fracture through the epiphysis (AP view), (2) Coronal — oblique fracture through the posterior metaphysis (lateral view), and (3) Axial — horizontal separation through the physis. This pattern results when the same SER injury occurs earlier in the transitional window while the posterior physis is still partially open. LOE: III

The classic Salter-Harris classification system, a 2D construct, fundamentally fails to describe these 3D transitional injuries. The triplane fracture, which appears as an SH-III on one radiographic view and an SH-II on another, is the quintessential example of this system's failure. LOE: III

Triplane Classification by Fragment Number

The surgically relevant classification of triplane fractures is based on the number of fragments, which dictates the fixation strategy. LOE: III

  • Two-part: The most common configuration. Typically a single anteromedial epiphyseal fragment and a large unified posterolateral fragment containing the posterior epiphysis and metaphyseal spike.
  • Three-part: Involves a separate anterolateral (Tillaux-type) fragment, a posteromedial epiphyseal fragment, and the posterior metaphyseal spike.
  • Four-part: A rarer, more comminuted variant.
  • Medial triplane: A rare variant resulting from an adduction force, not an SER mechanism.

🔬 Diagnostic Imperatives

Why computed tomography is non-negotiable

The management algorithm for transitional ankle fractures hinges on a single, critical variable: the precise measurement of intra-articular displacement. The failure of plain radiographs to provide this measurement reliably makes CT an indispensable diagnostic tool.

The Insufficiency of Plain Radiographs

While standard radiographs (AP, lateral, and mortise views) are the appropriate initial imaging study, they are fundamentally inadequate for definitive preoperative assessment. Plain films consistently and significantly underestimate the true extent of injury, the complex 3D geometry of the fragments, and the amount of articular displacement. LOE: III

One study comparing Salter-Harris classifications based on plain radiographs to those based on 3D-CT found the accuracy of plain radiographs was a mere 34%. LOE: III

The Mandate for CT

For any suspected transitional fracture, particularly those with any visible displacement on plain films, a CT scan is considered mandatory. It is unequivocally the "imaging modality of choice." LOE: III

The utility of CT is threefold:

  1. Delineating the Exact Fracture Pattern: CT, especially with 3D reconstructions, is the only modality that can reliably distinguish a two-part from a three-part or four-part triplane fracture. This distinction is the primary determinant of the surgical plan.
  2. Accurately Measuring Articular Displacement: CT allows for precise, sub-millimeter measurement of both articular gap (diastasis) and step-off (vertical incongruity), which is the principal indication for surgery.
  3. Assessing Articular Congruity: It provides a detailed map of the tibial plafond, allowing the surgeon to assess overall congruity of the joint surface.

The addition of CT to the diagnostic workup is not merely confirmatory; it actively and frequently alters clinical and surgical decision-making. LOE: III

Selective Radiographic Predictors for CT

Recent evidence has identified specific plain-film findings that serve as strong surrogate markers for significant articular displacement. A 2023 Level III study found the strongest correlation with CT-measured displacement was epiphyseal displacement on the lateral view (ρ = 0.55). LOE: III

Evidence-Based Heuristic Any displacement visualized on the lateral radiograph should be considered a high-risk finding that mandates a CT scan for definitive assessment. Metaphyseal displacement >1 mm on lateral XR is "strongly predictive of clinically relevant articular displacement on CT."

Limited Role of MRI

MRI plays a very limited role in the acute preoperative workup for fracture fixation. It is inferior to CT for delineating complex bony fragments. Its utility is reserved for assessing associated occult injuries (ligamentous damage, chondral fractures, soft-tissue interposition) and for follow-up evaluation of physeal bars or chronic postoperative pain. LOE: III

📐 Surgical Indications

Defining the threshold for intervention: the evolution from the 2-mm rule to the 2.5-mm functional threshold

The "2-mm Rule": The Traditional Anatomic Standard

For decades, the standard teaching has been that operative intervention is indicated for any transitional fracture with an intra-articular displacement (either gap or step-off) of >2 millimeters. This "2-mm rule" is based on the principle that failure to restore a congruent articular surface increases the long-term risk of post-traumatic ankle arthritis. LOE: III

Re-evaluating the Threshold: Lurie et al. (JBJS 2020)

A pivotal 2020 Level III retrospective study by Lurie et al., published in The Journal of Bone and Joint Surgery, has become central to the modern management of these injuries. LOE: III

Lurie et al. (JBJS 2020) — Key Findings

The study analyzed functional outcomes (FAAM and SANE Sports scores) of 57 adolescents with Tillaux and triplane fractures, focusing on the "grey zone" cohort: those with 2 to 5 mm of intra-articular gap after closed reduction. Mean follow-up: 4.5 years.

  • Negative predictors: Non-operative treatment, greater intra-articular gap, and presence of a complication.
  • Functional threshold: Patients with residual gap ≤2.5 mm had SANE Sports score of 90%; those with gap >2.5 mm scored 75%.
  • Conclusion: Surgical management conveys the greatest functional benefit when intra-articular gap exceeds 2.5 mm.

This finding is independently corroborated by Choudhry et al. (2014), who found that patients with residual articular displacement of up to 2.4 mm after definitive treatment did not have worse functional outcomes compared to those with anatomic reduction. LOE: III

The Modern Algorithm

The critical determinant of care is the displacement measured on a post-reduction CT scan. LOE: III

  1. Patient presents with suspected transitional ankle fracture.
  2. An attempt at closed reduction is performed.
  3. A post-reduction CT scan is obtained to assess residual articular displacement.
  4. If displacement ≤2.5 mm: Non-operative management with a short-leg cast and close radiographic follow-up is viable, associated with good-to-excellent functional outcomes.
  5. If displacement >2.5 mm: Surgical fixation (CRPP or ORIF) is indicated to maximize long-term functional recovery and return to sport.

Important Nuance: Gap vs. Step-Off The terms "gap" and "step-off" are often used interchangeably. However, biomechanically, a vertical step-off is far more detrimental to articular cartilage than a horizontal gap. The 2.5 mm threshold primarily analyzed gaps. Most surgeons would agree that a true 2.5 mm step-off at the weight-bearing plafond is biomechanically unacceptable and requires anatomic reduction regardless of this threshold.

🔧 Operative Technique: Tillaux Fracture

Anatomic reduction of the anterolateral epiphyseal fragment

Once surgical fixation is indicated (residual displacement >2.5 mm), the unyielding technical goal is anatomic reduction of the articular surface. LOE: III

Surgical Approaches

  • Open Reduction (ORIF): The standard anterolateral approach provides direct visualization of the fracture, allowing evacuation of hematoma and removal of interposed soft tissue. LOE: III
  • Mini-Open ORIF: A 2021 study of 22 patients demonstrated excellent results with mean operative time of only 21 minutes, mean AOFAS score of 93.6, and no complications. LOE: IV
  • Arthroscopy-Assisted Reduction (ARIF): Allows direct, magnified visualization of the entire articular surface, confirming a perfect "step-free" reduction. LOE: IV

Reduction Maneuvers

The primary closed reduction maneuver is internal rotation of the foot, which uses the AITFL and intact fibula to "pull" the fragment back into place. This may be combined with dorsiflexion or plantarflexion. LOE: III

Implant Selection and the Physeal "Controversy"

The standard of care is fixation with cannulated screws (3.5 mm or 4.0 mm), providing interfragmentary compression via the lag technique. LOE: III

To Cross or Not to Cross the Physis? In most pediatric physeal fractures, transphyseal fixation is avoided. In transitional fractures, this rule is superseded. These injuries occur in adolescents approaching skeletal maturity with minimal growth remaining. The physis is already physiologically closing. Therefore, the risk of iatrogenic physeal arrest is minimal, far outweighed by the high risk of post-traumatic arthritis from a non-anatomic joint. Anatomic articular reduction must be prioritized. LOE: III

Implant Material: Metallic vs. Bioabsorbable

A 2008 retrospective study compared metallic versus bioabsorbable screw fixation, finding similar clinical and radiographic outcomes. The bioabsorbable group had fewer complications (e.g., hardware irritation) and obviated the need for a second surgery for removal. LOE: III

🛠️ Operative Technique: Triplane Fracture

A more complex strategy entirely dependent on CT-defined fracture configuration

The surgical strategy for a triplane fracture is more complex and depends entirely on the fracture configuration (two-part, three-part, or four-part) as defined by the preoperative CT scan. LOE: III

The non-negotiable primary goal is anatomic restoration of the tibial plafond. The surgical plan follows an "articular-surface-first" principle: the epiphyseal (SH-III) component is reduced and fixed first, followed by fixation of the metaphyseal (SH-II) component. LOE: III

Surgical Approaches

  • Anterolateral approach: Most common; provides direct access to the epiphyseal component.
  • Anteromedial approach: Used if fracture lines extend medially or for the rare medial triplane variant.
  • The metaphyseal component is often addressed percutaneously with an anterior-to-posterior screw through a small stab incision.

Post-operative Protocol

A long-leg cast may be used for 3–4 weeks to provide rotational control, followed by a transition to a short-leg walking cast. LOE: III

⚖️ Current Controversies & Advanced Techniques

Evolving debates in transitional ankle fracture management

Controversy 1: Associated Fibular Fracture

A concurrent fibular fracture is common (estimated ~50% of triplane cases). The literature is in direct contradiction regarding fixation. LOE: III

  • "Fix" philosophy: The fibular fracture remains unstable after tibial fixation, so fixation is usually necessary.
  • "No-fix" philosophy: The fibula often reduces with tibial fixation and does not require separate consideration (AO Foundation).
Expert Consensus Synthesis The decision is strategic, not categorical. The fibula can be used as a reduction aid (via ligamentotaxis) and as a rotational guide to prevent malrotation. Fixation is required if the fibula remains malaligned after tibial fixation, or if the tibia is highly comminuted and needs the fibular buttress for stability.

Controversy 2: Arthroscopy-Assisted Reduction (ARIF)

Proponents argue ARIF is the only technique allowing direct, magnified visualization of the entire articular surface, ensuring perfect anatomic reduction. However, evidence is limited to case reports and Level IV studies, and the technique is "time-consuming, technically demanding, and requires a long learning curve." LOE: IV ARIF is best viewed as a specialized tool for highly complex articular patterns, not a routine replacement for well-executed mini-open or percutaneous techniques.

Controversy 3: Hardware Removal

A 2022 study compared outcomes in children with retained versus removed epiphyseal screws. At 4.4-year follow-up, there was no statistically significant difference in patient-reported outcomes between the two groups. LOE: III

Synthesis Routine hardware removal for functional improvement is not evidence-based. This strengthens the argument for bioabsorbable implants, which provide equivalent fixation while eliminating the need for a second procedure.

Post-Traumatic Osteoarthritis (PTOA)

PTOA is the most significant long-term concern and the primary justification for surgical fixation. However, there is a critical disconnect between radiographic arthritis and functional outcomes. Long-term follow-up of ankle fractures found that while radiographic arthritis was common (76% of patients), the majority were doing well and very few experienced pain or functional limitation. LOE: III

Key Clinical Pearl While surgery is performed to prevent PTOA, the primary evidence-based driver for surgery should be the preservation of function (which declines with gaps >2.5 mm), not simply the prevention of radiographic changes that may be asymptomatic.

Premature Physeal Arrest (PPA): A "Red Herring"

In transitional fractures, the risk of PPA is minimal and "generally not a concern." A large retrospective review identified zero cases of PPA in Tillaux fractures and only 7% in triplane fractures, supporting the physeal-sparing principle. LOE: III

Non-Operative Casting: Long-Leg vs. Short-Leg

A multicenter study of 159 patients found zero cases of fracture displacement, malunion, or nonunion in either group. However, the short-leg cast group had a 7-day shorter mean time to weight-bearing and a 12-day shorter mean time to return to full activity. LOE: III

Recommendation A long-leg cast provides no additional stability for these fractures and measurably slows recovery. A short-leg cast is the preferred method of immobilization for non-operative management.

✅ Expert Synthesis & Clinical Recommendations

Evidence-based summary of key management principles

  1. Pathophysiology: The specific fracture pattern (Tillaux vs. Triplane) is a direct reflection of the precise stage of asymmetric physeal closure. Triplane fractures occur earlier; Tillaux occurs later when the posterior physis has fused. LOE: III
  2. Diagnostics: CT is mandatory. Plain radiographs will lead to under-treatment of up to 39% of fractures meeting operative criteria. The lateral radiograph is the most sensitive screening view. LOE: III
  3. Surgical Indications: The modern, evidence-based functional threshold for operative intervention is an articular gap >2.5 mm on a post-reduction CT scan. LOE: III
  4. Surgical Technique: The primary goal is anatomic articular congruity. Transphyseal fixation is acceptable and often necessary. The surgical plan is entirely dependent on the CT-defined fragment configuration. LOE: III
  5. Controversies: Fibular fixation is strategic, not routine. ARIF is a specialty tool. Routine hardware removal is not evidence-based. LOE: III
  6. Outcomes: Long-term functional outcomes are excellent. The primary driver of poor function is residual displacement >2.5 mm. For non-operative fractures, short-leg cast is superior with a 12-day faster return to activity. LOE: III
