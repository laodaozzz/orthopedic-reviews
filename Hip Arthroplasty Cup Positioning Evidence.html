Intraoperative Landmarks & Bearing-Specific Positioning in THA

Exhaustive Analysis — TAL, Combined Anteversion & Tribological Optimization

Comprehensive Educational Summary

This document synthesizes current evidence on acetabular component positioning in Total Hip Arthroplasty, covering the Transverse Acetabular Ligament (TAL), the validity of the Lewinnek safe zone, Combined Anteversion (CA) techniques, and bearing-specific target angles for Ceramic-on-Ceramic (CoC) and Polyethylene (CoP) bearings.

1. Introduction: The Challenge of Acetabular Positioning

The orientation of the acetabular cup is one of the most critical determinants of success in Total Hip Arthroplasty (THA). Malpositioning is a potent catalyst for mechanical failure, serving as the primary etiology for dislocation — the leading cause of early revision surgery — and significantly contributing to accelerated bearing wear, component impingement, range-of-motion limitation, and aseptic loosening. Level I

Since Lewinnek et al. defined the widely adopted "safe zone" (inclination 40° ± 10°, anteversion 15° ± 10°) in 1978, this framework has dictated surgical teaching and practice for nearly half a century. However, the persistence of instability and the emergence of advanced bearing surfaces — specifically Ceramic-on-Ceramic (CoC) and Highly Cross-Linked Polyethylene (HXLPE) — have precipitated a critical re-evaluation of these boundaries. The modern arthroplasty landscape is undergoing a paradigm shift from generic, population-based "safe zones" toward patient-specific functional targets that account for:

  • Spinopelvic mobility — dynamic pelvic tilt during functional activities
  • Combined Anteversion — the cumulative rotational profile of both components
  • Tribological requirements — bearing-specific geometric tolerances
  • Internal anatomical landmarks — particularly the Transverse Acetabular Ligament (TAL)
Clinical Imperative In hard-on-hard bearings (CoC), deviations of mere degrees can lead to "edge loading" — where the femoral head articulates with the liner rim — causing stripe wear, audible squeaking, and even catastrophic component fracture. In hard-on-soft bearings (CoP), cup malposition leads to eccentric loading, rim impingement, particulate debris generation, osteolysis, and aseptic loosening. Level I

2. The Transverse Acetabular Ligament (TAL)

2.1 Anatomy and Surgical Relevance

The Transverse Acetabular Ligament is a robust, fibrous band bridging the acetabular notch, structurally continuous with the labrum but distinct in composition. Unlike the labrum — frequently degenerated, torn, or ossified in the arthritic hip — the TAL is often preserved even in advanced osteoarthritis, positioning it as a potentially invaluable landmark for determining "native" acetabular version. Level III

2.3 Surgical Techniques for TAL Exposure

Chisel vs. Reamer Technique

A comparative study evaluated two methods for exposing the TAL in Grade 3 hips. The reamer technique achieved a TAL identification rate of 94.2% (49/52 hips) compared to only 54.1% (26/48 hips) with the chisel technique. The technique involves using a small-diameter reamer (e.g., 38 mm) directed inferomedially to "peel" away the osteophytic curtain while protecting the soft tissues of the notch. Level II

TAL Suction Retractor

A novel, low-cost technique uses a thoracic suction catheter taped to a blunt Hohmann retractor, placed inferior to the TAL on the posterior ischial surface. This dual-function device retracts soft tissues and provides continuous suction at the dependent point, maintaining ligament visibility during reaming and trial reduction. Level IV

2.4 TAL Reliability: The Osteonecrosis vs. Osteoarthritis Divide

Critical Dichotomy The TAL's reliability as a positioning guide varies dramatically between Osteonecrosis (ON) and Osteoarthritis (OA), particularly OA secondary to developmental dysplasia of the hip (DDH). In dysplastic hips, blindly following the TAL can lead to catastrophic malpositioning. Level II

The "Dysplasia Trap" If a surgeon blindly aligns the acetabular cup with the TAL in a female patient with dysplastic OA, they are likely to place the cup in ~20° of anteversion. Combined with an anteverted femoral stem (common in dysplasia), cumulative anteversion could exceed 50–60°, leading to anterior instability and posterior impingement. The TAL should not be used as the sole guide in dysplastic OA — it must be cross-referenced with Combined Anteversion techniques. Level II

2.5 TAL and Inclination

While the TAL controls version relatively well in normal hips, it offers little guidance for inclination. The TAL lies in a transverse plane and cannot determine the correct abduction angle relative to the longitudinal axis. This results in high rates of inclination outliers, particularly for less experienced surgeons. However, the TAL remains the gold standard for defining the inferior limit of acetabular preparation, ensuring the native center of rotation is restored. Level II

3. The Lewinnek Safe Zone: Reappraisal

3.1 Original Definition

The Lewinnek safe zone (1978) defined targets of inclination 40° ± 10° and anteversion 15° ± 10° based on a retrospective review of only 300 hips with 9 dislocations — a sample size considered inadequate by modern standards. Level III

3.2 The "Safe Zone" Fallacy

Landmark Finding: Abdel et al. (Level III) In a cohort of 9,784 primary THAs with 206 dislocations, 58% of dislocated cups were "perfectly" positioned within Lewinnek's safe zone. Specifically, 84% of dislocated cups met safe-zone inclination criteria, and 69% met anteversion criteria. Being in the "safe zone" is not a proxy for stability. Level III

3.3 Methodological Confusion

A systematic review of 147 articles citing Lewinnek revealed that only 11% actually measured cup position according to the original supine radiographic definition. There are three geometrically distinct definitions of anteversion:

  • Radiographic Anteversion (RA): Measured on AP radiographs (Lewinnek's original)
  • Operative Anteversion (OA): Angle of the insertion handle relative to the sagittal plane
  • Anatomic Anteversion (AA): Measured on CT relative to the true acetabular rim
sin(RA) = sin(OA) × cos(OI)

For a cup with 45° inclination, an operative anteversion of 20° corresponds to a radiographic anteversion of approximately 14°. If a surgeon aims for 15° operative anteversion, the resulting radiographic anteversion may be as low as 11° — risking posterior instability. Level I

3.4 The Shift to Modern Metrics

The failure of the Lewinnek safe zone has catalyzed adoption of:

  1. Combined Anteversion (CA) — integrating femoral and acetabular version
  2. Functional Safe Zones — targets adjusted for dynamic pelvic tilt
  3. Bearing-Specific Targets — tighter tolerances for CoC versus CoP

4. Combined Anteversion (CA): The Functional Metric

Combined Anteversion posits that stability is determined not by the cup alone but by the sum of acetabular cup anteversion and femoral stem anteversion. A deficiency on one side can be compensated by excess on the other, provided the total falls within a physiological range. Level II

4.1 Mathematical Definitions

Ranawat/Dorr Definition

CARanawat = Cup AV + Stem AV
Target: 25°–50° (ideal: 35°–45°)

Gender specifics: Ranawat suggested ~45° for women and 20°–30° for men to account for differences in joint laxity. The "Ranawat Sign" is an intraoperative maneuver: the leg is placed in 0° extension and internally rotated 45°; if the femoral head base is coplanar with the cup face, CA is estimated at 45°. Level III

Widmer Definition

CAWidmer = Cup AV + (0.7 × Stem AV)
Target: 37.3° ± 10°

Widmer's finite element modeling demonstrated that cup anteversion has a more potent effect on flexion clearance than stem anteversion (coefficient 1.0 vs 0.7). This means relying solely on the simple sum (Ranawat) might lead to impingement if cup version is low and stem version is high, even if the sum is "normal." Level II

4.2 The "Stem-First" Technique

With cementless press-fit stems, the stem version is largely dictated by the native femoral canal anatomy. This constraint has given rise to the "Stem-First" technique:

1
Prepare Femur First — Broach and seat trial stem; measure resultant stem anteversion relative to the transepicondylar axis
2
Calculate Required Cup Version — Using the formula: Cup AV = Target CA − (0.7 × Stem AV)
3
Position Cup — Impact the cup to the calculated patient-specific angle rather than a generic 15°
Clinical Evidence Retrospective reviews comparing the stem-first CA technique against standard placement reported a dislocation rate of only 0.4% in the CA group versus 2.5% in the standard group. Notably, the CA technique resulted in wider variability of individual component angles (more "outliers" by Lewinnek), but the combined system was optimized. Level III

5. Bearing-Specific Target Angles

The choice of bearing surface dictates the geometric margin of error. Polyethylene is relatively forgiving; ceramics are brittle and intolerant of edge loading. Level I

5.1 Ceramic-on-Ceramic (CoC)

CoC bearings offer near-zero wear rates and biological inertness but introduce unique failure modes:

  • Edge Loading: Contact patch migrates to the liner rim, generating stripe wear. Caused by steep inclination (>45–50°), microseparation, or version extremes. Level II
  • Squeaking: Multifactorial acoustic phenomenon affecting 0.7–20% of CoC hips. Strongly correlated with malposition — CoC cups placed outside 35°–45° inclination and 15°–35° anteversion were 29 times more likely to squeak. Level II
  • Component Fracture: Catastrophic failure from cumulative edge-loading stress exceeding material strength.
CoC Target Angles Inclination: 40°–45° (strictly <45° preferred). >45° is a critical risk factor.
Anteversion: 20°–25°. Avoid <15° (posterior edge loading in flexion) and >35° (anterior edge loading in extension).
Precision is paramount. The forgiveness of CoC is effectively zero. Navigation/robotics highly recommended.

5.2 Ceramic-on-Polyethylene (CoP) / Metal-on-Polyethylene (MoP)

HXLPE, the most common bearing globally, deforms slightly under load, providing greater tolerance to edge loading.

  • Wear: Linearly correlated with contact stress. Wear rates increase significantly when inclination exceeds 55° (from 0.135 mm/yr at <50° to 0.403 mm/yr at >60°). Level II
  • Anteversion Sensitivity: Anterior instability linked to operative anteversion >31°; posterior instability linked to <19°. The safe zone for anteversion is narrower than for inclination. Level III
CoP Target Angles Inclination: 40°–50° (avoid >55°).
Anteversion: 15°–25° (sensitive for stability — avoid extremes).

6. Operative vs. Radiographic Definitions: The Conversion Problem

A pervasive source of error is the confusion between Murray's three reference frames for acetabular orientation. Surgeons view the cup intraoperatively using the "Operative" definition, but outcomes are reported using the "Radiographic" definition. Level I

Table 5 — Murray's Definitions of Acetabular Orientation
DefinitionInclinationAnteversion
Radiographic (RI / RA)Measured on AP radiograph; X-ray beam ⊥ coronal plane. Used by Lewinnek.
Operative (OI / OA)Angle of cup introducer relative to patient's body axis. What the surgeon sets.
Anatomic (AI / AA)True 3D angles relative to pelvic cardinal axes (APP). Measured on CT.
tan(RI) = tan(OI) / cos(OA)
sin(RA) = sin(OA) × cos(OI)

Key rule: Radiographic anteversion is always lower than operative anteversion. To achieve 15° RA with 40° RI, the surgeon must aim for approximately 20° OA and 42° OI. If the mechanical guide is set to 15° OA (matching Lewinnek), the resulting RA will be approximately 11° — potentially causing posterior dislocation. Level I

7. Synthesis: Integrated Surgical Algorithms

7.1 The Dysplastic Hip Algorithm

1
Identify TAL — Use to define the native inferior limit and depth, restoring the center of rotation
2
Assess Version — If TAL appears excessively anteverted (>20–25°), do NOT align cup face to it. Pivot to Combined Anteversion technique.
3
Stem-First — Broach femur, measure stem version
4
Calculate Cup Target — Use Widmer formula: Cup AV = 37 − (0.7 × Stem AV). Place cup to calculated angle, ignoring TAL orientation if necessary.

7.2 The Ceramic-on-Ceramic Algorithm

1
Strict Inclination Control — Aim for 40°. Accept 35°, but never exceed 45°.
2
Avoid Microseparation — Ensure precise restoration of offset and leg length to maintain myofascial tension
3
Navigation — Strongly consider CAS or robotics; manual guide variance (±10°) exceeds CoC safety margin

7.3 General Hierarchical Approach

  1. Analyze the Spine — Is it stiff? → Target higher anteversion/inclination
  2. Select the Bearing — Is it CoC? → Target strictly <45° inclination
  3. Prepare the Stem — What is femoral version? → Calculate cup version using CA
  4. Reference the Anatomy — Use TAL for depth and version confirmation (unless dysplastic)

8. Landmark Studies Summary

Study 1 — TAL Reliability in 160 Hips (CT Simulation)

Archbold et al. · CT-based computer simulation · Level II

Mean TAL anteversion: 6.2° in ON vs. 16° in OA (range −12° to 35°). Gender dimorphism in OA (females 20°, males 7°). 39% of OA hips deviated >10° from target when TAL-guided.

Study 2 — Dislocation Analysis in 9,784 THAs

Abdel et al. · Retrospective cohort · Level III

58% of 206 dislocated cups were within Lewinnek's safe zone. 84% met inclination criteria; 69% met anteversion criteria. Safe zone has poor sensitivity for identifying at-risk hips.

Study 3 — CoC Squeaking Risk (2,406 Patients)

Multi-center analysis · Level II

CoC bearings outside 35°–45° inclination and 15°–35° anteversion were 29× more likely to squeak. Edge loading from fluid film disruption identified as primary mechanism.

Study 4 — Polyethylene Wear and Inclination

Wear analysis with digitized radiographs · Level II

Linear wear rates: 0.135 mm/yr at inclination <50°; 0.403 mm/yr at >60°. High inclination reduces contact area, increasing contact stress exponentially.

Study 5 — Combined Anteversion Technique Dislocation Rates

Retrospective comparative study · Level III

CA technique: 0.4% dislocation vs. 2.5% standard technique. Despite wider individual component variability, the combined system was optimized, demonstrating superiority of CA over individual component targeting.

Study 6 — Functional CA and Impingement-Free ROM

Simulation study with vROM scoring · Level II

Functional CA correlated strongly with impingement-free ROM (R² = 0.39), while static Lewinnek measurements showed negligible correlation (R² = 0.02). Standing CA target: 30°–50°; Sitting CA target: 45°–65°.
