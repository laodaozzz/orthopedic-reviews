Clinical Guidelines: Dynamization of Statically Locked Intramedullary Nails

Femoral & Tibial Shaft Fractures — Evidence-Based Educational Summary

1. Introduction & Biomechanical Principles

Intramedullary nailing of diaphyseal fractures in load-bearing long bones (femur and tibia) represents one of the most significant advancements in modern orthopedic traumatology. Pioneered by Gerhard Küntscher in the mid-twentieth century, the intramedullary nail operates fundamentally as an internal splint. Unlike rigid plate osteosynthesis—which relies on load-bearing mechanics and primary bone healing through Haversian remodeling—intramedullary nailing utilizes a load-sharing biomechanical philosophy. This construct neutralizes pathological bending, torsional, and shear moments while permitting axial transmission of physiological weight-bearing forces across the fracture site.

The introduction of static interlocking screws allowed the nail to be locked both proximally and distally to the fracture zone, providing absolute control over limb length and rotational alignment—a critical necessity for highly comminuted, segmental, or rotationally unstable fracture patterns. Standard of Care

1.1 Perren's Interfragmentary Strain Theory

The rigid mechanical construct of a statically locked nail can occasionally bypass the optimal physiological parameters for robust secondary bone healing. According to Perren's interfragmentary strain theory, secondary bone healing—via cartilaginous soft callus and endochondral ossification—requires an optimal interfragmentary strain of 2 % to 10 %. If a diaphyseal fracture is rigidly fixed with static interlocking screws and a minor distraction gap remains, the resulting strain may fall below this biological threshold, arresting the healing cascade and leading to delayed union or non-union. Source [4][5]

1.2 The Concept of Dynamization

To rectify this mechanical stagnation, the targeted surgical intervention known as dynamization was introduced. It involves the strategic removal of either the proximal or distal static interlocking screws, converting the rigid static construct into a dynamic load-sharing system. This permits the nail to slide longitudinally within the medullary canal. Under physiological weight-bearing loads, controlled axial micro-motion:

  • Closes minor residual fracture gaps
  • Physically compresses opposing fracture ends
  • Triggers a piezoelectric and biological cellular response
  • Enhances vascularization of the fracture area
  • Increases expression of TGF-β and BMPs
  • Accelerates calcification and maturation of the periosteal bridging callus
Source [11]
⚠ Critical Warning Dynamization is neither a universal panacea nor an entirely benign intervention. Inappropriate patient selection, poorly timed interventions, misinterpretation of biological viability, or dynamization of inherently unstable fracture morphologies can precipitate catastrophic clinical failures: profound limb shortening, severe rotational malalignment, propagation of atrophic non-unions, and hardware fatigue leading to complete implant failure. Source [13]

2. Predictive Modeling & Non-Union Risk Assessment

Before considering dynamization, it is imperative to understand baseline risk factors that predispose a diaphyseal fracture to delayed union. Two validated predictive scoring systems have been introduced: Level II

2.1 NURD Score (Non-Union Risk Determination)

The NURD score evaluates patient-specific and injury-specific variables: ASA classification, percentage of cortical contact achieved post-reduction, patient sex, presence of open fractures (Gustilo-Anderson classification), chronic disease status (diabetes mellitus, active tobacco use), compartment syndrome incidence, and necessity for soft-tissue flap coverage. The necessity of a soft-tissue flap assigns 5 points and compartment syndrome assigns 4 points. Conversely, low-energy spiral fracture patterns subtract points. A NURD score > 12 indicates an extreme risk (61% probability) of non-union, mandating highly vigilant follow-up. Level II — Source [16]

2.2 LEG-NUI Score

The LEG-NUI score evaluates the anatomical site, extent of soft tissue damage, fracture morphology, degree of initial displacement, surgical method of reduction (open vs. closed), magnitude of the post-surgical fracture gap, mechanical stability of the IMN construct, and presence of infection. A high score strongly predicts failure of primary intramedullary nailing and signals the impending need for secondary operative interventions. Level II — Source [16]

🔬 Key Finding Both models identify postoperative fracture gap and lack of circumferential cortical continuity as the most potent single variables associated with delayed healing—underscoring the mechanical necessity for dynamization in appropriate candidates. Level II — Source [17]

3. Optimal Timing & Diagnostic Criteria for Delayed Union

3.1 Defining Delayed Union and Non-Union

For both femoral and tibial shaft fractures treated with reamed or unreamed intramedullary nailing:

  • Delayed union: Distinct lack of progressive bridging callus formation at 12–16 weeks postoperatively. Source [18]
  • Established non-union: Absolute cessation of all cellular healing activity—lack of radiographic progression over 3 consecutive months combined with persistent pain on weight-bearing beyond 6–9 months. Source [18]

3.2 The Critical Temporal Window

✅ Optimal Timing: 3–6 Months (12–24 Weeks) Exhaustive clinical consensus and robust meta-regression analyses indicate that the optimal physiological timing for dynamization falls strictly within the 3- to 6-month postoperative window. Level I — Ligunghung et al., Injury, 2025 [20][22]
Table 1. Dynamization Success Rates by Timing
Timing Category Success Rate Source & Evidence Level
Within 6 months (early dynamization) 77.2 % Ligunghung et al., 2025 Level I
10–24 weeks (femoral shaft) 83.3 % Wu et al., 2012 Level II
Beyond 24 weeks 33.3 % (p = 0.036) Wu et al., 2012 Level II
Tibial dynamization overall 89.6 % Garabano et al., 2021 Level I

3.3 Biological Rationale for the Temporal Window

Between 12 and 24 weeks, the delayed union site is typically populated by a hypervascular, robustly chondrogenic soft callus. The application of controlled axial compression during this phase drives terminal endochondral ossification of the cartilaginous matrix into woven bone. If dynamization is delayed excessively beyond 6–9 months, the un-united gap becomes filled with dense, avascular, highly organized fibrous tissue that physically and chemically arrests endochondral ossification—rendering isolated mechanical compression biologically futile. Source [5][6]

⛔ Premature Dynamization (< 12 weeks) Almost universally contraindicated. During the acute inflammatory and early soft-callus phases, rudimentary granulation tissue and early woven collagen matrix completely lack the structural integrity to resist excessive shear and torsional forces. Premature removal of static screws can lead to uncontrolled telescopic collapse, resulting in acute, severe limb shortening and dynamic rotational instability. Level I — Source [20]

4. Radiographic Quantification: RUST & mRUST Scoring Systems

Modern orthopedic traumatologists rely on validated radiographic scoring systems to eliminate subjectivity and determine the precise moment when dynamization is indicated. The RUST and mRUST scores are the preeminent tools for quantifying bridging callus progression. While initially designed for tibial fractures, extensive validation has proven both equally reliable for femoral shaft fractures. Level II — Source [26][27]

📊 Predictive Power An mRUST ≤ 8 or RUST ≤ 6 at the 3–4 month evaluation is highly predictive of impending delayed union and serves as the primary trigger for dynamization consideration. The mRUST total score has an AUC of 0.986 for predicting ultimate union. Level II/III — Litrenta et al., J Orthop Trauma, 2015 [31]

5. Fracture Healing Index (FHI) & Callus-to-Diaphysis Ratio

The Fracture Healing Index (FHI) is defined as the maximum transverse diameter of visible bridging callus divided by the native diameter of the adjacent healthy diaphysis. The presence of a robust pre-dynamization FHI confirms a highly active local biological environment capable of responding positively to axial compressive forces.

Table 4. FHI Thresholds and Clinical Significance
FHI Value Predictive Outcome Sensitivity / Specificity Source
> 1.17 Successful union post-dynamization 91.67 % / 100 % Ligunghung et al., 2025 Level I
1.19–1.47 Optimal biological response range Significant predictor Bizzoca et al., 2018 Level III
< 1.17 Success rate drops to ~50 % High risk of failure Source [40]
⚠ Sparse-Callus Warning Dynamizing a delayed union with FHI < 1.17 drastically lowers the anticipated success rate. The meager volume of cartilaginous tissue fails to constrain the sudden influx of rotational and shear forces unleashed by removal of stabilizing locking screws, leading to micro-vascular disruption and propagation of atrophic non-union. Level I — Source [40]

6. Fracture Morphology & Gap Size

Dynamization acts purely as an axial mechanical intervention; it facilitates compression strictly along the longitudinal axis of the medullary canal. It does not correct—and severely risks exacerbating—any pre-existing coronal, sagittal, and torsional malalignments.

6.1 The Critical Role of Fracture Gap Size

Table 5. Fracture Gap Size and Dynamization Implications
Gap Size Biological Environment Clinical Implication
< 2 mm High chondrocyte concentration, abundant cartilage, robust periosteal callus Ideal relative indication for early dynamization if delayed union ensues
2–5 mm Intermediate; depends on biological factors Proceed with caution; require confirmed FHI > 1.17
> 5 mm Soft tissue interposition, segmental bone loss, over-distraction Absolute contraindication for isolated dynamization

A multicenter analysis of 194 tibial non-unions demonstrated that the mere presence of a visible radiographic fracture gap precipitated a significant drop in success rate from 92% to 78% (p = 0.02). Level II — Litrenta et al., J Orthop Trauma, 2015 [34]

6.2 Winquist-Hansen Classification

The Winquist-Hansen (W-H) classification remains the most robust framework for evaluating shaft comminution and determining dynamization safety.

Table 6. Winquist-Hansen Classification & Dynamization Suitability
Type Morphology Stability Dynamization Suitability
0 / I No comminution or small butterfly fragment (< 25% width) Highly stable axially & rotationally ✅ Ideal Candidate
II Larger wedge/butterfly; > 50% cortical contact Axially stable ✅ Good Candidate
III Large comminution; < 50% cortical contact Axially & rotationally unstable ⚠ Relative Contraindication (consider controlled dynamization)
IV Complete segmental comminution; zero cortical contact Grossly unstable all planes ⛔ Absolute Contraindication

Routine dynamization of Type III and IV fractures consistently results in 1.5–3.0 cm of acute limb shortening and 5–15° of external rotational malalignment. Shin et al. found that leg length discrepancies exceeding 2 cm occurred exclusively in Type III/IV patients, universally precipitating clinical failure. Level II — Wu et al., 1997; Shin et al., 2007 [14][51]

6.3 Controlled Dynamization

To mitigate catastrophic collapse in highly comminuted (Type III) fractures while still harnessing the benefits of axial compression, the paradigm of "Controlled Nail Dynamization" has been introduced. This involves the strategic placement of Poller (blocking) screws directly adjacent to the nail within the medullary canal prior to removing static interlocking screws. The Poller screws restrict movement in the coronal and sagittal planes while permitting pure longitudinal axial compression. Level IV — Frontiers in Surgery, 2025 [12]

7. Biological Viability: Weber-Cech Classification

Dynamization is purely mechanical; it provides no biological stimulation—it delivers no osteoinductive factors and no osteoprogenitor cells. Its success is entirely tethered to the underlying biological viability of the delayed union, codified by the Weber-Cech classification.

Table 7. Weber-Cech Classification & Dynamization Suitability
Non-Union Type Radiographic Features Biological State Dynamization Suitability
Hypertrophic Exuberant "elephant-foot" or "horse-shoe" callus; fails to bridge gap Hypervascular, richly perfused, teeming with active osteoblasts and chondrocytes ✅ Absolute Ideal Candidate (90–95% success)
Oligotrophic Incomplete, lesser callus formation Sufficient baseline vascularity and dormant osteogenic potential ✅ Suitable if FHI > 1.17
Atrophic Rounded, sclerotic, osteopenic fracture ends; absolutely no callus (mRUST = 4) Severe avascularity; biologically inert ⛔ Absolute Contraindication — requires exchange nailing + bone graft

For atrophic non-unions, modern EBM mandates Exchange Nailing with aggressive medullary canal over-reaming, which functions as an internal bone graft by scraping active osteoprogenitor cells from the isthmus. Alternatively, augmentative compression plate combined with massive ICBG or advanced orthobiologics (BMP-2, BMP-7) is required. Source [6][12]

8.1 The Role of Augmentative Plating

In highly recalcitrant aseptic femoral shaft non-unions where exchange nailing is at high risk for primary failure, Augmentative Plating (AP) combined with massive autologous bone grafting may be biologically and mechanically superior to EN. Studies reveal that AP yields a notably shorter mean time to union (5.39 ± 1.29 months vs. 7.38 ± 1.97 months for EN, p < 0.001) and approaches a near-perfect union rate (~100%) compared to ~89% for EN cohorts. Level I — Ru et al., 2016 [63]

9. Synthesized Clinical Algorithm

Step-by-Step Decision Algorithm for Dynamization

1
Confirm Timing: Is the patient at 12–24 weeks post-index IM nailing? If < 12 weeks → Observe and wait. If > 24 weeks → Consider exchange nailing. Level I
2
Assess Radiographic Healing: Calculate mRUST and RUST scores. If mRUST ≤ 8 or RUST ≤ 6 at 3–4 months → Proceed to Step 3. If scores are in the "Progressing" zone (mRUST 9–12) → Continue observation. Level II/III
3
Evaluate Biological Viability: Calculate FHI (callus diameter / diaphysis diameter). If FHI > 1.17 → High probability of success (> 90%). If FHI < 1.17 → Success drops to ~50%; consider alternative interventions. Level I
4
Assess Fracture Morphology: Classify by Winquist-Hansen. Type I/II (> 50% cortical contact) → Safe for dynamization. Type III → Consider controlled dynamization with Poller screws. Type IV → Absolute contraindication. Source [1][3]
5
Measure Fracture Gap: If gap < 2 mm → Excellent candidate. Gap 2–5 mm → Proceed cautiously. Gap > 5 mm → Contraindicated; requires exchange nailing or bone graft. Level II
6
Confirm Non-Union Type: Hypertrophic or oligotrophic → Proceed with dynamization. Atrophic (mRUST = 4, no callus) → Contraindicated; requires exchange nailing + reaming ± bone graft. Source [21]
7
Perform Dynamization: Remove appropriate (proximal or distal) static interlocking screw. Instruct partial weight-bearing with progressive loading. Monitor with serial radiographs every 4–6 weeks until mRUST ≥ 13 or RUST ≥ 10.
