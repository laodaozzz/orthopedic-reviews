Fixation Techniques for Comminuted Inferior Pole Patella Fractures

Comprehensive Evidence-Based Review & Interactive Decision Support Tool

I. Introduction & Background

1.1 Incidence & Epidemiology Not specified in source

Patellar fractures constitute approximately 1% of all skeletal fractures, with an incidence of 9.5–13.1 per 100,000 person-years.5,13 Fractures of the inferior pole of the patella (IPPFs) represent a particularly challenging subset, accounting for 5% to 22.4% of all patellar fractures. These are most often extra-articular avulsion-type injuries, classified under the AO/OTA system as 34-A1.5,6

The demographic profile shows a bimodal age distribution:

  • Younger peak (10–19 years): Males, high-energy trauma (sports, road traffic accidents).1
  • Elderly peak (>60 years): Females, low-energy mechanisms (simple fall from standing height). This predilection suggests IPPFs in this group should be considered osteoporotic/fragility fractures, with profound implications for fixation strategy.3,5

Mechanism of Injury

  • Direct Impact: A blow to the anterior knee (e.g., fall onto flexed knee, dashboard injury) generates compressive forces → comminuted or stellate fracture pattern. The extensor retinaculum may remain intact, contributing to stability.1
  • Indirect Force: Powerful eccentric quadriceps contraction with the knee in flexion creates immense tensile stress → transverse or avulsion fractures of the inferior pole. Nearly always associated with retinacular disruption and loss of extensor mechanism integrity.1

1.2 The Unique Clinical Challenge of Comminuted IPPFs Level IV–V

The surgical management of comminuted IPPFs is recognized as one of the most formidable challenges in orthopedic trauma.18 This difficulty arises from a confluence of adverse factors:

Anatomical Challenge: The inferior pole is the smallest portion of the patella; fractures here are almost invariably comminuted, often reduced to "bone dust."6 This leaves diminutive, devitalized fragments with exceptionally poor bone stock for conventional implant purchase.4
Biomechanical Challenge: The patella increases the lever arm of the quadriceps by 30–50%.1 The patellar tendon inserts directly on the inferior pole, subjecting any fixation construct to powerful, continuous tensile forces—especially during knee flexion.17 A displaced IPPF inherently disrupts this extensor mechanism (clinically confirmed by inability to perform a straight leg raise).1
Biological Challenge: In the large elderly cohort, bone is frequently osteopenic or osteoporotic.3 Failure mode is rarely implant breakage, but bone-implant interface failure (screw/wire cut-out or pull-out). Age >65 years is a strong independent predictor of fixation failure.3

This duality—young patients with high-energy mechanical challenges vs. elderly patients with biological challenges—drives the need for distinct fixation strategies, as a technique successful in one group may fail in the other.

Finally, the superficial, subcutaneous location of the patella means hardware prominence leads to painful irritation—the most common complication after patellar ORIF and the leading indication for reoperation.1

II. Current Surgical Fixation Techniques

2.1 Tension Band Constructs Level IV

The foundational AO principle: apply a fixation device to the tension (anterior) surface to convert tensile forces into dynamic compressive forces across the articular surface. This requires a stable anterior cortical buttress—often absent in highly comminuted IPPFs, rendering the principle mechanically unsound.21

2.1.1 Traditional Tension Band Wiring (TBW)

  • Procedure: Two parallel 1.6–2.0 mm K-wires across fragments, with an 18-gauge stainless-steel figure-of-eight wire anteriorly, tightened symmetrically.24
  • Limitations: Poor K-wire purchase in small fragments; high complication rate—symptomatic hardware requiring reoperation in up to 50–60% of patients.1

2.1.2 Modified Tension Band Techniques

  • Cannulated Screws + Wire/Suture: Two parallel 4.0 mm cannulated screws provide active interfragmentary compression; tension band through cannulae. Biomechanically superior to K-wire TBW.23,30
  • Suture as Tension Band: High-strength suture (#2/#5 Ethibond or FiberWire) replaces steel wire with comparable strength and dramatically reduced symptomatic hardware.6
  • Bioabsorbable Implants: PLGA bioabsorbable cannulated screws with high-strength suture—provides stability during healing, then resorbs completely.35

2.2 Plate & Screw Fixation Level IV

Plating departs from the tension band principle, functioning as a force-neutralizing or buttressing construct. A fixed-angle locking plate creates a rigid internal scaffold spanning the comminution zone—ideal for multi-fragmentary fractures (AO 34-C3).21

2.2.1 Anterior Plating (Mesh & Locking Plates)

  • Low-profile mesh plates, pre-contoured anatomical locking plates (e.g., Arthrex Patella SuturePlate II), star-shaped plates.36,37
  • Secured with multiple 2.3–3.0 mm locking/non-locking screws.
  • Biomechanically superior stability vs. TBW; allows aggressive early ROM.1,22

2.2.2 Rim Plating

  • A small straight LCP (2.4 mm) contoured into an arc, inserted along the fractured inferior rim, deep to the patellar tendon insertion.40
  • Acts as a direct buttress against the patellar tendon pull—targeted solution for IPPFs.
  • May be augmented with anterior suture tension band.40

2.3 Suture-Based Fixation Level IV

These techniques completely avoid metallic implants, eliminating hardware irritation—the most common complication.

2.3.1 Transosseous Suture (TTS) Repair

  • High-strength suture woven through the patellar tendon (Krackow locking stitch), passed through 2–3 longitudinal tunnels in the proximal fragment, tied over the superior cortical bridge.6
  • Biomechanically superior to suture anchors in osteoporotic bone—reliance on strong superior cortical bone rather than weak cancellous bone.45
  • More extensive dissection required.6

2.3.2 Suture Anchor (AS) Fixation

  • Anchors (4.5–5.5 mm metallic or bio-composite) inserted into proximal fragment cancellous bone; sutures passed through tendon/fragments and tied.6
  • Less invasive, shorter operative time.
  • Risk of anchor pull-out in osteoporotic bone—a significant concern in the elderly.20,45

2.3.3 "Suture Bridge" Techniques

  • Double-row fixation principle borrowed from rotator cuff surgery: internal row secures tendon/fragments, external row compresses against bone.50
  • Technically complex; limited clinical data.50

2.4 Hybrid & Combination Constructs Level IV

  • Hook Plate + Cable Cerclage: Maximized rigidity.54
  • Transosseous Sutures + Metal Tension Band: Supplemental stability.55
  • Mesh Plate + Cerclage/Sutures: Anatomical reduction + added security.38
  • Caution: Hybrid suture-and-wire constructs may cause patella baja (76.5% rate in one study).55

2.5 Fragment Excision & Tendon Advancement (Partial Patellectomy) Level IV

Salvage Procedure Only: Reserved for "unfixable" fractures (too small, too comminuted, or too osteoporotic). The comminuted fragments are excised; the patellar tendon is advanced proximally and reattached via transosseous tunnels or suture anchors.1,20

Consequences: Permanent patella baja, altered kinematics, and up to 50% decrease in extensor strength.1

III. Comparative Analysis of Outcomes

3.1 Clinical Outcomes Level IV

Functional Scores (Böstman, KOOS)

Most modern techniques achieve good-to-excellent Böstman scores (high 20s/30).7 Comparative data shows:

  • Cannulated screw TBW produces better scores than K-wire TBW.66
  • Suture-based techniques (TTS/AS) produce comparable Böstman/Lysholm scores to metallic fixation, with fewer hardware complications.6
  • A hybrid screw-and-anchor technique reported mean KOOS of 85.64

Range of Motion

Successful modern techniques achieve >120–130° of flexion with minimal extension lag.7 Techniques providing rigid stability (plating) are advantageous because they permit early mobilization—the most effective prophylaxis against arthrofibrosis.36

Postoperative Pain

Chronic anterior knee pain persists in up to 80% of patients long-term.23 The primary contributor is prominent hardware. Suture-based and low-profile plating techniques significantly reduce this burden.6

3.2 Radiological Outcomes Level IV

  • Union Rate: >95–99% across all modern ORIF techniques—a poor differentiator.1
  • Loss of Reduction: 0–22% incidence. Traditional TBW: up to 22–30% displacement with early mobilization. Plating: lower rates.1,30
  • Patella Baja (Insall-Salvati <0.8): Classic complication of partial patellectomy. Hybrid constructs showed 76.5% rate in one study.55 Separate vertical wiring had lower rates than TBW (25.6% vs. 52.1%).10
  • Post-Traumatic Osteoarthritis: Risk factors include residual articular incongruity, direct chondral damage, and altered kinematics.1

3.3 Complications Level IV

  • Hardware Irritation & Removal: Traditional TBW: 27–60%. All other techniques combined: ~10%. Suture fixation: soft-tissue irritation in 7.7% vs. hybrid 35.3% vs. pure metal 50.8%.57,67
  • Infection: Closed fractures: 0–5%; open fractures: 10–11%.1

IV. Biomechanical Evidence

4.1 Study Design Laboratory studies

Most biomechanical studies use human cadaveric knee specimens.21 Standardized fractures are created, repaired with different techniques, then tested:

  • Cyclic Loading: Simulates early rehab loads (e.g., 500–1000 cycles through ROM). Primary outcome: interfragmentary displacement ("gapping").22
  • Load-to-Failure: Monotonically increasing load until catastrophic failure. Determines ultimate strength.23

4.2 Key Biomechanical Findings

  • Screws > K-wires: Modified TBW with cannulated screws exhibits less displacement and higher load-to-failure than K-wire TBW.23
  • Plating > Tension Banding: In comminuted models, anterior plating significantly outperforms TBW, with up to 5× less fracture displacement.17,22
  • TTS > Suture Anchors in Osteoporotic Bone: Dargel et al. demonstrated TTS was significantly more stable and stronger; AS failed by anchor pull-out.45
  • Novel Plates: New-generation plates withstood 1130 N vs. 680 N for TBW.72

4.3 The Biomechanical-Clinical Paradox

Key Insight: Biomechanical superiority does not always translate to improved clinical outcomes.75 A large, rigid plate may be exceptionally strong in the lab but cause extensive soft-tissue stripping, hardware irritation, and stiffness in practice. Conversely, a "biologically friendly" suture construct may show slightly more micromotion but stimulate a robust healing response and yield superior patient-reported outcomes by eliminating hardware complications.29,55 The optimal clinical choice balances sufficient mechanical stability with soft-tissue preservation and patient-centric outcomes.

V. Treatment Algorithm

Evidence Gap: The majority of studies are retrospective case series and biomechanical investigations. There is a paucity of high-level RCTs comparing modern fixation strategies.3 This algorithm synthesizes the best available evidence with expert consensus.

5.1 Key Decision Factors

  • Comminution Degree: Simple transverse → TB may suffice. AO 34-C3 → rigid neutralization (plating) required.3
  • Fragment Size: CT scan essential. "Bone dust" → salvage procedure indicated.2,20
  • Bone Quality & Age: >65 years → osteoporotic bone → avoid suture anchors and K-wires; prefer TTS or locking plates.3,45
  • Activity Level: Young, high-demand → maximally stable construct. Elderly, low-demand → less invasive option acceptable.

5.2 Proposed Decision Algorithm

History, physical exam (straight leg raise?), AP + lateral radiographs.

Decision: Is the extensor mechanism intact (able to perform SLR) AND displacement <2–3 mm AND articular step-off <2 mm?
YES → Non-operative management (hinged brace, early controlled ROM, full weight-bearing).1
NO → Proceed to surgical planning (Step 2).

CT scan recommended for all operative candidates.2

Decision: Are inferior pole fragments fixable (sufficient size and substance)?
YES → Proceed to patient stratification (Step 3).
NO ("bone dust") → Partial patellectomy + tendon advancement. Counsel patient on permanent extensor weakness.20
Young Patient (Good Bone Quality, <60 years):
• Moderate comminution → Modified TBW with cannulated screws + suture tension band30
• Severe comminution (C3) → Anterior locking plate or rim plate22,40
• Hardware-less preference → Suture anchor fixation (bone quality permits)6
Elderly Patient (Osteoporotic, >65 years):
AVOID: Traditional K-wire TBW, Suture anchor (AS) as primary technique45
First-line: Transosseous suture (TTS) repair—anchored on superior cortical bridge45
Alternative: Anterior locking plate—distributes forces over wider area1

VI. Future Directions

6.1 Evidence Gaps

  • Paucity of RCTs: Most evidence is Level IV–V. A prospective RCT comparing anterior locking plate vs. TTS for comminuted IPPFs, stratified by age/bone quality, is urgently needed.2,3
  • Long-term follow-up (>5–10 years): Missing for many newer techniques.
  • Osteoporotic-population-specific research: The fastest-growing demographic is underserved.

6.2 Emerging Technologies

  • Bioabsorbable Implants: PLGA screws + suture tension band provide stability during healing, then resorb completely—eliminating long-term hardware issues.35
  • Advanced Suture/Tape Constructs: Suture tapes with broader profiles; all-suture "mesh" or "cobweb" techniques for low-profile fragment containment.51,52
  • Next-Generation Plates: More anatomically contoured, lower-profile locking plates with specialized hooks for inferior pole capture.72
The Ideal Implant Concept: Provides robust stability during the 8–12-week healing period, then effectively "disappears"—either through bioabsorption or by being so low-profile and biocompatible that it causes no long-term irritation.

VII. Conclusion

Comminuted IPPFs represent a formidable surgical challenge defined by small/fragile fragments, powerful distracting forces, and frequent occurrence in osteoporotic bone. Traditional TBW is biomechanically ill-suited and associated with unacceptably high hardware-related complications.

Key Evidence-Based Conclusions:

  • Young, active patients with severe comminution: Anterior locking plate fixation provides the superior rigidity needed for early aggressive rehabilitation and high-level functional return.Level IV
  • Elderly patients with osteoporotic bone: Transosseous suture (TTS) repair is emerging as the most logical technique—biomechanically sound in poor-quality bone, anchoring on the strong cortical bridge rather than weak cancellous bone, and eliminating hardware-related morbidity.Level IV–V
  • Partial patellectomy remains a salvage procedure of last resort, with predictable permanent extensor weakness.
  • The overarching principle guiding successful treatment is achieving fixation stable enough to permit immediate active range of motion—the most effective prophylaxis against arthrofibrosis and the surest path to optimizing functional outcomes.
