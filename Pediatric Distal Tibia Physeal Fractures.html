1. Introduction & Epidemiological Context

Pediatric ankle fractures represent a significant and frequent pathology within pediatric orthopaedics, constituting a complex clinical challenge due to involvement of the physis (growth plate). These injuries account for approximately 5% of all pediatric fractures and roughly 15–20% of all physeal injuries, making the distal tibia the second most common site of physeal injury after the distal radius.1 Consensus

The distal tibial physis is a robust engine of growth, responsible for approximately 45% of total longitudinal tibial growth and contributing 15–20% of overall lower-extremity length.3 It grows at approximately 3–4 mm per year during adolescence, with growth typically continuing until age 14 in girls and age 16 in boys.3

⚠ Clinical Pearl
Improper treatment or unrecognized physeal damage can lead to leg-length discrepancy, angular deformity, and joint incongruity resulting in early post-traumatic arthritis.1

Epidemiology

  • Peak age: 10–15 years, with distinct male predominance.3
  • Common mechanism: sports-related trauma or vigorous play involving twisting (supination-inversion, supination-external rotation) applied to a planted foot.8
  • Pediatric ligaments (deltoid and lateral complexes) are biomechanically stronger than the cartilaginous physis — forces that cause sprains in adults sheared the physis in children.1
⚑ Axiom in Pediatric Trauma
"An ankle sprain is a diagnosis of exclusion." Clinical tenderness over the physis must be treated as a nondisplaced fracture until proven otherwise.1

This report synthesizes clinical practice guidelines and expert consensus from the American Academy of Orthopaedic Surgeons (AAOS), the Pediatric Orthopaedic Society of North America (POSNA), and the European Paediatric Orthopaedic Society (EPOS).1 Expert Consensus

2. Developmental Anatomy & Biomechanics

The ossification center of the distal tibial epiphysis typically appears between 6 and 24 months of age, expanding to form the medial malleolus by age 7–8.11

2.1 Sequence of Physeal Closure

Unlike the proximal tibia or distal femur (which close relatively symmetrically), the distal tibial physis fuses in a predictable, asymmetrical topographic sequence over an 18-month transitional period.4 Level III

  1. Central Zone: Fusion initiates in the middle of the physis (Kump's bump).
  2. Anteromedial Zone: Closure extends anteriorly and medially.
  3. Posteromedial Zone: Fusion progresses to the posterior aspect.
  4. Anterolateral Zone: The final portion to fuse.4
🔑 Key Concept — The Transitional Window
During the "transitional" phase (typically ages 12–15), the medial physis is fused and stable, while the anterolateral portion remains open and weak. External rotation torque avulses the anterolateral epiphyseal fragment → Tillaux fracture. If the force propagates through the posterior metaphysis → Triplane fracture.13,15

2.2 Ligamentous Anatomy & Mechanisms

The anterior inferior tibiofibular ligament (AITFL) attaches to the anterolateral tibial epiphysis (Chaput tubercle) and the distal fibula (Wagstaffe tubercle).15 In transitional fractures, the AITFL acts as a tether. In younger children, rotational forces are transmitted to the physis before the ligaments fail.

Fibular–tibial physis relationship: The distal fibular physis closes approximately 12–24 months after the distal tibial physis.12 This discrepancy means that in an adolescent with a closing tibial physis (susceptible to Tillaux/Triplane injuries), the fibular physis often remains open and vulnerable to standard Salter-Harris fractures.

3. Classification Systems

3.1 Salter-Harris Classification

The Salter-Harris (SH) system categorizes injuries based on anatomical involvement of the physis, epiphysis, and metaphysis. It is the universal language for prognosticating growth arrest risk.1

Table 1 — Salter-Harris Classification of Distal Tibial Physeal Fractures
Type Description Incidence Prognostic Significance
I Fracture purely through the physis; separation of epiphysis from metaphysis. Often radiographically occult. ~15% Low risk of arrest if nondisplaced; requires clinical diagnosis (physeal tenderness).1
II Traverses physis, exits through metaphysis → "Thurston-Holland" fragment. ~40–45% Most common. Generally good prognosis unless severe displacement.1,12
III Through physis, exits vertically through epiphysis into joint. Includes Tillaux fractures. ~25% Intra-articular. High risk of arthritis if unreduced; high risk of physeal bar.1,15
IV Vertical fracture traversing metaphysis, physis, and epiphysis. Includes Triplane and medial malleolar shear fractures. ~25% High risk of bony bar formation and joint incongruity. Anatomic reduction required.6
V Crush injury (compression) to the physis. Difficult to diagnose acutely. ~1% Poor prognosis; nearly universal growth arrest.17

3.2 Dias-Tachdjian Classification

Adapted from the Lauge-Hansen adult classification, this system classifies fractures by foot position at injury and direction of the deforming force. It is favored by POSNA and EPOS for determining the "reverse" maneuver required for closed reduction.1

Supination-Inversion (SI)

  • Stage I: Inversion force → distal fibular physis separation (SH I or II).
  • Stage II: Talus impacts medial malleolus → crushing vertical fracture of distal tibial epiphysis (SH III or IV). Notoriously prone to medial physeal bar and progressive varus deformity.1

Supination-Plantar Flexion (SPF)

  • Produces SH II fracture of distal tibia with posterior displacement. AP radiographs may be subtle; lateral view reveals the Thurston-Holland fragment displacement.1

Supination-External Rotation (SER)

  • Stage I: Spiral SH II fracture of distal tibia (starting anterolateral, running posteromedial).
  • Stage II: Spiral fracture of the fibular diaphysis (typically high).1

Pronation-Eversion-External Rotation (PEER)

  • Transverse fracture of distal fibula (or SH I/II) + SH II fracture of distal tibia, often with lateral displacement.1

4. Clinical & Radiographic Assessment

4.1 Physical Examination

Focus on localizing maximal tenderness. The Ottawa Ankle Rules have been validated in children to reduce unnecessary radiation. Radiography is indicated if there is inability to bear weight (walk four steps) or bone tenderness at the posterior edge or tip of either malleolus.1 Level II

Physeal tenderness in a child with negative X-rays should be managed as a nondisplaced SH I fracture.10

4.2 Imaging Modalities

Plain Radiography

Standard "ankle trauma series": AP, Lateral, and Mortise views.4

  • AP: Medial and lateral malleoli, physeal width.
  • Lateral: Thurston-Holland fragment (SH II), posterior displacement (SPF).4
  • Mortise: Joint space, syndesmotic integrity, subtle transitional fractures.4

Computed Tomography (CT)

Expert consensus from AAOS, POSNA, and EPOS strongly supports the low-threshold use of CT for adolescent ankle fractures.1 Consensus

  • Indications: Mandatory for all transitional fractures (Tillaux, Triplane) and any intra-articular fracture (SH III/IV) where displacement is ambiguous.1
  • Utility: Superior to plain film for quantifying articular step-off (key surgical indication), assessing fracture fragment number, and planning percutaneous screw trajectory.4
ℹ The Mercedes-Benz Sign
On axial CT of a triplane fracture, fracture lines radiating from the center resemble the Mercedes-Benz logo, representing the multiplanar nature (sagittal, coronal, transverse).4

Magnetic Resonance Imaging (MRI)

Rarely indicated for acute management. Studies show that treating clinically suspected SH I fractures with immobilization yields outcomes equivalent to MRI-confirmed cases, rendering MRI unnecessary for routine care.1 Level II

5. Management: Skeletally Immature Child (High Remodeling Potential)

In children with significant growth remaining (typically boys <12 years, girls <10 years), management prioritizes preservation of length and axial alignment, leveraging the biological potential of the physis.22

5.1 Remodeling Potential & Acceptable Alignment

The distal tibia possesses robust remodeling capacity, particularly in the sagittal plane (plane of joint motion). However, rotational deformities do not remodel and must be corrected acutely.5 Level III

5.2 Extra-Articular Fractures (SH I & II)

Non-Displaced Fractures

A controversy exists regarding cast type:

  • Long Leg Cast (LLC): Traditionally recommended to neutralize the gastrocnemius and control rotation.9
  • Short Leg Cast (SLC): Recent multicenter evidence and EPOS consensus suggest that for stable, reduced SH II fractures, SLC is equally effective while allowing earlier mobilization.1,28,29 Level II

Duration: typically 3–4 weeks due to rapid physeal healing.6

Displaced Fractures

  • Closed Reduction: Under conscious sedation or general anesthesia. The maneuver should reverse the Dias-Tachdjian mechanism (e.g., eversion for supination-inversion).6
  • "Two-Attempt" Rule: AAOS and POSNA emphasize a strict limit. Forceful, repeated manipulations destroy germinal cells and cause iatrogenic growth arrest. If acceptable reduction is not achieved after one or two gentle attempts, open reduction is indicated.6,17 Consensus
⚑ Periosteal Interposition
A common cause of irreducible SH II fractures is entrapment of the periosteum at the fracture site, suggested by residual physeal widening >3 mm after reduction. This necessitates open reduction to extract the flap.6

5.3 Intra-Articular Fractures (SH III & IV)

In younger children, SH III and IV fractures often manifest as medial malleolar injuries (MacFarland fractures) caused by supination-inversion forces.

  • Indication for Surgery: Any displacement >2 mm is an absolute indication for ORIF.6 Consensus
  • Fixation Technique: Ideally parallel to the physis (epiphyseal or metaphyseal screws). If crossing the physis is mandated by fracture pattern, use smooth K-wires or absorbable pins. Transphyseal screws are contraindicated in skeletally immature patients.6

6. Management: Adolescent (Transitional Fractures)

As the distal tibial physis begins asymmetric closure, remodeling potential is negligible. The primary goal shifts to anatomic restoration of articular congruity to prevent long-term degenerative joint disease.

6.1 The "2 mm Rule" & Emerging Evidence

📊 Surgical Threshold Debate
  • Historical / Expert Consensus: Articular displacement >2 mm (gap or step-off) on CT warrants surgical reduction.4 Consensus
  • Emerging Evidence: A lenient threshold of 2.5 mm may yield equivalent functional outcomes. Patients treated non-operatively with gaps <2.5 mm demonstrated superior sports-specific outcomes compared to those with larger gaps.31 Level III
  • Current Practice: Prevailing consensus remains reduce and fix if >2 mm to minimize post-traumatic arthritis risk.13

6.2 Tillaux Fractures

An avulsion of the anterolateral tibial epiphysis (SH III) mediated by the AITFL during external rotation.15

  • Non-Operative: Nondisplaced (<2 mm on CT). Long leg cast for 4 weeks (control rotation) → walking boot.13
  • Reduction: Internal rotation of the foot (relaxes AITFL) + direct pressure over anterolateral fragment. Post-reduction CT mandatory.15
  • Surgical Fixation (if closed reduction fails, >2 mm gap persists):
    • Anterolateral approach (protect superficial peroneal nerve).
    • 4.0 mm cannulated screw from anterolateral fragment into posterior metaphysis or medial epiphysis. Crossing the physis is generally acceptable in this specific age group.15

6.3 Triplane Fractures

A complex SH IV injury with components in three planes: transverse (physis), sagittal (epiphysis), and coronal (metaphysis).4

Subtypes

  • 2-Part: Most common. Medial epiphysis attached to tibia; lateral epiphysis + posterior metaphysis move as a unit.
  • 3-Part: Separate anterolateral epiphyseal fragment (Tillaux-like) + medial epiphyseal fragment + posterior metaphyseal fragment.
  • 4-Part: Includes comminution.4

Reduction Maneuver

  • Lateral Triplane: Internal rotation + dorsiflexion.
  • Medial Triplane: Eversion + external rotation (rare).4

Surgical Technique

  • Order of Fixation: Fix fibula first (if fractured) → restore length and rotation → tibial reduction (CRPP if anatomic; ORIF via anterolateral approach if needed).
  • Screw Trajectory: Epiphyseal screws placed transversely (parallel to joint) to close sagittal gap. Metaphyseal screws placed anterior-to-posterior to close coronal gap.4

8. Complications & Sequelae

8.1 Premature Physeal Closure (PPC)

The most significant complication, with rates ranging from 2% to >40% depending on injury severity and energy.30

Risk Factors

  • High-energy mechanism
  • Initial displacement >3–4 mm
  • SH III/IV patterns
  • Repeated reduction attempts6

Surveillance

Monitor for at least 1 year or until skeletal maturity. Radiographs at 6 and 12 months should be scrutinized for Harris Growth Lines (Park-Harris lines). If these lines remain parallel to the physis → normal growth; if they converge → bony bar forming.6

Management of PPC

  • Bar Resection (Langenskiöld procedure): Indicated if bony bar involves <50% of the physis and patient has >2 years of growth remaining. Bar is resected and void filled with fat graft or cranioplast.6
  • Epiphysiodesis: If bar is >50%, arrest is completed surgically (often contralaterally) to prevent angular deformity; leg-length discrepancy managed separately.17

8.2 Angular Deformity

  • Varus: Most common angular complication — medial physeal arrest (supination-inversion) or non-union of medial malleolus.17
  • Valgus: Distal fibular arrest (tethering lateral side) or tibial overgrowth.
  • Treatment: Guided growth (tension band plates / 8-plates) is gold standard in children with remaining growth. Osteotomy for skeletally mature adolescents.9

8.3 Post-Traumatic Arthritis

A long-term consequence of residual articular incongruity. Risk directly correlated with quality of reduction in SH III and IV fractures. Step-offs >2 mm lead to significantly worse functional outcomes and higher degenerative changes.4 Level III

9. Conclusion

The management of pediatric distal tibial and fibular fractures requires a stratified approach based strictly on skeletal maturity:

  • Younger child: Prioritize physis preservation. Limit reduction attempts to prevent iatrogenic arrest. Rely on metaphyseal remodeling potential for non-rotational deformities.
  • Adolescent: Treatment philosophy mirrors adult ankle trauma — anatomic restoration of the articular surface is paramount. Transitional fractures (Tillaux, Triplane) demand advanced imaging (CT) and precise surgical reduction.

Across all age groups, the unifying principle of "respecting the physis" — whether through gentle reduction maneuvers or physeal-sparing fixation — remains the cornerstone of expert consensus and clinical guidelines.1,6
