1 Pathophysiology & Biomechanics of the Injury Triad

The clinical scenario of a chronic posterior cruciate ligament (PCL) avulsion fracture coexisting with a physeal bar and progressive angular deformity in a skeletally immature patient represents a formidable orthopaedic challenge. This condition is not a static set of injuries but a dynamic pathophysiological cascade where instability, growth arrest, and deformity are mutually reinforcing.[1, 4]

1.1 The Primary Insult: Pediatric PCL Avulsion Fractures

Injuries to the PCL in children are uncommon, attributed to the unique biomechanical properties of the paediatric skeleton.[1] The principle of the "weakest link" dictates the injury pattern: in a child with open physes, the ligamentous structures are often stronger and more resilient than the adjacent cartilaginous physis and epiphyseal bone.[4]

While an adult might sustain a mid-substance PCL tear from a posterior-directed force on a flexed knee[6], a child subjected to the same forces is more likely to experience an avulsion fracture — the intact, robust PCL transmits tensile force to its bony insertion, pulling a fragment of bone from the epiphysis.[1]

⚠️ Salter-Harris Classification is Prognostically Critical

The tibial-sided avulsion is the classic presentation. Depending on the fracture pattern and its propagation relative to the proximal tibial physis, it is often classified as a Salter-Harris Type III (physis + epiphysis) or, more devastatingly, a Type IV (metaphysis + physis + epiphysis) injury.[7] This classification is not merely academic — it signifies a direct violation of the physeal cartilage and predicts growth arrest risk.

1.2 The Secondary Complication: Post-Traumatic Physeal Bar Formation

Physeal bar formation is the most dreaded complication of a paediatric physeal fracture and is the engine of the subsequent deformity.[9] A physeal bar is a bridge of bone or fibrous tissue that tethers the epiphysis to the metaphysis, obliterating growth potential at that location.[7]

Mechanisms of Bar Formation:

  1. Traumatic: The initial fracture healing process itself causes the bar if fracture lines crossing the physis are not anatomically realigned — bone callus spans the physeal gap.[7]
  2. Iatrogenic (Critical & Preventable): During acute ORIF of the PCL avulsion fragment, surgical hardware (K-wires, screws) may inadvertently cross the physis, serving as a nidus for bar formation and growth arrest.[2]
🔬 Anatomical Consideration

The proximal tibial physis has a complex, undulating 3D topography with "mammillary body" interdigitations.[13] While this provides enhanced resistance to shear stress, it also means that injuries are more likely to create a physeal bar compared to simpler physeal structures.

1.3 The Tertiary Consequence: Progressive Angular Deformity

Once formed, the physeal bar acts as a "growth chokehold".[14] Healthy physis continues to grow while the tethered segment remains static, causing differential growth and progressive angular deformity.[7]

  • Sagittal Plane Deformity (Procurvatum): The PCL avulses from the posterior facet of the tibial plateau.[16] A physeal bar in this posterior location tethers posterior growth while the anterior physis grows normally → pathologic increase in posterior tibial slope.[4]
  • Coronal Plane Deformity (Varus/Valgus): If the posterior bar is asymmetric (e.g., postero-medial or postero-lateral), a multi-planar deformity develops, such as procurvatum combined with tibia vara.[17]

1.4 The Biomechanical Vicious Cycle

⚠️ THE CRITICAL PRINCIPLE

The PCL-deficient knee demonstrates uncontrolled posterior tibial translation (posterior sag).[6] Simultaneously, the specific deformity created — procurvatum (increased posterior tibial slope) — exacerbates this very instability. Biomechanical studies confirm that increasing the posterior tibial slope dramatically increases posterior sag and in situ forces on the PCL or graft.[23]

Attempting a PCL reconstruction in a tibia with uncorrected procurvatum is biomechanically futile. The graft will be placed under excessive, unphysiologic tension and is destined for failure.[23] The malalignment MUST be corrected before or during any attempt to restore ligamentous stability.

2 Comprehensive Diagnostic & Pre-Operative Planning

Managing this complex triad requires a meticulous, multi-modal diagnostic workup. No single imaging study can adequately capture all three pathologies. The goal is to precisely quantify each component to inform the surgical strategy.[3]

2.1 Advanced Imaging: Mapping the Triad

2.1.1 Radiographic Deformity Analysis

Simple AP and lateral knee radiographs may reveal the chronic displaced avulsed fragment (sometimes a subtle bone fleck)[16] or indirect signs of PCL insufficiency (posterior sag).[16]

The gold standard for quantifying deformity is a standing full-length AP lower extremity radiograph (orthoroentgenogram)[26], enabling critical measurements:

  • Mechanical Axis Deviation (MAD): Quantifies overall varus/valgus thrust.[28]
  • Anatomical Medial Proximal Tibial Angle (aMPTA): Coronal plane deformity at the proximal tibia.[29]
  • Posterior Proximal Tibial Angle (PPTA): Measured on lateral view — definitive measure of procurvatum deformity / posterior tibial slope.[29]

2.1.2 CT with 3D Reconstruction

CT is the definitive modality for physeal bar mapping.[5] Thin-slice (1 mm) scans through the proximal tibial physis create 3D reconstructions and a 2D "physeal map."[32] This allows calculation of the percentage of physis involved by the bar — arguably the most critical variable in the entire treatment algorithm.[31] CT also characterizes the chronic bony avulsion fragment (size, comminution, displacement, nonunion).[3]

2.1.3 MRI

MRI is essential for:[5]

  1. Ligament Assessment: Evaluates PCL status (healed but elongated vs. frank nonunion) and associated meniscal/chondral/ligament damage.[16]
  2. Physeal Health: The only modality that visualizes a fibrous physeal bar (radiolucent on CT but equally effective at tethering growth).[35] Assesses the signal intensity and viability of the remaining open physis.[36]

2.3 Determining the "Growth Chokehold": The Biologic Clock

The entire treatment algorithm bifurcates based on one master variable: remaining growth potential, determined not by chronologic age but by skeletal age (bone age).[9]

Assessment methods include:

  • Left hand-and-wrist radiograph assessed via Greulich-Pyle atlas[41] or Sanders Maturity Scale.[43]
  • Growth-remaining charts: Green-Anderson charts[9], Moseley straight-line graph[41], and White-Menelaus method.[41]
📋 Clinical Threshold

A physeal bar resection is generally only indicated if the patient has at least 2 years of growth remaining or an anticipated >2.5 cm of growth.[9]

3 Operative Principles: A Staged or Simultaneous Approach

The surgical plan must address three distinct problems. The following "tools" are available, and their selection and sequencing are dictated by the diagnostic findings.[4, 9]

3.1 Principle 1: Restoring Physeal Potential (Physeal Bar Resection)

Procedure: Physeal Bar Resection (Langenskiöld procedure).[9]

Goal: Surgically excise the bony or fibrous bridge, "un-tethering" the physis to allow resumption of normal growth and gradual deformity correction.[9]

🚨 Critical Indications (Both MUST Be Met)

1. Remaining Growth: >2 years or >2.5 cm anticipated.[9]

2. Bar Size: <50% (ideally <30%) of total physeal area on CT map.[9] Resection of bars >50% has a high failure rate and is generally contraindicated.

Surgical Technique:

  • A 3D-printed model from CT data can be invaluable for planning.[31]
  • For a central bar, a metaphyseal cortical window is created; the bar is resected with a high-speed burr under fluoroscopic or arthroscopic guidance.[34]
  • Resection is complete when healthy white physeal cartilage surrounds the defect.[9]
  • Arthroscopic visualization provides superior illumination with minimal morbidity.[11]

Interposition Materials:

  • Autologous Fat: Most common, easily harvested, effective.[9]
  • Methylmethacrylate (Bone Cement): Inert, provides structural support.[9]
  • Other: Silicone rubber (Silastic), bone wax — also described.[31] Superiority of any one material remains controversial.[52]

3.2 Principle 2: Correcting Limb Alignment

Option A: Guided Growth (Hemiepiphysiodesis)

This technique harnesses remaining growth.[53] A tension-band plate (e.g., "8-plate") or staples are applied to the convex (long) side of the deformity, tethering growth there. The concave (short) side grows and "catches up," gradually correcting the deformity.[15]

  • Indication: Younger children with sufficient growth remaining.[58]
  • Applicable to sagittal plane: for procurvatum, a plate placed anteriorly slows anterior growth.[61]

Option B: Proximal Tibial Osteotomy (HTO)

Involves surgically cutting the bone, realigning acutely, and fixing with plates/screws/external fixation.[58]

Indications:

  1. At or near skeletal maturity (<2 years growth remaining).[58]
  2. Severe angulation (>20°) unlikely to fully correct with guided growth.[9]
  3. Sagittal plane deformity — HTO is the primary tool for acutely correcting posterior tibial slope.[25]
💡 The "Dual-Purpose" Slope-Changing HTO

An anterior-based closing wedge HTO simultaneously decreases the posterior tibial slope (correcting procurvatum) and corrects concomitant varus/valgus alignment.[25] This is a critical technical consideration for restoring normal knee kinematics and protecting the future PCL graft.[4, 63]

3.3 Principle 3: Restoring Knee Stability (Chronic PCL)

Non-Operative Management Failure

Non-operative treatment is insufficient for a chronic case with established instability, healed deformity, and failed primary fixation, leading to high rates of residual instability, meniscal tears, and poor outcomes.[66]

Physeal-Sparing PCL Reconstruction Techniques:

  • All-Epiphyseal: Tunnels drilled entirely within the epiphysis, never crossing the physis.[72]
  • All-Inside, Physeal-Sparing: Retrograde drilling creates sockets rather than full tunnels.[73]
  • "Steep Tunnel" Drilling: Transphyseal but at >60° angle to minimize physeal cross-section.[21]
  • Suture-Based / Suture Tape Augmentation (STA): "Braces" a PCL repair in a physeal-sparing manner.[76, 77]

Staged Reconstruction (Delay until Maturity):

A valid and often safer principle is to delay ligament reconstruction entirely until skeletal maturity, then perform a definitive "adult-style" PCL reconstruction. This eliminates all risk of iatrogenic physeal injury from graft tunnels.[4]

4.2 Scenario A: "Growth Restoration" Pathway

SCENARIO A

Profile: Younger child (e.g., Skeletal Age <14 males, <12 females), significant growth remaining (>2–3 years).

Key Finding: Resectable physeal bar (<50% of physeal area).[31]

Stage 1: Alignment & Growth Correction

Combined: Physeal Bar Resection (Langenskiöld) + Fat Interposition + Guided Growth (8-Plate on convex side).[31, 50, 79]

Rationale: Bar resection permits tethered physis to resume growth[44]; the 8-plate directs resumed growth to gradually correct angular deformity.[79]

↓

Interim (6–24 months)

Non-operative PCL management (bracing, activity modification).[4] Serial long-axis radiographs monitor deformity correction. 8-plate removed once alignment is neutral.

↓

Stage 2: Stability Restoration

Physeal-Sparing PCL Reconstruction.[70, 72] Performed in a mechanically favourable, realigned limb — drastically increasing graft success.

4.3 Scenario B: "Deformity Correction" Pathway

SCENARIO B

Profile: Older adolescent, limited growth remaining (<2 years).

Key Finding: Non-resectable bar (>50%) or insufficient growth for resection to be worthwhile.[9]

Stage 1: Definitive Alignment Correction

Corrective Proximal Tibial Osteotomy (HTO) + Completion Epiphysiodesis.[9, 25, 58]

Rationale: HTO acutely corrects coronal and sagittal (procurvatum/slope) deformity.[25] Completion epiphysiodesis permanently stops all growth, preventing further asymmetric growth or new LLD.[9]

↓

Interim (3–6 months)

Allow osteotomy to fully consolidate.

↓

Stage 2: Stability Restoration

Adult-Style PCL Reconstruction.[4, 84] Physis is now iatrogenically closed — surgeon free to use robust, standard transphyseal tunnels without growth disturbance risk.

4.4 Scenario C: Single-Stage Approach (High-Risk, Not Recommended)

⚠️ Not Generally Recommended

Combining Corrective HTO + Completion Epiphysiodesis + PCL Reconstruction in one operation avoids a second surgery[84] but is technically demanding with significantly higher complication rates. A systematic review of multi-ligament injuries found that staged reconstruction may yield better functional outcomes and lower rates of arthrofibrosis (post-operative stiffness).[75]

5.2 Post-Operative Rehabilitation

After Stage 1 (Bone Work): Focus on bone healing.[51] Protected/non-weight-bearing period with early, gentle ROM to prevent stiffness.

After Stage 2 (Ligament): Focus on graft protection. PCL rehab emphasises immobilisation in extension and rigorous early quadriceps strengthening — the primary dynamic stabiliser against posterior tibial sag.[25]

5.3 Long-Term Outcomes

Reported functional outcomes for surgically treated paediatric PCL injuries are generally good: high rates of return to sport and good functional scores (Lysholm, Pedi-IKDC).[1] Physeal-sparing techniques carry very low risk of iatrogenic growth arrest.[1]

⚠️ The Ultimate Concern: Premature Osteoarthritis

Despite favourable functional outcomes, the most significant long-term concern is premature post-traumatic OA.[19] Even in surgically treated paediatric PCL cases, studies report radiographic signs of OA in 21–50% of patients at mid-to-long-term follow-up.[21]

This underscores the ultimate goal: this complex, multi-stage algorithm is effectively a salvage procedure.[84] The primary long-term objective is to restore normal limb alignment and knee kinematics to delay or prevent debilitating, early-onset OA.[19]

6 Conclusion & Key Takeaways

The management of a chronic PCL avulsion with coexisting physeal bar and angular deformity is defined by a dynamic pathophysiological cascade. The instability and deformity are biomechanically linked — the procurvatum deformity is a "graft-killing" lesion.[23]

🎯 Central Recommendations

1. Alignment must be restored FIRST before any ligament reconstruction.[25]

2. Two master variables determine the path: % physeal bar involvement[31] and skeletal age / remaining growth.[9]

3. Younger patients (<50% bar, >2 years growth): "Growth Restoration" — bar resection + guided growth + physeal-sparing PCL reconstruction.[50]

4. Older patients (>50% bar, <2 years growth): "Deformity Correction" — completion epiphysiodesis + slope-changing HTO + adult-style PCL reconstruction.[4]

5. Long-term monitoring until skeletal maturity and beyond is mandatory due to high risk of premature OA.[3, 21]
