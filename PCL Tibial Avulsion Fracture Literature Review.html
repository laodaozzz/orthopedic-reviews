Biomechanics & Pathoanatomy

Anatomy and Kinematics of the Native PCL

The PCL is universally recognized as the strongest and thickest intra-articular ligament in the human knee, possessing an ultimate tensile strength approximately twice that of the ACL.L-II [2] The ligament originates from the anterolateral aspect of the medial femoral condyle and inserts into a depressed bony facet—the posterior intercondylar fossa (fovea)—on the posterior proximal tibia.L-II [6]

Structurally, the PCL is dichotomized into two primary bundles:

  • Anterolateral bundle (ALB): The larger, more robust bundle; becomes maximally taut during deep knee flexion.
  • Posteromedial bundle (PMB): The comparatively smaller bundle; achieves peak tension in full extension.

This synergistic architecture ensures continuous, dynamic stability across the entire arc of knee motion, preventing posterior tibial translation beneath the femoral condyles.

Critical Neurovascular Anatomy: The distance between the posterior knee capsule and the popliteal artery changes dynamically with knee positioning.L-II [8]
  • Full extension: Popliteal artery is only 3.9 mm from the posterior capsule at the joint line.
  • 90° flexion: Safe distance increases to approximately 7.6 mm.
All posterior dissection, trans-septal portal creation, and trans-tibial tunnel drilling must be performed at 90° flexion to mitigate iatrogenic neurovascular injury.

Mechanisms of Injury

PCL tibial avulsion fractures predominantly occur in the young, active adult population as a consequence of high-energy trauma.L-II [9] This stands in contrast to ACL tibial avulsion fractures (tibial spine avulsions), which are far more prevalent in pediatric populations where the native ligament is stronger than the unfused proximal tibial physis.L-II [9]

The classic mechanism is the "dashboard injury"—a massive, posteriorly directed force to the anterior proximal tibia with the knee in 90° of flexion during motor vehicle or motorcycle collisions.L-II [11,12] Alternative non-contact mechanisms include extreme hyperflexion or severe hyperextension during contact sports.L-II [11]

When acute tensile forces exceed the ultimate failure load of subchondral bone but remain below mid-substance ligament failure, a distinct osteochondral fragment is avulsed—a "peel-off" injury.L-II [14] The PCL substance remains structurally intact and attached to the displaced fragment, yet functionally incompetent due to loss of its osseous anchor.L-II [12]

Biomechanical Consequences of Untreated Injury: Loss of PCL integrity leads to pathologically increased posterior tibial translation, severely altered joint contact mechanics, focal elevation in medial tibiofemoral and patellofemoral contact pressures, and reliable progression to chronic instability, secondary meniscal tears, and premature, irreversible osteoarthritis.L-II [8]

Critical Size & Thresholds for Biomechanical Instability

Historically, any fracture displaced > 3–5 mm was an absolute indication for surgical fixation.L-II [2] Contemporary studies suggest acute PCL avulsions with up to 5 mm (and in select cohorts up to 6.7 mm) of posterior displacement may be managed nonoperatively with rigid immobilization in 15° flexion.L-II [13]

However, in the reality of high-energy trauma, most clinically apparent avulsions present with displacement significantly exceeding 10 mm, easily visualized on lateral radiographs as complete discontinuity of the posterior tibial contour.L-II [18,10] For such injuries, surgical fixation is unconditionally mandated.

Beyond displacement, the volumetric footprint of the fragment dictates construct selection:

  • Minimum fragment diameter of 1.0 cm is generally required for traditional cannulated lag screw fixation.L-II [11]
  • Fragments below this critical dimension, or those exhibiting significant comminution, are prone to iatrogenic fragmentation upon screw insertion.L-II [11]
  • Small or comminuted avulsions mandate load-distributing fixation strategies (suture bridges or suspensory cortical buttons).L-II [19]

Classification Systems

Accurate classification is essential for standardizing research, anticipating concomitant injuries, and guiding surgical decision-making. Three primary systems are discussed below.

Modified Meyers & McKeever Classification

Originally designed for ACL tibial spine avulsions in pediatric patients, this legacy system has been historically extrapolated to PCL avulsions.L-III [7]

  • Type I: Non-displaced or minimally displaced (<3 mm).L-III [21]
  • Type II: Displaced with intact anterior or posterior cortical hinge.L-III [21]
  • Type III: Completely displaced, entirely devoid of osseous contact.L-III [21]
  • Type III+ / Type IV: Completely displaced, structurally rotated, or heavily comminuted.L-III [21]
Limitation: This system relies exclusively on 2D lateral radiographs, intrinsically fails to appreciate the 3D volumetric footprint of the PCL insertion, and frequently misses hidden comminution.L-III [7]

Griffith Classification (2004)

Proposed specifically for PCL avulsion injuries, this system directly correlates morphology with treatment algorithms.L-I [14,23]

  • Type I (Minimally Displaced): Fragment remains in situ. Universally managed nonoperatively with immobilization.L-I [13]
  • Type II (Hinged Avulsion): Slight posterior elevation with intact anterior cortical hinge. Equivocal management—conservative or arthroscopic based on sag severity.L-I [13]
  • Type III (Completely Detached): Total loss of osseous continuity. PCL tension pulls fragment into the joint space. Unequivocally requires surgical fixation.L-I [13]

Novel 3D-CT Based Classifications

Recent literature emphasizes routine 3D-CT for meticulous preoperative planning, categorizing fractures by number of discrete fracture lines and anatomical involvement of medial/lateral articular surfaces.L-II [18,19]

  • Type III (Complex/Comminuted): Two or more fracture lines—explicitly precludes cannulated screws due to catastrophic failure risk.L-II [19]
  • Type IV: Fractures extending to weight-bearing articular cartilage of the medial or lateral tibial plateaus, demanding step-free anatomical reduction.L-II [7]

Surgical Interventions: Open vs. Arthroscopic

The primary surgical objective is precise, anatomic reduction with rigid internal fixation to permit early ROM protocols and prevent arthrofibrosis. The posterior approach is technically demanding due to the deep location of the PCL fovea and proximity to the popliteal neurovascular bundle.L-II [3]

Open Posterior Approach (ORIF)

The modified posteromedial approach (Burkhalter / inverted L-shaped incision) remains utilized in settings lacking arthroscopic infrastructure or in massive multi-fragmentary fractures.L-II [27] Key steps include:

  1. Patient prone; curvilinear incision along medial border of the medial gastrocnemius head.L-II [33]
  2. Blunt dissection of the fascial interval between medial gastrocnemius and semimembranosus.L-II [30]
  3. Medial gastrocnemius retracted laterally to shield the popliteal neurovascular bundle.L-II [30]
  4. Deep posterior capsule incised; fragment reduced and fixed with cannulated lag screws (3.5–4.0 mm ± washer).L-II [27]

Advantages: Direct macroscopic visualization, no specialized arthroscopic equipment needed, excellent for large monolithic fragments.L-II [3]

Disadvantages: Extensive soft tissue dissection, higher wound complications, posterior capsular scar → arthrofibrosis, no access to anterior/medial/lateral compartments for concomitant injuries (present in up to 28% of PCL avulsions).L-II [3,38]

Arthroscopic Trans-Septal Portal Techniques

All-arthroscopic fixation has emerged as the modern standard of care.L-I [5] The arthroscope navigates through the intercondylar notch; a trans-septal portal pierces and resects the posterior septum, enabling panoramic visualization of the posterior tibial slope.L-II [9]

Key advantages over open approach:

  • Drastically minimized surgical morbidity; no posterior musculature dissection.L-II [8]
  • Reduced risk of iatrogenic neurovascular injury.
  • Full evaluation and treatment of concomitant intra-articular pathologies.L-II [29]

Arthroscopic Fixation Constructs

1. Suture Bridge Technique

Adapted from rotator cuff repair mechanics: multiple double-stranded high-strength sutures or FiberTapes pass through the ligament base, cross in an '8' configuration over the fragment, and are secured on the anterior tibial cortex via trans-tibial tunnels.L-I [6]

Forkel et al. (2020) demonstrated that the suture bridge yields significantly lower construct elongation (4.5 ± 2.1 mm) compared to traditional transtibial pullout (12.4 ± 3.0 mm) under cyclic loading.L-I [43] Any construct displacement >3 mm during early healing predicts permanent functional laxity.

Limitation: Exceptionally demanding technique with steep learning curve; requires complex intra-articular suture management and multiple tunnel drilling.L-I [6]

2. Suspensory Button Fixation (TightRope / EndoButton)

A single trans-tibial tunnel is drilled; a high-strength traction suture passes through the ligament and through the adjustable loop of the cortical button. The button is shuttled through the joint and flipped over the fragment (or deployed on anterior tibia), then the loop is cinched to provide dynamic elastic compression.L-I [6,20]

Key advantages:

  • Procedurally simpler—fewer tunnels, less entanglement risk, faster tensioning.L-I [6]
  • Protects against fragment shatter in severe comminution or osteoporotic bone.L-II [20]
  • Clinical Lysholm scores >93, normal IKDC in >95%, full ROM restoration.L-II [8]

Clinical Outcomes & Complications

Fracture Union & Return to Sport

Nonunion is exceptionally rare in surgically managed patients: >99% union rate with consolidation within 8–12 weeks.L-I [40] Nonoperative management drops union rates to ~87% with higher long-term pseudoarthrosis risk.L-II [4]

The overall RTS rate is 96.0% following any surgical intervention. Crucially, arthroscopic cohorts achieve a near-perfect 100% RTS, compared to 89.5% for open surgery (p < 0.05).L-I [40] This superiority is attributed to complete preservation of posterior musculature and native neuromuscular proprioceptive pathways.L-I [41]

Postoperative Complications

Arthrofibrosis & Loss of Motion

Historically the most common complication. Open posterior approaches induced massive capsular scarring.L-II [29] Modern arthroscopic interventions limit extrarticular scarring, but over-tensioning or delayed therapy can still cause stiffness.L-II [4] Evidence strongly advocates immediate, protected, progressive passive ROM using a dynamic PCL rebound brace.L-II [35] Full functional ROM achieved in >93% (arthroscopic) and 96% (open) of cases.L-I [40]

Residual Posterior Laxity

Success defined as side-to-side posterior translation difference ≤ 3 mm (KT-1000/2000 or stress radiography). Arthroscopic suture/button constructs restrict residual translation to 0.6–3.2 mm; open screws 1.7–3.1 mm.L-I [40] Nonoperative management of displaced fractures yields unacceptable residual laxity averaging ≥ 5 mm, directly predisposing to accelerated cartilage degeneration.L-I [40]

Hardware Irritation & Iatrogenic Comminution

Up to 10% of open lag screw patients require secondary hardware removal due to symptomatic prominence.L-II [33] Over-tightening in borderline fragments can precipitate catastrophic intraoperative comminution.L-II [11] Arthroscopic suture and button constructs largely eliminate these risks.L-II [15]

Landmark Papers

Five paradigm-shifting publications that define the current evidence base for PCL tibial avulsion fracture management.

1
Griffith JF, Antonio GE, Tong CW, Ming CK. "Cruciate ligament avulsion fractures."
Arthroscopy, 2004 · PMID: 15292920 · Level I
Takeaway: Established the ubiquitous Griffith Classification system, moving the field past pediatric ACL-focused Meyers & McKeever criteria to provide a PCL-specific surgical decision-making algorithm based on fracture hinge displacement.[14]
2
Forkel P, Lacheta L, et al. "Modified suture-bridge technique for tibial avulsion fractures of the PCL: a biomechanical comparison."
Arch Orthop Trauma Surg, 2020 · PMID: 31559489 · Level I
Takeaway: Cadaveric cyclic loading model proved suture-bridge technique provides significantly superior resistance to construct elongation (4.5 mm) vs. traditional transtibial pullout (12.4 mm), establishing the biomechanical rationale for multi-limbed bridge constructs.[43]
3
Zheng W, Hou W, Zhang Z, et al. "Results of arthroscopic treatment of acute PCL avulsion fractures with suspensory fixation."
Arthroscopy, 2021 · PMID: 33539975 · Level I
Takeaway: Demonstrated that adjustable suspensory cortical button (EndoButton) yields near-perfect restoration of native knee kinematics and excellent functional PROMs, entirely without the morbidity or shatter risk of rigid screw fixation.[55]
4
Zhao D, Zhong J, Zhao B, et al. "Clinical outcomes of acute displaced PCL tibial avulsion fracture: A retrospective comparative study between suture and EndoButton fixation."
Orthop Traumatol Surg Res, 2021 · PMID: 33310185 · Level II
Takeaway: Direct comparison validated that both suture bridge and suspensory button deliver statistically comparable, highly favorable rates of radiologic healing and stability—empowering surgeons to choose based on fracture morphology and technical comfort.[28]
5
Sundararajan SR, et al. "Systematic Review and Meta-analysis of Clinical Outcomes After Management of PCL Tibial Avulsion Fractures."
Orthop J Sports Med, 2023 · PMID: 37724253 · Level I
Takeaway: Analyzing 1,440 patients across 58 studies, this definitive meta-analysis confirmed 99% union for both approaches, while arthroscopic management provides significantly less residual posterior tibial translation, superior Lysholm scores, and higher RTS rates.[41]
