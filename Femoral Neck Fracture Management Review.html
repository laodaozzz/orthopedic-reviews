Management of Displaced Femoral Neck Fractures in Young Adults

Systematic Review: Internal Fixation Versus Primary Arthroplasty — Evidence-Based Educational Summary

📋 Overview & Clinical Dilemma

Femoral neck fractures (FNFs) in young adults represent a fundamentally distinct clinical entity from fragility fractures in the elderly. The etiology is predominantly high-energy trauma — motor vehicle collisions, falls from height, or forceful collisions [1,2]. This high-energy mechanism imparts substantial force to bone of normal density, producing specific fracture patterns with significant biomechanical consequences and a profound risk to the femoral head's vascularity.

The management is governed by a central paradox: the conflict between preserving the native joint (the traditional imperative) and providing a definitive, durable solution (the pragmatic need). Recent literature reports alarmingly high complication rates with internal fixation (IF), while advancements in implant technology have improved arthroplasty (AR) survivorship, shifting the debate toward a more nuanced, evidence-based approach [6,14].

⚠ Critical Definitional Challenge

There is no standardized definition of "young adult." Studies use cutoffs ranging from <50 to ≤65 years [2,12,13,18]. This heterogeneity masks physiologically distinct subgroups and is a primary driver of the ongoing clinical controversy.

🦴 Pathoanatomy & Biomechanics

Fracture Characteristics

Unlike impacted, stable fractures in osteoporotic bone, FNFs in young adults are frequently characterized by a more vertical orientation of the fracture line, corresponding to a Pauwels Type III classification (fracture angle >50° from horizontal) [1,3]. This vertical orientation is biomechanically unstable, creating:

  • High shear forces across the fracture site
  • Varus moments that resist compression
  • Predisposition to fixation failure, varus collapse, and nonunion

Vascular Anatomy & the Threat of AVN

The blood supply to the femoral head is tenuous, primarily derived from the ascending cervical branches of the medial femoral circumflex artery (MCFA), which course along the femoral neck within the joint capsule [2]. Significant fracture displacement can disrupt or kink these vessels, leading to immediate ischemic insult — the principal determinant of avascular necrosis (AVN) [2,6].

A secondary vascular insult may arise from the tamponade effect of an intracapsular hematoma, where increased pressure within the intact joint capsule further compromises perfusion [2].

Avascular Necrosis: The Central Threat

AVN can lead to progressive collapse of the femoral head articular surface, joint incongruity, severe pain, secondary osteoarthritis, and almost invariably necessitates conversion to total hip arthroplasty [2,8].

The argument for primary arthroplasty may be strongest in the older segment of this cohort.

🔧 Internal Fixation (IF)

Surgical Rationale & Techniques

The fundamental objective of IF is to achieve anatomic or near-anatomic reduction and apply a stable implant construct to promote primary bone healing and preserve the native femoral head [2,3].

Commonly employed implants include:

  • Multiple parallel cannulated cancellous screws (MCS)
  • Dynamic hip screw (DHS) with an anti-rotation screw
  • Femoral Neck System (FNS) — a more recently developed device

Comparative studies have generally found similar rates of major complications (AVN, fixation failure) among these implant types, suggesting that quality of reduction is more critical than specific hardware [13]. However, DHS may be associated with longer operative times and greater blood loss compared to MCS or FNS [13].

Advantages of IF

  • Less invasive surgery with less soft tissue dissection
  • Shorter operative duration
  • Reduced intraoperative blood loss and transfusion requirements
  • Decreased risk of deep periprosthetic joint infection [10]

The Specter of Avascular Necrosis (AVN)

The reported incidence of AVN following IF is alarmingly high: 14.3% to 36% in recent large-scale studies [8], with historical series reporting up to 86% [2].

Risk factors and modifying variables:

  • Degree of initial displacement — major predictor of vascular disruption [8]
  • Quality of reduction — anatomic reduction unkinks retinacular vessels [2]
  • Timing of surgery — controversial; some evidence suggests delay >24 hrs increases failure risk [9], while other studies find no significant difference [8]

Reoperation & Treatment Failure

A large contemporary series reported an overall treatment failure rate of 23% in patients ≤60 years, comprising loss of fixation (5.4%), nonunion (4.5%), and osteonecrosis (14.3%) [18]. Nonunion rates range from 10% to 33% [2,8].

Landmark Reoperation Data (Slobogean et al. 2021): In patients aged 40–49, IF had an OR for reoperation of 2.35 at 1 year (95% CI 1.22–4.54) and 5.68 at 3 years (95% CI 2.21–14.60) compared to primary THA [6].

Two Distinct Failure Timelines

The pattern of failure after IF manifests across two distinct timelines:

  • Acute mechanical failure (nonunion, loss of fixation) — apparent within the first year [12]
  • Latent biological failure (AVN with head collapse) — can be clinically silent for several years [2]
Latency Implication

Studies with short-term follow-up (1–2 years) will underestimate true failure rates. The surge in OR from 2.35 to 5.68 between 1 and 3 years demonstrates this delayed failure phenomenon [6]. A patient appearing successful at 2 years may still face major reconstructive surgery for AVN 5+ years later.

🏥 Primary Arthroplasty (AR)

Rationale & Procedure Selection

Primary arthroplasty provides a definitive, single-stage surgical solution that bypasses the biological uncertainties of IF by immediately eliminating the risks of AVN and nonunion [6,14]. It facilitates immediate postoperative weight-bearing and accelerated rehabilitation [10].

Total Hip Arthroplasty (THA) vs. Hemiarthroplasty (HA)

For young, active, cognitively intact patients, THA is the procedure of choice [27]:

  • THA provides more predictable and durable pain relief and superior long-term function vs. HA [5]
  • HA in younger patients is associated with progressive acetabular cartilage wear and eventual conversion to THA [29]
  • Bipolar HA survivorship: 96% at 15 years but drops to 60% at 20 years [29]

Outcomes: Low Reoperation & High Survivorship

The most significant advantage is the dramatic reduction in reoperation risk. Meta-analyses show an ~80% relative risk reduction for arthroplasty vs. IF (RR ≈ 0.17–0.30) [10,11,14,23]. Modern bearing surfaces (e.g., highly cross-linked polyethylene) have extended implant longevity substantially [6].

Inherent Risks of Arthroplasty

  • Greater surgical invasiveness — longer operative times, higher blood loss [10]
  • Deep prosthetic joint infection (PJI) — RR 1.81 vs. IF in some meta-analyses [23]
  • Instability / Dislocation — 5-year cumulative incidence ~1.4% with modern techniques [31]
  • Periprosthetic fractures — 5-year cumulative incidence ~7% [31]
  • Aseptic loosening — the ultimate long-term failure mechanism due to wear debris [10]
The Risk Trade-Off Paradigm

IF exposes patients to unpredictable biological risk (AVN, nonunion), while AR exposes them to predictable, time-dependent mechanical risk (wear, loosening). Modern implant technology has extended the predictable THA lifespan, making the known long-term mechanical failure a more acceptable prospect than the unpredictable short-term biological failure of IF.

The Critical Impact of Salvage Arthroplasty

A THA performed as salvage after failed IF (conversion THA, cTHA) is a fundamentally different and more challenging operation than a primary THA (pTHA) [4,33].

The "Hidden Cost" of IF Failure: The choice is not merely between IF and primary THA. If IF fails (~25% probability), the patient is committed to a salvage pathway with demonstrably higher infection, fracture, and reoperation rates than if a primary THA had been performed initially. This refutes the notion that attempting fixation is a "no-risk" endeavor.

Functional Outcomes & Quality of Life

Arthroplasty offers advantages in early functional recovery, with significantly higher Harris Hip Scores at 3, 6, and 12 months, though this difference is no longer significant by 24 months [11].

For the subset of successful IF patients (77%), long-term outcomes can be excellent: median Oxford Hip Score of 47/48 and EQ-5D index of 1.00 at 7-year follow-up [24]. However, the 23% who failed had significantly inferior outcomes across every domain (OHS 44.5, EQ-5D 0.825, UCLA 4.5 vs. 6.5) [24].

Decline in activity level is common regardless of procedure; one study noted a significant drop in UCLA activity score from 6.8 pre-injury to 6.0 post-injury [24].

🎯 Evidence-Based Recommendations

A Framework for Shared Decision-Making

There are no specific clinical practice guidelines from major orthopedic bodies (AAOS, BOA) tailored to this population [32,38]. The treatment decision must be individualized through comprehensive shared decision-making, moving beyond simple chronological age to assess physiological age — integrating activity level, comorbidities, and bone quality [12].

Key Decision Factors:

  1. Patient-Specific Factors: Age, occupation, recreational demands, pre-existing hip OA (strong indication for THA), comorbidities (e.g., excessive alcohol use = risk factor for IF failure) [5,18]
  2. Fracture-Specific Characteristics: Degree of displacement; Pauwels angle — a high-angle vertical fracture (Type III, >50°) is a very strong predictor of IF failure [1]
  3. Outcome Evidence: ~23–25% IF failure rate; >5× higher reoperation risk vs. THA; salvage THA outcomes are inferior to primary THA [6,18,33]
Strongest Indications for Primary THA

Based on the evidence synthesis, primary THA is most strongly supported for: patients over age 40; those with high Pauwels angle (Type III) fractures; patients with pre-existing symptomatic hip OA; and those with high functional demands where a single-operation outcome is paramount.

Where IF Remains Compelling

The case for IF remains most compelling in truly young, healthy patients (<40–45 years) with favorable fracture patterns, where the potential for excellent native hip function is highest and the long-term risks of prosthetic wear are most consequential.

Evidence Gaps & Future Directions

  • Critical need for multicenter RCTs directly comparing modern IF vs. primary THA in young adults (<60 years) [10]
  • Studies must employ granular subgroup analyses (e.g., <45 vs. 45–60 years; Pauwels Type III vs. I/II)
  • Long-term follow-up (10–20+ years) is essential to understand lifetime burden of reoperation for both strategies
The source document notes a significant evidence gap: current evidence base consists primarily of retrospective cohort studies and extrapolations from meta-analyses of elderly populations. No high-level RCTs specific to this young adult population were identified.
