Percutaneous Sacroiliac Screw Fixation

Educational Module Source-based summary • Interactive decision support

Introduction & Clinical Rationale

The posterior pelvic ring is the primary weight-bearing structure of the appendicular skeleton and the critical biomechanical bridge between the axial spine and the lower extremities. High-energy trauma — motor vehicle collisions, falls from height — commonly produces sacroiliac (SI) joint dislocations, crescent fractures, and transforaminal or transalar sacral fractures. Because the posterior pelvis bears the majority of vertical shear and rotational forces, anatomical reduction and rigid internal fixation are mandatory to restore stability, enable early mobilization, and prevent chronic pain. Level II

The Shift from Open to Percutaneous Fixation

Historically, unstable posterior pelvic ring injuries required extensive open reduction and internal fixation (ORIF). While ORIF permits direct visualization, it is associated with severe perioperative complications:

  • Profound secondary blood loss from disrupting the local vascular supply and protective pelvic hematoma through extensile posterior approaches.
  • Wound breakdown and deep infection rates approaching 27% in traumatized, attenuated soft-tissue envelopes. Level II

In response, the early 1990s saw a paradigm shift with the popularization of percutaneous iliosacral screw fixation. This technique uses precise, fluoroscopically guided pathways to achieve robust biomechanical stability while minimizing soft-tissue stripping, hemorrhagic risk, and operative time. Level I

Gold-Standard Status Percutaneous sacroiliac screw placement is the definitive gold standard for posterior pelvic ring stabilization, with markedly superior infection rates and immediate postoperative morbidity compared with ORIF.

Technical Challenges & Complication Landscape

Despite its advantages, the procedure is one of the most technically demanding in orthopaedic trauma. Key challenges include:

  • Highly complex, three-dimensionally variable posterior pelvis anatomy.
  • Exceptionally narrow intraosseous corridors bordered by critical neurovascular structures (L4/L5 nerve roots, cauda equina, superior gluteal artery, internal iliac vessels).
  • Historically reported screw malposition rates of 3–25% and iatrogenic neurological damage rates of up to 18% in specific cohorts. Level II
Role of Navigation & Robotics CT navigation, 3D fluoroscopic matching, and robotic-assisted systems significantly reduce cognitive burden and improve accuracy. However, their routine use is often precluded by immense capital costs, lengthened setup times, and lack of availability in emergent settings. Non-navigated, 2D fluoroscopy-guided percutaneous fixation remains the foundational standard worldwide.

The safe execution of the non-navigated procedure demands an internalized, encyclopedic comprehension of sacral osteology and the ability to seamlessly interpret 2D fluoroscopic projections — true lateral, pelvic inlet, and pelvic outlet — into an accurate 3D mental model. Level II

Preoperative Planning & the Anatomical Safe Zones

Mandatory Imaging

A high-resolution pelvic CT scan — with trans-axial images and 2D multiplanar reformations (MPR) in the sagittal and coronal planes — is strictly mandatory. This imaging is used to:

  • Analyze the specific fracture configuration and degree of displacement.
  • Assess baseline bone quality.
  • Map the dimensions and trajectory of potential intraosseous corridors. Level II

The Vestibule Concept

The safe placement framework is encapsulated by the "vestibule" concept, first defined by Carlson et al. (2000). When the corridor spanning from the lateral ilium, across the SI joint, through S1 or S2 is visualized, it assumes the morphology of an hourglass (diabolo): widest laterally within the iliac wings and medially within the sacral body, but severely constricted at the transitional zone between the sacral ala and the sacral body. Level II

This narrowest passage — the vestibule — dictates the absolute angular tolerance and maximum hardware diameter. It is ovoid/elliptical in sagittal cross-section and bounded by:

  • Anteriorly: cortical slope of the sacral wing.
  • Superiorly: L5 nerve root and ilio-lumbar vasculature.
  • Posteriorly: ventral margin of the spinal canal.
  • Inferiorly: roof of the corresponding neural foramen (S1 or S2). Level II
Critical Spatial Orientation The vestibule invariably points anteriorly and superiorly. A perfectly horizontal (transverse) screw trajectory often provides the slimmest margin of safety and the highest risk of cortical breach.

If the maximum vestibule diameter is compromised by comminution, dysplasia, or inadequate reduction, placement of a standard 7.0/7.3 mm cannulated screw may be anatomically impossible.

Denis Classification & Screw Length Optimization

The Denis system divides the sacrum into three longitudinal zones:

  • Zone I — sacral ala (extraforaminal).
  • Zone II — sacral neural foramina.
  • Zone III — central spinal canal.

For isolated Zone III fractures, percutaneous iliosacral screws are generally contraindicated. Lag screw fixation requires all threads to cross the fracture line, but the central canal corridor is too short to accommodate even a 16 mm thread length. Alternative methods (posterior tension band plating, lumbopelvic fixation) are required. Level II

For combined Zone II + III fractures, the aggregate corridor length reliably exceeds 32 mm in >90% of subjects, enabling effective compression. The screw must always exceed the anatomical midline and engage the dense cortical bone of the contralateral hemi-sacrum.

Sacral Dysmorphism: Identification & Surgical Modifications

Sacral dysmorphism affects 30–40% of the general population, producing a corridor that is abnormally narrow, steeply angulated, and perilous. Failure to recognize dysmorphism preoperatively is a primary driver of iatrogenic L5 nerve root injury. Level II

Radiographic Identification — Routt Criteria

The classic signs of a dysmorphic sacrum include:

  1. "Sacrum not recessed" — upper sacral segment elevated relative to iliac crests, often indicating sacralized L5.
  2. Acute alar slope — anterior cortex slopes acutely from cranial-posterior-central to caudal-anterior-lateral, drastically reducing AP vestibule depth.
  3. Mammillary processes — prominent residual transverse processes on the anterior mid-alar region.
  4. Non-circular neural foramina — S1 foramina deformed into irregular, elliptical shapes.
  5. Residual upper sacral disc — persistent unossified disc space between S1 and S2.
  6. "Tongue-in-groove" morphology — anterior SI joints remain straight/parallel, while posterior articulations form an interlocking, corrugated pattern on axial CT.
"Main Dysmorphic" Sacrum The combination of sacrum not recessed + acute alar slope defines a "main dysmorphic" anatomy (≈30.3% of studied population). In these patients, a standard horizontal trans-iliosacral screw is biomechanically impossible without breaching the anterior cortex and injuring the L5 nerve root.

Surgical Modifications for Dysmorphic Anatomy

When main dysmorphism is identified, the standard horizontal technique is abandoned for a highly modified oblique trajectory:

  • Anteversion (posterior → anterior): Averages ~20° to follow the retreating slope of the dysmorphic ala. [Exact sex-specific values not available in source — image placeholders]
  • Inclination (caudal → cranial): Averages ~30° to avoid elevated neural foramina. [Exact sex-specific values not available in source]

The starting point must be shifted significantly downward and backward — deep in the postero-inferior quadrant of the ilium, in the posterior one-third of the AIIS-to-PSIS line. Level II

Critical Sequencing Pearl When fixing both S1 and S2 in a dysmorphic patient, place the S2 screw first. The S1 dysmorphic screw's steep caudal-to-cranial trajectory will overlap the S2 corridor and obscure the anterior cortex on inlet views, making safe S2 tracking impossible if done secondarily.

Standard Fluoroscopic Views & Essential Landmarks

Mastery of multidimensional fluoroscopic imaging is the absolute cornerstone of the non-navigated technique. If any of the three core views cannot be reliably obtained (extreme habitus, morbid obesity, bowel gas), the procedure must be aborted and converted to open. Level II

True Lateral Projection

The most critical initial image, providing dual-purpose guidance:

  • Alignment confirmation: Left and right greater sciatic notches, and both femoral heads, must perfectly superimpose.
  • Iliac Cortical Density (ICD): The defining landmark — a radiodense, curvilinear line representing the posterior ilium as it dips anteriorly into the SI joint. It parallels the sacral alar slope and marks the anterosuperior boundary of the safe zone.
  • S1 Vertebral Body Diagonal: Drawn from posterosuperior to anteroinferior corner of S1. The neural recess consistently lies posterior to this line — a wire anterior to the diagonal but posterior to the ICD is guaranteed intraosseous.
  • "In-Out-In" Risk: If the starting point is too anterior/cranial (violating the ICD), the drill perforates the medial iliac cortex, exits into the alar groove (potentially transecting L5 nerve root), then re-enters S1. The lateral view is the sole reliable safeguard. Level II

Pelvic Inlet Projection

Assesses anteroposterior orientation:

  • C-arm tilted craniocaudally 20–30° until S1 and S2 anterior cortices superimpose (bright "double-cortical density").
  • Posterior boundary: anterior wall of the spinal canal.
  • Anterior boundary: double-cortical density.
  • Wire must track through the posterior half of the sacral ala, posterior to the nerve root tunnel exits but anterior to the spinal canal.

Pelvic Outlet Projection

Dictates cephalocaudad trajectory:

  • C-arm tilted caudocranially 35–45° until the pubic symphysis overlies S2 foramina.
  • The "Spica Cast" sign — a perfectly aligned outlet view resembles a hip spica cast, where the central "torso" is the midline spinal canal and the "legs" are the upper sacral nerve root tunnels (USNRT).
  • Screw must be placed strictly superior to the S1 foramen — above the "leg" of the spica cast.

Teardrop (Obturator Outlet Oblique) View

Essential for S2-Alar-Iliac (S2AI) and supra-acetabular screws:

  • Obtained by combining 20–30° cranial-caudal tilt with 40–50° oblique C-arm rotation.
  • Generates a "teardrop" shape formed by the confluence of PSIS, AIIS, and the greater sciatic notch.
  • The screw must remain perfectly centered within the teardrop's cortical margins — straying medially threatens the sciatic nerve; straying laterally breaches the external table.

Step-by-Step Surgical Technique

1Patient Positioning & Preparation

  • Supine on a fully radiolucent table.
  • A radiolucent bump (rolled towel or 1 L saline bag) placed midline under sacrum/lower lumbar spine to elevate the pelvis — prevents drill/hand collision with table during steep trajectories.
  • Arms abducted 90° to avoid interference with C-arm arcs.
  • Anesthetic consideration: Avoid nitrous oxide — it causes bowel distension, obscuring foraminal landmarks on fluoroscopy.

2Closed Reduction

Percutaneous iliosacral screws are fixation devices, not reduction tools. Attempting to lag a widely displaced SI joint risks entrapping nerve roots in the fracture gap. Reduction must be achieved and verified before hardware insertion, using:

  • Skeletal traction (distal femoral pin).
  • Temporary anterior external fixators.
  • Posterior pelvic C-clamps.
  • Percutaneous Schanz pins in supra-acetabular corridors as "joysticks." Level II

3Start Site Localization & Skin Mapping

Under live true lateral fluoroscopy, the surgeon superimposes a free guide wire over the exterior skin, aligning with the ICD and anterior sacral slope. These lines are drawn on the skin with a sterile marker, creating an external map of the internal "safe zone." The ideal entry point is strictly posterior and slightly caudal to the marked ICD line. A small stab incision is made; blunt dissection is carried down to the lateral iliac cortex to prevent gluteal fascia from wrapping around the drill. Level II

4Guide Wire Advancement & Tri-Planar Tracking

A 2.8 or 3.2 mm threaded guide wire is introduced using a cannulated drill guide ("pig sticker") embedded into the outer iliac table.

  1. Lateral view: Wire tapped 2–3 mm into cortex, verified posterior/caudal to ICD, aimed toward S1 body diagonal. Advance ~1 cm into the sacral ala.
  2. Inlet view: Wire must track within the posterior half of the ala, posterior to the anterior cortex and anterior to the spinal canal.
  3. Outlet view: Wire must sit clearly above the S1 neural foramen, heading toward the upper contralateral corner of the sacral body.
  4. Final advancement: Across SI joint into contralateral hemi-sacrum for maximal purchase.

5Drilling Strategy & Screw Insertion

Partial drilling pearl (Aprato et al.): Drill only through the lateral iliac cortex, the dense SI joint bone, and the lateral sacral cortex. Leave the central cancellous sacral bone undrilled — this forces the self-tapping threads to compress the trabeculae, radically increasing pull-out strength. Level II

  • A 7.0/7.3 mm cannulated screw is inserted over the wire.
  • Heavy washer is mandatory — without it, the screw head can plunge through the thin iliac outer table.
Thread Selection Rationale
  • Partially threaded lag screw → pure ligamentous SI joint dislocations (dynamic compression → arthrodesis).
  • Fully threaded position screw → transforaminal sacral fractures (Denis Zone II). A lag screw across a foraminal gap compresses bone into the nerve root, causing severe radiculopathy. Level II

6Final Assessment & Verification

Depth is verified fluoroscopically (inlet + obturator views) to ensure the screw head is flush but not prominent. The guide wire is withdrawn, and multi-planar final images (AP, inlet, outlet, true lateral) are saved to confirm intraosseous containment and maintained reduction.

Clinical Pearls & Complication Avoidance

Optimizing Fluoroscopic Clarity

  • Manual bowel gas displacement: During image acquisition, an assistant applies firm abdominal massage to sweep gaseous shadows away from the pelvic field — provides a brief, pristine clarity window. Level II
  • "Pinhole effect" (beam collimation): In obese patients, aggressively cone down the C-arm aperture to focus solely on the S1/S2 target. This reduces photon scatter, dramatically increases contrast and edge sharpness, and paradoxically lowers radiation dose. Level II

Aprato's Pearls for Procedural Excellence

  • Defeating the table via leg flexion: Obtaining the 35–45° outlet view often causes the C-arm housing to collide with the table pedestal. Shift the patient distally and actively flex hips/knees to flatten lumbar lordosis and reduce required C-arm angulation.
  • Navigating crescent fractures (OTA/AO 61-B2.2): The standard posterior starting point captures only the small, mobile posterior fragment. The entry point must be shifted significantly anteriorly to bridge the fracture gap and stabilize the main anterior pelvic ring.
  • Orthogonal trajectories: SI joint dislocations → entry slightly posterior, screw aimed anteriorly for maximal compressive force. Sacral ala fractures → entry anteriorly, screw directed parallel to the coronal plane as a position screw.
  • Post-operative sitting biomechanics: Sitting at 90° generates massive shear forces across the SI joint. Sitting at 45° still transmits 70% of torso load; reclining to 15° drops it to 26%. Patients must be restricted from sitting >40° for the first 40 days. Level II

Avoiding the "In-Out-In" Phenomenon

~4% of the population has partial or complete lumbarization of S1, deepening the sacral alar groove. A screw initiated too far anteriorly on the ilium perforates the medial cortex, exits into the groove (compressing/severing L5), and re-enters S1. The true lateral view is the only reliable safeguard. If a patient awakens with radiculopathy, urgent CT and hardware removal is required — symptoms resolve rapidly if the nerve was compressed, not transected. Level II

Conclusion

Non-navigated, fluoroscopically guided percutaneous sacroiliac screw placement represents a pinnacle of orthopaedic traumatology — a procedure demanding seamless integration of 2D radiographic interpretation with complex, highly variable 3D anatomical reasoning. While CT-navigation and robotics offer compelling advantages in trajectory precision, the conventional freehand technique remains indispensable, particularly in resource-constrained or highly acute polytrauma settings.

Success depends entirely on precise acquisition and interpretation of the true lateral, pelvic inlet, and pelvic outlet views; strict adherence to the vestibule boundaries; rigorous screening for sacral dysmorphism; and implementation of nuanced pearls — beam collimation, partial drilling, external skin mapping, and appropriate thread selection. The success of percutaneous pelvic fixation is not dictated by the sophistication of hardware, but by the surgeon's deep, spatial respect for the unforgiving topography of the posterior pelvic ring.
