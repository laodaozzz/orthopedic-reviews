I. The Unique Pathological Landscape of the Neglected Terrible Triad Injury

A. Defining the Chronic or Neglected State

A "neglected" or "chronic" elbow dislocation is clinically defined as a dislocation that has remained unreduced for three weeks or more.[1] This temporal distinction marks a critical biological transition point: the initial hematoma and acute inflammatory response are replaced by organized, dense fibrous tissue, transforming a problem of acute instability into one of profound, recalcitrant stiffness.[1]

This presentation is particularly common in developing nations, where patients may initially seek care from traditional "bonesetters" who employ massage and forcible manipulations.[2] Such improper management not only delays definitive treatment but represents a form of iatrogenic, repetitive trauma that can exacerbate muscle damage and inflammation, likely accelerating the formation of heterotopic ossification (HO).[4]

The "terrible triad" injury (TTI) itself—a complex combination of elbow dislocation, radial head fracture, and coronoid process fracture[3]—is notoriously challenging even in its acute state due to simultaneous disruption of the elbow's key bony and ligamentous stabilizers.[6] When this injury is neglected for several weeks, it evolves into one of the most formidable reconstructive challenges in orthopaedic surgery.

B. The Vicious Cycle: From Instability to Arthrofibrosis

Key Concept — Biological Inversion: The primary problem of an acute TTI is gross instability; the surgical goal is to restore stability to allow early motion and prevent stiffness.[7,8] Conversely, the primary problem of a neglected TTI is profound stiffness (arthrofibrosis).[1] The joint is pathologically "stable"—locked by dense scar, contracted soft tissues, and often bridges of abnormal bone.[3]

This fundamental shift dictates that the surgical strategy must also be inverted. The procedure becomes a two-part salvage operation:

  1. Deconstruction: Aggressively release the pathological stiffness through extensive excision, which effectively re-creates the original, gross instability of the TTI in a hostile, scarred tissue bed.
  2. Reconstruction: Rebuild stability from scratch using implants and grafts to create a joint that can once again be mobilized.

C. Secondary Pathologies: The True Surgical Challenge

The difficulty in treating a neglected TTI stems from the constellation of secondary pathologies that develop. The surgical plan is dictated not by the original fractures, but by these acquired deformities.

1. Intra-articular Adhesions and Capsular Contracture

In the neglected state, the joint capsule undergoes marked thickening and fibrotic contracture.[12] Dense intra-articular adhesions obliterate the joint space.[14] The olecranon and coronoid fossae become completely filled with dense, organized scar tissue.[11]

2. Muscular Retraction and Fibrosis

Significant shortening and retraction occur in major muscle-tendon units, particularly the triceps and brachialis.[3] The collateral ligaments become part of the amorphous scar, shortening into contracted, fibrotic tethers.[3]

3. Heterotopic Ossification (HO)

HO is a common and devastating complication of neglected elbow trauma.[1] It is incited by trauma to bone, ligament, and muscle,[5] frequently found in the posterolateral tissues adjacent to the radial head.[11] In severe cases, it can completely encase neurovascular structures, most commonly the ulnar nerve.[5]

4. Osseous Deformity

Original fractures will either heal in a non-anatomic position (malunion) or fail to heal entirely (nonunion).[16] A malunited radial head mechanically blocks forearm rotation.[16] The coronoid fragment may be resorbed or heal displaced, rendering the ulnohumeral joint permanently unstable.[17]

II. Advanced Preoperative Assessment and Strategic Formulation

A. The Central Role of 3D Computed Tomography (CT)

Preoperative planning is contingent on advanced imaging. While radiographs confirm the chronic dislocation,[1] they are grossly insufficient for surgical planning.

CT with multiplanar and three-dimensional (3D) reconstructions is mandatory.[1] In the chronic injury, the CT scan becomes a deconstruction blueprint. The surgeon uses 3D-CT to:

  • Map the precise location, volume, and anatomical relationships of all HO.[1]
  • Identify "safe" surgical corridors and anticipate neurovascular structures at risk.[18]
  • Define the state of osseous stabilizers: extent of coronoid deficiency and radial head malunion.[1]

B. Assessing Neurovascular Structures and Soft Tissue

A thorough preoperative neurological examination is critical, with specific attention to ulnar nerve function.[15] MRI is sometimes considered of "limited benefit" in chronic dislocations due to fibrosis artifact,[14] but can assess triceps and brachialis retraction. In cases of massive HO, a CT Angiogram (CTA) may be warranted to map the brachial artery course.

C. Formulating Surgical Goals and Patient Counseling

Target Functional Arc of Motion:
• Flexion arc: 30° extension to 130° flexion (100° arc)
• Rotational arc: 50° pronation + 50° supination (100° arc)[22]

It must be explicitly stated that a "normal" elbow is not achievable.[1] Preoperative counseling must cover the formidable nature of the procedure, high complication risk (persistent stiffness, nerve injury, chronic pain, post-traumatic arthritis), and the high probability of secondary surgeries.[1]

III. Operative Strategy: Single-Stage vs. Staged Reconstruction

A. Rationale for Single-Stage Comprehensive Reconstruction

For most specialists, the preferred strategy is single-stage comprehensive reconstruction.[11] The operative flow follows a logical sequence:

  1. Exposure and Neurolysis: Wide exposure with immediate ulnar nerve identification and neurolysis.
  2. Extensive Arthrolysis: Complete capsulectomy and all HO excision.
  3. Osseous Reconstruction: Radial head arthroplasty; coronoid autograft reconstruction.
  4. Ligamentous Reconstruction: Formal LCL reconstruction; MCL if unstable.
  5. Stabilization: Hinged external fixator application.

B. Indications and Technique for Staged Approaches

In the most severe, rigidly fibrosed dislocations, a single-stage release may be deemed impossible. A staged protocol has been described:[26]

  • Stage 1 — Tissue Pre-conditioning: In situ ulnar nerve neurolysis. Hinged external fixator application. Gradual distraction at 4 mm/day for ~15 days to stretch contracted tissues.[26]
  • Stage 2 — Open Reduction and Fixation: Two weeks later, posterior open reduction performed. Reduction held with temporary transarticular K-wires.[26]
  • Stage 3 — Mobilization: Two weeks later, K-wires removed. External fixator unlocked for active/passive mobilization. Fixator removed ~2 weeks after that.[26]
Strategic Choice: The surgeon must decide between a single aggressive "excision and reconstruction"[11] or a slower multi-stage "distraction and preservation" approach.[26]

IV. Surgical Exposure and Management of the Triceps

A. Standard Approaches

A single, extensive posterior skin incision is most advantageous, allowing full-thickness medial and lateral skin flaps for circumferential access.[7] Combined medial and lateral incisions are an alternative.[11] Limited approaches (single lateral, olecranon osteotomy) are generally insufficient.[28,29]

V. The Foundational Procedure: Extensive Arthrolysis and Neurolysis

A. Systematic Open Capsular Release (Capsulectomy)

The technique involves a circumferential release of all contracted tissues:[11]

  1. Lateral Release: Develop lateral muscle interval (e.g., Kocher). Elevate brachialis and brachioradialis off anterior humerus; elevate anconeus posteriorly.[21]
  2. Medial Release: Develop medial interval after ulnar nerve management. Split or elevate flexor-pronator mass.[21]
  3. Anterior Capsulectomy: Excise entire fibrotic anterior capsule from humerus and coronoid base. Releases adherent brachialis.[13]
  4. Posterior Capsulectomy: Excise entire posterior capsule; elevate triceps from posterior humerus.[13]
  5. Fossa Debridement: Clear olecranon and coronoid fossae of scar tissue and HO with rongeurs and curettes.[11]
  6. Articular Scar Excision: Peel adherent scar off preserved cartilage with a 15-blade.[11]
Important: This extensive capsulectomy is a controlled, iatrogenic dislocation. The surgeon is methodically excising the very tissues that have fibrosed to "lock" the joint,[13] leaving behind a "naked" and grossly unstable joint.[11] This act of "creating motion" necessitates the comprehensive reconstruction that must follow.

B. Excision of Heterotopic Ossification (HO)

All ectopic bone blocking motion or impinging on neurovascular structures is completely resected.[11] This is often the most tedious and high-risk portion of the dissection. Urgent surgical excision is definitively indicated for HO causing progressive, compressive ulnar neuropathy.[15]

C. Ulnar Nerve Management: Neurolysis and Anterior Transposition

Identification and management of the ulnar nerve is the first and most critical step of any medial or posterior approach.[11]

  • Neurolysis: Meticulous dissection and freeing from all scar tissue, HO, or fascial constrictions.[11]
  • Anterior Transposition: In the neglected TTI, anterior transposition is mandatory.[13] A simple in situ release is almost always insufficient.
Rationale: In a chronic extension contracture, the ulnar nerve is scarred and shortened in its posterior groove.[21] If the surgeon successfully gains flexion without transposing the nerve, the un-transposed nerve will be stretched taut, causing severe iatrogenic traction neuropathy.[2] Anterior transposition moves the nerve anterior to the axis of rotation, providing "slack" during flexion.[36]

VI. Stepwise Reconstruction of the Triad Components

Following the "deconstruction" phase, the surgeon is left with a grossly unstable elbow. All three components of the terrible triad must be surgically rebuilt. The algorithm is inverted from acute TTI: first total arthrolysis, then total reconstruction of all stabilizers.[23]

A. Component 1: The Radial Head

In the chronic setting, radial head fragments are often comminuted, malunited, resorbed, and avascular.[16] Radial head arthroplasty (replacement) is the strong and consistent preference.[14]

  • Excision of the radial head is absolutely contraindicated — it is a critical secondary stabilizer.[16]
  • ORIF on poor-quality bone is associated with high failure, nonunion, and osteonecrosis rates.[8]
  • Meta-analyses show arthroplasty provides better functional outcomes than ORIF for comminuted fractures.[38]
Technical Warning — "Overstuffing": Placing a prosthesis that is too long or too wide increases joint-reaction force, preventing concentric ulnohumeral reduction. This leads to medial gapping, persistent subluxation, pain, and implant loosening.[17]

B. Component 2: The Coronoid Process

The coronoid is the primary anterior buttress resisting posterior subluxation.[7] A deficient coronoid must be reconstructed.[17] The goal is to restore near-normal coronoid height of 13–19.6 mm.[40]

C. Component 3: Ligamentous Stabilization

Following total capsulectomy, native collateral ligaments are gone. This is a reconstruction problem, not a "repair" problem.

Lateral Collateral Ligament (LCL) Reconstruction

The LCL, specifically the LUCL component, is the primary restraint to posterolateral rotatory instability (PLRI).[7] Reconstruction is mandatory.[8] An autograft (e.g., palmaris longus) or allograft is passed through a bone tunnel in the ulna at the supinator crest and fixed to the lateral epicondyle's isometric center.[43]

Medial Collateral Ligament (MCL) Management

In acute TTI, the MCL often heals if lateral stability is restored. This principle does not apply to the neglected TTI. The extensive medial release destroys residual MCL tissue. If valgus or rotatory instability persists after lateral reconstruction, the MCL must be formally reconstructed.[7]

Advanced Technique: Circumferential "Box-Loop" Grafting

For global, multidirectional instability, a circumferential graft technique may be employed.[45] A single long tendon graft is passed through bone tunnels drilled through the humerus (along the axis of rotation) and ulna (from sublime tubercle to supinator crest), creating a "box" that simultaneously reconstructs both MCL and LCL.[45]

VII. Management of Persistent Instability: External Fixation

A. Indications for Adjunctive Fixation

An external fixator is indicated when instability is noted intraoperatively after all internal fixation is complete.[7] In the neglected TTI, however, its use is often prophylactic and routine.[14]

Dual Paradoxical Role: The external fixator simultaneously provides rigid stability to protect fragile ligament reconstructions AND allows immediate controlled motion required to prevent recurrent arthrofibrosis.[46]

B. Hinged External Fixator Application

The absolute principle is that the fixator hinge axis must be coaxial with the elbow's true axis of rotation.[48] Technique:

  1. Place Schanz pins in humerus (5 mm, lateral) and ulna (4 mm, posterolateral).[14]
  2. Under fluoroscopy, drill a guidewire across the joint targeting the exact center of articular surfaces.[48]
  3. Place the fixator hinge directly over this guidewire.[48]
  4. Connect to humeral and ulnar pins via rods.
  5. Remove guidewire; confirm concentric reduction through full arc under fluoroscopy.[48]

C. Static vs. Dynamic Fixator Debate

Practice-Challenging Evidence (Level III): AlQahtani et al.[53] found no significant difference in final ROM, complications, or revision rates between static and dynamic external fixation groups for unstable elbow fracture-dislocations.[47] The authors concluded that "static external fixation is our preferred treatment" citing its "ease of application."[53]

This finding suggests that the type of fixator is less important than achieving a stable, concentric reduction for the initial 3- to 6-week healing period. Surgeons may be able to significantly simplify this highly complex operation without compromising final motion outcomes.

VIII. Postoperative Rehabilitation: The Battle Against Recurrent Stiffness

A. The Imperative of Early, Protected Mobilization

The entire goal of the surgical construct is to achieve enough stability to permit immediate mobilization.[8] The postoperative period is a "race against scar formation".[57] The patient has a narrow window of approximately three months to beat the recurrence of stiffness.

Central Paradox:
1. Arthrolysis demands immediate, aggressive motion to prevent excised fibrotic tissue from reforming.
2. Reconstruction (grafts, osteotomies) demands quiet, protected healing for tissue incorporation.
→ The hinged external fixator resolves this paradox by protecting the reconstruction while allowing motion.[46]

B. Specific Protocols

  • Overhead Motion Protocols: Patient lies supine, uses contralateral arm to assist operative arm. Gravity helps reduce ulna/radius onto humerus, protecting lateral ligament reconstructions from varus stress.[16,17]
  • Splinting: When not exercising, the arm is placed at 90° flexion with full pronation (most stable position for LCL-deficient elbow).[7]
  • Static Progressive Splinting: After initial 4–6 week healing phase, these devices apply low-load, prolonged stretch to "creep" out residual contractures.[7]

B. The "Subtle Triad" and Long-Term Durability

The most significant long-term challenge is post-traumatic osteoarthritis (PTOA), developing in up to 38% of patients.[24] This has led to the concept of the "subtle triad":[64] while modern techniques prevent gross dislocation, they may leave behind a persistent, sub-clinical microinstability that causes abnormal cartilage loading, leading to progressive arthritis years after the index procedure.

Next Frontier: Diagnosing and fixing the "subtle triad" to ensure long-term joint durability represents the next major challenge in elbow surgery.[64]

X. Expert Synthesis and Strategic Conclusions

The neglected TTI remains a "terrible" surgical entity. The pathology is a "transformed" joint: stiff, locked, non-functional, encased in dense scar, blocked by ectopic bone, and tethered by contracted musculotendinous units.[1]

The operative strategy is a salvage operation conceptualized in two opposing phases:

  1. Phase 1 — Deconstruction: Aggressive circumferential arthrolysis including total capsulectomy, all HO excision, and ulnar nerve neurolysis with anterior transposition. This creates motion by re-creating gross, global instability.[11]
  2. Phase 2 — Reconstruction: Total joint stabilization requiring radial head arthroplasty,[38] coronoid autograft reconstruction,[41] and formal graft-based LCL (and frequently MCL) reconstruction.[45]

The lynchpin is the adjunctive external fixator — the only tool capable of bridging the paradox of needing both stability and motion simultaneously.[46,47]

While "good" functional outcomes are achievable (MEPS mid-80s, functional arc restored),[23] the path is arduous, with a reoperation rate exceeding 20%.[61] The final frontier is not stability, but durability — achieving a truly congruent, biomechanically sound joint that can last a lifetime.
