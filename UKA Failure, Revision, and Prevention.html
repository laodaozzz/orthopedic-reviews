I. Executive Summary

This educational resource provides an expert-level synthesis of the failure, prevention, and management of mobile-bearing unicompartmental knee arthroplasty (MB UKA). It integrates epidemiological data from systematic reviews and national joint registries, biomechanical principles, and evidence-based surgical techniques derived directly from the source document.[1, 5, 15]

Key Concept
MB UKA failure is dominated by bearing dislocation — a mechanically distinct failure mode from the polyethylene (PE) wear and aseptic loosening that characterize fixed-bearing (FB) designs. These failures are predominantly early events (< 5 years), strongly linked to correctable surgical technique errors. Late failures (> 10 years) are driven by progression of osteoarthritis (OA) in unreplaced compartments.[1, 5]

Part I: Etiology of Failure

Registry and systematic review data reveal that MB UKA failure is dominated by bearing dislocation. These signature MB failures are predominantly early events occurring at less than 5 years post-implantation.[5] They are strongly linked to correctable surgical technique errors including component malposition and flexion-extension gap imbalance.[1] Late failures (> 10 years) are primarily driven by the biological process of progression of OA in the unreplaced tibiofemoral or patellofemoral compartments.[5]

Part II: Prevention via Surgical Technique

Prevention of technical errors is paramount. The optimal femoral intramedullary (IM) rod entry point — specific to modern UKA instrumentation (e.g., Oxford Microplasty) — is approximately 1 cm anterior to the PCL insertion and 2–3 mm lateral to the medial wall of the intercondylar notch.[9] Deviation from this point causes iatrogenic varus/valgus malalignment[10] and dramatically increases the risk of anterior femoral cortex perforation.[12]

Part III: Revision Strategies

When failure occurs, conversion to TKA is the gold standard. Evidence demonstrates that UKA-to-UKA revision has a prohibitively high 2-year failure rate of 25–45%, compared to a re-revision rate of approximately 4% for TKA conversion.[14] The primary technical challenge is managing tibial bone loss.[3] Large registry data demonstrates that use of a tibial stem extension during conversion significantly improves 10-year survivorship.[18] Functional outcome data confirms that a well-executed UKA-to-TKA conversion yields results equivalent to a primary TKA.[19]

II. Etiology & Mechanisms of Mobile-Bearing UKA Failure

2.1 The Modern UKA Failure Landscape

UKA offers significant advantages over TKA, including bone preservation, quicker recovery, greater range of motion, and superior patient-reported functional outcomes, with many patients reporting a more "normal" feeling knee.[20] However, this procedure is associated with a higher overall revision rate, as consistently demonstrated by large national joint registries.[15]

This high revision rate is not uniform over the life of the implant; it is heavily skewed toward the early postoperative period (< 5 years).[24] This pattern strongly suggests that many failures are attributable to surgical technical errors or incorrect patient selection rather than implant longevity.[24]

Design-Specific Failure Signatures
  • Mobile-Bearing (MB) UKA: Features a fully conforming polyethylene insert articulating with both components.[29] The high conformity minimizes contact stress and PE wear[28] but introduces the implant-specific failure of bearing dislocation — consistently the predominant mechanism of failure for MB UKA.[1] Unexplained pain is also significantly more common in MB designs.[5]
  • Fixed-Bearing (FB) UKA: Features a PE insert fixed to the tibial baseplate with less-conforming articular surfaces. Polyethylene wear and aseptic loosening are the main causes of failure.[1]

While this distinction is a reliable general principle, clinical data presents a more complex picture. A 2015 systematic review by van der List et al. identified aseptic loosening as the most common overall failure mode for medial UKA (36%), and found loosening to be more common in MB implants (35%) than in FB implants (28%).[5] This is not a contradiction but a reflection of a different causal pathway: in FB designs, aseptic loosening is often a primary event from wear debris-induced osteolysis; in MB designs, it is frequently a secondary consequence of primary instability.[32]

The timing of failure provides critical information about its underlying cause. The systematic review by van der List et al. offers a precise temporal breakdown:[5]

2.3 Bearing Dislocation: Multifactorial Mechanisms

Causes of Early Bearing Dislocation (Technical Error)

Early dislocations are almost invariably due to technical errors at the index surgery:[1]

  • Component Malposition: Improper rotation, sizing, or alignment of the femoral or tibial component.[1]
  • Flexion-Extension Gap Imbalance: The most critical factor. An unbalanced gap allows the bearing to subluxate and "spin out" of its captured position. Meticulous ligament balancing without soft tissue releases is essential.[1, 32]
  • Impingement: Residual osteophytes[26], posterior meniscal remnants[27], or extruded cement debris can lever the bearing out of place.[1]
  • Iatrogenic Ligament Laxity: Inadvertent MCL release creates excessive laxity, precipitating dislocation.[1]

Causes of Late Bearing Dislocation

Late dislocation is often the "end-stage" manifestation of another chronic failure mode:[33]

  • Ligamentous Attenuation: Chronic micro-instability leads to progressive attenuation of collateral ligaments, eventually precipitating gross instability.[33]
  • PE Wear: After many years, wear creates excess mobility within the joint space.[33]
  • Component Loosening: Aseptic loosening creates gross instability that can present as bearing dislocation.[1]
⚠ Bearing Fracture Risk

A rare but catastrophic complication is fracture of the polyethylene bearing. The primary risk factor is selection of thin polyethylene inserts (< 4 mm).[33] One case report detailed a late, atraumatic bearing fracture-dislocation where the initial 3-mm bearing was implicated.[33] This creates a direct conflict between bone conservation goals and long-term implant durability.

⚠ Lateral UKA: High Dislocation Risk

Bearing dislocation is significantly more common in lateral UKA than medial UKA.[32] The LCL is lax in flexion (vs. MCL tight in flexion), and the lateral femoral condyle is convex, creating an inherently less-constrained environment. Many experts recommend using only fixed-bearing implants for lateral UKA.[32]

2.4 Underlying Causes of Aseptic Loosening & OA Progression

Aseptic Loosening

Often secondary to instability in MB UKA, but primary causes include:[1, 8]

  • Component malalignment (especially varus tibial cut)
  • Undercorrection of preoperative varus deformity
  • Excessive posterior tibial slope
  • ACL deficiency
  • All-polyethylene tibial components (associated with higher loosening rates)[1]

Progression of OA

The dominant cause of late failure — often iatrogenic:[1]

  • Overcorrection of leg mechanical axis (varus → too much valgus) → lateral compartment overload → accelerated degeneration
  • Oversizing the femoral component → impingement against patellar cartilage → patellofemoral arthritis

Paradigm Shift

Success of UKA appears less limited by the patient's demographics (age, weight) and more by the surgeon's technical ability to manage biomechanics — for example, correctly managing tibial slope in an ACL-deficient knee or precisely restoring the constitutional axis without iatrogenic overcorrection.[42]

III. Surgical Technique for Optimal Femoral Component Alignment

3.1 The Intramedullary Rod Entry Point

Precise component alignment is fundamental to UKA success and prevention of the failure modes detailed above.[45] While extramedullary (EM) cutting guides are available, intramedullary (IM) referencing remains a common, accurate, and reproducible technique for femoral alignment.[35]

⚠ Critical Difference from TKA

In TKA, the IM rod entry hole is made in an area subsequently resected and resurfaced. In UKA, the entry hole is made in the trochlea — an area NOT resurfaced that remains as native articular cartilage.[46] An inaccurately placed entry point not only compromises alignment but creates iatrogenic damage to a load-bearing articular surface.

3.2 Anatomical Landmark Guidance

Evidence from TKA (The Guiding Principle): The ideal entry point is slightly medial and anterior to the apex of the intercondylar notch (AIN):[11, 48, 52]

  • Wangroongsub et al.: 1.5 mm ± 2.01 mm medial and 12 mm ± 2.72 mm superior to the top of the intercondylar notch[48]
  • CAD studies: 3.6–4.3 mm medial and 9.2–9.9 mm anterior to AIN[11]
  • Xiao et al. (sex-specific): Males: 0.17 mm medial / 12.37 mm anterior; Females: 0.02 mm lateral / 16.13 mm anterior[52]

UKA-Specific Technique (Actionable Landmarks): The current Oxford Microplasty instrumentation guide provides precise and reproducible landmarks:[9]

✓ Optimal IM Entry Point (Oxford Microplasty)

The hole should be situated approximately 1 cm anterior to the insertion of the PCL and 2–3 mm lateral to the medial wall of the intercondylar notch.[9] The rod should be aimed toward the patient's anterior superior iliac spine (ASIS).[9]

Table 2: Anatomical Landmarks & Biomechanical Consequences of IM Rod Entry Point Deviation[9–13]
Entry Point Deviation Biomechanical Effect Resulting Malalignment Clinical Failure Mode
Medial Rod toggles into valgus (5 mm medial ≈ 2.4° error)[10] Increased medial resection → Varus Malalignment[10] Undercorrection → implant overload → Aseptic Loosening[1]
Lateral Rod toggles into varus (5 mm lateral ≈ 2.3° error)[10] Decreased medial resection → Valgus Malalignment[10] Overcorrection → lateral overload → Progression of OA[1]
Posterior Rod deviates into flexion[11] Rod tip engages and perforates anterior cortex[12] Iatrogenic Supracondylar Fracture[12, 13]
Anterior Rod deviates into extension[11] Femoral component flexion malalignment Gap mismatch → Bearing Dislocation[1]

3.3 Biomechanical Consequences of Entry Point Error

Coronal Plane Deviation

The relationship is critical and somewhat counter-intuitive:[10]

  • A medial entry point causes the rod to toggle into valgus → increased medial resection → final component in varus malalignment → undercorrection → aseptic loosening.[1]
  • A lateral entry point causes the rod to toggle into varus → decreased resection → valgus malalignment → overcorrection → progression of OA.[1]

Sagittal Plane Deviation

⚠ Counter-Intuitive Fracture Mechanism

A surgeon might logically assume an anterior entry point risks anterior cortex perforation. The biomechanical evidence shows the opposite. A posterior entry point forces the straight IM rod into flexion alignment relative to the bone's anterior bow. As it is advanced, its tip travels in a straight line, failing to follow the femur's curve, and perforates the anterior cortex distally.[12] To avoid perforation, the entry point must be placed sufficiently anterior.[12] This risk is particularly high in patients with significant lateral femoral bowing.[54]

IV. Decision-Making Framework for Failed UKA Revision

4.1 Algorithmic Approach to Revision

The optimal strategy is dictated entirely by the specific cause of failure. Three primary revision pathways exist:[1]

1

Polyethylene Insert Exchange

Indications: Isolated bearing dislocation or PE wear only if all components are stable, well-aligned, no severe osteolysis, and all ligaments intact.[1]

2

Revision UKA-to-UKA

Indications: Sufficient bone stock, complete ligament integrity, no OA progression. A Mayo Clinic study found it was most commonly performed for acute periprosthetic joint infection (PJI) (71%), followed by PE dislocations (12%) and aseptic loosening (12%).[14]

3

Conversion to TKA — The Gold Standard

Indications: Progression of OA, aseptic loosening, recurrent bearing dislocation, chronic PJI, periprosthetic fracture, unexplained pain.[1]

Periprosthetic Fracture Conversion to TKA Requires stemmed component to bypass fracture.[1]

4.2 Comparative Survivorship: Why Conversion to TKA is the Gold Standard

UKA-to-UKA revision carries a prohibitively high failure rate:[14, 15]

  • Scandinavian registry analysis: Of 35 cases revised to another UKA, 45.7% required a second revision. Of 485 cases converted to TKA, only 12.5% required re-revision.[15]
  • Mayo Clinic 2025 study: 2-year survivorship free of re-revision was only 75% for UKA-to-UKA vs. 96% for TKA conversion — a six-fold higher failure rate.[14]
  • Even for acute PJI (the most common UKA-to-UKA indication), survivorship was only 67%.[14]
⚠ Evidence-Based Conclusion

Conversion to TKA is the definitive standard of care for failed UKA due to vastly superior implant survivorship.[14] UKA-to-UKA revision should be considered a high-risk, interim procedure, and patients must be counseled on the high probability of requiring subsequent conversion to TKA.

4.3 Surgical Technique for UKA-to-TKA Conversion

Conversion is more complex than primary TKA. Surgeons must anticipate: atypical surgical exposures[58], difficult component removal[3], joint line restoration[56], instability management, and — most importantly — significant bone defects.[3]

Analysis of 4,438 UKA-to-TKA conversions from the Australian Orthopaedic Association National Joint Replacement Registry (AOANJRR) demonstrated:[18]

  • 10-year CPR without stem: 19% vs. with stem: 13% (p=0.007)
  • Adding modular augments on top of a stem provided no additional benefit (p=0.251)

Use of a tibial stem extension appears mandatory to improve long-term survivorship.[18]

V. Concluding Insights & Expert Recommendations

Recommendation 1: Etiology Awareness

Surgeons must recognize that MB UKA failure is distinct. The predominant failure mode — bearing dislocation[1] — is overwhelmingly an early event (< 5 years)[5] directly linked to technical errors in component positioning, osteophyte removal, and flexion-extension gap balancing.[1]

Recommendation 2: Prevention

Patient Selection: Adopt modern criteria. Do not exclude based on age (< 60) or high BMI — meta-analyses show these do not increase revision risk.[42] Focus on biomechanical contraindications: correctable deformity, intact ligaments (or plan to reconstruct), no inflammatory arthritis.

Surgical Technique: Use precise, reproducible landmarks — 1 cm anterior to the PCL insertion and 2–3 mm lateral to the medial notch wall[9] — to prevent iatrogenic varus/valgus malalignment[10] and catastrophic anterior femoral perforation.[12]

Recommendation 3: Revision

  • Avoid UKA-to-UKA as a definitive solution (25–45% 2-year re-revision rate).[14]
  • Embrace UKA-to-TKA conversion as the gold standard.[1]
  • Routinely use a tibial stem extension — proven by large-scale registry data to improve 10-year survivorship.[18]
✓ Final Conclusion

The long-standing argument that UKA "burns bridges" is false. A well-performed primary UKA provides functionally superior results to TKA.[20] A failed UKA converted to TKA with modern techniques (i.e., with a tibial stem) yields functional outcomes equivalent to a primary TKA.[19] This reliable and effective revision pathway should embolden surgeons to use this bone-preserving, high-functioning primary option in all appropriate candidates.
