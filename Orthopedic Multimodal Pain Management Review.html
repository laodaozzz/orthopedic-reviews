1. Foundational Principles & Guidelines for Multimodal Analgesia

The Core Concept: Synergistic Analgesia Across Multiple Pathways

Multimodal analgesia (MMA), also termed "balanced analgesia," is defined by the simultaneous use of multiple analgesic medications and techniques that act on different receptors and sites along the peripheral and central pain pathways. Level I[1, 2, 8]

The rationale is grounded in the complex cascade of surgical pain: tissue injury triggers inflammatory mediators (prostaglandins, IL-6) that sensitize peripheral nociceptors, leading to primary hyperalgesia. Sustained peripheral input drives central sensitization — neuroplastic changes in the spinal cord and brain that amplify pain signals and may contribute to chronic postsurgical pain. Level II[2, 4, 11]

🎯 Four Targets of Effective MMA
  • Inflammation: NSAIDs & corticosteroids inhibit prostaglandin synthesis [1]
  • Peripheral Nociception: Local anesthetics via regional blocks or infiltration [1]
  • Central Processing: Acetaminophen, opioids, NMDA antagonists (ketamine) [3]
  • Conduction Modulation: Gabapentinoids (role now debated) [1]

The primary clinical goals of MMA are to provide superior pain relief, facilitate earlier mobilization and rehabilitation, shorten hospital length of stay, and decrease reliance on systemic opioids — thereby reducing respiratory depression, sedation, PONV, pruritus, urinary retention, ileus, and the risk of new persistent opioid use. Level I[2]

📋 Paradigm Shift: From Clinical Practice to Public Health Strategy

The engagement of AAOS, ASA, and ASRA with the CDC on national prescribing guidelines and the development of the ASA/AAOS "Pain Toolkit" demonstrate a profession-wide response to the opioid epidemic. MMA is no longer merely a best practice — it is a cornerstone of opioid stewardship. [1, 5, 7]

Synthesis of Professional Society Guidelines

A strong and consistent consensus exists across major North American medical societies endorsing MMA as the definitive standard of care. Level I[5]

Implementing MMA Across the Perioperative Continuum

Effective MMA spans the entire perioperative period and requires close multidisciplinary collaboration. Level II[2]

1
Pre-emptive Analgesia — Administered 1–2 hours before surgery to prevent central sensitization. Common regimen: celecoxib 200–400 mg + acetaminophen 1000 mg ± gabapentinoid. [10, 11]
2
Intraoperative Phase — Regional anesthesia (neuraxial or PNB), local infiltration analgesia (LIA) with dilute ropivacaine cocktail, systemic adjuvants (IV acetaminophen, ketorolac, dexamethasone, sub-anesthetic ketamine). [1, 2, 22]
3
Postoperative Phase — Scheduled (ATC) non-opioid analgesics (acetaminophen + NSAID); opioids relegated to PRN rescue role. Dynamic reassessment using validated pain scales. [1, 7]
🔑 Key Concept: "Checklist, Not Recipe"

A recipe implies rigid instructions for every patient. A checklist ensures all analgesic categories are systematically considered, then empowers the clinician to select or omit based on individual assessment (age, renal/hepatic function, cardiovascular history, allergies, opioid tolerance, psychological factors). [8]

2. NSAIDs & Acetaminophen

Mechanism and Efficacy

NSAIDs inhibit cyclooxygenase (COX) enzymes, reducing prostaglandin production that sensitizes peripheral nociceptors following surgical trauma. Recent evidence also suggests a central analgesic effect via endogenous opioid peptides. Level II[10, 34]

For many types of postoperative pain, NSAIDs demonstrate analgesic efficacy comparable to opioids but with a markedly superior side-effect profile. The combination of ibuprofen 400 mg + acetaminophen 1000 mg achieves an NNT of 1.5 for ≥50% pain relief — indicating very high efficacy. Level I[8]

Recent analyses suggest that NSAIDs + dexamethasone may provide the greatest reduction in postoperative opioid consumption. [33]

The Bone Healing Controversy

This remains the most debated topic in orthopedic analgesic pharmacology.

Basic Science & Animal Data

Prostaglandins synthesized via COX-2 are critical for osteoblast differentiation and function during fracture healing. Animal studies consistently demonstrate that NSAIDs impair callus formation and reduce mechanical strength. Level III (animal data)[35]

Human Clinical Evidence — The Paradigm Shift

A critical 2016 systematic review revealed that studies reporting a negative effect of NSAIDs on bone healing were of significantly lower methodological quality than those concluding safety — suggesting publication and confirmation bias. Level I (SR)[38]

📊 Most Compelling Evidence (2023 Meta-Analysis)

A meta-analysis encompassing 20 studies and 523,000+ patients found no significant difference in non-union or delayed union between NSAID users and non-users (pooled adjusted OR = 1.11; 95% CI: 0.99–1.23). Level I[39]

A 2018 meta-analysis did find overall NSAID exposure associated with increased odds of non-union (OR 2.07), but this effect was absent in pediatric patients and those with low-dose/short-duration exposure. [41]

NSAID Class Non-Union Risk (Adjusted OR) Clinical Implication
Non-selective NSAIDs (e.g., ibuprofen, naproxen) 1.07 (95% CI: not significant) [42] No statistically significant increase in non-union risk
COX-2 Selective Inhibitors (e.g., celecoxib) 1.84 (95% CI: significant) [42] Significantly greater risk — prefer non-selective agents in fracture care

⚠️ Clinical Paradox

Withholding NSAIDs has no proven scientific benefit and "may even cause harm by increasing narcotic requirements." The default should be to use short-term, non-selective NSAIDs for acute postoperative pain, even after fracture surgery, unless specific high-risk factors exist. [38]

NSAID Precautions & Contraindications

Cardiovascular & Renal Risk

All NSAIDs (except possibly naproxen ≤1000 mg/day) carry a black box warning for serious cardiovascular thrombotic events. They are contraindicated after CABG surgery and in advanced renal disease. Risk increases with dehydration, heart failure, or concurrent use of diuretics, ACE inhibitors, or ARBs. Level I[2, 8, 44]

Gastrointestinal & Hematologic Risks

Non-selective NSAIDs increase risk of dyspepsia, gastritis, peptic ulcer disease, and GI bleeding — especially in elderly patients, those with prior ulcer history, or concurrent corticosteroids/anticoagulants. NSAIDs impair platelet aggregation and are typically discontinued ~1 week before elective surgery. [10, 45]

Acetaminophen: The Cornerstone Non-Opioid

Acetaminophen acts primarily within the CNS (COX inhibition, serotonergic, cannabinoid, and nitric oxide modulation). It is an effective analgesic and antipyretic but lacks significant peripheral anti-inflammatory activity. [2, 10]

Its favorable safety profile makes it the first-line alternative for patients with NSAID contraindications (renal disease, peptic ulcer, anticoagulant use). Primary contraindication: severe hepatic disease or active heavy alcohol use. Level I[2]

Notably, a 2025 analysis found that within robust MMA regimens, the combination of NSAIDs + dexamethasone provided the greatest reduction in pain and opioid use in the first 24 hours — suggesting acetaminophen's independent contribution may be less pronounced than previously thought. [33]

3. Opioid Stewardship in the Orthopedic Perioperative Setting

The Evolving Role of Opioids

Within a modern MMA framework, opioids are relegated to a secondary, "rescue" role for breakthrough pain not controlled by foundational non-opioid analgesics. Orthopedic surgeons are among the highest prescribing specialties, placing significant responsibility on the field. Level II[1, 50]

Evidence-Based Prescribing Principles

🚨 Critical Principle: Lowest Feasible Dose, Duration, and Quantity

The risk of chronic opioid use increases dramatically with longer initial prescription durations. Initial prescriptions for acute pain should be limited to 3–7 days. The probability of long-term use rises sharply when an initial prescription exceeds 5–7 days. [18, 29]

Only immediate-release (IR) formulations should be prescribed for acute postoperative pain. Extended-release/long-acting (ER/LA) formulations have no role in acute, resolving postoperative pain. [18]

Procedure-Specific Prescribing Guidelines

A more sophisticated strategy involves establishing procedure-specific guidelines and upper prescribing limits based on anticipated postoperative pain levels. [50, 52]

Preoperative Risk Stratification

Preoperative identification of patients at high risk for problematic opioid use is critical. Key risk factors include: Level II[53]

  • Preoperative opioid use — the single most powerful predictor [53]
  • Personal/family history of substance use disorder [53]
  • Psychiatric conditions (depression, anxiety, PTSD) [53]
  • Pain catastrophizing [52]
  • Younger age, tobacco use [53]

Validated screening tools include the Opioid Risk Tool (ORT) and the SOAPP-R. Their predictive validity in the acute surgical setting is still under investigation. [53, 55]

🏥 Conceptual Shift: "Pain Clearance"

Data support reframing preoperative assessment to include a formal "pain clearance" — analogous to cardiac or pulmonary clearance — elevating chronic opioid use from a "behavioral issue" to a significant medical comorbidity that directly impacts surgical risk and outcomes. [54]

Managing the Opioid-Tolerant Patient

Patients on chronic opioid therapy require specialized, multidisciplinary management: coordinate care with their prescriber, continue baseline opioids throughout the perioperative period to prevent withdrawal, manage acute pain with additional short-acting opioids, and deploy enhanced MMA with adjunctive agents (e.g., low-dose IV ketamine). [3, 52, 58]

4. Gabapentinoids — Evidence Reappraisal

Mechanism of Action

Gabapentinoids (gabapentin, pregabalin) bind the α₂-δ subunit of voltage-gated calcium channels, reducing release of excitatory neurotransmitters (glutamate, norepinephrine, substance P) and dampening neuronal hyperexcitability in states of central sensitization. [15]

Efficacy: A Critical Re-evaluation

A landmark systematic review by Verret et al. (281 RCTs, ~25,000 patients) found that while small, statistically significant reductions in pain scores existed at early time points, the magnitude was below clinical significance (<10 points on a 100-point scale). No clinically significant analgesic effect and no effect on preventing chronic postsurgical pain were found. Level I[59]

Outcome Gabapentin Pregabalin
Pain Reduction (TJA) No impact vs. placebo at any time point [63] Mixed evidence; modest benefit in some studies [65]
Opioid-Sparing Effect No effect [65] Moderate reduction in opioid consumption [65]
Prevention of Chronic Pain No effect demonstrated [59]
Table 4: Gabapentinoid Efficacy in Total Joint Arthroplasty (TJA). Based on AAHKS CPG systematic review. [63, 65]

Adverse Effects — A Growing Safety Concern

CNS Effects

Sedation, dizziness, visual disturbances, and ataxia — all of which impede early mobilization and functional recovery. Pregabalin is associated with a moderately increased risk of postoperative sedation. [8, 59, 65]

Elderly Population: Delirium Risk

🔴 Critical Safety Signal in Elderly (≥65 years)

A large cohort study found perioperative gabapentin use associated with significantly increased risk of:

  • Postoperative delirium (RR 1.28)
  • New antipsychotic use (RR 1.17)
  • Pneumonia (RR 1.11)

Level II [68]

Respiratory Depression with Concomitant Opioids

The combination of gabapentinoids + opioids potentiates respiratory depression. The 2019 AGS Beers Criteria specifically recommends avoiding this combination in older adults. A meta-analysis found the combination associated with increased CNS depression and mortality compared to opioids alone. [71]

📌 Current Consensus Recommendations
  • Avoid routine, protocol-driven use for all orthopedic patients [59]
  • Consider targeted use only in select patients (e.g., pre-existing severe neuropathic pain, severe opioid tolerance) [15]
  • Use extreme caution or avoid in elderly patients [68]
  • If used, prescribe lowest effective dose for shortest duration [65]

Gabapentinoids represent a powerful case study in de-implementation — the difficult process of removing a widely adopted practice from ingrained protocols once new evidence demonstrates lack of benefit or unacceptable harm. [59]

5. Local Anesthetics — Benefits & Critical Safety Considerations

Regional Anesthesia in Orthopedic MMA

Regional anesthesia provides potent, site-specific analgesia superior to systemic therapies. Strongly recommended by ASA and ASRA. Level I[8, 9]

  • Neuraxial: Spinal/epidural for lower extremity arthroplasty [21]
  • Peripheral Nerve Blocks: Interscalene/supraclavicular (shoulder), adductor canal/femoral (knee), popliteal/sciatic (foot/ankle). Continuous catheters can extend analgesia for days. [8]
  • Local Infiltration Analgesia (LIA): Surgeon-administered high-volume, dilute local anesthetic cocktail (± ketorolac, epinephrine) into periarticular tissues. [1]

Local Anesthetic Systemic Toxicity (LAST)

LAST is a rare (2–2.8 per 10,000 blocks) but life-threatening event from excessive systemic local anesthetic concentration. [73]

Clinical Presentation

CNS toxicity (typically first): Circumoral numbness, metallic taste, tinnitus, confusion → seizures → coma → respiratory arrest. CVS toxicity: Hypertension/tachycardia → myocardial depression → hypotension, bradycardia, widened QRS → ventricular arrhythmias → asystole. Bupivacaine is particularly cardiotoxic. [73]

Recent ASRA advisories highlight delayed presentations (onset 10–60+ minutes post-injection). [73]

ASRA Management Checklist

Step Action
1. STOP Immediately cease local anesthetic injection
2. Call for HELP Request LAST Rescue Kit and additional personnel
3. Airway Ventilate with 100% O₂; secure airway if needed (hypoxia/acidosis worsen LAST)
4. Seizures First-line: Benzodiazepines (midazolam, lorazepam). Avoid neuromuscular blockade before seizure suppression
5. Lipid Emulsion 20% Intralipid: Bolus 1.5 mL/kg (or 100 mL if >70 kg) over 2–3 min → infusion 0.25 mL/kg/min (or 250 mL/15–20 min). Begin at first sign of significant toxicity — do not wait for arrest.
6. Arrhythmias IV fluids + vasopressors for hypotension. Modified ACLS: small epinephrine doses (≤1 µg/kg); amiodarone preferred; AVOID vasopressin, CCBs, beta-blockers, lidocaine
7. Monitoring Observe ≥2 hours (isolated CNS) or ≥4–6 hours (CVS instability)
Table 5: ASRA Checklist for LAST Management. [73, 76, 77]

Local Anesthetic-Induced Chondrotoxicity

Chondrotoxicity is a direct cytotoxic effect on articular chondrocytes, leading to cell death and potential chondrolysis. Unlike LAST, it is insidious — damage occurs at exposure, but clinical presentation (progressive joint pain, stiffness, accelerated OA) may not manifest for months to years. [79]

Risk Factors

  • Prolonged exposure: Continuous intra-articular infusions ("pain pumps") are definitively linked to post-arthroscopic chondrolysis and are now contraindicated [79]
  • Higher concentrations: Significant cytotoxicity with lidocaine >1% and bupivacaine >0.175% [80]
  • Adjuvants: Corticosteroids and epinephrine may potentiate chondrotoxic effects [82]
Local Anesthetic Relative Chondrotoxicity Clinical Recommendation
Bupivacaine 🔴 Highest Avoid for intra-articular use when possible
Lidocaine 🟠 Moderate–High Use lowest concentration; avoid prolonged exposure
Ropivacaine 🟡 Lower Preferred agent; ≤0.5% concentration recommended
Levobupivacaine 🟢 Lowest Least studied but potentially safest option
Table 6: Relative Chondrotoxicity of Common Intra-articular Local Anesthetics. Based on in vitro systematic reviews. Co-administration with corticosteroids may worsen toxicity for all agents. [79, 80, 82]

6. Patient-Specific, Procedure-Specific Protocol Construction

Illustrative Protocol: Primary Total Knee Arthroplasty (TKA)

Preoperative (1–2 hrs before incision)

  • Acetaminophen 1000 mg PO
  • Celecoxib 200–400 mg PO (if no contraindications)
  • Avoid routine gabapentinoids unless specific indication and low-risk patient (<65 yrs, good renal function)

Intraoperative

  • Anesthesia: Neuraxial (spinal) preferred
  • Regional Block: Adductor canal block ± IPACK block with ropivacaine or liposomal bupivacaine
  • LIA: Periarticular injection with low-concentration ropivacaine (0.2–0.5%)
  • Systemic Adjuvants: Dexamethasone 8–10 mg IV, Ketorolac 15–30 mg IV

Postoperative

  • Scheduled: Acetaminophen 650–1000 mg PO q6–8h AND NSAID (naproxen 500 mg q12h or celecoxib 200 mg q12h) for ≥72 hours, up to 2 weeks
  • Rescue: Oxycodone 5 mg PO q4–6h PRN for severe breakthrough pain only
  • Non-pharmacologic: Cryotherapy/ice application

Based on source document protocols. [30]

Illustrative Protocol: Lumbar Spinal Fusion

Preoperative (1–2 hrs before incision)

  • Acetaminophen 1000 mg PO
  • Celecoxib 200–400 mg PO (controversial — requires surgeon-anesthesiologist discussion regarding fusion risk)
  • Gabapentin 300–600 mg may be considered cautiously, weighing benefit against sedation risk

Intraoperative

  • LIA: Local anesthetic infiltration of incision and paraspinal muscles prior to closure
  • Systemic Adjuvants: Low-dose ketamine infusion (0.25 mg/kg/hr), IV lidocaine infusion, dexamethasone IV

Postoperative

  • Scheduled: Acetaminophen 1000 mg IV/PO q6–8h
  • NSAIDs: Highly debated. If used: short course (<5 days) of IV ketorolac; many surgeons avoid entirely due to fusion risk
  • Adjuvants: Muscle relaxant (cyclobenzaprine, methocarbamol) for paraspinal spasm
  • Opioids: PCA with hydromorphone/morphine for first 24–48 hours, transitioning to oral PRN

Based on source document protocols. [12, 31, 88]

Emerging Concepts

  • Pharmacological: Extended-release local anesthetic formulations, refined roles for dexmedetomidine and ketamine [2]
  • Regenerative Medicine: PRP, stem cell therapies for tissue repair and pain modulation [92]
  • Non-Pharmacologic: Preoperative cryoneurolysis, virtual reality distraction, wearable biosensors, AI-based pain trajectory prediction [86, 94]
  • Cannabinoids: Growing area of investigation [95]

7. Landmark Studies Summary

⚠ Source Limitation: The source document is a narrative review that synthesizes evidence from multiple meta-analyses and guidelines rather than presenting original RCTs. The following key studies are those most prominently cited and influential within the document's evidence base.
Verret et al. — Perioperative Gabapentinoids Systematic Review & Meta-analysis
281 RCTs, ~25,000 patients | Level I
No clinically significant analgesic effect for perioperative gabapentinoids; no effect on chronic postsurgical pain prevention. Effect magnitude <10/100 points — below clinical significance threshold. [59]
2023 Large-Scale Meta-analysis — NSAIDs and Bone Healing
20 studies, 523,000+ patients | Level I
No significant difference in non-union/delayed union between NSAID users and non-users (adjusted OR 1.11; 95% CI: 0.99–1.23). Challenged decades of clinical dogma. [39]
JBJS Retrospective Cohort — COX-2 vs. Non-selective NSAIDs and Non-Union
Large retrospective cohort | Level II
COX-2 selective inhibitors: OR 1.84 for non-union (significant). Non-selective NSAIDs: OR 1.07 (not significant). Agent-specific risk differentiation has direct protocol implications. [42]
2016 Systematic Review — NSAID Quality Bias Assessment
Systematic review of clinical studies | Level I (SR)
Studies reporting negative NSAID effects on bone healing were of significantly lower methodological quality than those concluding safety — suggesting publication/confirmation bias in the literature. [38]
Large Cohort Study — Perioperative Gabapentin in Elderly (≥65)
Large cohort, major surgery, age ≥65 | Level II
Gabapentin associated with increased risk of postoperative delirium (RR 1.28), new antipsychotic use (RR 1.17), and pneumonia (RR 1.11). Strong argument against routine use in older adults. [68]

8. Key Recommendations for Clinical Practice

1

Embrace MMA as the default standard. Implementation is driven by superior analgesia for recovery and professional responsibility of opioid stewardship.

2

Build on a non-opioid foundation. Scheduled ATC acetaminophen + NSAID. Opioids reserved exclusively for breakthrough pain.

3

Use NSAIDs wisely. Fear of non-union not strongly supported by high-quality human data for short-term, non-selective agents. Withholding may increase opioid requirements.

4

Re-evaluate gabapentinoid role. Routine protocolized use not supported. Limited efficacy outweighed by significant risks (especially delirium in elderly). Restrict to select patients with specific indications.

5

Leverage regional anesthesia safely. Powerful opioid-sparing tool. Requires institutional preparedness: LAST rescue kit availability and regular staff training.

6

Prioritize preoperative risk assessment. Identify patients at high risk for problematic opioid use — especially chronic opioid users, those with SUD history, or significant psychiatric comorbidities.

7

Individualize the "checklist." Transition from rigid protocols to dynamic, procedure-specific, patient-tailored pain management plans.
