1. Introduction: Defining the Maisonneuve Fracture Complex

The Maisonneuve fracture represents a severe, unstable injury to the ankle and lower leg that is frequently misdiagnosed due to its complex pathoanatomy and misleading clinical presentation. Its significance lies not in the conspicuous proximal fibula fracture, but in the profound disruption of the ankle mortise that the fibula fracture signifies. A nuanced understanding of its historical context, pathoanatomic components, and biomechanical origins is fundamental to successful diagnosis and management, which aims to prevent the devastating long-term sequelae of chronic instability and post-traumatic arthritis. Narrative Review [1]

Historical Context and Eponym

The injury is named after the 19th-century French surgeon Jules Germain François Maisonneuve, who, in 1840, first elucidated the underlying mechanism of injury through cadaveric experiments. Cadaveric Study [3] Maisonneuve demonstrated that a forceful external rotation applied to the foot could produce a predictable sequence of ligamentous and bony failures. He observed that if the primary ankle stabilizers (the malleoli) remained intact, the rotational force would first rupture the tibiofibular ligaments. As the force continued, it would propagate superiorly along the interosseous membrane until it exited through the proximal third of the fibula. Cadaveric Study [4]

2. Pathoanatomy: The Triad of Injury

The Maisonneuve fracture is a complex injury constellation, not a single fracture. Its classic definition encompasses a triad of injuries that collectively render the ankle mortise unstable. Narrative Review [1]

  1. Proximal Fibula Fracture: The hallmark and eponymous feature. Typically a spiral or oblique fracture located in the proximal one-third or one-quarter of the fibular diaphysis, often near the fibular neck. Radiographic analysis frequently reveals a characteristic fracture line orientation running from anterosuperior to posteroinferior, a direct reflection of the external rotation force vector. CT Study [3]
  2. Tibiofibular Syndesmosis Disruption: The critical element responsible for the profound instability. This involves a complete rupture of the anterior inferior tibiofibular ligament (AITFL), the interosseous ligament (IOL), and frequently the posterior inferior tibiofibular ligament (PITFL). This ligamentous failure creates a diastasis (widening) between the tibia and fibula. Narrative Review [1]
  3. Medial Ankle Injury: The initiating event involves failure of the medial stabilizing structures—either a complete rupture of the deep fibers of the deltoid ligament or a transverse/avulsion fracture of the medial malleolus. Narrative Review [1]
💡 Key Concept
Integral to this pattern is the associated tear of the interosseous membrane, the strong fibrous sheet connecting the tibia and fibula. This membrane serves as the conduit for the injurious force, transmitting rotational energy from the disrupted syndesmosis proximally to the point of failure in the fibula. While historically inferred, the extent of interosseous membrane tearing has been more accurately characterized with modern MRI. MRI Study [11]

3. Biomechanics: The Pronation-External Rotation Cascade and Variants

The classic mechanism of injury is a pronation-external rotation (PER) force, as described by the Lauge-Hansen classification system, corresponding to a stage III or IV injury. Narrative Review [1]

1
Stage I: Foot in pronation + external rotation → tension on medial structures → rupture of deep deltoid ligament or medial malleolus fracture.
2
Stage II: Continued external rotation of the talus → rupture of AITFL and interosseous ligament (IOL).
3
Stage III: Rotational energy ascends the leg via the interosseous membrane.
4
Stage IV: Force exits as a spiral/oblique fracture of the proximal fibula.

The SER Variant

Clinical studies have identified a less frequent supination-external rotation (SER) variant, reported in up to 13% of cases in some series. This variant can present with intact medial structures, as the injury sequence begins with failure of the anterior structures (AITFL) before progressing to the fibula fracture, potentially sparing the deltoid ligament. Case Series [12]

⚠️ Clinical Implication
The Maisonneuve fracture is fundamentally an injury of the entire tibiofibular unit. The proximal fibula fracture is merely the most overt bony sign of a complete ligamentous catastrophe that destabilizes the ankle. The true pathology is the failure of the syndesmosis and interosseous membrane. This explains why the proximal fibula fracture itself is rarely the target of surgical fixation. Review [8]

4. Diagnostic Pathway: From Clinical Suspicion to Definitive Imaging

Diagnosis is notoriously challenging and relies on a high index of clinical suspicion, meticulous physical examination, and a specific radiographic protocol. The most common error is the failure to examine the entire lower leg in a patient presenting with what appears to be a simple ankle sprain or isolated medial malleolus fracture. Imaging Review [18]

Clinical Evaluation

  • Palpation of the Entire Fibula: The single most critical maneuver. Systematically palpate from the lateral malleolus to the fibular head. Proximal fibular tenderness in a patient with an apparent ankle injury is the cardinal sign and mandates further imaging. Narrative Review [1]
  • Squeeze Test: Compressing tibia and fibula at mid-calf elicits pain at the syndesmosis if injured. Clinical Study [6]
  • External Rotation Stress Test: Stabilizing the tibia and externally rotating the foot reproduces syndesmotic pain. Clinical Study [6]
  • Neurological Examination: Assess common peroneal nerve function (foot drop risk) due to its course around the fibular head. Review [7]

Radiographic Assessment

Standard ankle radiographs (AP, Mortise, Lateral) must be scrutinized for indirect signs of syndesmotic disruption. The following measurements are critical:

Full-length tibia-fibula radiographs are MANDATORY whenever ankle radiographs demonstrate signs of medial-sided injury or syndesmotic widening without a corresponding distal fibula fracture. Failure to adhere to this principle is the most common reason for a missed diagnosis. Cadaveric/Clinical Study [4]

5. Advanced Imaging Modalities

Computed Tomography (CT)

CT scanning has become an indispensable tool in the modern management of Maisonneuve fractures. A preoperative CT is essential for: CT Study [10]

  • Characterizing associated posterior malleolus fractures (present in approximately 80% of cases) CT Study [3]
  • Identifying occult, non-displaced fractures missed on plain films Clinical Study [6]
  • Assessing syndesmotic congruity and detecting subtle rotational/translational malalignment CT Study [10]
  • Postoperative verification of syndesmotic reduction accuracy—the gold standard, as intraoperative fluoroscopy can be unreliable Clinical Study [24]

Magnetic Resonance Imaging (MRI)

While not routinely required for diagnosis, MRI offers unparalleled direct visualization of soft-tissue structures: MRI Study [10]

  • Confirming deltoid or syndesmotic ligament rupture when radiographs are equivocal Case Report [5]
  • Direct visualization of interosseous membrane injury extent—the only modality capable of this MRI Study [11]
  • Identifying associated osteochondral lesions of the talus Clinical Study [22]
  • Guiding non-operative management in rare stable variants Case Report [5]

6. The Keystone of Instability: Syndesmotic Assessment & Management

The defining feature of a Maisonneuve fracture is the complete disruption of the tibiofibular syndesmosis, which transforms a stable ankle mortise into an unstable joint destined for early arthritis if untreated.

Intra-operative Dynamic Stress Testing

The gold standard for confirming syndesmotic instability is intra-operative dynamic stress testing, performed under fluoroscopy after any associated malleolar fractures have been anatomically fixed. Diagnostic Study [31]

  • External Rotation Stress Test: Applies external rotation to the foot. Biomechanical studies suggest it is more sensitive for deltoid competence (MCS widening) than pure tibiofibular instability. Diagnostic Study [31]
  • Hook Test (Cotton Test): Considered more reliable and sensitive. A bone hook around the distal fibula applies direct lateral or AP force. Positive if fibula displacement > 2 mm or tibiofibular clear space > 5 mm on lateral fluoroscopy. Sagittal plane assessment appears more sensitive. Diagnostic Study [31]
  • Ankle Arthroscopy: Provides direct visualization of gapping and can guide anatomic reduction. The "drive-through sign" indicates mortise instability. Clinical Study [24]

Achieving Anatomic Reduction

🚨 Critical Principle
The most critical determinant of a successful long-term outcome is the anatomic reduction of the syndesmosis. Malreduction, even by a few millimeters in translation or a few degrees in rotation, dramatically alters joint contact mechanics and leads inexorably to chronic pain, stiffness, and early-onset degenerative arthritis. Review [1] Closed reduction techniques are associated with unacceptably high rates of malposition. Clinical Study [30]

The modern standard of care requires a specific process: (1) open reduction of the syndesmosis, (2) application of a reliable fixation method, and (3) routine postoperative CT scanning to verify anatomic reduction. Clinical Study [24]

7. The Medial Column: Deltoid Ligament Management

The deltoid ligament complex is the primary stabilizer of the medial ankle. Its failure is the initiating event in the classic injury cascade.

Assessment

Clinically: tenderness, swelling, and ecchymosis over the medial malleolus. Radiographically: MCS on mortise view. An MCS > 5 mm on external rotation stress view is highly predictive of complete deep deltoid rupture. Clinical Study [22] MRI can provide direct visualization in equivocal cases. Case Report [5]

The Evolving Debate: Repair vs. No Repair

Traditional View (No Repair): For decades, direct deltoid repair was not performed. The rationale: once the syndesmosis is anatomically reduced and stabilized, the medial joint space closes, and the deltoid heals in physiologically appropriate length and tension. Historical Practice [17, 45]

Emerging View (Selective Repair): A growing body of evidence suggests direct surgical repair offers significant advantages. Systematic reviews show malreduction rates of 0–9% in deltoid repair groups compared to 20–35% in non-repair groups treated with syndesmotic screws alone. Systematic Review [40]

✅ Biomechanical Rationale
The deltoid ligament functions as a crucial indirect stabilizer of the syndesmosis. An incompetent deltoid allows the talus to shift laterally within the mortise, directly levering the distal fibula out of the tibial incisura. Repairing the deltoid creates a robust medial buttress that prevents talar shift and facilitates accurate syndesmotic reduction. Systematic Review [40]

Indications for Deltoid Repair Systematic Review [40]

  • Bimalleolar equivalent fractures (e.g., Maisonneuve pattern) with persistent medial instability after syndesmotic stabilization
  • Potential alternative to trans-syndesmotic fixation to avoid second surgery for hardware removal Comparative Study [46]
  • High-demand athletes or patients with residual valgus instability Systematic Review [47]
  • Weber C type fractures with high instability Systematic Review [47]

8. The Posterior Malleolus: More Than an Incidental Finding

Posterior malleolus (PM) fractures are exceedingly common in the Maisonneuve pattern, with a prevalence of up to 83%. CT Study [3] The PM fragment serves as the primary bony attachment site for the PITFL—the strongest syndesmotic ligament. A displaced PM fracture is therefore a sign of posterior syndesmotic incompetence.

💡 Key Concept: Bony Analogue of the PITFL
When a displaced PM fragment is anatomically reduced and fixed, it effectively performs a "ligamentotaxis," restoring proper length and tension to the attached PITFL and thereby stabilizing the posterior syndesmosis. In some cases, PM fixation alone can restore overall syndesmotic stability. Clinical Study [54]

The posterolateral approach (patient prone or lateral decubitus) uses the interval between peroneal tendons and flexor hallucis longus for direct access. Small reducible fragments may be fixed with AP lag screws; larger/comminuted fragments are best stabilized with a posterior buttress plate. Clinical Study [36]

9. Integrated Treatment Algorithm

Successful management hinges on a logical, sequential, hierarchical treatment algorithm. While rare stable variants may be considered for non-operative management, the vast majority require surgical intervention. Case Report [5]

0
Initial Management: Well-padded short leg splint, PRICE protocol, strict non-weight-bearing. Clinical Study [24]
P
Preoperative Planning: Full-length tibia-fibula radiographs + CT scan of ankle (mandatory for all operative cases). Clinical Study [30]
1
Posterior Malleolus First: Address PM fracture (posterolateral approach, prone). Anatomic reduction reconstructs the posterior buttress and restores the fibular notch. Clinical Study [30]
2
Medial Column: ORIF for medial malleolus fracture or deltoid ligament repair. Prevents lateral talar shift. Clinical Study [35]
3
Syndesmosis Assessment & Fixation: Intra-operative hook test. If unstable → open reduction of fibula into incisura → suture-button or trans-syndesmotic screws. Proximal fibula fracture is almost never surgically addressed. Review [8]
✓
Post-fixation Verification: Postoperative CT scan to confirm anatomic reduction. Clinical Study [24]

10. Post-operative Rehabilitation & Complications

Rehabilitation Phases

  • Phase 1 (0–6 weeks): Wound healing, swelling control. Splint/cast/boot, strict non-weight-bearing. Gentle non-weight-bearing ROM exercises may begin ~2 weeks post-op if fixation is stable. Rehab Protocol [58, 59]
  • Phase 2 (6–12 weeks): Progressive weight-bearing as tolerated in walking boot. PT focuses on ROM, strengthening, proprioception. Patients with suture-button devices may progress faster. If syndesmotic screws used, removal between 8–12 weeks (especially younger patients). Rehab Protocol [58, 16]
  • Phase 3 (> 12 weeks): Wean to supportive footwear. Advanced strengthening, agility drills, sport/work-specific training. Unrestricted high-impact activities at ~4–6 months. Rehab Protocol [58]

Potential Complications

  • Syndesmotic Malreduction — most common and consequential; may require revision surgery Clinical Study [28]
  • Wound Dehiscence / Infection — standard wound care principles apply Comparative Study [37]
  • Compartment Syndrome — rare but limb-threatening; requires emergent four-compartment fasciotomy Case Report [62]
  • Hardware Failure — more common with syndesmotic screws (breakage/loosening); symptomatic hardware requires removal Clinical Study [36]
  • Post-Traumatic Arthritis — ultimate consequence of untreated/missed/malreduced injury; staged management progressing to arthrodesis or total ankle arthroplasty Review [1]
  • Chronic Instability — from missed diagnosis or inadequate stabilization; may require late ligamentous reconstruction Clinical Study [64]

11. Conclusion: Key Principles & Future Directions

📋 Seven Key Principles for Modern Management
  1. High index of suspicion: Palpate the entire fibula to the knee in every ankle injury.
  2. Appropriate imaging: Full-length tibia-fibula radiographs are mandatory when ankle films show medial injury or syndesmotic widening without distal fibula fracture.
  3. Preoperative CT: Standard of care for all operative Maisonneuve fractures.
  4. Dynamic instability confirmation: Intra-operative stress testing after malleolar fracture fixation.
  5. Anatomic reduction: Primary surgical goal. The proximal fibula fracture is an indicator, not the target.
  6. Hierarchical surgical sequence: "Posterior-first" → medial → syndesmosis.
  7. Verify reduction: Postoperative CT is essential.

Areas for Future Investigation

  • High-quality, multicenter RCTs to establish definitive indications for routine deltoid repair vs. modern syndesmotic fixation Systematic Review [40]
  • Continued innovation in dynamic fixation methods for the syndesmosis
  • Advanced intra-operative imaging or computer-assisted navigation to reduce syndesmotic malreduction rates
