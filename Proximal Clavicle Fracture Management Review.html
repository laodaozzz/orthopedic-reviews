I. Introduction: The Clinical Challenge

A. Epidemiology and Mechanism of Injury

Clavicle fractures are common orthopedic injuries, representing 2% to 10% of all adult fractures and approximately 35% of all shoulder girdle injuries.[1] The incidence follows a bimodal distribution: the first peak occurs in young, active males under 30 years of age, and the second in the elderly population, often due to falls.[2]

While midshaft fractures are most prevalent (up to 82% of cases), proximal (medial) third fractures are notably rare, constituting only 2%–5% of all clavicle fractures.[2] This infrequency creates a significant challenge for evidence-based management, resulting in a notable gap in the orthopedic literature.

The mechanism of injury for proximal clavicle fractures is distinct from midshaft fractures. While midshaft fractures often result from indirect force (e.g., a fall onto the lateral shoulder), proximal third fractures are almost exclusively associated with high-energy, direct trauma to the anterior chest,[1] including motor vehicle accidents and high-impact sports collisions.[6] Consequently, these fractures are frequently seen in polytrauma patients and may be associated with:

  • Neurovascular damage
  • Pneumothorax / hemothorax
  • Rarely, cardiac trauma[6]

Associated injuries often dictate initial management priorities, sometimes superseding immediate fracture treatment.

⚠️ Key Clinical Pearl
The presence of concomitant life-threatening injuries in proximal clavicle fractures means the orthopedic assessment may be secondary to ATLS-based resuscitation in the polytrauma setting.

B. Relevant Anatomy and Biomechanics

The clavicle, an S-shaped bone, serves as the sole osseous link between the axial skeleton and the upper extremity. Its proximal end articulates with the manubrium at the sternoclavicular (SC) joint. Stability is conferred by a robust ligamentous complex:[1]

  • Anterior and posterior sternoclavicular ligaments
  • Interclavicular ligament
  • Costoclavicular (rhomboid) ligament (anchors clavicle to the first rib)

This strong ligamentous support accounts for the inherent stability of the proximal clavicle and the rarity of significantly displaced fractures in this region.[1]

🚨 Critical Anatomical Consideration

Immediately posterior and inferior to the proximal clavicle and SC joint lie the subclavian and brachiocephalic arteries/veins, brachial plexus, trachea, and esophagus.[1] A posteriorly displaced proximal clavicle fragment can directly compress or lacerate these structures, transforming an orthopedic injury into a life-threatening emergency.[10]

Developmental consideration: The medial clavicular physis is the last ossification center in the body to fuse, often not complete until 22–25 years of age.[14] Therefore, in adolescents and young adults, an apparent medial clavicle fracture may actually be a Salter-Harris type physeal injury.

⚠️ Evidence Gap

High-quality evidence from RCTs and meta-analyses is almost exclusively focused on midshaft fractures.[5] Systematic reviews on medial fractures have concluded that meta-analysis is not feasible due to heterogeneity and low quality of available literature (primarily case series).[7]

Clinicians frequently extrapolate management principles from midshaft studies to proximal fractures. However, this practice is potentially flawed due to profound anatomical and biomechanical differences:[1]

  • Midshaft instability = opposing muscle forces (sternocleidomastoid superiorly, arm weight/pectoralis major inferiorly)
  • Proximal stability = dictated by strong ligamentous attachments

Applying midshaft surgical indications (e.g., shortening >2 cm) to proximal fractures may be clinically inappropriate without specific supporting evidence.

II. Non-Operative Management

A. Foundational Principles and Patient Selection

Non-operative management has long been the traditional standard of care for the majority of clavicle fractures and remains the definitive treatment for minimally displaced or non-displaced fractures across all locations.[1]

For proximal third fractures, conservative treatment is indicated for all stable patterns, particularly those with anterior displacement, where the robust posterior ligamentous structures remain intact.[1]

Preferred candidates for non-operative care:[3]

  • Patients with low functional demands
  • Patients with significant medical comorbidities elevating surgical/anesthetic risk
  • Frail elderly patients where physiological stress of surgery may outweigh benefits[22]

💡 Evolution in Philosophy

The focus has shifted from achieving anatomical reduction with cumbersome devices to prioritizing patient comfort and early functional recovery with a simple sling and structured rehabilitation. Perfect radiographic alignment is not a prerequisite for excellent functional outcome.

Immobilization is typically maintained for 2–4 weeks, with gradual weaning as pain subsides.[25]

C. Phased Rehabilitation Protocols

📋 Structured Rehabilitation Protocol

Phase 1: Protection Phase (Weeks 0–4)[13,26]

  • Goals: Pain/inflammation control, fracture protection
  • Methods: Consistent sling use, cryotherapy, analgesia
  • Exercises: Active ROM for elbow, wrist, hand (sling removed several times daily); gentle pendulum exercises for shoulder (within first 2 weeks)
  • Restrictions: No active shoulder elevation; no lifting

Phase 2: Mobilization Phase (Weeks 4–8)[25,26]

  • Goals: Restore shoulder motion as fracture consolidates
  • Sling: Weaning — primarily for comfort in public or when fatigued
  • Exercises: Progress from passive ROM → active-assisted ROM using pulleys, wands, canes
  • Restrictions: Lifting limited to weight of a cup of coffee

Phase 3: Strengthening Phase (Weeks 8–12+)[23,25]

  • Prerequisites: Clear clinical and radiographic evidence of union
  • Sling: Discontinued entirely
  • Exercises: Isometric → isotonic exercises (resistance bands, light weights); rotator cuff and periscapular stabilizer focus
  • Full return: Unrestricted activities (heavy lifting, contact sports) at 3–6 months with physician clearance[3]

III. Surgical Management

A. Indications for Operative Intervention

Absolute Indications (Urgent Intervention Required)

  • Open fractures — high risk of osteomyelitis; urgent debridement and fixation required[3]
  • Impending open fracture (skin tenting) — risk of delayed perforation; surgical urgency[1]
  • Acute neurovascular compromise — subclavian artery/vein or brachial plexus injury requiring exploration and decompression[1]
  • Posteriorly displaced medial clavicle fracture — immediate threat to mediastinal structures; requires emergent reduction[1]

Relative and Controversial Indications

  • Significant displacement and shortening: Complete displacement (>100%) and/or shortening >1.5–2.0 cm correlates with increased nonunion risk[1]
  • Comminution: Multiple fragments, especially "Z-type" butterfly fragments, are biomechanically less stable[3]
  • "Floating shoulder": Ipsilateral clavicle + displaced scapular neck fracture creates highly unstable shoulder girdle[1]
  • Patient-specific factors: Polytrauma patients (early mobilization), high-demand athletes/laborers, bilateral fractures, seizure disorders, cosmetic concerns[1,34]

B. Pre-operative Planning and Imaging

  • Radiographs: AP view + 15–45° cephalic tilt view[1]
  • CT scan: Mandatory for proximal clavicle fractures — assesses direction/degree of displacement, comminution, intra-articular extension, and rules out posterior mediastinal impingement[6]
  • CT angiogram: If vascular injury is clinically suspected[3]

C. Surgical Techniques for Internal Fixation

ORIF with Plate and Screws (Gold Standard)[20]

The patient is positioned in a "beach-chair" position.[38] A transverse incision in Langer's lines (often inferior to clavicle) minimizes scar visibility and supraclavicular nerve injury risk.[39] A pre-contoured, low-profile locking plate is secured with ≥3 bicortical screws per side.[37]

Plate positioning options:

  • Superior: Strongest biomechanical construct against bending; higher hardware prominence[3]
  • Anteroinferior: Less prominent; requires more soft tissue dissection[3]

Intramedullary (IM) Fixation

Performed through smaller, cosmetic incisions with less soft-tissue stripping.[3] Implants include titanium elastic nails (TENs), Knowles pins, and Hagie pins.[20]

Limitations: Biomechanically inferior to plating (poor rotational control); higher rates of hardware migration, implant irritation, nonunion, and need for removal. Not suitable for comminuted fractures.[3]

Novel Fixation for Proximal-End Fractures

The small medial fragment and proximity to vital structures make standard fixation challenging. The reversed distal clavicle locking plate is a promising innovation: the fan-shaped end contains multiple small, locked screw holes allowing several points of fixation within the small medial fragment.[11]

IV. Comparative Outcomes: Systematic Review of Evidence

A. Healing and Alignment

Level I Nonunion rates (midshaft): Non-operative treatment is associated with a nonunion rate of 10–20%, compared to 2–3% with surgical fixation.[15,18] Meta-analyses report a relative risk ratio of ~0.1–0.2 for nonunion with surgery, an 80–90% relative risk reduction.[20]

Level IV Nonunion rates (proximal): Limited data suggest non-operative treatment is associated with a nonunion rate approaching ~15%, whereas surgical case series report union rates approaching 100%.[7]

Level I Symptomatic malunion: Meta-analyses show a risk ratio of 0.1–0.3 in favor of surgery, an 87% relative risk reduction.[20]

B. Functional Recovery

Level I Short-term (<6 months): Operative treatment leads to significantly better DASH and Constant scores, less pain, and less disability.[15]

Level I Long-term (≥1 year): Most meta-analyses find no statistically significant or clinically meaningful difference in DASH or Constant scores between groups.[9] Even when statistical differences persist, the magnitude often fails to meet the minimal clinically important difference (MCID).

💡 Reframing the Clinical Conversation

The primary durable benefit of surgery is not to achieve a superior long-term functional state, but to act as an "insurance policy" against the ~15% risk of nonunion or symptomatic malunion with non-operative care. Surgery dramatically increases the probability of reaching an acceptable healed state.

C. Return to Activity

  • Time to union: Operative group mean 16.4 weeks vs. non-operative 28.4 weeks (p=0.001)[3]
  • Return to sport (athletes): Similar overall RTP rate (operative 92% vs. non-operative 91%), but higher rate of return to pre-injury level (92% vs. 78%) and faster mean time to RTP (3.1 vs. 3.9 months) with surgery[57]
  • Proximal fractures in athletes: Limited data suggest 100% RTP rate with operative treatment[57]

V. Complication Profiles

A. Non-Operative Risks

The predominant complications are biological — sequelae of inadequate healing:[15]

  • Nonunion and symptomatic malunion — occurring in up to 20% with displaced fractures
  • Secondary sequelae: persistent pain, decreased shoulder strength/endurance, cosmetic dissatisfaction ("bump"), neurological symptoms (brachial plexus irritation, thoracic outlet syndrome)[3,8]

B. Surgical Risks

Surgical intervention introduces iatrogenic complications:[14]

  • Hardware irritation/prominence: Most common; 9–64% in some series[14]
  • Infection: ~2–6% (superficial or deep)[3]
  • Iatrogenic nerve injury: Supraclavicular nerve branches at risk; numbness is very common, often permanent to some degree[3]
  • Implant failure: Broken plate or loose screws, especially with early return to strenuous activity[18]
  • Catastrophic neurovascular/pulmonary injury: Extremely rare but devastating; subclavian vessel laceration, brachial plexus injury, pneumothorax — higher risk with medial fractures[12]

C. The Hardware Removal Paradox

⚠️ The "Hidden Cost" of Surgery

10–25% of patients who undergo plate fixation will require a secondary operation for symptomatic hardware removal.[2,18] One database study found that 1 in 4 patients had a re-operation within two years, with implant removal being the most frequent reason.[18]

This creates a clinical paradox: choosing surgery to avoid the ~15% risk of nonunion means accepting a ~20% risk of requiring a second surgery. This supports a selective, rather than routine, use of operative fixation.[5]

VI. Special Considerations in Patient Populations

A. The High-Demand Athlete

Level III For athletes, particularly elite/professional level, the primary goals are predictable return to pre-injury function while minimizing time lost.[34] Surgery offers:

  • Reliable restoration of clavicle length and alignment
  • Prevention of subtle strength deficits from malunion
  • Accelerated rehabilitation with earlier return to sport

This paradigm extends to adolescent athletes nearing skeletal maturity, where surgical fixation is increasingly considered for displaced fractures.[34]

B. The Elderly Patient

Level IV Management in the elderly is governed by different principles:[22]

  • Focus shifts from optimizing high-level function to minimizing risk and maintaining independence
  • Lower functional demands; higher comorbidity prevalence
  • Osteoporotic bone challenges fixation and increases implant failure risk
  • Non-operative management is the mainstay, even for many significantly displaced fractures
  • Nonunion/malunion may be well-tolerated in less active individuals
  • Surgical indications reserved for absolute indications or intractable symptoms
💡 Key Insight: Divergent Strategies

The divergent strategies for athletes vs. elderly patients underscore that the "optimal" treatment is highly context-dependent. A single fracture-based algorithm is insufficient. The evidence provides outcome probabilities, but the patient's unique values, goals, and physiology must determine the final choice — making shared decision-making essential.[18]

VII. Conclusion: Synthesizing the Evidence

A. Summary of Key Findings

For Proximal (Medial) Clavicle Fractures Low-Level Evidence

  • Non-operative treatment of displaced fractures is often unsatisfactory (nonunion ~15%)[7]
  • Posteriorly displaced fractures are a surgical emergency
  • Operative fixation with novel techniques (reversed distal clavicle plates) shows high union rates (~100%) but ~30% hardware irritation rate[11,50]
  • Evidence gap: Only case series available; no RCTs or meta-analyses for proximal fractures

For Midshaft Clavicle Fractures High-Level Evidence

  • Operative fixation provides clear advantage in reducing nonunion and symptomatic malunion
  • Superior short-term functional outcomes
  • Long-term outcomes converge by 1 year
  • Decision involves trading ~15% nonunion risk for ~20% hardware removal risk

B. Gaps in the Literature

  1. High-quality studies on proximal fractures: Critical need for prospective, multicenter comparative studies and RCTs
  2. Predictive modeling: Identifying patients most likely to fail non-operative treatment
  3. Long-term outcomes: Most studies limited to 1–2 years; need >5-year data
  4. Implant innovation: Lower-profile, anatomically contoured implants or bioabsorbable materials to reduce hardware removal rates
