1. Epidemiological & Educational Ecosystem

The integration of national joint registries and high-fidelity surgical video repositories underpins modern evidence-based mastery of the DAA. These resources provide both macroscopic surgical trend data and microscopic technical guidance essential for safe adoption.

Level III The AJRR is the preeminent U.S. arthroplasty database. The 2024 Annual Report encompasses over 3.7 million procedures (2012–2023), revealing critical trends relevant to DAA practice.[4]

Key findings from the 2024 AJRR report include:[4]

  • Primary THA: 32.4% of all captured procedures; primary TKA: 51.1%.
  • THA for fracture: Utilization for femoral neck fractures stabilized at 27.7% — introducing unique DAA challenges requiring specialized traction table setups.
  • Dual-mobility constructs: Peaked at 10.7% (2020), stabilized at 7.5% (2023), reflecting targeted rather than ubiquitous application for instability risk.
  • Robotic-assisted surgery: Eight-fold increase from 1.8% (2017) to 15.9%, frequently paired with DAA to optimize acetabular cup positioning.

1.2 Australian Orthopaedic Association National Joint Replacement Registry (AOANJRR)

Level III Uniquely among global registries, the AOANJRR explicitly captures the surgical approach for every hip replacement, enabling direct epidemiological comparisons. The DAA has been utilized for 26.2% of all hip replacements in Australia since 2014.[7]

Critical AOANJRR Finding (2024): A highly powered Level III analysis (Rodríguez et al.) of 59,113 components in patients ≥75 years demonstrated that collared cementless stems yielded a significantly lower all-cause revision risk versus cemented components starting at 6 months post-op (HR 0.78; 95% CI 0.64–0.96; p=0.018). This reduction was driven by decreased revisions for periprosthetic fracture and infection.[8]

A concurrent 2024 AOANJRR study (Rele et al.) corroborated these findings specifically within the DAA corridor, concluding that collared cementless femoral components are mandatory in primary THA via the anterior approach to prevent subsidence-related failures.[8]

1.3 National Joint Registry (NJR) — England, Wales, Northern Ireland & Isle of Man

Level III The NJR has meticulously recorded over 4.5 million procedures since 2003. The 22nd Annual Report documented 116,901 hip procedures in a single year.[9,11] Its massive statistical power enables tracking of extremely rare adverse events and cause-specific revision models across differing approaches and geographic trusts, acting as both a patient safety safeguard and a driver of national procurement guidelines.[12]

1.4 Cognitive Simulation & Video Repositories

Transitioning from the posterior approach to the DAA requires overcoming supine positioning challenges, mastering traction table use, and understanding anterior retractor nuances. Platforms such as VuMedi, AAOS Orthopaedic Video Theater (OVT), OrthoBullets, and TouchSurgery provide peer-reviewed technique videos and interactive cognitive simulations essential for visualizing intraoperative nuances not conveyable through static text.[13–15]

2. Anatomical Navigation & Intraoperative Execution

The hallmark of the DAA is its utilization of true internervous and intermuscular planes, theoretically minimizing muscle trauma and enhancing dynamic joint stability. However, the anterior hip anatomy is dense with critical neurovascular structures.[1,2]

2.1 The Smith-Petersen Interval

Positioning: Supine — facilitating intraoperative fluoroscopy for leg length, offset, and component verification.[13]

Incision: Originates ~2 cm lateral and slightly distal to the ASIS, extending 8–10 cm distally toward the lateral patella.[15]

Superficial Internervous Corridor

Bordered medially by sartorius (femoral nerve) and laterally by TFL (superior gluteal nerve).[15] The deep fascia must be incised over the medial aspect of the TFL muscle belly — not over the intermuscular gap — to protect the LFCN.[15]

Deep Internervous Corridor

Located between rectus femoris (femoral nerve) and gluteus medius (superior gluteal nerve).[15] The rectus (direct head from AIIS; reflected head above acetabular rim) and iliopsoas are retracted medially; gluteus medius retracted laterally. In difficult cases, a rectus tenotomy may be performed to enhance visualization and prevent excessive femoral nerve traction.[15]

⚠ Critical Technical Note: After adequate exposure, an H-shaped, T-shaped, or longitudinal capsulotomy is performed. The hip is placed in adduction and external rotation to tension the anterior capsule prior to incision, facilitating dislocation and neck osteotomy.[15]

2.2 Vascular Hazards: The Lateral Circumflex Femoral Artery (LCFA)

Level II A defining and mandatory step is the identification, isolation, and definitive management of the ascending branches of the LCFA. The LCFA arises from the profunda femoris artery (occasionally from the common femoral artery) with a robust diameter of ~5 mm, trifurcating into ascending, transverse, and descending branches.[18]

The ascending branch predictably crosses the surgical field between sartorius and TFL. Failure to manage it leads to:

  • Profound intraoperative hemorrhage and obscured visualization
  • Severe postoperative hematoma → exponentially increased PJI risk
  • Potential necessity for early reoperation for hematoma evacuation

Best practice: Proactively identify ascending branches within intermuscular adipose tissue; skeletonize and securely ligate via suture or titanium vascular clips (not electrocautery alone for the primary branch) to prevent secondary bleeding upon anesthetic reversal.[15,18]

🔵 Level I Evidence — Bone Wax RCT (JBJS 2022): A 152-patient RCT demonstrated that applying bone wax to the femoral neck cut significantly reduced perioperative blood loss from a median of 1,021 mL (control) to 767 mL (intervention), p<0.001, without increased complications.[22]

3. Complication Profiles & Learning Curve

The DAA eliminates sciatic nerve injury risk but introduces a new set of anatomic vulnerabilities: LFCN injury and proximal femoral periprosthetic fractures, both inextricably linked to the surgeon's learning curve.[2]

3.1 Lateral Femoral Cutaneous Nerve (LFCN) Neuropathy

Level II–IV The LFCN is a purely sensory nerve with a highly variable anatomic course relative to the ASIS:[23,24]

Reported incidence: 0–83% depending on testing stringency; ~30% in modern robust cohorts.[25,26] Injury mechanisms include sharp laceration, excessive retractor traction, and mistaken ligation during LCFA management.[15]

Level I A network meta-analysis confirmed markedly higher LFCN neurapraxia in DAA vs. posterior approach (OR 1.78; 95% CI 0.52–6.17; p=0.358 — limited by sample constraints).[27] However, the majority of LFCN injuries are transient, with complete sensory recovery typically by 3–6 months.[25,27]

3.2 Proximal Femoral Periprosthetic Fractures

Level III Femoral exposure in the DAA requires physical elevation and anterior translation of the femur without posterior capsular release. Inadequate release leads to varus impingement of broach/stem on the greater trochanter → trochanteric avulsion, calcar fissures, or diaphyseal splits.[2,28]

Intraoperative femoral fracture rates: ~1.7–2.7% for DAA vs. 1.0–6.9% for PA (varies by cohort and experience).[28] AOANJRR data highlights the critical necessity of collared cementless stems in patients >75 years to prevent subsidence-related fractures.[8]

4. Synthesis of Top-Tier Evidence (2021–2026)

The central clinical question: do the theoretical muscle-sparing benefits translate into statistically significant, clinically meaningful long-term improvements?

4.1 Early Functional Recovery vs. Long-Term Convergence

🔵 Landmark RCT — Roberts et al. (JOA, 2024): 7.5-year follow-up of randomized DAA vs. Mini-Posterior cohort confirmed that early functional differences completely dissipated over time, with no clinically meaningful differences in PROMs, complication rates, reoperation, or implant revision.[35]

Length of stay: DAA cohorts consistently shorter by 0.31–0.56 days, critical for same-day discharge protocols.[32]

4.2 Dislocation, Infection & Cost-Effectiveness

Dislocation Risk

Level III Horberg et al. (BJJ, 2021) — 2,831 consecutive DAA hips: overall dislocation rate 0.46%. Post-learning curve: 0.15% vs. 1.14% during learning curve.[37]

Level I Meta-analysis: PA carries significantly higher dislocation risk vs. DAA (OR 0.201; 95% CI 0.08–0.53; p=0.001).[27]

Infection

Level III After BMI adjustment, no significant difference in deep PJI or superficial infection requiring reoperation between DAA and other approaches.[40]

Cost-Effectiveness

Level II A Markov model (90-day horizon) found DAA dominates standard care — generating $3,851–$4,766 savings per patient — driven by reduced LOS, higher home discharge rates, and fewer early complications despite +13.74–18.92 min operative time.[41]

6. Clinical Conclusions

Level I–III Synthesis The Direct Anterior Approach is a highly efficacious, anatomically logical surgical strategy validated by Level I/II evidence and massive registry datasets (AJRR, AOANJRR, NJR). It provides:

  • Superior early postoperative pain control and accelerated rehabilitation up to 6 weeks
  • Significantly reduced dislocation risk (OR 0.201 vs. posterior approach)
  • Cost-effectiveness via reduced LOS and home discharge rates ($3,851–$4,766 savings/patient)
  • Absolute long-term equivalence at 7.5 years in PROMs, complications, and revisions

⚠ Critical Caveats:

  • Learning curve: 50–100 cases before complication profiles normalize (HR 1.6 → 1.0)[30]
  • LFCN injury: ~30% incidence; mostly transient; prevent via lateral fascial incision over TFL[25,26]
  • LCFA management: Mandatory formal ligation of ascending branches[15,18]
  • Implant selection: Collared cementless stems mandatory in patients >75 years[8]
  • Patient selection: Avoid severe obesity and large abdominal pannuses during early learning curve[2]
