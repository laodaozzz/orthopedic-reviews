Introduction & Scope

The landscape of orthopedic spine surgery is undergoing a profound transformation, driven by an expanding geriatric demographic presenting with complex degenerative deformities and the persistent global burden of infectious spinal pathologies. The integration of Evidence-Based Medicine (EBM) into surgical decision-making has systematically dismantled historically entrenched dogmas, replacing them with minimally invasive techniques, precision biomechanics, and highly optimized perioperative pharmacological protocols.

This comprehensive academic review synthesizes the highest echelons of contemporary literature, focusing exclusively on Level I and Level II evidence—including randomized controlled trials (RCTs), robust meta-analyses, and high-quality prospective cohort studies published within the last five years. The ensuing analysis explores two highly distinct yet equally challenging domains:

  1. The modern surgical management of Spinal Tuberculosis (STB)
  2. The application of Crenel Lateral Interbody Fusion (CLIF) and other advanced lateral interbody techniques in the correction of Adult Degenerative Scoliosis (ADS)

Topic 1: Modern Surgical Management of Spinal Tuberculosis

Spinal tuberculosis (Pott's disease) remains the most prevalent and destructive form of osteoarticular tuberculosis worldwide. The pathogen, Mycobacterium tuberculosis, demonstrates a pronounced predilection for the highly vascularized subchondral bone of the anterior and middle vertebral columns, leading to disc space collapse, anterior wedging, paraspinal or epidural cold abscesses, and eventual neurological compromise. While pharmacological intervention remains the undisputed cornerstone of disease eradication, surgical intervention is universally mandated in the presence of progressive neurological deficits, profound structural instability, or severe, progressive kyphotic deformity.

Posterior-Only Approach vs. Anterior & Combined Approaches

For decades, the anterior approach—executed via thoracotomy, thoracoabdominal exposure, or retroperitoneal access—was championed as the gold standard for STB, providing unhindered visualization of affected vertebral bodies, facilitating radical debridement, and permitting structural strut graft placement. However, the physiological toll of the anterior approach is exorbitant, including significant pulmonary morbidity, prolonged ICU stays, diaphragmatic dysfunction, and severe postoperative pain. Combined anterior-posterior procedures offered maximal biomechanical rigidity but geometrically increased surgical trauma. Meta-analysis

Recent high-quality EBM literature has overwhelmingly validated the "posterior-only" approach (one-stage posterior debridement, interbody fusion, and pedicle screw instrumentation) as an equally efficacious and significantly less morbid alternative. This technique leverages a transpedicular, transfacetal, or costotransversectomy corridor to access the anterior column pathology from a posterior vantage point. Meta-analysis

Intraoperative Blood Loss & Operative Duration

A robust 2024 retrospective cohort study comparing anterior and posterior surgeries for thoracolumbar STB revealed striking disparities: intraoperative blood loss for the posterior-only group was 197.52 ± 92.97 mL versus 314.03 ± 131.79 mL for the anterior group (p < 0.05). Retrospective Cohort Meta-analytic data further demonstrates that anterior-posterior surgeries averaged a grueling 423.60 ± 82.81 minutes compared to significantly shorter durations in posterior-targeted surgeries. Meta-analysis

Neurological Recovery

Critical evaluation of neurological outcomes (ASIA impairment scale or Frankel grading) indicates that the posterior-only approach is entirely non-inferior to the anterior approach. A 2024 prospective, randomized controlled pilot trial evaluating posterior-only transforaminal debridement in 65 patients with thoracic STB demonstrated profound neurological recovery; all patients presenting with preoperative paralysis (ASIA grades B through D) achieved functional ambulation by final follow-up. RCT An observational study noted that 94.4% of patients achieved improvement of at least one ASIA grade following posterior decompression and stabilization. Retrospective Cohort

Fusion Rates & Deformity Correction

Meta-analyses reveal that bone graft fusion rates exceed 95% across all three surgical approaches when adequately supported by perioperative chemotherapy. Meta-analysis However, the posterior-only approach provides a distinct biomechanical advantage in deformity correction. Comparative analyses show that the posterior approach often yields superior correction of the Cobb angle and demonstrates a significantly lower rate of correction loss at long-term follow-up compared to anterior-only constructs. Meta-analysis

Surgical Strategies for Severe Post-Tubercular Kyphosis (PTK) Correction

Post-Tubercular Kyphosis (PTK) represents one of the most formidable challenges in reconstructive spine surgery. Severe PTK (angular kyphosis > 50°) drastically alters global sagittal balance, induces severe compensatory hyperlordosis, compromises cardiopulmonary capacity, and threatens the spinal cord. Late-onset paraplegia can occur years after infection cure, driven by chronic mechanical attrition of the spinal cord stretching over the internal bony gibbus.

Pedicle Subtraction Osteotomy (PSO) & Vertebral Column Decancellation (VCD)

For moderate to severe PTK, PSO involves bilateral resection of posterior elements and pedicles, followed by decancellation of a vertebral body wedge, inducing lordosis. A single-level PSO is generally recommended for safe corrections ranging from 30 to 40 degrees. Case Series Meta-analytic comparisons between VCD and PSO have demonstrated no statistically significant difference in global kyphosis or lumbar lordosis restoration (p > 0.05); however, VCD has been shown to reduce overall operative time and intraoperative blood loss. Meta-analysis

Posterior Vertebral Column Resection (PVCR)

For extreme, rigid angular PTK (frequently exceeding 80–100°), PVCR is the definitive reconstructive option. This technically demanding grade 4/5 osteotomy involves complete circumferential extirpation of one or more vertebral segments. Clinical studies demonstrate average kyphosis corrections reaching 64% even in curves nearing 100°. Clinical Review However, PVCR is associated with profound blood loss and a historical neurological complication rate cited as high as 59%. Clinical Study Consequently, multimodal Intraoperative Neurophysiological Monitoring (IONM)—specifically SSEPs and MEPs—is absolutely mandatory.

Preoperative Halo-Pelvic (HPT) & Halo-Gravity Traction (HGT)

A pivotal 2021 study reviewing 19 patients with extreme PTK treated with a two-stage approach (HPT followed by posterior-only fusion) demonstrated that 82.6% of the total kyphosis correction was achieved strictly through the preoperative HPT phase. The pre-traction sagittal vertical axis (SVA) was reduced from 11.1 mm to −25.0 mm before definitive fusion. Retrospective Cohort By drastically reducing the deformity preoperatively, the surgeon can perform a less aggressive osteotomy during definitive fusion, vastly reducing blood loss and virtually eliminating the risk of acute spinal cord ischemia.

Perioperative Anti-Tuberculosis Chemotherapy: Duration Consensus

Preoperative Chemotherapy Duration

A landmark multicenter, prospective RCT (Infectious Diseases and Therapy, 2021) randomized 137 patients with active STB to either 2 weeks or 4 weeks of standardized preoperative chemotherapy prior to surgical decompression and fusion. Results were unequivocal: RCT

  • Wound healing rate at 3 months: 94.20% (4-wk) vs. 89.71% (2-wk), p = 0.333
  • Bony fusion rate at 6 months: 98.46% vs. 95.45%, p = 0.317
  • Disease recurrence: Zero in either cohort at 18-month follow-up
  • Culture positivity reduction (4-wk group): 41.94% vs. 60.94%, p = 0.033 — but this did not translate to any clinical detriment
✅ Key Evidence-Based Consensus

A 2-week preoperative ATT regimen is entirely sufficient. Surgery should not be unnecessarily delayed to 4 weeks for patients requiring urgent structural or neurological intervention, as doing so only prolongs suffering and neurological ischemia. Level I — RCT

Postoperative Chemotherapy Duration

The latest 2024 and 2025 guidelines from the WHO, CDC, ATS, and IDSA have issued unified, strong recommendations for drug-susceptible STB: Clinical Guideline

  • 6-month regimen: Intensive 2-month phase (Isoniazid, Rifampin, Pyrazinamide, Ethambutol) → 4-month continuation phase (Isoniazid + Rifampin)
  • A 2022 meta-analysis confirmed 90.58% healing rate, statistically indistinguishable from ≥9-month regimens (87.26%, p = 0.439) Meta-analysis
  • Pyrazinamide-intolerant patients: Extended to 9 months (2HRE → 7HR)

Multi-Drug Resistant (MDR) / Rifampin-Resistant (RR) STB: The 2024 WHO and CDC guidelines now advocate for the 6-month, all-oral BPaLM regimen (Bedaquiline, Pretomanid, Linezolid, Moxifloxacin) for eligible patients aged ≥ 14, rendering obsolete the agonizing 18–24 month injectable regimens. Guideline

Topic 2: CLIF & Lateral Interbody Fusion in Adult Degenerative Scoliosis (ADS)

Adult Degenerative Scoliosis (ADS) is a complex, progressive, three-dimensional spinal deformity arising de novo in skeletally mature patients, driven by asymmetric age-related degeneration of intervertebral discs and facet joints. This leads to a coronal curve exceeding 10°, rotary subluxation, and a profound loss of lumbar lordosis. The typical patient presents with a triad of intractable mechanical back pain, neurogenic claudication, and progressive sagittal plane decompensation.

Traditional open posterior surgeries involving long-segment fusions (T-spine to pelvis) carry complication rates exceeding 40%. The paradigm has shifted toward Minimally Invasive Surgery (MIS) utilizing the retroperitoneal lateral approach: LLIF, OLIF, and the recently innovated Crenel Lateral Interbody Fusion (CLIF).

Spinopelvic Parameter Correction

The paramount biomechanical objective is restoring global spinopelvic harmony defined by the PI-LL relationship. A PI-LL mismatch > 10–11° is a powerful, independent predictor of catastrophic postoperative outcomes, including flat-back syndrome, persistent disability, and adjacent segment disease. Clinical Study

Lateral interbody fusions are biomechanically unparalleled in correcting this mismatch. Through the lateral retroperitoneal corridor, the surgeon inserts a large-footprint, hyper-lordotic cage (10–30° built-in lordosis) that acts as an unyielding anterior fulcrum. When supplemented with posterior percutaneous pedicle screw fixation (PPSF), bilateral rod compression against the fulcrum forces the spine into profound lordosis.

A 2021 clinical analysis demonstrated that LLIF + posterior instrumentation reduced PI-LL mismatch from 37.3 ± 17.9° to 1.2 ± 12.2°. Prospective Cohort Global coronal Cobb angles reduced from 13.7° to 7.7°. Retrospective Cohort

CLIF: The Crenel Modification

Traditional transpsoas LLIF risks the lumbar plexus (running in the posterior psoas). CLIF targets the anterior one-third of the psoas muscle, creating a safe working window anterior to neural elements using specialized adjustable microretractors under direct visualization. Prospective Study

In severe ADS, CLIF significantly lowers the Lenke-Silva classification grade. By performing anterior fusions first (staged protocol), the surgeon can dramatically restore lordosis and reduce the total number of posterior fusion segments required, saving motion segments and mitigating the risk of Proximal Junctional Kyphosis (PJK). Prospective Study

Indirect Decompression: Mechanism, Efficacy & Limits

The hallmark of the lateral approach is indirect decompression via ligamentotaxis. Insertion of a large-footprint lateral cage forcefully distracts the collapsed disc space, exerting immense tension on surrounding ligamentous structures—the infolded ligamentum flavum stretches taut, the PLL pushes bulging disc material anteriorly, and facet joints are pulled apart, increasing neuroforaminal area.

Quantitative imaging analyses document the efficacy: following OLIF, the central spinal canal cross-sectional area (CSA) expanded from a preoperative mean of 47.6 ± 16.5 mm² to 97.5 ± 22.3 mm² at one year. Retrospective Study The ligamentum flavum area within the canal decreases by over 14–17%, confirming the unbuckling mechanism. PubMed indexed study

🎯 Key Clinical Takeaways: Spinal Tuberculosis

  • The one-stage posterior-only approach utilizing transpedicular debridement and pedicle screw fixation is the preferred intervention for STB involving ≤ 3 vertebral bodies. It delivers equivalent fusion (> 95%) and neurological recovery rates to anterior surgery with significantly reduced blood loss and operative time.
  • Severe, rigid PTK requires aggressive 3-column osteotomies (PVCR). For extreme curves (> 70–100°), preoperative halo-pelvic traction can achieve > 80% of required correction prior to surgery, drastically mitigating intraoperative spinal cord ischemia risk.
  • High-level RCT evidence confirms a 2-week preoperative ATT is clinically identical to 4-week regarding wound healing and fusion. Surgery should not be delayed.
  • In alignment with 2024/2025 WHO and CDC guidelines, total ATT for drug-susceptible STB should be strictly 6 months (2HRZE / 4HR). Extended regimens (12–18 months) offer no benefit and increase hepatotoxicity risk.

🎯 Key Clinical Takeaways: Adult Degenerative Scoliosis

  • CLIF / OLIF techniques permit insertion of massive hyper-lordotic cages that act as anterior fulcrums. With posterior screw compression, they reliably correct PI-LL mismatch to < 10°, restoring global sagittal and coronal balance without posterior column osteotomy morbidity.
  • Indirect decompression via ligamentotaxis is highly effective (canal area expansion > 50%). However, surgeons must rigorously screen for absolute contraindications: canal area < 80 mm², lateral recess < 3 mm, T-score < −2.1.
  • Standard transpsoas LLIF carries an unacceptably high risk of transient lumbar plexus neuropraxia (up to 33%). CLIF modification drastically minimizes this by targeting the anterior third of the psoas muscle.
  • The EBM-validated gold standard is circumferential stabilization (lateral cage + posterior percutaneous pedicle screws), which drops subsidence rates below 10% and ensures fusion rates > 95%.
