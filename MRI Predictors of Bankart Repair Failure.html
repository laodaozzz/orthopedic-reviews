Predictors of Failure in Arthroscopic Bankart Repair

Evidence-Based Radiological & Clinical Analysis for Anterior Shoulder Instability

1. Introduction & Pathoanatomy

Traumatic anterior shoulder instability is the most common major joint instability, accounting for ~50% of all dislocations. The typical injury mechanism involves abduction and external rotation, forcing the humeral head anteriorly and inferiorly Level I [1].

The primary pathological consequence is a Bankart lesion: an avulsion of the anteroinferior labrum and attached inferior glenohumeral ligament (IGHL) from the glenoid rim [5]. This compromises the labrum's "bumper" effect and destabilizes the primary static restraint against anterior dislocation [8].

🔄 Cascade of Secondary Pathology

Each instability event can cause progressive damage: attritional glenoid bone loss (bony Bankart), a Hill-Sachs lesion on the humeral head, capsular stretching, and potential HAGL lesions [5]. This cumulative damage lowers the threshold for recurrence.

Arthroscopic Bankart Repair

The procedure aims to anatomicly reattach the capsulolabral complex to the glenoid rim using suture anchors, restoring the IGHL and performing capsular plication if needed [15, 19]. Modern techniques with suture anchors have significantly improved outcomes compared to older methods [20].

Defining Surgical Failure

Failure encompasses any recurrent instability (redislocation, subluxation, or apprehension) [9]. Contemporary definitions also include poor patient-reported outcomes (PROMs) like WOSI, Rowe, or DASH scores, even without a documented instability event [29].

3. Osseous Predictors: MRI Quantification

3.1 Glenoid Bone Loss (GBL)

GBL disrupts the concavity-compression mechanism, creating an "inverted pear" glenoid [5, 8]. It is the most critical factor; >80% of surgical failures are associated with bone loss [21].

📉 Paradigm Shift: The "Subcritical" Threshold

While historically 20-25% was considered "critical," recent evidence shows GBL ≥10% is a powerful independent predictor (OR 7.47) [26]. Defects of 13.5-20% are now termed "subcritical" and may lead to poor outcomes, redefining clinical significance [18, 55].

MRI Measurement Technique: The best-fit circle method on an en face sagittal oblique view. A qualitative red flag is the "anterior straight-line sign" [42].

3.2 Hill-Sachs Lesion (HSL)

Prevalence approaches 100% in recurrent instability [4]. The key is whether it is "engaging"—oriented to catch the glenoid rim during motion [4, 62]. Recently, inferior craniocaudal extension below the humeral head equator has been identified as a critical risk factor (OR 3.29-4.80) [63].

4. The Glenoid Track Concept: Bipolar Bone Loss

This concept unifies the assessment of glenoid and humeral bone loss. The glenoid track is the zone of contact as the arm moves into abduction and external rotation [16, 41].

A Hill-Sachs lesion is "on-track" if its medial margin remains within the track, and "off-track" if it extends beyond, leading to engagement [51]. Critically, GBL reduces the track width, making an otherwise stable HSL become off-track [41].

Table 3: MRI Measurement Protocol for Glenoid Track Assessment
Measurement Protocol Clinical Application
Glenoid Diameter (D) Measure width of inferior glenoid circle on sagittal oblique MRI Baseline for track calculation
Glenoid Bone Loss (d) Width of anterior bone deficit using best-fit circle method Quantifies track width reduction
Glenoid Track Width (GTW) GTW = 0.83D - d Normal glenoid track width minus defect [41]
Hill-Sachs Interval (HSI) Distance from medial HSL margin to rotator cuff insertion on axial MRI Measures lesion extent
On/Off-Track Determination Compare HSI to GTW HSI > GTW = Off-Track (High-Risk)
📊 Prognostic Power

Off-track lesions have an OR of 5.53 for recurrence and a staggering 74% recurrence rate with isolated Bankart repair, versus 27% for on-track lesions [2, 22]. Emerging concepts like "near-track" lesions (Hill-Sachs occupancy ≥75%) identify borderline cases [9].

5. Critical Soft-Tissue Lesions

5.1 Humeral Avulsion of Glenohumeral Ligament (HAGL)

The soft-tissue mirror of a Bankart lesion. Present in up to 18% of revision surgeries, it is a frequently missed cause of failure [23]. The pathognomonic MRI finding is the "J sign" on coronal oblique MR arthrography [39].

West Point Classification: Categorizes by location (anterior/posterior), bony involvement (HAGL vs. bony HAGL), and associated labral pathology (floating IGHL) [39].

Clinical Implication: Failure to diagnose and repair a HAGL guarantees failure of an isolated Bankart procedure [73].

5.2 Other Associated Pathologies

  • ALPSA Lesion: Labrum heals medially and inferiorly, requiring mobilization during repair [20].
  • Capsular Deficiency: Requires capsular shift/plication in hyperlaxity or chronic cases [19, 24].
  • Concomitant Rotator Cuff Tears: Especially in patients >40; subscapularis integrity is key [76].
  • SLAP Lesion: Meta-analysis shows it is not an independent risk factor for recurrence [1].

6. Evidence-Based Surgical Algorithm

A pre-operative MRI is mandatory for surgical planning, providing quantitative data on GBL, HSL, track status, and soft tissues [77, 81].

1
Isolated Arthroscopic Bankart Repair

Indications: Ideal candidate with <10% GBL, on-track HSL, no HAGL, and no high-risk patient factors (e.g., non-contact athlete, no hyperlaxity) [18, 77, 78].

2
Bankart Repair + Remplissage

Indication: Off-track HSL with subcritical GBL (<20-25%). The remplissage tenodeses infraspinatus into the HSL, preventing engagement and creating a posterior checkrein [68, 83, 85].

Expanding indications include high-risk "near-track" lesions, especially in collision athletes [63].

3
Bone Block Procedure (Latarjet)

Indication: Critical GBL (>20-25%) or revision in high-risk patients. Provides a "triple effect": bony reconstruction, dynamic sling (conjoint tendon), and capsular repair [14, 89, 90].

Table 4: MRI-Based Surgical Decision-Making Summary
Patient Profile & MRI Findings Recommended Procedure Evidence Rationale
Low-risk patient, <10% GBL, on-track HSL, no HAGL Isolated Arthroscopic Bankart High success rate in select population [77]
High-risk patient (young, contact sport, hyperlaxity) OR subcritical GBL (10-20%) OR off-track HSL Bankart + Remplissage or consider Latarjet Off-track lesions have 74% recurrence with Bankart alone [22]
Critical GBL (>20-25%) OR failed prior Bankart Latarjet (Bone Block) Required to restore glenoid arc and stability [14]
Any patient with HAGL lesion identified HAGL Repair (often with Bankart) Omission guarantees failure [73]

8. Conclusion & Recommendations

The high-risk patient for Bankart failure is typically a male under 22 in contact sports with hyperlaxity. Radiologically, risk is magnified by GBL >10% and/or an off-track HSL. A HAGL lesion is an absolute contraindication to isolated repair.

Central Role of Pre-operative MRI

Actionable reports must include:

  1. Quantitative GBL percentage (best-fit circle method).
  2. Detailed HSL characterization (width, location, inferior extension).
  3. Formal glenoid track calculation (on/off-track status).
  4. Systematic assessment for HAGL (J-sign) and capsular volume.

Future Directions

Advances include validation of 3D MRI for bone loss quantification [45], refined thresholds for "subcritical" and "near-track" lesions, and AI/deep learning models for automated measurement, aiming to improve accuracy and efficiency [94].
