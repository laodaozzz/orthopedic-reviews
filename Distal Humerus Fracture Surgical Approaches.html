A Comparative Analysis of Surgical Approaches for Distal Humerus Fractures

An evidence-based interactive review of indications, outcomes, complications, and algorithmic decision-making — derived from a comprehensive literature synthesis.

1. Foundational Principles

1.1 Anatomic & Biomechanical Complexities

The distal humerus presents a formidable reconstructive challenge owing to its two-column architecture — a robust medial column terminating at the trochlea and a slender lateral column ending at the capitellum — supporting an articular surface angled approximately 40 degrees forward relative to the shaft. Not specified in source

The trochlear axis is externally rotated 3–8° relative to the epicondylar axis, adding a three-dimensional complexity to reduction. The normal elbow ROM spans 0–140° of flexion, yet the critical functional arc for activities of daily living is merely 30–130°. Not specified in source

Clinical Pearl

The surgical goal is not necessarily restoring full anatomic motion, but reliably achieving the functional arc of 30–130°. Any fixation construct must resist axial, varus-valgus, and torsional loads to permit early mobilisation. Not specified in source

1.2 Preoperative Imaging

CT with 2D and 3D reconstructions has become near-mandatory for modern management. Studies demonstrate that 3D CT significantly improves diagnostic sensitivity, enhances treatment strategy formulation, and increases interobserver agreement regarding fracture characteristics. III Traction radiographs can supplement imaging but offer less detail than 3D CT. Not specified in source

1.3 Core Tenets of Internal Fixation

The AO principles for articular fracture management apply directly: Not specified in source

  1. Anatomic articular reduction with stable internal fixation.
  2. Restoration of axial and rotational alignment.
  3. Immediate initiation of active and passive ROM.

O'Driscoll's guidelines for maximising distal fixation include: every screw passes through a plate; screws are as long as possible engaging the opposite column; maximum screws in distal fragments; screws engage maximum articular fragments; medial and lateral screws interdigitate to create a fixed-angle "keystone" construct; plates compress at the supracondylar junction. Not specified in source

Key Risk Factor

The supracondylar junction is the most common site of fixation failure and nonunion. Adherence to interdigitation principles and compression plating at this level is critical. Not specified in source

1.4 Parallel vs. Orthogonal Plating

The debate between parallel (medial & lateral surfaces) and orthogonal (90/90) (medial & posterolateral) plating configurations is central to fixation strategy.

Critical Paradox

Parallel plating reduces fixation failure (1% vs 6%) but increases overall complications (54% vs 45%), driven by ulnar nerve irritation, hardware prominence, and wound dehiscence. In-vitro stability does not always translate to superior clinical results — the biological and soft-tissue environment is a co-determinant of success. I

2. Posterior Approaches: Articular Exposure vs. Extensor Integrity

2.1 Olecranon Osteotomy — The Gold Standard

The olecranon osteotomy provides unparalleled articular visualization (median 57% of the articular surface exposed) and is indicated for complex, multifragmentary intra-articular fractures, particularly AO/OTA Type C3. Not specified in source

Technique highlights: Distally based chevron osteotomy aimed at the non-articular "bare area" of the trochlear notch. Cut initiated with oscillating saw, completed with osteotome for interlocking surface. Fixation via intramedullary screw ± tension band wire, or precontoured olecranon plate. Not specified in source

Complication Profile

Overall complication rate: 26.3% across 1,700 osteotomies. I Key risks include:

  • Symptomatic hardware removal: 8–46% (depending on fixation method) IV
  • Osteotomy nonunion: 0–9% I
  • Wound infection: 4.2% I
  • Deep infection: 1.4–4.2%

2.2 Triceps-Sparing (Paratricipital) Approach

The least invasive posterior option, preserving the entire extensor mechanism. Ideal for Type A and Type B/C1 fractures. Not specified in source

Exposure is obtained via medial and lateral "windows," elevating the triceps from the intermuscular septa. Articular exposure is limited to <35%, making it unsuitable for complex comminution. Not specified in source

Functional Superiority

  • Triceps strength recovery: 88.9% of contralateral arm vs. 49.4% with triceps-splitting III
  • Normal strength (Grade 5/5): 84% of patients IV
  • Greater final flexion and less extension contracture compared to splitting approaches III
  • DASH scores not significantly different between approaches III

2.3 Triceps-Splitting (Campbell) Approach

A historical technique involving direct midline split of the triceps. Modern indication is largely limited to open fractures where the humeral shaft has already perforated the triceps. Not specified in source

Outcomes are inferior to triceps-sparing in ROM and strength. However, a systematic review found no significant differences in MEPS, ROM, or complication rates between triceps-splitting and olecranon osteotomy. I

2.4 Triceps-Reflecting (Bryan-Morrey / TRAP) Approaches

A compromise technique: the triceps insertion is detached and reflected proximally as a single unit, providing ~46% articular exposure without osteotomy. Not specified in source

A retrospective review of 24 patients found no significant loss of flexion or extension strength vs. contralateral arm at 51-month follow-up (mean MEPS 91). IV A comparative study found shorter operative time and lower blood loss vs. olecranon osteotomy, though osteotomy provided greater accuracy in articular reduction. III

Tiered Decision-Making Strategy

The evidence supports a hierarchy of escalating invasiveness: begin with triceps-sparing for simpler patterns → convert to triceps-reflecting or olecranon osteotomy if inadequate reduction is achieved. This adaptable strategy matches approach morbidity to injury complexity. Not specified in source

3. Alternative & Specialised Surgical Corridors

3.1 Anterolateral Approach

Indicated for extra-articular distal humerus shaft fractures (AO/OTA 12-A, 12-B). Advantages include supine positioning (beneficial for polytrauma), shorter operative time, and lower radial nerve injury risk (0% vs 8% in posterior approaches). III

No significant differences in union rates, ROM, or MEPS vs. posterior approaches for this indication. A higher rate of implant removal was noted in the posterior group. III

3.2 Medial & Lateral Approaches

Lateral approach: Standard for isolated lateral condyle fractures (B1), lateral epicondyle avulsions (A1.1), and capitellar coronal shear fractures (B3). Not specified in source

Medial approach: Used for isolated medial epicondyle avulsions (A1.2). The ulnar nerve must be identified and protected at all times. Not specified in source

Core Principle: "The Approach Follows the Fracture"

Bicolumnar injuries → posterior approach. Unicolumnar injuries → limited lateral/medial approach. Using an extensive approach for an isolated fracture represents unnecessary iatrogenic injury. Not specified in source

4. Special Populations & Unreconstructable Fractures

4.1 Total Elbow Arthroplasty (TEA)

Primary indication: Severely comminuted fracture (C3) in a physiologically elderly, low-demand patient with osteoporosis. Not specified in source

TEA vs. ORIF in the Elderly

  • Patients >65 years: TEA associated with higher MEPS, greater arc of motion, and lower reoperation rates at 2-year follow-up III
  • Immediate ROM initiation — critical in elderly to avoid irreversible stiffness
  • Lifelong restriction: No lifting >10 lbs (~4.5 kg)
  • Risks: deep infection, aseptic loosening, polyethylene wear, periprosthetic fractures

4.2 Nonoperative Management ("Bag of Bones")

Reserved for the most frail, low-demand patients unfit for surgery. A systematic review found no clinically important difference in mean MEPS between nonoperative management, ORIF, and TEA in older adults. I

5.1 Neurological Complications

Ulnar nerve: Injured in 9–13% of posterior approaches. Identification and protection are mandatory. Whether to perform routine anterior transposition or in-situ decompression remains debated; some evidence suggests transposition may increase neuritis rates. Not specified in source

Radial nerve: At risk during proximal extension of posterior approaches (~14–15 cm proximal to lateral epicondyle in spiral groove) and anterolateral approaches. Not specified in source

5.2 Heterotopic Ossification (HO)

Clinically significant HO occurs in approximately 8% of cases. Risk factors: concomitant head injury, floating elbow, and delayed fixation. Routine prophylaxis is generally not warranted due to concerns about nonunion. Not specified in source

6. Innovations & Future Directions

6.1 Minimally Invasive Plate Osteosynthesis (MIPO)

Cadaveric studies confirm feasibility for both extra-articular and intra-articular distal humeral fractures using MIPO principles, including a minimally invasive olecranon osteotomy through a 4 cm incision. V One prospective study reported satisfactory mid-to-long-term functional outcomes with low complications for anterior MIPO of extra-articular fractures. III

Limitation: Clinical data remains limited. Larger comparative studies are needed to validate safety and efficacy. Not specified in source

6.2 Advances in Implant Technology

  • Precontoured locking plates: Anatomic fit, reduced intraoperative bending, enhanced stability in osteoporotic bone via fixed-angle "internal fixator" mechanism. Not specified in source
  • Variable-angle locking screws: Allow deviation from nominal trajectory, mitigating the risk of joint penetration in the constrained distal humerus anatomy. Not specified in source
  • Novel anteromedial/anterolateral double-plate system: Finite element analysis showed greater stiffness and stability vs. traditional orthogonal plating for C1, comminuted, and coronal shear fracture patterns. V

Pitfall: Fixed-Angle Plate Positioning

With precontoured locking plates, screw trajectory is predetermined. Provisionally place a critical distal locking screw before anchoring the plate proximally to ensure optimal position for articular fixation. Not specified in source

7. Evidence-Based Algorithmic Decision-Making

Fracture-Based Approach Selection Algorithm

Step 1: Comprehensive Assessment

All displaced distal humerus fractures → clinical evaluation (neurovascular exam) + high-quality imaging including CT with 2D/3D reconstructions.

Step 2: Patient Factor Stratification

Elderly, low-demand, osteoporotic, C3 fracture? → Consider Primary TEA.
Medically unfit / non-compliant with rehab? → Nonoperative ("bag of bones")

Step 3: Fracture Pattern — Extra-articular Type A

Preferred: Triceps-sparing posterior approach. Alternative: Anterolateral (shorter OR time, lower radial nerve risk).

Step 4: Fracture Pattern — Partial Articular Type B or Simple Complete C1/C2

Preferred: Triceps-sparing or triceps-reflecting. Maintain low threshold to convert to olecranon osteotomy if anatomic reduction cannot be achieved.

Step 5: Fracture Pattern — Multifragmentary Type C3

Preferred: Olecranon osteotomy — unparalleled exposure is necessary to reconstruct the articular surface. Accept the known morbidity profile.

Step 6: Isolated Unicolumnar / Coronal Shear

Lateral approach for lateral condyle/capitellum fractures. Medial approach for medial epicondyle fractures (with ulnar nerve management).

Concluding Insights

There is no single "best" approach for all distal humerus fractures. Success requires meticulous preoperative planning, respect for the soft tissue envelope, strict adherence to biomechanical fixation principles, and an approach precisely matched to the fracture personality. Not specified in source

The literature remains dominated by retrospective case series and biomechanical studies, with a notable scarcity of prospective randomised controlled trials. High-quality RCTs are urgently needed to resolve ongoing debates regarding plating configuration and posterior approach selection. I

Evidence Gap

This source document is a comprehensive narrative review. While it synthesises evidence from systematic reviews, meta-analyses, biomechanical studies, and retrospective/prospective cohorts, no landmark RCTs are individually identified as primary data sources. Evidence levels have been inferred from the study designs described within the document. The largest clinical dataset cited encompasses 2,362 elbows from a systematic review and meta-analysis of complications. I
