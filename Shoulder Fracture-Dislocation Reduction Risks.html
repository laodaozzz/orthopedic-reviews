Iatrogenic Fracture Risk in Shoulder Fracture-Dislocation Reduction

An Evidence-Based Interactive Management Guide based on Systematic Review

Anterior Shoulder Dislocation with Proximal Humerus Fracture Comprehensive Risk Assessment & Clinical Management Algorithm

1. Executive Summary & Core Dilemma

The concomitant presentation of anterior glenohumeral dislocation and proximal humerus fracture constitutes a complex orthopaedic emergency. The treating clinician faces a critical dilemma: the urgent need to reduce the joint to prevent chondrolysis and neurovascular compromise must be balanced against the mechanical fragility of the fractured humerus. Applying standard reduction forces, particularly torque and traction, carries a substantial risk of iatrogenic fracture propagation. This can convert a non-displaced, reconstructible injury into a displaced, devascularized fracture-dislocation requiring arthroplasty.

Key Risk Dichotomy Identified

Greater Tuberosity (GT) Fracture-Dislocations generally tolerate closed reduction with high success (>90%) and low iatrogenic risk.
Surgical Neck (SN) Fractures represent a high-risk state where the risk of completing the fracture and dissociating the head is substantial, particularly in elderly osteoporotic women (where up to 80% of iatrogenic fractures occur).

2. Critical Risk Stratification by Fracture Type

The risk of closed reduction is not uniform. It depends on the anatomical site of the fracture and the integrity of the bone-soft tissue construct. The following classification, adapted from the source literature, provides a practical guide.[7, 11]

Table 1: Risk Stratification of Anterior Shoulder Fracture-Dislocations

Group Fracture Pattern Key Anatomical Feature Iatrogenic Risk Primary Recommendation
Group 1 Anterior Dislocation + Isolated GT Fracture Head-shaft continuity intact via periosteum. Low (1-8%)[11] Safe for closed reduction in ED under sedation.
Group 2 Anterior Dislocation + Non-displaced Surgical Neck Fracture Fragile periosteal hinge; fragments move as unit. High (Up to 20% displacement)[11] Do NOT reduce in ED. Reduce in OR under General Anesthesia with fluoroscopy.
Group 3 Anterior Dislocation + Displaced Surgical Neck Fracture Complete dissociation; head is a free fragment. Contraindicated Closed reduction contraindicated. Proceed directly to Open Surgery.

3. Systematic Evidence on Reduction Safety

3.1 Evidence for GT Fracture-Dislocations (Group 1)

Multiple large series validate the safety of closed reduction for this pattern.

  • Robinson et al. (2019): 61 patients with GT fracture-dislocations had a 94% success rate under ED sedation with zero cases of iatrogenic propagation.[7] Level II
  • General Consensus: The GT fragment typically reduces to a near-anatomic position due to re-tensioning of the rotator cuff. Only 10-14% require secondary surgery for displacement (>5mm).[13] Level III

3.2 Evidence for Surgical Neck Fractures (Groups 2 & 3)

Data paints a starkly different picture, characterized by high failure and severe morbidity.

  • Adhikari et al. (2021): Identified women over 60 as significantly more likely to sustain iatrogenic fractures (P=0.026). Conscious sedation was a risk factor vs. General Anesthesia.[4] Level II
  • Hersche & Gerber (1994): Documented six cases where unrecognized SN fractures were displaced during reduction. Consequences were severe: 4 required hemiarthroplasty.[8] Level IV
  • Wong-Chung & Quinn (1998): Attributed iatrogenic spiral fractures to the Kocher maneuver in elderly patients.[14] Level V

The Diagnostic Pitfall

Standard AP X-rays in dislocation can miss occult surgical neck fractures. A low threshold for pre-reduction CT scanning in elderly patients with low-energy trauma is strongly advocated to identify high-risk Group 2 patterns.[15]

4. Comparative Analysis of Reduction Maneuvers

Technique selection is a critical modifiable variable. Leverage-based maneuvers are hazardous, while alignment/relaxation techniques are safer.

Table 2: Biomechanical Comparison of Closed Reduction Techniques

Technique Primary Biomechanical Force Risk in Fx-Dislocation Clinical Recommendation
Kocher (Leverage) Torque / Rotation CRITICAL (Spiral Fx) CONTRAINDICATED in all fracture-dislocations.
Hippocratic (Traction) Distraction / Traction HIGH (Separation) Use with extreme caution; gentle only in axis of deformity.
Milch / Zero Position Alignment / Gentle Pulsion LOW RECOMMENDED. Neutralizes deforming muscle forces.
Scapular Manipulation Glenoid movement (None on humerus) VERY LOW RECOMMENDED, especially for GT fractures. Safe for neck fractures.
FARES Oscillation + Traction LOW-MODERATE Acceptable; use gentle oscillations if neck integrity is uncertain.

Evidence Level II: Systematic Reviews & Meta-analyses of techniques[17, 18]

5. The Critical Role of Anesthesia

Overcoming muscle spasm with pharmacology, not force, is the key to safety.

  • The "Sedation Trap": Inadequate conscious sedation leads to persistent spasm, prompting the clinician to use excessive force, causing fracture propagation.[4, 22]
  • General Anesthesia (GA) with Paralysis: Provides the ideal environment. Muscles are flaccid, allowing reduction with fingertip pressure. Strongly recommended for all Group 2 injuries and elderly patients.[7] Level III
  • Intra-Articular Anesthesia (IAA): Insufficient for muscle relaxation and unreliable in complex injuries.[22]

6. Proposed Clinical Management Algorithm

Based on the synthesized evidence, the following unified guideline is proposed. This algorithm integrates fracture type, patient factors, imaging, and technique selection.

Algorithm A: Isolated Greater Tuberosity Fracture (Group 1)

Setting: Emergency Department is acceptable.
Anesthesia: Deep Sedation (Propofol) or Interscalene Block.
Technique: Use Milch / Zero Position or Scapular Manipulation. Avoid Kocher and heavy traction.
Post-Reduction: Confirm with X-ray. If GT >5mm displaced, consider ORIF.[13]

Algorithm B: Non-Displaced Surgical Neck Fracture (Group 2) - HIGH RISK

Setting: Operating Room (STRONGLY preferred) with airway control and fluoroscopy.
Anesthesia: General Anesthesia with neuromuscular blockade.
Technique: Gentle manipulation under fluoroscopy using Zero Position or direct pressure. NO leverage or heavy traction.
One Attempt Rule: If fracture site distracts on fluoro, STOP. Proceed to immediate open reduction.

Algorithm C: Displaced Surgical Neck Fracture (Group 3)

CRITICAL: Do NOT attempt closed reduction.
Management: Admit for surgery. Plan for ORIF (younger, good bone) or Arthroplasty (elderly, poor bone).

7. Conclusion

Management of anterior shoulder fracture-dislocation requires a risk-stratified approach that departs from simple dislocation protocols. Iatrogenic fracture propagation is a devastating complication, predominantly in elderly women with surgical neck fractures.

This evidence-based guide supports: 1) Safe reduction of GT fractures under sedation; 2) OR-based reduction under GA for surgical neck fractures; 3) Contraindication of leverage techniques; and 4) Liberal use of pre-reduction CT in the elderly. By respecting bone biology and using adequate anesthesia, catastrophic outcomes can be minimized.
