1. Introduction: The Evolution and Modern Rationale of IMN

Proximal humeral fractures represent approximately 5–6% of all adult fractures and their incidence is rising in parallel with an aging global population. Level II While locking plate fixation (ORIF with LCP) gained widespread popularity, the orthopedic community has recognized a concerning complication profile: varus collapse, screw cutout (intra-articular penetration), and extensive soft tissue stripping potentially leading to avascular necrosis (AVN).[2]

Intramedullary nailing (IMN) of the proximal humerus has consequently experienced a renaissance, driven by the advent of third-generation straight nail designs and a refined understanding of insertion biomechanics. Unlike their curvilinear predecessors, which required a lateral entry point that compromised the rotator cuff footprint, modern straight nails utilize a medialized entry point at the humeral zenith, preserving critical tendon insertions and offering a load-sharing mechanical profile theoretically superior for resisting the varus forces typical of surgical neck fractures.[4]

Scope of This Review: This document synthesizes data from systematic reviews, RCTs, and expert technical guides to address technical pearls and pitfalls, reduction techniques for complex fractures, and entry point optimization — providing a definitive reference for avoiding complications in proximal humeral nailing (PHIN).

1.1 Why Nailing? — The Treatment Gap

The clinical decision-making process for displaced proximal humerus fractures oscillates between non-operative management (good functional results in the elderly but risk of malunion) and ORIF (anatomic restoration but significant biological costs).

  • Load-Sharing Mechanics: The intramedullary nail's central position within the medullary canal reduces the lever arm acting on the implant, diminishing strain on the implant-bone interface compared to a lateral plate (load-bearing cantilever beam). In osteoporotic bone, this is critical for maintaining reduction against varus deforming forces.[6] Level II
  • Biological Preservation: Nailing is inherently minimally invasive — inserted without extensive dissection of the fracture hematoma or periosteal stripping. This preservation of the "biological envelope" may reduce rates of nonunion and AVN, particularly in fractures meeting Hertel criteria for ischemia.[8] Level III

1.2 Historical Context: From Curvilinear to Straight Nails

Understanding the evolution of nail design is essential, as many "pitfalls" cited in older literature are specific to obsolete implants.

Table 1: Evolution of Nail Design — Curvilinear vs. Straight

Entry Point Lateral (Greater Tuberosity) Medial (Humeral Head Zenith) Lateral entry violates rotator cuff insertion; medial entry spares the footprint[8]

⚠ Key Insight: The transition to straight nails has fundamentally altered the technique. Surgeons trained on older systems must "unlearn" the lateral entry point. Applying a lateral starting point to a straight nail will inevitably result in iatrogenic varus malreduction or lateral cortex blowout.[8]

2. Surgical Anatomy & "Safe Zones"

A granular understanding of the neurovascular anatomy is the primary defense against complications that plagued early adoption of humeral nailing. The trans-deltoid approach used for nailing places the axillary nerve and the rotator cuff at risk, necessitating precise identification of "safe zones."

2.1 The Axillary Nerve: The "Danger Zone"

The axillary nerve is the structure most at risk during PHIN, particularly during insertion of proximal locking screws and distal interlocks.

  • Anatomic Course: The nerve traverses the quadrangular space and winds around the surgical neck from posterior to anterior, supplying the deltoid and teres minor muscles.[12]
  • Distance from Acromion: Traditionally taught as 5 cm distal to the acromion, recent systematic reviews indicate a range of 3.5–7.0 cm depending on arm length and deltoid bulk.[13] Level II
  • The "5.5 cm Rule": A pivotal clinical study recommends considering 5.5 cm from the anterolateral acromial edge as the upper limit of the "safe zone" for the trans-deltoid approach.[15] Any dissection or screw placement beyond this point without direct visualization carries high injury risk.
  • Anterior Motor Branch: Runs horizontally ~6 cm distal to the acromion; particularly vulnerable during AP locking screw placement.[16]

⚠ Danger: The nerve moves cranially with abduction. Positioning the arm in 30° abduction can relax the deltoid but effectively brings the nerve closer to the surgical field. The nerve index (ratio of distance to arm length) is only 18% predictable — absolute measurement is safer.[15]

2.2 The Rotator Cuff Footprint

The "critical point" for entry is defined by the insertion of the supraspinatus tendon. Lateral entry points violate the tendinous insertion (footprint), leading to difficult repair, chronic pain, and tear propagation. A straight nail allows entry through the muscular portion of the supraspinatus, medial to the footprint, preserving tendon biomechanical integrity.[6] Level II

2.3 Vascularity and Hertel Criteria

The posterior humeral circumflex artery (PHCA) provides the majority of humeral head perfusion (64%).[19]

Hertel's Predictors of Ischemia:
  1. Metaphyseal extension (calcar) < 8 mm
  2. Disruption of the medial hinge
  3. Fracture dislocation
Relevance to Nailing: Nailing is generally safe for fractures with >8 mm of calcar attached to the head. Even with disrupted hinges, minimal dissection may preserve remaining soft tissue attachments and vascularity.[9]

3. Preoperative Planning & Evaluation

3.1 Imaging Modalities

Standard trauma series radiographs (AP, Scapular Y, Axillary Lateral) are mandatory but often insufficient for complex fractures.

  • CT Scan: Essential for 3-part and 4-part fractures. Assesses tuberosity integrity (comminution of GT affects fixation), head-split fractures (contraindication for nailing), and glenoid involvement.[20]
  • MRI: Rarely indicated acutely unless massive cuff tear is suspected, which may alter the surgical plan (e.g., to Reverse Shoulder Arthroplasty).

4. Surgical Technique — Phase 1: Preparation & Approach

4.1 Positioning: The Beach Chair

Place the patient in the beach chair (semi-Fowler) position, head of bed elevated ~30–45°.[9]

  • Head: Secured in neutral position to prevent C-arm interference.
  • Arm: Draped free for full ROM — ability to extend and adduct is critical for correct entry point without damaging the rotator cuff or acromion.[23]
  • C-Arm Setup: Position at the head of bed, parallel to the anesthesia screen. Allows conversion between AP (Grashey) and Axillary Lateral views by rotating the arc.

4.2 The Approach

A limited anterolateral deltoid-splitting approach is standard:

  1. Incision: 2–3 cm longitudinal incision starting at the anterolateral acromion, extending distally.
  2. Deltoid Split: Identify the raphe between anterior and middle deltoid. Dissect sharply to the subdeltoid bursa.
  3. Nerve Safety: Stay proximal to the 5.5 cm safe zone. Do not extend distally without identifying the nerve.[15]
  4. Bursectomy: Thorough bursectomy to visualize the rotator cuff and fracture lines.

5. Reduction Strategies for Complex Fractures

🔴 The Golden Rule: The nail is not a reduction tool. Reduction must be achieved before reaming and nail insertion. Attempting to reduce with the nail leads to iatrogenic comminution, malunion, or loss of fixation.[25]

5.1 General Principles

  • Mobilization: The proximal fragment is mobile and rotated by cuff muscles; the shaft is pulled medially by the pectoralis major.
  • Control: Both fragments must be controlled. The proximal fragment via "joysticks" or sutures; the distal fragment via arm manipulation.

5.2 The "Joystick" Technique Level IV

For varus or valgus angulated heads, direct manipulation is required.

  • Technique: Insert a threaded Schanz pin (2.5–3.0 mm) or heavy K-wire into the humeral head fragment as a "joystick."
  • Placement: Laterally or anteriorly — must not obstruct the central nail path.
  • Maneuver: Use the joystick to derotate the head and lift it out of varus/valgus while the assistant manipulates the shaft to match.[8]
💡 Pearl: In varus impacted fractures, the head is often "locked." Use the joystick to disimpact the medial hinge before attempting reduction.[28]

5.3 Sutures as Reduction Tools ("Tugging") Level IV

Displaced tuberosities are difficult to control with clamps.

  • Place heavy, non-absorbable sutures (#5 FiberWire, FiberTape) through the tendon-bone interface of GT (supraspinatus/infraspinatus) and LT (subscapularis) before reduction.[2]
  • These "tag" sutures allow pulling tuberosities into reduction and serve as fixation later.
  • Vector Control: Pulling the supraspinatus tag corrects varus; pulling the subscapularis tag corrects rotation.

5.4 The "Cobb" or "F-Tool" Maneuver

For correcting shaft displacement (usually medial/anterior due to pectoralis major):

  • Insert a Cobb elevator or F-tool medial to the proximal shaft but lateral to the head.
  • Lever the shaft laterally to align with the head while applying traction.[26]

5.5 Specific Fracture Patterns

Table 4: Reduction Strategies by Fracture Pattern
Pattern Challenge Reduction Strategy
Valgus Impacted (4-part) Head crushed down; tuberosities splayed Elevate head using periosteal elevator through fracture site (from lateral) to restore height. Reduce tuberosities under head ("containment").
Varus Displacement Medial hinge disruption Abduct arm to relax deltoid. Use joystick to lift head into valgus. Hyper-reduce slightly into valgus to lock the medial cortex.[7]

6. Entry Point Optimization

Selecting the correct entry point is the single most critical step in minimizing complications and ensuring anatomic alignment.

6.1 The "Zenith" Concept

  • Target: The optimal entry point for a straight nail is the highest point (zenith) of the humeral head, in line with the medullary canal axis.[11]
  • Location: Typically at the junction of the articular cartilage and the greater tuberosity, or slightly medial to it — in sharp contrast to the lateral entry of older bent nails.
  • Anatomical Correlate: Entry is through the muscular portion of the supraspinatus or the rotator interval, avoiding the tendinous insertion.[6]
💡 Surgical Pearl: A specialized modified lateral view (the "Precipice View" — approximately 45° orbit over the top) is highly recommended. It visualizes the profile of the greater tuberosity and ensures the entry point is not too anterior or posterior.[26]

6.2 Radiographic Verification

Biplanar fluoroscopy is non-negotiable:

  • AP View (Grashey): Guide wire should enter at the articular margin or just medial, aligned with the intramedullary shaft axis.
  • Lateral View (Scapular Y): Wire centered in the humeral head, bisecting anterior and posterior cortices.[11]

6.3 Making the Portal

  • Supraspinatus Split: Make a longitudinal incision in the supraspinatus tendon (in line with fibers) rather than transverse — preserves blood supply and mechanical integrity.[8]
  • Entry Reaming: Use a cannulated reamer (8–10 mm) over the wire. Ream only the proximal humerus; shaft is usually left unreamed to preserve endosteal blood supply.[23]
  • Use a soft tissue protection sleeve during reaming to prevent the reamer from catching the rotator cuff or deltoid.[24]

7. Implant Insertion & Locking

7.1 Nail Insertion and Depth

  • Insertion: Manual or gentle tapping. Avoid forceful hammering, which can distract the fracture.
  • Depth Control: The proximal end of the nail must be buried subchondrally — 2–5 mm below the cartilage surface.[23] A proud nail is the most common cause of reoperation for pain.[31]
⚠ Critical: Verify nail height relative to the articular surface with the C-arm. Most systems have a jig with a depth gauge or radiographic ruler for this purpose.

7.2 Proximal Locking Strategies

Table 5: Proximal Locking Strategies
Strategy Description Indication / Pearl
Screw-in-Screw Smaller screw threads through the head of a larger locking screw, creating a fixed-angle beam[21] Highly recommended for osteoporosis; significantly increases pull-out strength
Multiplanar Screws Convergent or divergent patterns to maximize "grasping volume"[24] Direct screws posteriorly and superiorly to capture GT fragments
Calcar Screws Inferomedial screw along the medial cortex — mechanical buttress against varus collapse[7] Substitutes for the medial hinge if comminuted. Warning: Monitor depth to avoid joint penetration or axillary nerve injury

7.3 Distal Locking

Performed percutaneously using the targeting jig. Level IV

💡 Safety Technique for Distal Locking:
  1. Skin incision at the target site.
  2. Blunt dissection with a hemostat down to bone — spreads deltoid fibers and sweeps the nerve out of the path.
  3. Insert the trocar/drill sleeve directly onto bone before drilling.
  4. Never drill blindly through soft tissue.[23]

8. Tuberosity Fixation: The "Cerclage" Imperative

In 3-part and 4-part fractures, screw fixation alone is often insufficient to hold osteoporotic tuberosity fragments against rotator cuff pull. Level II

8.1 Rationale

Biomechanical studies confirm that suture augmentation significantly increases load-to-failure compared to screws alone.[34] It neutralizes the superior pull of the supraspinatus and the posterior pull of the infraspinatus.

8.2 Technique: The "Lasso" and "Nice Knot" Level IV

  1. Step 1: Heavy sutures (FiberTape/Wire) placed in the tendon-bone interface early in the case (see Section 5.3).
  2. Step 2: After the nail is locked proximally, secure sutures to the implant:
    • Through the nail: Some nails have specific eyelets for sutures.
    • Around the screws: Tie sutures around proximal locking screw heads before they are fully seated. Washers can trap knots.[24]
  3. Step 3: Circumferential cerclage around the tuberosities and nail shaft (if exposed) or through the rotator cuff interval to "bundle" tuberosities against the shaft.
💡 The "Lasso" Maneuver: For a split GT, pass the suture through the infraspinatus, reduce the fragment, and tie it to the proximal screw — effectively "lassoing" the fragment into the construct.[24]

9. Complications: Prevention & Management

Complication rates in PHIN range from 10–30% depending on fracture severity.[2]

9.1 Intraoperative Complications

Table 6: Intraoperative Complications — Prevention Strategies
Complication Cause Prevention
Iatrogenic Fracture Forcing nail into unreamed canal or mismatched entry Ream entry adequately. Use smaller nail if resistance met. Do not hammer.[26]
Drill Bit Breakage Drill skives off hard cortical bone Start drilling perpendicular to bone surface before correcting angle.
Nerve Injury Violation of safe zones Adhere to 5.5 cm limit; blunt dissection for all distal screws.[15]

9.2 Postoperative Complications

Table 7: Postoperative Complications — Prevention & Management
Complication Cause Prevention / Management
Screw Cut-out Screws too close to subchondral bone (primary) or varus collapse (secondary) Leave screws 3–5 mm short of subchondral line. Use calcar screws. Intraoperative dynamic views.[2]
Subacromial Impingement Proud nail height Bury nail subchondrally (2–5 mm). If persistent after union, removal of hardware (ROH) is reliable.[36]
Rotator Cuff Dysfunction Lateral entry damaging footprint Use straight nails with medial entry. Repair supraspinatus split meticulously.[5]
Avascular Necrosis (AVN) Vascular disruption (4–10% incidence) If symptomatic, conversion to Reverse Shoulder Arthroplasty (RSA) is salvage of choice. Nailing preserves tuberosity accessibility for RSA.[31]

10. Outcomes & Evidence-Based Comparison

10.1 Systematic Reviews & Expert Consensus

  • Zhu et al. (2020) & Wong et al. (2016): No significant difference in Constant scores, DASH scores, or ROM between IMN and locking plates for 2- and 3-part fractures.[37] Level I
  • Reoperation Rates: Historically higher for nails due to cuff pain/hardware prominence, but recent data on 3rd-generation nails shows convergence with plates (~13–15%).[39]

10.2 The Lopiz et al. RCT (2014) Level I

This pivotal study compared straight vs. curvilinear nails. While union rates were similar, straight nails had significantly lower rates of rotator cuff pain and dysfunction compared to curved nails.[10] This study cemented the shift toward straight nail designs as the standard of care.

Evidence Summary: Based on available systematic reviews and the Lopiz RCT, IMN with modern straight nails provides equivalent functional outcomes to locking plates, with a distinct advantage in biological preservation and lower screw cut-out rates, at the cost of a modestly higher risk of shoulder pain if technical execution is suboptimal.
Source Limitation: The provided document references multiple systematic reviews (Zhu 2020, Wong 2016) and one RCT (Lopiz 2014). If additional landmark trials exist in the broader literature, they are not covered in this source document. Evidence levels are inferred from study designs described in the text.

11. Conclusion

Proximal Humeral Intramedullary Nailing has matured into a sophisticated, reliable treatment option that addresses the biological and mechanical limitations of locking plates. The modern "straight" nail, when combined with a medial entry point, anatomic reduction prior to reaming, and tuberosity cerclage, offers a robust construct promoting healing while minimizing soft tissue morbidity.

Success in PHIN is technique-dependent, not implant-dependent. The surgeon must rigidly adhere to:
  • Respecting the axillary nerve safe zone (5.5 cm)
  • Utilizing joysticks for reduction
  • Employing suture augmentation for tuberosity control
  • Burying the nail subchondrally (2–5 mm)
When executed with precision, PHIN provides a distinct advantage in osteoporotic fractures, allowing early rehabilitation and predictable functional recovery.
