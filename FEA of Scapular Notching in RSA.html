1. The Biomechanical Paradigm of RSA and Scapular Notching

1.1 Principles of Reverse Shoulder Arthroplasty Level V

Reverse shoulder arthroplasty (RSA) fundamentally alters joint anatomy by replacing the concave glenoid with a convex glenosphere and the humeral head with a concave polyethylene liner. This design medializes and inferiorizes the center of rotation (CoR), significantly increasing the deltoid's mechanical advantage and recruitment, allowing it to compensate for deficient rotator cuffs.

Primary Indications: Cuff tear arthropathy, severe arthritis with massive cuff tears, revision of failed anatomic TSA, and complex proximal humerus fractures in the elderly.

1.2 Pathophysiology of Scapular Notching

Scapular notching—progressive erosion of the inferior scapular neck—is a highly prevalent complication (incidence 10-96%) with a dual etiology:

  • Mechanical: Repetitive impingement of the medial rim of the humeral polyethylene cup against the scapular pillar during adduction/extension.
  • Biological: Generated polyethylene wear debris triggers a chronic foreign-body inflammatory response (macrophage-mediated osteolysis), exacerbating bone loss.

This creates a self-perpetuating cycle: mechanical erosion generates wear debris → biological osteolysis worsens bone loss → altered kinematics may increase impingement severity.

Clinical Significance: While initially debated, longer-term data now associates notching with inferior clinical outcomes (lower functional scores, reduced ROM, higher pain). High-grade notching (Grades 3-4) is strongly correlated with radiolucent lines around the baseplate, indicating potential aseptic loosening and increased revision risk.

1.3 Sirveaux Classification of Scapular Notching

Standardized radiographic grading system based on extent of erosion:

  • Grade 1: Confined to the scapular pillar.
  • Grade 2: Erosion reaches the inferior screw of the baseplate.
  • Grade 3: Bone loss extends superiorly over the inferior screw.
  • Grade 4: Defect undermines the central peg of the baseplate.

2. Finite Element Analysis (FEA) as a Predictive Tool

FEA is a computational method that simulates the mechanical behavior of bone-implant constructs under load. It allows for virtual surgery—systematic manipulation of surgical parameters in a controlled, repeatable, non-invasive environment.

2.1 FEA Workflow

1. Image Acquisition & Segmentation: High-resolution CT scans are used to create 3D digital models of the scapula and humerus by isolating bone from soft tissues.
2. Meshing & Material Properties: The 3D geometry is discretized into finite elements. Bone elastic modulus is derived from CT Hounsfield Units, simulating inhomogeneous cortical/trabecular bone. Implant material properties (titanium, cobalt-chromium, UHMWPE) are assigned from engineering data.
3. Boundary Conditions & Loading: The model is constrained (e.g., distal scapula fixed) and loaded with simulated muscle forces and joint reaction forces for specific activities.
4. Virtual Implantation: CAD models of RSA components are positioned within the patient-specific bone model, allowing precise variation of surgical parameters.

2.2 Key FEA Outputs

FEA calculates critical biomechanical metrics:

  • Stress/Strain Distribution: Identifies high-stress regions (risk of fracture) or low-stress regions (risk of stress shielding and bone resorption).
  • Micromotion: Measures minute displacement at the bone-implant interface. Level III Exceeding ~150 µm inhibits osseointegration, leading to fibrous tissue formation and aseptic loosening.
  • Impingement-Free ROM (Notch Angle): Quantifies the arc of motion before humeral component contacts the scapular neck.
Validation & Limitations: FEA model credibility requires validation against physical experiments (e.g., cadaveric testing, ASTM F2028-14). Limitations include simplified muscle force modeling and challenges in replicating soft tissue viscoelasticity and biological healing processes. Transparent reporting of assumptions is critical.

3. The Decisive Role of Glenoid Component Positioning

3.1 The Mandate for Inferiorization Level III

Inferior placement of the glenoid baseplate, ideally flush with the inferior glenoid rim, is the single most effective maneuver to prevent notching. This creates an "inferior overhang" of the glenosphere, increasing the physical distance between the articulating surface and the scapular neck.

A seminal 2D computer modeling study by de Wilde et al. found the relationship to be exponential: the first 2 mm of inferior overhang provides a 27° gain in the notch angle (impingement-free adduction). This provides a clear, quantifiable surgical target.

3.2 The Inferior Tilt Dilemma Level IV

Implanting the baseplate with a degree of inferior tilt (e.g., 10°-15°) angles the humeral cup away from the scapula, further increasing impingement-free ROM (approx. 1° gain per 1° of tilt).

However, this comes at a biomechanical cost. A cadaveric study by Oh et al. demonstrated that a 10° inferior tilt significantly increased micromotion (44±16 µm vs. 28±9 µm for neutral) and led to a 60% catastrophic failure rate under fatigue loading, compared to 17% for neutral tilt. FEA corroborates this, showing increased stress on the inferior screw and baseplate-bone interface.

Clinical Trade-Off: Inferior tilt improves geometric clearance for impingement but may jeopardize fixation stability, especially in osteopenic bone. Its use must be patient-specific, ideally guided by pre-operative computational planning.

3.3 Malpositioning: Superior Tilt & Cranial Placement

There is universal agreement that superior (cranial) placement or superior tilt of the baseplate are catastrophic surgical errors. They minimize the distance to the scapular neck, guaranteeing early and severe notching. Pre-existing superior glenoid erosion (Type E2) can predispose to this error, necessitating meticulous 3D pre-operative planning.

4. Lateralization as a Primary Strategy for Notching Prevention

Lateralization moves the CoR laterally to a more anatomic position, with three goals: 1) Reduce notching, 2) Improve rotational ROM, 3) Enhance stability via increased "deltoid wrapping."

4.1 Glenoid-Sided Lateralization: Prosthetic vs. Bony

Lateralization can be achieved via the prosthesis (offset baseplates, augmented glenospheres) or bone graft (BIO-RSA).

Level IV An FEA study by Denard et al. revealed critical differences: for the same amount of lateralization, bony lateralization induced significantly higher stress and micromotion at the baseplate-bone interface. They advised limiting bony lateralization to ~5 mm, while prosthetic lateralization up to 10-15 mm appeared mechanically acceptable before exceeding the 150 µm micromotion threshold for osseointegration.

4.2 Humeral-Sided Lateralization

Altering the humeral neck-shaft angle (NSA) from the classic 155° (Grammont) to a more varus 135°-145° provides lateral offset and angles the humeral cup away from the scapula.

Level III A meta-analysis of over 2000 RSAs found notching rates were significantly lower with a 135° NSA (2.8%) compared to a 155° NSA (16.8%). Clinical comparisons between glenoid and humeral lateralization show mixed results, suggesting the choice is not just about notching but also affects soft tissue tensioning and shoulder contour.

5. Implant Design Evolution

5.1 Glenosphere Diameter and Eccentricity

Larger diameter glenospheres (e.g., 42mm) naturally create more inferior overhang when seated flush, reducing notching. Eccentric glenospheres (2-4mm inferior offset) provide a direct, predictable overhang without requiring aggressive baseplate positioning.

5.2 Novel Geometries: Managing Impingement

Notched Humeral Inserts: Level IV FEA by Kocsis et al. showed a novel insert with a built-in inferior notch increased adduction by ~11.2° and reduced peak contact stress on the scapula from 22.4 kPa to 4.7 kPa.

Inverted-Bearing RSA (IB-RSA): Uses a polyethylene glenosphere and metal humeral liner. The rationale is to eliminate the biological component of notching—when impingement occurs, it's metal-on-bone, not polyethylene-on-bone, theoretically preventing PE wear debris-induced osteolysis. This aims to limit notching to mechanical erosion only (Grades 1-2).

6. Integrating Analysis into Surgical Practice

The translation of biomechanical research into clinical practice is occurring through 3D pre-operative planning software (e.g., Virtual Implant Positioning™ systems) and Patient-Specific Instrumentation (PSI).

Surgeons can upload patient CT data, create 3D models, and virtually simulate RSA, optimizing baseplate position, screw length, and glenosphere size. Custom 3D-printed PSI guides then ensure accurate transfer of this plan to the operating room, improving placement accuracy.

The next frontier is integrating patient-specific FEA directly into planning software, allowing surgeons to compare stress and micromotion outcomes for different implant positions and designs for a specific patient's bone quality and morphology.

7. Synthesis, Recommendations, and Future Horizons

7.1 Consolidated Biomechanical Drivers

Scapular notching is driven by surgical/implant factors that fail to create clearance between the humeral component and inferior scapular neck. The core trade-off is between preventing impingement (via lateralization/inferior tilt) and maintaining fixation stability.

7.2 Evidence-Based Recommendations

Glenoid Preparation & Positioning:

  1. Prioritize Inferior Placement: Baseplate flush with inferior glenoid rim to maximize overhang.
  2. Avoid Superior Tilt: Meticulous reaming for neutral or slight inferior tilt. Use 3D planning for E2 glenoids.
  3. Use Inferior Tilt Judiciously: Weigh geometric benefit against fixation risk. Consider only with excellent bone stock, guided by planning tools.

Implant Selection:

  1. Favor Lateralized Designs over highly medialized Grammont-style.
  2. Prefer Prosthetic to Bony Lateralization for glenoid-sided offset due to better mechanical stability.
  3. Consider Lower NSA (135°) for effective humeral-sided lateralization.

7.3 Future Directions

Key questions remain regarding the optimal total and distribution of lateralization, the integration of dynamic musculoskeletal models, long-term performance of novel designs, and the development of dynamic/probabilistic FEA for more robust guidelines.
