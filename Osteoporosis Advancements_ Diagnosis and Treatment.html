Clinical Advancements in Osteoporosis Diagnosis & Management

A Comprehensive Review of 2023–2025 Guideline Updates and Therapeutic Paradigms · Evidence-Based Educational Summary

I Executive Summary of Major Clinical Advancements (2023–2025)

The 2023–2025 period represents a definitive paradigm shift in osteoporosis management—moving from a generalized "bisphosphonate-first" model to a highly stratified, "anabolic-first" approach for high-risk individuals. This evolution is driven by new high-level evidence, major guideline publications, and the maturation of advanced diagnostic tools.

Four most significant advancements defining this era:

  1. The "Anabolic-First" StrategyLevel I — The concept of "very high risk" and "imminent risk" (fracture within 1–2 years) is now codified in major international guidelines. The 2024 ASBMR/BHOF position statement1, 2024 UK NOGG guidance3, and 2023 German DVO guidance4 explicitly recommend initiating potent osteoanabolic agents (teriparatide, abaloparatide, romosozumab) as first-line therapy for this population, followed by an antiresorptive to maintain gains.
  2. Refined Risk AssessmentLevel II — The 2023 launch of FRAXplus5 provides a mechanism to adjust fracture risk scores for type 2 diabetes, fall history, recency of fracture, and more. Concurrently, the 2023 ISCD Official Positions6 have solidified Trabecular Bone Score (TBS) as a standard clinical tool.
  3. Landmark Guideline for Men (ESCEO 2024)Level I — The first comprehensive, GRADE-assessed international guideline for male osteoporosis7, establishing a clear, risk-stratified treatment algorithm mirroring the advanced approach for postmenopausal women.
  4. The 2024 Endocrine Society Vitamin D GuidelineLevel I — Advises against routine population screening for 25(OH)D in the general population8, promoting targeted, evidence-based supplementation instead.
⚠ Critical New Challenge

The complex management of the severe "rebound effect" following denosumab discontinuation is now the single greatest unresolved clinical challenge, detailed in 2025 literature reviews11 and addressed with new urgency in 2023 national guidelines12.

II Advancements in Diagnosis and Risk Assessment

A. The Evolution of FRAX: FRAXplus (2023)

FRAX has been the foundational algorithm for estimating 10-year fracture probability13. However, its clinical utility was limited by well-known constraints—it treated a vertebral fracture from 10 years ago as equivalent to one from 10 weeks ago and did not quantitatively account for type 2 diabetes-associated fragility.

The 2023 launch of FRAXplus5 represents a direct response to these limitations. Validated post-FRAX adjustments include:

  • Recency and site of fracture (within past two years) — directly addresses "imminent risk"1
  • Number of falls (previous year)
  • Trabecular Bone Score (TBS) — integrates bone microarchitecture
  • Duration of type 2 diabetes mellitus — accounts for increased fracture risk at any BMD
  • Higher dose of glucocorticoids
  • Discordance between femoral neck and lumbar spine BMD
  • Hip axis length
💡 Clinical Significance

FRAXplus is the enabling technology for the "anabolic-first" paradigm. Clinicians can now use a validated tool to demonstrate that a patient meets "very high risk" criteria, justifying first-line use of more potent (and expensive) anabolic agents14.

B. Solidification of Trabecular Bone Score (TBS)

TBS is a textural index derived from standard lumbar spine DXA scans, providing a non-invasive proxy for bone microarchitecture6. Its clinical value lies in explaining why a majority of fragility fractures occur in patients whose T-scores are in the non-osteoporotic range15. In 2023, TBS completed its transition from research metric to globally endorsed clinical tool, driven by:

  1. 2023 ISCD Official Positions6: TBS is used to calculate adjusted FRAX probabilities. The L1–L4 TBS value should be used without vertebral exclusions, even with degenerative changes. TBS is not recommended for monitoring treatment response.
  2. 2023 ESCEO/IOF Position Paper17: Based on a systematic review of 96 studies using GRADE methodology, with 22 statements supporting TBS's role in fracture risk prediction and treatment initiation.
✅ Key Paradigm

Where BMD measures bone mass, TBS is now the validated, standardized clinical proxy for bone quality.

C. Radiofrequency Echographic Multi-Spectrometry (REMS)

REMS uses ultrasound to assess BMD at axial sites—non-ionizing, portable, and reportedly as accurate as DXA19. However, the 2023–2025 literature presents a sharp and unresolved conflict regarding clinical readiness:

  • Pro: A 2025 BMJ "State of the Art Review" highlights REMS as having "potential roles in specific populations"14. It is incorporated into Italian national guidelines18.
  • Con: The UK's NOGG 2024 Guideline explicitly states REMS is "not recommended for the diagnosis of osteoporosis"22.

The IOF reinforces that tools other than DXA exist but are "not all recognised by healthcare systems for the diagnosis of osteoporosis"25. Clinical reality for 2025: REMS remains emerging and has not yet met the evidentiary bar for inclusion in major international guidelines.

D. Clinical Utility of Bone Turnover Markers (BTMs)

BTMs—serum P1NP (formation) and serum CTX (resorption)—reflect the dynamic rate of bone remodeling26. The 2023–2025 consensus is unanimous: BTMs are not for diagnosis29, but for management and monitoring28. Validated uses include:

  1. Assessing adherence to antiresorptives: Suppressed CTX confirms adherence to oral bisphosphonates7.
  2. Monitoring anabolic response: Rapid P1NP rise confirms therapeutic effect within 3–6 months28.
  3. Monitoring denosumab rebound: Sharp CTX/P1NP increase signals the dangerous rebound state11.
💡 Analogy

DXA is the "annual balance sheet" for bone. BTMs are the "real-time metabolic dashboard"—essential for managing the more potent therapies of the "anabolic-first" era.

III The New Therapeutic Paradigm: "Anabolic-First" & Goal-Directed Treatment

A. The 2024 ASBMR/BHOF Position Statement: "Goal-Directed Osteoporosis Treatment"

The most significant philosophical shift in 2024. This document1 moves osteoporosis management into a modern chronic disease specialty with a "treat-to-target" model:

  • The Target: T-score of ≥ −2.51
  • Preferred Site: Total Hip (TH) BMD — best reflects subsequent risk for both vertebral and non-vertebral fractures in treated patients114

This framework aligns osteoporosis with diabetes (A1c target) and hypertension (BP target). By establishing a clear T-score goal, the guidelines compel clinicians to select an initial therapy capable of reaching that target. A patient with T-score −3.8 cannot reach >−2.5 with an oral bisphosphonate alone—providing the intellectual justification for "anabolic-first"1.

B. Identifying the "Very High Risk" & "Imminent Risk" Patient

The "imminent risk" concept recognizes that a recent fragility fracture (especially within 1–2 years) is a sentinel event30 predicting extremely high near-term subsequent fracture risk, "largely independent of baseline T-score"1. International guidelines now converge on definitions:

C. Guideline Consensus on "Anabolic-First" Sequencing

The strategy involves initiating therapy with an anabolic agent (teriparatide, abaloparatide) or dual-action agent (romosozumab) for its full limited duration (12–24 months), followed immediately by a potent antiresorptive (denosumab or zoledronic acid) to "lock in" gains2.

⚠ Critical Sequence Warning

Initiating an anabolic after prolonged antiresorptive use may blunt the anabolic effect as the bone has been made metabolically "quiet"32. The clinician's first choice of therapy is their most important—choosing a bisphosphonate first for a "very high risk" patient may be an irreversible therapeutic error.

  • NOGG 2024: "Consider teriparatide or romosozumab as first-line treatment options in postmenopausal women at very high fracture risk"3
  • DVO 2023: "Osteoanabolic treatment is recommended with high priority if the patient is judged to have a very high risk of fracture"4
  • ESCEO: "The key conclusion is that the current evidence base supports an 'anabolic first' approach in patients found to be at very high risk of fracture"2

D. New Pharmacological Data and Approvals (2023–2025)

Abaloparatide

  • NICE (UK) Approval (August 2024): Recommended for treating postmenopausal osteoporosis, benefiting over 14,000 people in England33.
  • ESCEO 2024 (Men): Specifically named as "appropriate first-line treatment for men at very high risk"7.
  • ESCEO 2025 Review: Reviews head-to-head data (ACTIVE trial), suggesting potential greater efficacy vs. teriparatide for major osteoporotic fractures34.

Romosozumab

  • Real-World Effectiveness (2025): Systematic review of 362 records confirming significant BMD improvements at 12 months, with gains "significantly larger" in treatment-naïve patients—strong support for "anabolic-first"35.
  • BRIDGE Trial (Men): Greater BMD gains vs. placebo36.
  • CKD Patients: Post-hoc analysis of FRAME/ARCH trials shows efficacy and similar safety in Stage 1–3 CKD36.
  • Cardiovascular Safety (2024 Meta-analysis): In Asian populations, no significant difference in serious cardiovascular events37.

A. Osteoporosis Canada: 2023 Clinical Practice Guideline Update

A comprehensive, pragmatic document12 with strong emphasis on non-pharmacological interventions: balance and functional training (>2×/week) and progressive resistance training (>2×/week). While bisphosphonates remain first-line for most, it provides a "softer" anabolic-first recommendation—suggesting clinicians "seek advice from an osteoporosis expert" for severe/multiple vertebral fractures.

Its most critical advancement lies in long-term management protocols12:

  • Bisphosphonate Holiday: Initial therapy 3–6 years before considering a holiday.
  • Denosumab: Explicitly recommends "long-term uninterrupted therapy." For high-risk stopping after ≥5 doses: "Good practice includes seeking advice from a consultant with expertise in osteoporosis." This "call an expert" recommendation is a formal admission that this scenario is too complex for a standard algorithm12.

B. NOGG (UK): 2024 Guideline Update

Fully embraces the new personalized paradigm22, updating FRAX-based thresholds and adding new risk factors (Parkinson's disease, lower limb amputation, adult learning disabilities). Expands first-line options:

  1. Anabolics: Abaloparatide newly added; teriparatide and romosozumab recommended as first-line for very high risk3
  2. HRT: Now recommended as first-line for younger postmenopausal women (≤60 yr) with high fracture risk but low thromboembolic risk22

C. German DVO: 2023 Guideline Update

Notable for its unique 3-year fracture risk model4, rather than the 10-year FRAX. "Very high risk" is defined as ≥10% risk in the next three years. The DVO model inherently amplifies the risk of recent events by shortening the risk window—an elegant quantitative method to identify the same "very high risk" patients as NOGG, via a different computational path4.

D. USPSTF (US): 2025 Final Recommendation

Published January 14, 202541:

  • Women: Grade B recommendation to screen ≥65 yr or postmenopausal <65 with risk factors.
  • Men: "I" Statement (Insufficient Evidence) — "current evidence is insufficient to assess the balance of benefits and harms of screening in men."
⚠ US Clinical Gap

This "I" statement creates a profound gap for US practitioners. It was published simultaneously with the massive ESCEO guideline on how to diagnose and treat men7. The concerning implication is that osteoporosis in US men will continue to be dangerously under-diagnosed44 until after a fracture occurs.

V Management of Special Populations

A. ESCEO 2024: Management of Osteoporosis in Men

One in five men over 50 will experience an osteoporotic fracture, with mortality rates following hip fracture substantially higher in men7. The 2024 ESCEO guideline—the first comprehensive, GRADE-assessed international consensus—provides practice-changing recommendations:

  1. Diagnosis: A female reference database should be used for densitometric diagnosis. This revolutionary recommendation standardizes the disease by a single threshold of bone fragility, regardless of sex.
  2. Risk Assessment: FRAX enhanced with TBS.
  3. Treatment Initiation: All men with prior fragility fracture should be considered for treatment.
  4. Stratified Treatment:
    • High Risk: Oral bisphosphonates first-line.
    • Very High Risk: Sequential therapy starting with a bone-forming agent. Abaloparatide specifically named7.
  5. Monitoring: Biochemical markers of bone turnover are the appropriate tool for assessing adherence.

B. Glucocorticoid-Induced Osteoporosis (GIOP)

GIOP is the most common iatrogenic form of osteoporosis (30–50% fracture rate on long-term therapy45). Key recommendations from 2023 JSBMR guidelines45:

  • Start concurrently with glucocorticoid therapy—do not delay.
  • Anabolic-first for GIOP: "Recombinant teriparatide and an anti-RANKL antibody are more effective than bisphosphonates for preventing vertebral fractures"45.

The "anabolic-first" logic is especially strong for GIOP: glucocorticoids not only increase resorption but potently inhibit formation. A pure antiresorptive addresses only half the pathology45.

C. The "Oldest Old": ENDO 2025 Research

A large retrospective study of 88,676 patients aged ≥80 presented at ENDO 202548 provides a definitive answer to therapeutic nihilism:

✅ Key Finding

Patients ≥80 who suffered a fracture and were treated with osteoporosis medications had a lower risk of subsequent hospitalization and lower all-cause mortality over 5-year follow-up compared to untreated patients48. Preventing the next fracture is a life-saving intervention.

VI Non-Pharmacological and Long-Term Management

A. The 2024 Endocrine Society Vitamin D Guideline: A Major Reversal

In July 2024, the Endocrine Society published its new guideline on Vitamin D8, representing a profound, evidence-based reversal:

  1. Against Routine Screening: Explicitly suggests against routine 25(OH)D testing in the general, asymptomatic population8.
  2. Healthy Adults <75: Defers to RDA of 600–800 IU/day; advises against high-dose empiric supplementation9.
  3. Empiric Supplementation Only for High-Risk Groups810:
    • Children and adolescents (1–18 yr)
    • Adults ≥75 yr (to lower mortality risk)
    • Pregnant people (to reduce pre-eclampsia and preterm birth risk)
    • Adults with high-risk prediabetes (to reduce progression to diabetes)

This guideline de-medicalizes vitamin D for the general public and refocuses clinicians on ensuring sufficient intake (RDA) for most, reserving empiric doses for evidence-backed high-risk populations8.

B. Updated Protein Recommendations

A new consensus from PROT-AGE, ESPEN, and 2023 French guidelines argues the traditional 0.8 g/kg RDA is insufficient for older adults5354:

  • Healthy older adults (≥65): 1.0–1.2 g/kg/day
  • Acute/chronic illness: 1.2–1.5 g/kg/day

Rationale: Higher protein preserves muscle mass (sarcopenia prevention) → stronger muscles → fewer falls → fewer fractures52.

C. Managing Long-Term Antiresorptive Therapy: The Denosumab Crisis

The Denosumab Dilemma (2025)

Denosumab demonstrates continuous BMD increases over 10 years56. The crisis is in its discontinuation11:

🚨 The "Rebound Effect"

Discontinuation is associated with a severe rebound effect: aggressive, rapid loss of all gained BMD, sharp BTM rise, and a ~20% risk of multiple vertebral fractures in postmenopausal women who stop11.

The "Therapeutic Impasse"11: A patient must stop denosumab (often due to severe CKD prompting an FDA boxed warning in January 2024, or rarely ONJ/AFF). The only mitigation is bridging to a potent bisphosphonate (e.g., zoledronic acid). However, bisphosphonates are contraindicated in severe CKD and after AFF. This creates a "checkmate": forced to stop with a 1-in-5 risk of multiple disabling vertebral fractures.

Bisphosphonate Drug Holidays

In contrast, bisphosphonate management is well-established5760:

  • Reassess after 5 years oral or 3 years IV bisphosphonate.
  • If now low risk (T-score > −2.5, no new fractures): consider a holiday3.
  • If still high risk: continue treatment60.
⚠ Clinical Decision Framework

Starting a bisphosphonate = 3–5 year commitment with a safe "holiday" option. Starting denosumab = "long-term uninterrupted therapy"—effectively a permanent decision12. The therapeutic impasse is the single greatest unresolved challenge in osteoporosis management in 202511.

VII Concluding Analysis and Future Outlook

The 2023–2025 era will be remembered as the period when osteoporosis management pivoted from "one-size-fits-all" to a personalized, goal-directed model defined by:

  • Personalized Risk: Advanced tools like FRAXplus5 and TBS6
  • Personalized Goals: "Treat-to-target" (T-score > −2.5) per ASBMR/BHOF 20241
  • Personalized Therapy: Stratified first-line selection based on risk level

Dominant clinical questions have shifted from "who to treat?" to "how to sequence anabolics and antiresorptives?"2 and, most critically, "how to manage the denosumab rebound and therapeutic impasse?"11

The next frontier is already visible: the role of cellular senescence14, with next-generation senolytics and senomorphics targeting root cellular mechanisms of aging, combined with AI in diagnostics61.
