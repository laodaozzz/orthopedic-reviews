Part 1 — Foundational Principles of Knee Arthroplasty

1.1 Defining the Procedures: TKA vs. UKA

Total Knee Arthroplasty (TKA) is a tricompartmental resurfacing procedure — the established standard of care for end-stage, diffuse arthritis of the knee. It replaces the articular surfaces of all three compartments: medial tibiofemoral, lateral tibiofemoral, and patellofemoral, and typically sacrifices the anterior and posterior cruciate ligaments.[1]

Unicompartmental Knee Arthroplasty (UKA) is a "bone- and tissue-sparing procedure" designed to replace only the single, diseased compartment. The most common indication is isolated medial compartment disease (≈90% of UKAs), followed by isolated lateral (≈10%) and isolated patellofemoral disease.[1,2]

🔑 Key Philosophical Distinction

TKA is a "replacement" strategy — it addresses the problem by removing the entire joint environment to treat all current and potential future arthritic development. UKA is a "preservation" strategy — it addresses only the focal, end-stage pathology while preserving native bone stock, cruciate ligaments, and the non-diseased compartments.[1]

Reframing "Failure" — The Stepwise Treatment Concept

A critical third-order implication exists in how failure is defined. UKA is associated with a higher long-term revision rate compared to TKA.[7] However, the nature of revision is fundamentally different. Revision of a failed UKA to a TKA is "generally a simpler procedure than revision of TKA to TKA" and can typically be accomplished with primary TKA components because bone stock and soft tissues of un-operated compartments remain pristine.[2]

Data from a private practice registry study supports this: the 5-year re-revision rate of UKA-to-TKA conversion (4.1%) was similar to primary TKA (3.2%) and much lower than aseptic TKA-to-TKA revision (12.6%).[2] This reframes UKA as a "stepwise" treatment: a patient receives UKA, achieves 10–15 years of high-level function, and if the implant fails, the fallback primary TKA yields outcomes nearly equivalent to a planned primary TKA. This is a fundamentally different risk profile than a failed primary TKA requiring complex, bone-losing revision.[8]

1.2 General Indications for Total Knee Arthroplasty

Per AAOS guidelines, TKA is indicated only after failure of non-operative management (activity modification, physical therapy, NSAIDs, injections).[12] Primary indications include:[12]

  • Severe daily pain and stiffness limiting everyday activities (walking, stair climbing, rising from a chair)
  • Moderate or severe pain at rest, day or night
  • Chronic knee inflammation, swelling, and deformity not resolving with rest or medication

While osteoarthritis (OA) is the most common diagnosis, TKA is also the definitive indication for end-stage inflammatory arthritis such as Rheumatoid Arthritis (RA).[15] This distinction is critical: inflammatory arthritis is a systemic contraindication to UKA.[1] RA attacks the synovium of the entire joint, not a single compartment. Performing UKA in RA patients is clinically illogical, as the inflammatory disease will inevitably destroy preserved compartments, leading to rapid failure. A diagnosis of systemic inflammatory arthritis automatically directs the patient to the TKA pathway.

⚠️ Outcome Confounding Variable

Prospective cohort data shows OA patients have significantly better postoperative KSS scores at 6 and 12 months compared to RA patients.[17] The inclusion of the RA population (≈3.4% of TKA patients[20]) — which has systemically worse functional outcomes — may depress the "average" functional outcome reported for TKA in large registry studies. This could marginally inflate the apparent functional superiority of UKA (performed almost exclusively for OA) in comparative analyses.

Part 2 — Patient Selection for UKA: An Evolving Paradigm

2.1 The Classic Framework: Kozinn & Scott Criteria (1989)

In response to high failure rates of first-generation implants, Kozinn and Scott consolidated recommendations in 1989 that became the "gold standard" for patient selection.[22] These criteria were surprisingly specific[5] and highly restrictive:

These criteria were not based on high-level evidence but on expert opinion designed to mitigate failures of outdated technology.[5] A 1993 study by Insall et al. found that strictly applying these criteria rendered only 6% of patients suitable candidates, cementing UKA's status as a "niche option."[24,26]

The Surgeon Volume Confounder

Because UKA was relegated to a niche procedure, only 10% of orthopaedic surgeons perform unicompartmental replacements.[26] National registries (capturing all surgeons) consistently show high revision rates, while case series from high-volume centres report excellent 10- and 15-year survival rates (>90–95%).[6] The 2019 BASK/EKS consensus explicitly recommends that "surgeons should avoid low-volume use of UKA to optimise outcomes."[11]

2.2 The Modern Expansion

A landmark prospective study of 1000 consecutive medial UKAs[28] intentionally did not apply classic contraindications. 68% (678 knees) would have been considered contraindicated by Kozinn & Scott criteria, yet these patients "did as well as, or even better than, those without contraindications."[6]

A survey of experienced UKA surgeons confirmed that routine practice was not in line with classical criteria. With modern evidence-based indications, UKA candidacy could increase from the historical 6–12% to as high as 50% of knee arthroplasty patients.[30]

🔧 Technological Drivers of Expansion

The Kozinn & Scott criteria were a behavioral solution (restrict the patient population) to a technological problem. Now that technology has largely solved those problems, the behavioral restrictions are obsolete:

  • Highly cross-linked polyethylene (HXLPE) — significantly enhanced wear resistance[31]
  • Robotic-assisted techniques & new instrumentation — improved alignment accuracy[3,19,32]

2.3 Modern Absolute Contraindications for UKA

The modern absolute contraindications are concise and logically focused:

  1. Inflammatory Arthritis (e.g., RA, Psoriatic Arthritis)[4,18,19]
  2. Diffuse, Multicompartmental OA — clear radiographic and/or symptomatic evidence of significant, end-stage arthritis in >1 compartment[18]
  3. Gross Ligamentous Instability — significant functional instability (excluding isolated, correctable ACL deficiency)
  4. Severe, Fixed Deformity — frontal plane deformity >15° that is not passively correctable to neutral[33]

Part 3 — Deep-Dive: Controversial UKA Selection Criteria

3.1 The Anterior Cruciate Ligament (ACL)

Traditional Contraindication: ACL deficiency was absolute.[1] The biomechanical rationale was that an incompetent ACL creates instability, predisposing to accelerated polyethylene wear and aseptic loosening.

Modern Evidence: A 2021 systematic review and meta-analysis[34] found "no significant differences" in revision rates or functional outcomes between ACL-deficient and ACL-intact UKA patients. Level I

🎯 Critical Clinical Distinction

The key insight is differentiating ACL "deficiency" (an anatomic state, often degenerative tear on MRI in elderly OA patients) from ACL "instability" (a functional, symptomatic state). The critical selection question is not "Is the ACL intact on MRI?" but rather "Is the knee functionally stable on physical examination?" (e.g., negative Lachman/anterior drawer tests and by patient report).[31]

The "À La Carte" Solution (UKA + ACLR): For young, active patients[37] with symptomatic instability from chronic ACL tear and concomitant isolated medial OA, a combined procedure has emerged. A 2022 systematic review[38] concluded that combined UKA + ACLR is a "viable option" with implant survival comparable to ACL-intact UKA patients and higher than ACL-deficient UKA alone. Level I

3.2 The Patellofemoral (PF) Joint

Traditional Contraindication: Any PFA was a contraindication.[1]

Modern Evidence: A 2020 meta-analysis[42] demonstrated that pre-operative anterior knee pain and mild-to-moderate PFA do not affect functional outcomes or survivorship. Level I

📐 The Location-Specific Rule

The contraindication is not "PFA" in general, but specifically severe wear of the lateral facet with bone loss and grooving.[6] Conversely, "damage to the medial side, however severe, do not compromise… so should not be considered contraindications."[19] This is biomechanically logical: a medial UKA corrects varus deformity, which unloads the medial patellar facet and central trochlea, but does not reliably unload the lateral facet.

3.3 Patient Demographics: Age, BMI, and Activity Level

Age

Kozinn & Scott specified >60 years. Modern evidence states age is not a contraindication.[6] UKA has emerged as ideal for patients at both extremes of the age spectrum:

  • Young Patients (<60): Now considered "ideal candidates."[19] While some registry studies note higher revision risk[1,10], 10-year survival remains >90%.[44] This cohort has low satisfaction with TKA[19] and values faster return to sport and more "natural" knee function.
  • Older Patients (>75): UKA offers lower perioperative complication rates, shorter hospital stays, and faster recovery than TKA.[44] It is a safer option for this group.

BMI

Kozinn & Scott specified <82 kg. Modern meta-analyses find that high BMI (obesity) is not a contraindication.[1] AAOS guidelines for TKA carry a "strong recommendation" warning of increased infection risk for BMI >40.[13] For a morbidly obese patient with isolated OA, the less invasive UKA — with shorter operative time and lower complication profile — may represent a safer surgical option specifically to mitigate the high-risk TKA profile.[8,9,13]

3.4 Coronal Deformity and Flexion Contracture

Coronal Deformity: Classic indication allowed <5° correctable varus.[5] Modern indications have expanded to <15° passively correctable frontal deformity.[19,33] The critical factor remains reducibility: a 14° varus deformity that is passively correctable to neutral implies functional collateral ligaments and is valid.

Flexion Contracture: Classic indication was <5°.[5] Modern indications have expanded to <15°.[19] A 2021 study[49] matched patients with severe (≥15°) flexion contractures to controls (<5°) and found "no difference in PROMs, satisfaction rates, and mid-term survivorship" — 10-year survivorship of 97% in the severe group vs. 94% in controls. Level II

Part 4 — Comparative Effectiveness & Patient-Reported Outcomes

4.1 The Central Trade-Off: Revision Rate vs. Functional Recovery

Survivorship: The TKA Advantage

Multiple systematic reviews[8] and large-scale registry studies[7] consistently demonstrate a lower long-term revision rate for TKA:

  • 15-year revision rate: 18.2% for UKA vs. 6.2% for TKA[9]
  • Main UKA failure reasons: aseptic loosening, polyethylene wear, and progression of OA in unreplaced compartments[4,10]
  • TKA mechanically eliminates the failure mode of disease progression

Recovery & Complications: The UKA Advantage

  • Shorter hospital stay: UKA patients discharged 1.2–1.73 days sooner[8]
  • Fewer complications: Significantly lower rates of major cardiac events and VTE[8,9]
  • Faster rehabilitation: Better functional scores (OKS, KOOS) at 2, 6, and 12 months[56]
  • Return to sport: 5.24 weeks faster[8]
  • Return to work: 3–6 weeks (UKA) vs. 3 months (TKA)[45]

4.2 Functional Outcomes and Patient Satisfaction

UKA consistently demonstrates superiority in functional quality and patient satisfaction:

  • Knee Function: Superior KSS Function Scores and significantly greater postoperative ROM[8,52]
  • Stair Climbing & Walking: Better ability and higher likelihood of unlimited walking distance[58]
  • Patient Satisfaction: 75.2% "very satisfied" (UKA) vs. 62.1% (TKA)[59]
  • "Forgotten Joint Score": Significantly better (higher) FJS — patients are less aware of their implant in daily life[46,59]
📊 The MCID Paradox

Multiple meta-analyses[54] report that while functional scores (OKS, KSS, WOMAC) are statistically superior for UKA, the mean difference often fails to meet the Minimal Clinically Important Difference (MCID). For crude pain relief and basic function, TKA and UKA may be equally good.

However, the difference in Quality of Life (EQ-5D VAS) is both statistically and clinically significant, favoring UKA.[59] The superiority of UKA lies in metrics active patients value most: a "normal" feeling knee (FJS), better high-level kinematics, faster return to work/sport, and higher overall quality of life.

5.3 Ideal Patient Profiles

UKA — Profile 1: The Young & Active Patient (<65)

  • High functional demands, seeks return to sport[8]
  • End-stage OA truly isolated to medial or lateral compartment (often post-traumatic/post-meniscectomy)
  • No inflammatory arthritis; ligaments functionally intact (or candidate for combined UKA+ACLR)[38]
  • PFA absent or mild/medial-central[19]
  • Patient prioritizes functional quality over implant longevity; accepts higher revision risk for 10–20 years of high-level function[8]

UKA — Profile 2: The Elderly & Frail Patient (>75)

  • Isolated unicompartmental OA with significant medical comorbidities[43]
  • Patient prioritizes minimizing perioperative risk over long-term longevity
  • UKA offers shorter, less complicated hospital stay and lower systemic complication risk[8,44]
  • The "15-year revision rate" is secondary and often irrelevant

TKA — Ideal Candidates

  1. Patients with systemic inflammatory arthritis (RA, psoriatic)[15]
  2. Patients with multicompartmental OA, especially severe lateral PFA with bone loss[6]
  3. Patients with severe, fixed deformity >15° or gross multi-planar ligamentous instability[33]
  4. The "one-and-done" prioritizer — patient whose priority is the lowest possible revision risk, accepting longer recovery and less natural-feeling knee[8]

5.4 Conclusion: The Role of Shared Decision-Making

The decision between UKA and TKA for isolated unicompartmental disease is a classic "preference-sensitive" decision. Both are "viable options."[8,11] The evidence does not show one is universally "better"; it shows a clear trade-off.

🩺 The Surgeon's Twofold Role
  1. Accurate Diagnosis: Rigorously confirm isolated disease and rule out all modern absolute contraindications.
  2. Patient Counseling: Present the evidence-based trade-off clearly — UKA offers faster, safer recovery and higher-quality, more "natural" function, in exchange for a definitively higher risk of requiring revision in the long term.

The final decision must be shared,[8] aligning the patient's priorities (peak performance, perioperative safety, or long-term durability) with the surgical option that best delivers that outcome.
