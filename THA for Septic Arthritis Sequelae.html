1 Executive Summary & Pathophysiological Context

The management of the adult hip following septic arthritis represents one of the most formidable challenges in reconstructive orthopaedic surgery. Unlike primary osteoarthritis (OA), where the surgical landscape is defined by degenerative wear in a relatively predictable anatomical environment, the sequelae of septic arthritis — often termed "quiescent" infection — present a heterogeneous complex of severe anatomical distortion, compromised soft tissue envelopes, and the persistent, latent threat of bacterial recrudescence. Level II

⚠️ The "Double Jeopardy" Principle The condition is characterized by a dual risk: (1) the technical difficulty of reconstructing a dysplastic or ankylosed joint, and (2) a biological environment that remains susceptible to periprosthetic joint infection (PJI) at rates significantly higher than the general population. A landmark case-control study identified a ten-fold increased risk of PJI in patients with a history of septic arthritis compared to OA (Hazard Ratio = 13 for any infection; HR = 10 for PJI). Level II [14]

Pathophysiology of Septic Sequelae

The sequelae of septic arthritis — historically referred to as Tom Smith's arthritis when occurring in infancy — result from the rapid chondrolysis and osteonecrosis induced by proteolytic enzymes and bacterial toxins during the acute phase of infection. Review

  • Pediatric population: Destruction often involves the capital femoral epiphysis and the triradiate cartilage of the acetabulum. This early insult arrests normal development, leading to deformities ranging from mild dysplasia to complete dislocation (Crowe IV) or spontaneous bony ankylosis.
  • Adult patients: Manifestations include severe limb length discrepancy (LLD), abductor insufficiency, and extensive scarring.
  • Biological milieu: Scar tissue is poorly vascularized, and bone may harbor sequestra containing biofilm-encapsulated bacteria that remain dormant for decades, evading host immune surveillance until the insult of surgical intervention reactivates them.

2 Preoperative Evaluation: The Diagnostic Imperative

🔑 Cardinal Rule Presume infection until proven otherwise. The distinction between a "quiescent" hip (history of septic arthritis with no clinical signs of active inflammation and normalized serological markers) and a subclinical active infection is the primary determinant of surgical strategy.

2.1 Serological Screening: ESR & CRP

Inflammatory markers — Erythrocyte Sedimentation Rate (ESR) and C-Reactive Protein (CRP) — serve as the first line of defense in distinguishing sterile inflammation from septic activity. Level I [1]

Diagnostic Thresholds

Current guidelines from the Musculoskeletal Infection Society (MSIS) and the International Consensus Meeting (ICM) utilize conventional thresholds of >30 mm/hr for ESR and >10 mg/L for CRP to flag potential infection. Level I [1]

⚡ Reliability Caveat In the specific context of quiescent septic arthritis, these markers operate with variable specificity. The combination of normal ESR and CRP yields a high negative predictive value (~99% for ruling out active sepsis). Level II [2] However, approximately 4% of primary arthroplasty patients may present with elevated markers due to obesity, smoking, or inflammatory arthropathies, and in 10.2% of cases both markers may be elevated without infection. Level II [3]

The "Gray Zone" & False Negatives

A "normal" CRP does not definitively exclude infection in the post-septic hip. Evidence suggests that up to 10% of confirmed septic arthritis cases — particularly those caused by low-virulence organisms like Kingella kingae in children or Cutibacterium acnes in adults — may present with a CRP <10 mg/L. Level III [4] The sensitivity of these markers is also compromised in patients with chronic suppression or recent antibiotic use. The risk of PJI is noted to be 18-fold higher in patients with preoperative elevations in both markers compared to those with normal levels. Level II [3]

2.2 Synovial Fluid Analysis: Aspiration Criteria

When serological markers are elevated or clinical suspicion remains high, hip aspiration (arthrocentesis) is the definitive preoperative diagnostic maneuver. Level I [5]

🚨 Key Finding Using a strict 50,000 cutoff would miss nearly 17% of infections that presented with counts between 25,000 and 50,000 cells/µL. Level III [6] A PMN percentage >90% is highly suggestive of infection, even if the absolute WBC count is equivocal. Level III [7]

Culture Duration & Methodology

The standard culture duration of 5–7 days is insufficient for quiescent hips. Guidelines recommend extending cultures to 14 days to detect slow-growing organisms such as Cutibacterium acnes. Level I [8] The use of blood culture bottles for synovial fluid inoculation improves sensitivity over standard agar plates. Aspiration should be performed at least 2 weeks after cessation of antibiotic therapy to minimize false-negative results. Level III [4]

2.3 Advanced Imaging & Nuclear Medicine

  • MRI: Useful for defining soft tissue abscesses, osteomyelitis, and sinus tracts; however, signal hyperintensity related to edema can persist for years after infection resolution, limiting specificity for active sepsis. Level III [13]
  • Nuclear Medicine (Indium-111 / Tc-99m): Offers higher specificity (~98%) than MRI for differentiating infection from aseptic inflammation or marrow conversion. Particularly valuable in distinguishing "quiescent" remodeling from low-grade osteomyelitis in the proximal femur. Level II [2]

3.1 The Case for Two-Stage Arthroplasty

Two-stage reconstruction remains the gold standard for active infection or cases where "quiescence" cannot be definitively established. Level II [16]

Mandatory two-stage indications:

  • Clinical signs of active infection (sinus tract, erythema)
  • Elevated inflammatory markers with positive or inconclusive aspiration
  • History of resistant organisms (MRSA, VRE, fungal infection) Level II [14]
  • Intraoperative findings of purulence or positive frozen section (>5 PMN/HPF)

The use of an antibiotic-loaded cement spacer (static or articulating) during the interim delivers high local antibiotic concentrations while maintaining soft tissue tension. However, the interval period is associated with significant morbidity including muscle atrophy, scar formation, and functional decline. Level II [15]

3.2 The Case for One-Stage Arthroplasty

One-stage arthroplasty is increasingly favored for confirmed quiescent infections due to its functional superiority and cost-effectiveness. In strictly selected cohorts, eradication rates of 94–100% are achieved — comparable to two-stage protocols. Level II [16, 18]

3.3 Risk Stratification: The "Rule of Ten" & Time Intervals

📊 Time as the Strongest Predictor The timing of the index infection relative to THA is the most potent predictor of reinfection. Level II [14]
  • 5-Year Danger Zone: Risk of recrudescence significantly higher if THA within 5 years (HR = 3.1). [14]
  • 10-Year Safety Margin: Quiescent period ≥10 years correlates with reinfection risk approaching zero, supporting one-stage procedures. [20]

5 Technical Considerations for Anatomical Deformities

The anatomical sequelae of septic arthritis are distinct from degenerative OA. The destruction of the capital epiphysis and triradiate cartilage in childhood leads to complex deformities categorized by Kim et al. into secondary OA, dislocation (Crowe IV equivalent), and ankylosis. Level III [24]

5.1 The Ankylosed Hip

Spontaneous bony ankylosis occurs when the joint space is obliterated by bridging osteophytes or solid bone, often fusing the femur to the ilium in a non-functional position. A posterolateral approach is standard, though a direct lateral or trochanteric osteotomy may be required to visualize the acetabulum and protect the sciatic nerve. Level III [25] Fusion takedown essentially creates a new joint line; surgeons must use fluoroscopy or landmarks such as the vastus ridge to estimate the neck cut level. Level III [27] Conversion of ankylosed hips yields dramatic improvements in back pain and contralateral knee pain, with high patient satisfaction due to restoration of sitting and walking mechanics. Level III [28]

5.2 High Dislocation (Crowe IV Equivalent)

In severe sequelae, the femoral head dissolves or dislocates and the proximal femur migrates superiorly, forming a "pseudoacetabulum" on the iliac wing. This results in significant LLD (often >5 cm). The goal is to place the cup in the true acetabulum (paleocotyle) to restore the anatomic center of rotation. Level III [22]

🔧 Subtrochanteric Shortening Osteotomy (STO)

To safely reduce the hip without traction injury to the sciatic nerve, an STO is performed. A segment of proximal femoral diaphysis (typically 2–4 cm) is resected below the lesser trochanter.

  • Step-cut advantage: Provides inherent rotational stability and larger surface area for healing; allows simultaneous correction of excessive femoral anteversion. [30]
  • Fixation: Bridged with a modular, fluted, tapered stem (e.g., S-ROM); distal flutes engage the isthmus for rotational stability. [31]

5.3 Acetabular Bone Loss & Femoral Considerations

  • Hemispherical cups require ≥50% host bone contact with mandatory supplementary screw fixation. [32]
  • Jumbo cups may bridge gaps for rim fixation when reaming to bleeding bone creates large defects. [33]
  • Trabecular metal (tantalum) augments reconstruct severe rim deficiencies (Paprosky IIIA/IIIB). [34]
  • Custom triflange acetabular components (CTAC) are engineered from CT scans for pelvic discontinuity or catastrophic bone loss. [35]
  • The femoral canal is typically narrow ("stove-pipe" or "champagne-flute"); prophylactic cerclage wiring is recommended before broaching. [22]

7 Postoperative Antibiotic Protocols

Standard 24-hour prophylaxis protocols are inadequate for the 10-fold infection risk presented by septic sequelae. Level II [40]

7.1 Perioperative Prophylaxis

  • Drug Selection: Tailored to historical organism if known. If unknown, a broad-spectrum regimen covering Staphylococcus aureus (including MRSA) and Gram-negative bacilli is prudent (e.g., Cefazolin + Vancomycin). [40]
  • Standard High-Risk Protocol: IV antibiotics continued until final intraoperative cultures return negative (typically 5–7 days). [40]
  • Extended Oral Prophylaxis: Recent evidence suggests that 7 days of extended oral antibiotic prophylaxis (e.g., cephalexin) significantly reduces PJI rates in high-risk populations. Level II [41, 42]

7.2 Therapeutic Management of Positive Cultures

If intraoperative cultures return positive (unexpected active infection), the patient is managed as an acute PJI. Guidelines recommend pathogen-specific IV therapy for 2–6 weeks, often followed by oral therapy. For Staphylococcal infections with implant retention (DAIR), the addition of Rifampin is critical for penetrating biofilm. Level I [43] Recent trials (DATIPO, OVIVA) suggest that 6 weeks of therapy may be non-inferior for certain organisms, provided surgical debridement was adequate. Emerging evidence [44, 45]

7.3 Suppressive Antibiotic Therapy (SAT)

For patients with multi-drug resistant organisms, multiple prior infections, or significant host compromise, lifelong oral SAT may be considered. Common agents include Doxycycline, Minocycline, or Cotrimoxazole. Success rates for controlling symptoms and preventing device failure range from 60% to 86%. Level III [46, 47]

✅ Conclusion THA for sequelae of septic arthritis offers the potential for profound functional restoration in patients crippled by pain and deformity. Success is predicated on a rigorous approach to excluding active infection, with the distinction between "quiescent" and active infection serving as the fulcrum upon which surgical success pivots. By adhering to comprehensive guidelines — respecting the 10-fold increased risk of infection and the biological fragility of the post-septic joint — surgeons can achieve durable, life-changing outcomes. Level II [14, 20, 22]
