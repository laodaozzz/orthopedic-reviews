Primary Versus Secondary Glenohumeral Osteoarthritis

A comprehensive, evidence-based synthesis of pathoanatomy, morphological classifications, and surgical treatment algorithms for glenohumeral osteoarthritis. This educational resource draws from contemporary orthopaedic literature (JSES, JBJS, AJSM) to provide a nuanced framework for advanced clinical decision-making, distinguishing between primary OA (typically intact rotator cuff) and secondary OA (arising from biomechanical derangement).

1. Pathoanatomy & Biomechanical Interplay

The foundational stability of the glenohumeral joint is predicated on a complex interplay between static osseous geometry and dynamic muscular compression. To fully comprehend the divergent evolutionary pathways of primary versus secondary glenohumeral OA, one must precisely quantify the failure mechanisms of these stabilizing forces, specifically focusing on the translation of joint reaction forces.

The Concavity-Compression Mechanism

The native glenohumeral joint is inherently unstable due to the profound surface area mismatch between the large, spherical humeral head and the shallow, minimally constrained glenoid fossa. Stability in the mid-range of motion—where the capsuloligamentous structures are lax—is almost entirely dictated by the "concavity-compression" mechanism. This biomechanical phenomenon was rigorously quantified in a seminal cadaveric study by Lippitt and Matsen, published in JSES in 1993.Level V [1]

📐 Stability Ratio

The stability ratio (SR) is defined as the maximum translational shear force stabilized in a specific direction divided by the applied compressive force: SR = Fshear / Fcompressive. With an intact labrum and normal osseous anatomy, the humeral head can resist tangential shear forces equivalent to 60% of the compressive load (SR = 0.6).[1]

The intact glenoid labrum functions as a critical static stabilizer, effectively deepening the socket. Level V biomechanical data indicates that the labrum contributes to approximately 20% of concavity-compression effectiveness. Disruption or progressive degenerative loss of the labrum profoundly reduces the stability ratio, leaving the joint highly susceptible to pathological translation under physiologic loads.[1]

Joint Reaction Forces in Primary Osteoarthritis

In primary glenohumeral OA, the rotator cuff complex typically remains functionally intact. However, as articular cartilage degrades and eccentric bone wear progresses—most frequently posteriorly—the morphologic concavity of the glenoid is severely compromised.[2]

According to Level V computational musculoskeletal simulations (e.g., UK National Shoulder Model), joint reaction forces in the shoulder can reach up to 2.4 times total body weight.[9] In primary OA, because dynamic stabilizers remain active, these high-magnitude compression forces drive the humeral head relentlessly into the increasingly retroverted or flattened glenoid surface. This creates a destructive biomechanical loop: normal compressive forces become inherently destructive due to the altered bony vector, accelerating concavity loss and exacerbating posterior eccentric wear—the primary driver behind Walch B2/B3 deformities.[10,11]

Biomechanical Derangement in Secondary OA: Cuff Tear Arthropathy

Secondary OA arising from massive, irreparable rotator cuff failure—Cuff Tear Arthropathy (CTA)—presents a diametrically opposed biomechanical failure mode.Level V

The defining pathophysiological event in CTA is the complete uncoupling of the coronal and transverse force couples. When the supraspinatus and infraspinatus tendons fail, the primary drivers of concavity compression are extinguished. Force-Dependent Kinematics (FDK) simulations demonstrate:[14]

  • Variance in superior-inferior shear forces increases exponentially
  • Unopposed deltoid pull generates severe superior shear vectors
  • Center of rotation migrates superiorly, causing cranial translation and destructive acromiohumeral impingement
  • Compressive joint reaction forces paradoxically spike to 236–331 N (vs. 104–135 N in healthy models), resulting from pathological bone-on-bone contact[14]
⚠️ Critical Biomechanical Distinction

This catastrophic loss of both horizontal and vertical stability obliterates the functional fulcrum. Biomechanical evidence dictates that anatomic reconstruction is mathematically and physically impossible in CTA without replacing the fixed mechanical fulcrum—a requirement uniquely met by the reverse total shoulder arthroplasty (rTSA) design.[4]

2. Primary Glenohumeral OA & Morphological Classifications

Precise preoperative characterization of glenoid morphology, bone stock, and version is an absolute prerequisite for successful arthroplasty. The modified Walch classification (original 1999, modified 2016) utilizing 3D CT serves as the gold standard.Level III[11]

A Level III study reported intraobserver reliability for 3D CT classification is substantial (0.73), while interobserver reliability remains moderate (0.55).[11]

A2: Major central erosion with deep humeral head protrusion		 Humeral head remains concentric. Standard aTSA typically yields excellent long-term outcomes.

B2: Biconcave glenoid (anterior paleoglenoid + posterior neoglenoid), retroversion

C (Dysplastic) C1: Dysplastic glenoid with >25° retroversion
C2: Biconcave from acquired bone loss on congenital dysplasia		 Non-acquired retroversion; requires differentiation from B3 for implant planning.
D (Anterior Subluxation) Glenoid anteversion or anterior humeral head subluxation (<40°) Rare morphology; requires anterior asymmetric reaming or structural grafting.

B2 vs. B3 Distinction

The B2 glenoid is strictly biconcave: it retains a distinct anterior paleoglenoid and a posterior eroded neoglenoid. The B3 glenoid demonstrates such advanced posterior wear that the paleoglenoid is completely obliterated, resulting in a monoconcave, highly retroverted joint line with catastrophic bone loss.[12]

Longitudinal evidence shows B1 glenoids rapidly progress: in a JBJS CT study of 65 patients over ≥24 months, 17/19 B1 glenoids progressed to B2 or B3, while concentric Type A glenoids rarely developed posterior subluxation.[11]

Managing Glenoid Retroversion and the 15° Threshold

🔬 The 15° Rule for Eccentric Reaming

Level III Eccentric reaming should not exceed 15° of retroversion correction. Reaming beyond this threshold results in critical medialization, destroying the subchondral bone plate and forcing implant seating in soft cancellous bone—compromising press-fit and cement interdigitation.[20] Patients with severe B2 deformities subjected to aggressive reaming show significant radiographic failure at 8 years, with up to 21% posterior subluxation at final follow-up.[23]

Augmented Glenoid & Bone Grafting

For retroversion >15°, alternative strategies include:

  • Posterior glenoid bone grafting (PGBG): Level III data shows inconsistent results—increased loosening rate (10.7% at 64 months[24]) and up to 40% failure risk at 105 months due to graft resorption.[24]
  • Posteriorly augmented glenoid (PAG) components: Favorable short-to-midterm survivorship for B2 deformities by preserving anterior host bone while correcting version.Level III[25] Long-term data on peg-bone interface stresses remains under investigation.

The "Rocking Horse" Phenomenon

When an aTSA is implanted in a shoulder with residual eccentric wear (B2/B3) or unrecognized cuff insufficiency, the prosthetic head translates to the extreme edge of the polyethylene component. This eccentric force acts as a lever, applying massive torque and toggling the glenoid base on its keel/pegs. Repeated loading destroys the bone-cement interface, causing aseptic loosening—which represents up to 39% of all long-term aTSA complications.[27]

3. Secondary Glenohumeral OA Subtypes

Secondary OA develops downstream from precipitating pathology that irreversibly alters joint kinematics. The three most prevalent etiologies are: Cuff Tear Arthropathy, Instability-Arthropathy, and Post-Traumatic OA.

3.1 Rotator Cuff Tear Arthropathy (CTA) — Hamada Classification

Massive, irreparable rotator cuff tears precipitate a predictable sequence of radiographic degradation. Severity is staged using the Hamada Classification.Level III[32,33]

Table 2 — Hamada Classification of Rotator Cuff Tear Arthropathy
GradeRadiographic CharacteristicsClinical Status
1AHI ≥ 6 mmMassive cuff tear present, kinematics compensated. No bony changes.
2AHI ≤ 5 mmSuperior migration commenced. Increased risk of irreversible subscapularis fatty infiltration.
3AHI ≤ 5 mm + Acetabularization of coracoacromial archChronic superior impingement causes reciprocal remodeling; secondary socket forms.
4aGlenohumeral joint space narrowing, no prominent acetabularizationTrue secondary OA has developed. Complete failure of dynamic stabilizers.
4bGlenohumeral narrowing + prominent acetabularization
5Humeral head osteonecrosis / collapseEnd-stage CTA. Severe architectural distortion requiring salvage arthroplasty.

Treatment Correlated with Hamada Grade

  • Grade 1: Surgical rotator cuff repair or superior capsular reconstruction should ideally be performed while AHI is preserved to prevent irreversible progression.Level III[32]
  • Grade 2+: Once AHI collapses ≤ 5 mm, irreversible fatty degeneration (especially subscapularis) renders anatomic repair biomechanically futile.[32]
  • Grades 3–5: aTSA is absolutely contraindicated. rTSA is the gold standard. The reverse design bypasses the absent rotator cuff, with excellent 10-year survivorship regardless of Hamada grade.Level III[37]

3.2 Instability-Arthropathy — Samilson-Prieto Classification

Recurrent anterior shoulder instability and its surgical management are potent risk factors for secondary OA. Staging uses the Samilson-Prieto classification based on inferior osteophyte size.[41,42]

Table 3 — Samilson-Prieto Classification of Dislocation Arthropathy
GradeOsteophyte SizeClinical Significance
1 (Mild)< 3 mmEarly degenerative changes; often asymptomatic.
2 (Moderate)3–7 mmProgressive chondral loss; stiffness and activity-related pain.
3 (Severe)> 7 mmAdvanced OA; severe pain, profound loss of external rotation.
📊 Landmark Finding — Hovelius Study

Level I The 25-year longitudinal study by Hovelius (JBJS) demonstrated that even a single primary anterior dislocation is associated with Samilson-Prieto osteoarthritis in 56% of patients over 2.5 decades.[44]

Bankart vs. Latarjet — Long-Term Comparison

A Level IV systematic review (40 studies, >3,000 shoulders) compared arthroscopic Bankart vs. open Latarjet:[47]

  • Recurrent instability: Latarjet 0.0%–22.6% vs. Bankart 13.3%–82.2%
  • Total revision rates: Latarjet 0.0%–17.9% vs. Bankart 4.8%–42.9%
  • Radiographic arthritis rates remain high in both: Latarjet 10.3%–71.4% vs. Bankart 16.7%–89.3%

Neither procedure reliably halts progression of instability-arthropathy.Level IV

3.3 Post-Traumatic OA — Boileau Classification

Post-traumatic OA following complex proximal humerus fractures presents an extraordinarily hostile environment for reconstruction, involving chondral loss, soft-tissue contracture, tuberosity malunion/nonunion, and AVN.[50]

Table 4 — Boileau Classification of Proximal Humerus Fracture Sequelae
CategoryDescriptionReconstructive Implications
Type 1Humeral head collapse/AVN, minimal tuberosity malunionCan often be managed with aTSA or hemiarthroplasty.
Type 2Chronic locked dislocations / neglected fracture-dislocationsSevere capsular contracture; managed based on cuff integrity.
Type 3Nonunions of surgical neck (extracapsular)Historically aTSA, but high failure rates have shifted preference to rTSA.
Type 4Severe tuberosity malunions (extracapsular)Most challenging. Tuberosities in non-anatomic positions. rTSA is salvage of choice.
✅ rTSA for Type 4 Sequelae

Level III rTSA circumvents the need for an intact, tensioned rotator cuff. Even if tuberosity osteotomy fails to unite (healing rates 40%–84%), the medialized center of rotation allows reliable forward elevation gains of ~60°. Patient satisfaction exceeds 90%.[55,58] However, active external rotation is rarely fully restored due to irreparable infraspinatus/teres minor disruption.[55]

4. Treatment Algorithms & EBM Guidelines

The modern paradigm has shifted from a rigid "aTSA for OA, rTSA for cuff-deficient" boundary to a nuanced, biomechanically driven decision matrix based on 3D glenoid morphology, retroversion measurements, and soft-tissue balance.

Table 5 — Modern Evidence-Based Treatment Algorithm for Glenohumeral OA
Clinical PathologyIndicationEvidence-Based Rationale
Primary OA: Walch A & B1 aTSA Intact cuff, preserved version. Excellent long-term survivorship, replicates native kinematics.
Primary OA: Walch B2 & B3 rTSA (or aTSA + Augmented Glenoid) Level III Uncorrected retroversion >15° in aTSA guarantees rocking-horse failure. rTSA complication rate 4.4% vs 7.8%; revision 1.6% vs 5.2%.[25]
CTA (Hamada 3–5) rTSA Absolute contraindication for aTSA. rTSA recruits deltoid via medialization/distalization. 10-year survivorship regardless of Hamada grade.[17]
Instability Arthropathy (Samilson-Prieto 3) aTSA (with soft-tissue balancing) Preferred if cuff intact; must address severe anterior capsular contracture to prevent posterior subluxation.
Post-Traumatic (Boileau Type 4) rTSA aTSA relies on tuberosity healing and anatomic cuff tension—irreparably destroyed in Type 4. rTSA restores up to 60° flexion.[58]

📝 Evidence Gap Note

The source document is heavily weighted toward Level III–V evidence. True Level I randomized controlled trials comparing aTSA vs. rTSA head-to-head for specific Walch subtypes are not identified in this source. Most comparative evidence derives from systematic reviews and retrospective cohort studies. Future high-level RCTs would strengthen treatment recommendations.
