1. Introduction & Epidemiology

The management of subaxial cervical spine injuries, particularly facet dislocations, remains one of the most challenging arenas in spinal surgery. The subaxial cervical spine (C3–C7) accounts for 50–65% of all cervical fractures and dislocations.[1] The transition from the lordotic cervical spine to the rigid thoracic spine creates a fulcrum of stress, most frequently at C5–C6 and C6–C7.[1]

Injuries are predominantly from high-energy trauma (motor vehicle accidents, falls from height), though a bimodal distribution exists with lower-energy falls causing significant injury in the elderly population with osteoporotic or ankylosed spines.[4]

Clinical Presentation

  • Unilateral facet dislocation: ~25% neurologically intact, ~37% with radicular deficits, remainder with varying spinal cord injury.[1] Level II
  • Bilateral facet dislocation: Up to 80% present with significant spinal cord injury due to catastrophic failure of the disco-ligamentous complex (DLC).[4] Level II

Treatment Goals

  1. Immediate decompression of neural elements to facilitate recovery
  2. Restoration of spinal alignment to prevent long-term deformity
  3. Rigid stabilization to allow mobilization and rehabilitation[1]
Key Controversy
The choice of surgical approach—anterior, posterior, or combined (360°)—is debated intensely. This decision is heavily influenced by traumatic disc herniation (TDH) and locked facet geometry.[9]

2. Pathophysiology & Biomechanics

2.1 Mechanism of Distractive Flexion

The primary vector is distractive flexion — a hyperflexion moment coupled with distraction that separates the articular surfaces. As translation reaches ~25% of vertebral body width, "perched facets" result. If force continues, the inferior facet overrides completely, dropping into the intervertebral foramen, creating a "locked" facet.[2,4] Level IV

2.2 Ligamentous Disruption

The posterior ligamentous complex (PLC) disruption is the hallmark. In bilateral dislocations, disruption is circumferential (PLC + annulus + PLL + often ALL), creating a grossly unstable "floating vertebra."[4] In unilateral dislocations, asymmetric injury creates rotational instability; the intact contralateral facet acts as a hinge, resisting closed reduction.[1,7] Level IV

2.3 Traumatic Disc Herniation Pathogenesis

TDH involves tearing of the posterior annulus fibrosus and PLL during hyperflexion-distraction. Incidence rates vary from 15% to over 50% depending on imaging modality.[13] The "pincer mechanism" — realignment of vertebral bodies "guillotining" the cord against a fixed disc mass — is the primary risk driving the recommendation for pre-reduction MRI.[12]

Critical: Intradural Disc Herniation
A rare but critical subtype where disc material penetrates the dura. Often missed on standard MRI. Requires anterior surgical exploration with durotomy capability.[20] Level V

3. Classification Systems

3.1 Allen & Ferguson Classification (1982)

Mechanism-based system describing Distractive Flexion (DF) stages 1–4:[4]

  • DF1: Facet subluxation — spinous process divergence, minor kyphosis
  • DF2: Unilateral facet dislocation — ~25% anterior displacement
  • DF3: Bilateral facet dislocation — ~50% displacement ("perched" stage)
  • DF4: Complete dislocation — 100% displacement, "floating" vertebra
Limitation: Does not incorporate neurological status or MRI findings (DLC, disc herniation).

3.2 SLIC System (2007)

Three-parameter scoring:[22] Level II

  1. Injury Morphology: Compression (1), Burst (+1), Distraction (2), Translation/Rotation (3)
  2. DLC Integrity: Intact (0), Indeterminate (1), Disrupted (2)
  3. Neurological Status: Intact (0) → Complete (2) / Incomplete (3)
Clinical Threshold
SLIC > 4 = surgical intervention. Facet dislocations typically score ≥5 (3 morphology + 2 DLC), unequivocally operative.[22] However, SLIC does not guide the specific approach.[24]

3.3 AO Spine Subaxial Classification

The most comprehensive system incorporating facet morphology and case-specific modifiers:[15,16]

  • Type A: Compression injuries
  • Type B: Tension band injuries
  • Type C: Displacement/Translation injuries — facet dislocations[15]

Facet Descriptors (F)

  • F1: Nondisplaced facet fracture
  • F2: Facet fracture with potential instability
  • F3: Floating lateral mass
  • F4: Pathologic subluxation / perched / dislocated facet[15]

Modifiers (Critical for Approach Selection)

  • M1: Posterior capsuloligamentous complex injury without complete disruption
  • M2: Critical disc herniation — disc protruding posterior to vertebral body line. Strongly favors anterior-first approach.[16]
  • M3: Stiffening/Metabolic bone disease (AS, DISH) — alerts to long-segment fixation need.[16]
  • M4: Vertebral artery injury — influences screw trajectory and reduction maneuvers.[15]

4. Diagnostic Evaluation & Pre-Reduction Management

4.1 Imaging Modalities

  • CT: Gold standard for bony anatomy. Essential for F-grade classification and detecting endplate fractures (risk factor for anterior construct failure).[27,29] Level II
  • CTA: Increasingly recommended given vertebral artery proximity. Identifies VAI (M4 modifier).[15] Level III
  • MRI: Only modality for direct spinal cord and disc visualization. Essential for M2 modifier and PLC assessment.[4] Level II

4.2 The "MRI-First" vs. "Reduction-First" Debate

The Central Dilemma
Should MRI be obtained before any reduction attempt (risking cord ischemia from delay) or should closed reduction proceed immediately (risking iatrogenic cord injury from disc compression)?

Case for "MRI-First"

15–50% of facet dislocations have associated TDH.[13] Reduction with disc in canal could "guillotine" the cord. Eismont et al. recommend MRI first; if TDH present, anterior discectomy before reduction.[13] Level III

Case for "Reduction-First" (Early Traction)

"Time is cord." Delaying reduction allows secondary injury cascades. Systematic reviews show permanent neurological deterioration from closed reduction is only 0.5–1%.[31] Awake reduction allows continuous monitoring; successful reduction decompresses the canal.[12] Level II

Current Synthesis (2025 Guidelines)

Stratified Approach
  1. Awake, cooperative patient with deficit: Immediate closed reduction is reasonable. MRI obtained after reduction.[4]
  2. Obtunded/unexaminable patient: MRI must be obtained prior to reduction.[4]
  3. Failed closed reduction: MRI mandatory before open surgery.[4]

4.3 Closed Reduction Protocol

  • Initial weight: 5–10 lbs (2.3–4.5 kg)
  • Increment: +5–10 lbs every 10–15 minutes with serial lateral X-rays and neuro exams
  • Maximum: Up to 140 lbs (63.5 kg) described; most reductions at ~40–50 lbs[7]
  • Success rates: Bilateral ~83% vs. Unilateral ~62% (contralateral intact facet acts as mechanical block)[18] Level IV

5. The Role of Traumatic Disc Herniation (TDH)

TDH is the single most significant variable influencing surgical approach selection. Its presence effectively dictates a specific surgical sequence to ensure safety.

5.1 Prevalence & Detection

Reported prevalence ranges from 15% to 54% depending on imaging modality and definition.[13] In AO Spine classification, clinically significant herniations are designated as the M2 modifier.[16] It is critical to distinguish small contained protrusions (may resorb) from large extruded fragments (immediate threat).[12]

5.2 Intradural Disc Herniation

In high-energy trauma, disc material can penetrate the dura mater. This is notoriously difficult to diagnose on standard MRI, often mimicking epidural hematoma.[20] The surgical implication is profound: a posterior-only approach cannot visualize or remove the intradural fragment. Anterior exploration with durotomy capability is required.[20] Level V

5.3 Management Strategy for M2 Injuries

Non-Negotiable Safety Standard
When M2 modifier (critical TDH) is identified, the algorithm shifts to an Anterior-First strategy. The anterior approach allows discectomy before any reduction maneuver, eliminating the "anvil" against which the cord could be crushed.[9] Posterior-only reduction with a compressive disc is contraindicated.[10]

6. Surgical Approaches

6.1 Anterior Approach (ACDF/ACCF)

Indications

  • Presence of TDH (M2)[10]
  • Most unilateral and bilateral dislocations (reducible or irreducible)[9]
  • Supine positioning required (polytrauma)[9]

Technique

Smith-Robinson approach → complete discectomy → Caspar pins for distraction to disengage locked facets → interbody spacer + anterior cervical plate.[2]

Outcomes

Lower postoperative pain, less blood loss, shorter hospital stays. Restores cervical lordosis reliably.[14] Complications: dysphagia (up to 30% transient), recurrent laryngeal nerve palsy, esophageal injury.[37] Biomechanically weaker for shear forces in DLC-disrupted spines — risk of late kyphosis, particularly with endplate fractures.[29] Level II

6.2 Posterior Approach (PCF)

Indications

  • Irreducible dislocations failing anterior attempts[39]
  • Absence of TDH (M2 negative)[8]
  • Osteoporotic bone requiring multiple fixation points[1]

Technique

Prone positioning with Mayfield fixation → facet exposure → reduction via elevator leverage/towel-clip traction → partial facetectomy if needed → lateral mass screws (C3–C6) or pedicle screws (C7).[7,18]

Outcomes

Superior biomechanical stability (tension band reconstruction).[8] Higher infection rates (~4–10% vs. <1% anterior), greater blood loss, significant postoperative muscle pain.[14] Prone positioning risks.[41] Level II

6.3 Combined (360°) Approach

Indications

  • Ankylosing Spondylitis (M3): Long lever arms fracture plates or pull out screws in isolation[6]
  • Irreducible dislocations with TDH: Anterior → Posterior → Anterior sequence[39]
  • Severe instability (Type C): Comminuted vertebral bodies or endplate fractures[29]

Sequencing

  • AP (most common): Anterior discectomy/cage → flip → posterior instrumentation[43]
  • PA: Posterior reduction → anterior support (no disc herniation, anterior column deficient)[43]
  • APA: TDH exists + anterior reduction fails → anterior release → flip → posterior reduction/fixation → flip → anterior graft/plate[39]

Key Inflection Points
Inflection Point 1: Endplate Fractures
If vertebral body endplate is fractured, anterior-only constructs are prone to failure (screws lack purchase). Recommendation: Combined Fixation or Posterior Fixation.[29,45]
Inflection Point 2: Chronicity (>3 weeks)
Soft tissue fibrosis prevents anterior reduction. Recommendation: Combined Approach (Posterior release → Anterior reduction/fusion → Posterior fixation).[9,39]
Inflection Point 3: Ankylosing Spondylitis (M3)
Strict rule: Always Long-Segment Posterior or Combined Fixation. Short-segment ACDF has unacceptably high failure rate.[6]

8. Special Populations

8.1 Ankylosing Spondylitis (AS) & DISH (M3)

The fused "bamboo spine" fractures like a long bone, creating massive lever arms. Standard short-segment fixation fails because stresses pull screws from osteoporotic bone.[48] Standard of care: Long-Segment Posterior Fixation (3 above + 3 below) or Combined 360°.[6] Level III

8.2 "Old" or Neglected Dislocations (>3 weeks)

Fibrosis and callus formation render closed reduction impossible by 3 weeks.[9] Requires Combined Approach:

  1. Posterior stage: Osteotomy/facetectomy to release fibrosed joints
  2. Anterior stage: Discectomy and cage/plate for column height and lordosis
  3. Posterior completion: Instrumentation to lock reduction[39]
Level IV

8.3 Intradural Disc Herniations

Suspect in high-velocity injuries with disproportionate neurological deficit. Anterior Approach only — allows durotomy and exploration. Surgeons must be prepared for CSF leaks.[20,21] Level V

9. Outcomes & Prognosis

Systematic reviews indicate that while perioperative metrics differ, long-term neurological outcomes are generally equivalent between approaches, provided decompression is achieved.[14,36] Level II

  • Neurological recovery: No significant difference in ASIA score improvement between anterior and posterior groups. Recovery driven by injury severity and timing of decompression.[36]
  • Pain & QoL: Anterior surgery scores better on VAS for neck pain and NDI long-term (muscle-sparing dissection).[14]
  • Kyphosis: Anterior surgery superior for restoring/maintaining lordosis. Posterior surgery risks postoperative kyphosis without careful rod contouring.[8]
  • Fusion rates: Both approaches >90%. Construct failure (screw pullout, graft subsidence) slightly more common in anterior-only bilateral dislocations.[29]

10. Conclusion

The management of subaxial cervical spine dislocation is a complex, algorithmic process. Current evidence supports a tailored strategy where Traumatic Disc Herniation (TDH) acts as the primary fork in the decision tree:

  1. Safety First: Critical disc herniation (M2) mandates an Anterior-First approach to prevent iatrogenic cord injury.[9,10]
  2. Anterior as Default: For most reducible injuries, ACDF offers optimal balance of decompression, fusion, and low morbidity.[14]
  3. Posterior as Problem Solver: Indispensable for irreducible dislocations, osteoporotic spines, and tension-band reconstruction.[8]
  4. Combined for Complex: Definitive solution for AS, endplate fractures, or neglected chronology.[6,29]
