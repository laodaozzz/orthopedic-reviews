Introduction & Purpose

The transition from supervised trainee to independent on-call decision-maker is one of the most critical phases in an orthopedic resident's maturation. Emergencies involving the distal extremities—specifically the hand and the foot—are particularly unforgiving. Mechanisms of injury in these regions frequently result in concurrent damage to bone, tendon, nerve, and the surrounding microvascular soft-tissue envelope. Any delay in diagnosis or deviation from evidence-based management can lead to catastrophic outcomes, including severe functional deficits, chronic osteomyelitis, or limb amputation. Level I

This document synthesizes contemporary evidence-based guidelines from the American Academy of Orthopaedic Surgeons (AAOS), the American Society for Surgery of the Hand (ASSH), and the AO Surgery Reference, combined with curated multimedia learning resources from VuMedi, Orthobullets, and the ASSH Hand.e network, to serve as an authoritative preparation guide for the independent orthopedic resident on call. Level I – AAOS/ASSH CPGs

1. Triage Algorithms & System-Based Trauma Decision Making

Before evaluating isolated extremity injuries, the on-call resident must contextualize the patient within the broader framework of Advanced Trauma Life Support (ATLS). Polytraumatized patients require a systemic approach to determine the appropriate timing and extent of orthopedic intervention. The decision to proceed with early total care (ETC) versus damage control orthopedics (DCO) relies on an accurate assessment of the patient's physiological reserve and the severity of combined injuries. Level III

Scoring Systems for Triage Decisions

  • Injury Severity Score (ISS): The foundational anatomic scoring system, calculated as the sum of the squares of the highest AIS grades in the three most severely injured body regions. An ISS > 40 (or > 15 with concurrent severe thoracic or head trauma) strongly indicates a physiological state prone to the "second hit" phenomenon, directing the surgeon toward damage control interventions. Level III
  • New Injury Severity Score (NISS): Refines ISS by utilizing the three highest AIS scores regardless of anatomical region, providing a highly predictive metric for systemic complications and mortality. Level III
  • Mangled Extremity Severity Score (MESS): Predicts the necessity of primary amputation following devastating lower extremity trauma, factoring in skeletal/soft-tissue injury, limb ischemia, shock, and patient age. Level III
ASSH/ACEP Joint Guidelines
The ASSH and ACEP jointly developed comprehensive Guidelines for Hand Surgery Consultation and Transfer to standardize triage, mitigate risks of delayed care, and optimize allocation of specialized surgical resources across hospital networks. Not specified in source

2. Management of Open Fractures & Soft-Tissue Disruption

Management of open fractures across both upper and lower extremities is predicated on a race against bacterial proliferation. The primary objective is the prevention of surgical site infections (SSIs), chronic osteomyelitis, and delayed union. Level I – AAOS CPG 2022

Prophylactic Antibiotics

The AAOS 2022 Clinical Practice Guideline recommends that appropriate intravenous antibiotics be initiated immediately upon the patient's arrival at the ED. The regimen is dictated by the Gustilo-Anderson classification: Level I

Table 2. Gustilo-Anderson Classification & Antibiotic Regimens
Type Soft-Tissue Characteristics Fracture Pattern / Energy Antibiotic Regimen
I Clean wound, < 1 cm Simple transverse/short oblique; low energy First-generation cephalosporin (e.g., cefazolin)
II Laceration > 1 cm without extensive soft-tissue damage Simple/moderate comminution; moderate energy First-generation cephalosporin
IIIA Extensive laceration with adequate bone coverage Highly comminuted/segmental; high energy Cephalosporin + aminoglycoside
IIIB Extensive soft-tissue loss with periosteal stripping; requires flap Highly comminuted; massive high-energy trauma Cephalosporin + aminoglycoside
IIIC Associated arterial injury requiring microvascular repair Any pattern with vascular compromise Cephalosporin + aminoglycoside (+ penicillin if agricultural)
Antibiotic Duration Caution
The AAOS guidelines note that indiscriminate or prolonged use of broad-spectrum agents beyond the recommended 72-hour window increases adverse events and drives emergence of multidrug-resistant pathogens without additional prophylactic benefit. Level I

The resident must also be familiar with the Tscherne classification, which grades closed soft-tissue injuries, predicting compartment syndrome and wound breakdown risk even without open dermal breach. Not specified in source

AO Principles of Surgical Debridement & Fixation

Systemic antibiotics cannot penetrate avascular, necrotic tissue. Early and radical surgical debridement is the cornerstone of open fracture management. Level I – AO Surgery Reference

  • Timing: Thorough debridement within 24 hours is acceptable for most Type I and II injuries. For severe Type III injuries (especially Type IIIB open tibial fractures), emergent debridement within 12 hours is strongly advocated. Level III
  • Irrigation: High-volume, low-pressure irrigation with normal saline or Ringer's lactate. High-pressure pulsatile lavage is generally discouraged, as it drives contaminants deeper and damages microcirculation. Level I
  • Muscle Viability (4 C's): Color, consistency, capacity to bleed, contractility. Failed tissue must be radically excised. Not specified in source
  • Fixation Strategy: Lower-grade injuries → immediate internal fixation (IMN for tibiae, volar plates for distal radius). Severe Type IIIB/IIIC → temporizing modular external fixator. Level I
  • Wound Closure: Avoid closing contaminated wounds under tension. Use temporary open wound management (sterile dressings or NPWT). Mandatory "second look" at 48–72 hours. Level I
Critical Principle
A wound closed tightly over a traumatized, edematous extremity is almost certain to become infected and undergo marginal necrosis. Level I – AO Surgery Reference

3. Acute Hand Emergencies: Suppurative Infections

3a. Pyogenic Flexor Tenosynovitis (PFT)

PFT is a fulminant bacterial infection within the closed synovial space of the digital flexor tendon sheath. The synovial fluid acts as an ideal culture medium, and rising hydrostatic pressure compromises epitenon microcirculation, risking rapid devitalization of FDS and FDP tendons. Not specified in source

Kanavel Signs (sensitivity > 90%): Level III

  1. Fusiform swelling — symmetric "sausage-like" digit swelling
  2. Flexed posture — involuntary resting flexion to maximize sheath volume
  3. Tenderness over the flexor sheath — exquisite tenderness from DIP to A1 pulley
  4. Pain on passive extension — the most sensitive and earliest indicator

Anatomical Spread: The thumb (radial bursa) and small finger (ulnar bursa) sheaths communicate via the Space of Parona. An unchecked infection can create a devastating "horseshoe abscess." Not specified in source

Management Algorithm

  • Early presentation (<24–48h), equivocal diagnosis: Nonoperative trial — admission, elevation, splinting, IV antibiotics (vancomycin + piperacillin-tazobactam or 3rd-gen cephalosporin). Re-evaluate within 12–24 hours. Not specified in source
  • Established purulence or late presentation: Emergent surgical decompression — closed sheath irrigation via proximal (A1 pulley) and distal (A5 pulley) incisions. Not specified in source
  • Severe/necrotic cases: Full open Bruner incision with radical debridement. Not specified in source

3b. Human Bite Wounds ("Fight Bites")

Fight bites are sustained when a clenched fist strikes incisor teeth. The mechanism is deceptively catastrophic: when the fist is clenched, the tooth penetrates skin and extensor tendon, breaching the MCP dorsal capsule and inoculating the joint with oral flora. Upon finger extension, the tendon and skin glide proximally, sealing bacteria deep within the joint space. Not specified in source

Clinical Axiom
Any small, irregular, transverse laceration overlying the dorsal 3rd or 4th MCP joint must be treated explicitly as a fight bite until proven otherwise. Not specified in source
  • Key pathogen: Eikenella corrodens (present in up to 29% of human bites; uniquely destructive to articular cartilage) Not specified in source
  • Management: Primary closure is strictly forbidden. Obtain radiographs for fractures/foreign bodies. Formal surgical exploration, irrigation, and debridement. Wound left open. Not specified in source
  • Antibiotics: IV ampicillin-sulbactam → oral amoxicillin-clavulanate. Not specified in source

3c. Paronychia, Felons & Herpetic Whitlow

Herpetic whitlow must never be surgically incised — this can induce severe bacterial superinfection and systemic viral dissemination. Not specified in source

4. High-Pressure Injection Injuries

These injuries represent one of the most deceptive and devastating orthopedic emergencies. The classic patient is a young industrial worker with a seemingly trivial pinpoint puncture wound to the non-dominant index finger from a paint sprayer, grease gun, or hydraulic line. Not specified in source

Pathophysiology

Industrial equipment delivers substances at velocities approaching 400 mph, generating forces of 3,000–10,000 PSI. Since only 100 PSI breaches human dermis, the injected fluid acts as a violent internal projectile — dissecting along fascial planes, infiltrating tendon sheaths, and inducing acute compartment syndrome with immediate ischemia. Not specified in source

Table 4. Prognostic Stratification by Injected Substance
Substance Chemical Effect Prognosis
Industrial solvents, oil-based paints, diesel Dissolve cellular lipid membranes → liquefactive necrosis Grim — amputation in ~50% of cases
Water-based paints, grease, veterinary vaccines Less chemically destructive; primarily mechanical compartment syndrome Better, though still severe
Absolute Contraindication
Do NOT administer digital blocks. The injection introduces additional fluid volume into an already compromised compartment, while epinephrine induces further vasospasm — guaranteeing irreversible ischemic necrosis. Not specified in source

Management

  • Immediate, emergent surgical exploration for all cases (except pure air/water) Not specified in source
  • Time critical: Delays beyond 6–10 hours correlate exponentially with higher amputation rates Not specified in source
  • Extensive Bruner or midaxial incisions for full exposure; radical debridement; flexor sheaths completely incised Not specified in source
  • Wounds left open; mandatory "second look" at 48–72 hours Not specified in source
  • Average time to physician evaluation approaches 9 hours — maintain high index of suspicion Not specified in source

5. Flexor Tendon & Peripheral Nerve Lacerations

5a. Flexor Tendon Injuries

Flexor tendon injuries demand prompt surgical intervention, ideally within 7–14 days. Delays beyond 3 weeks result in myostatic contracture and sheath obliteration, necessitating complex multi-stage reconstructions. Not specified in source

Table 5. Flexor Tendon Zones of Injury
Zone Anatomical Boundaries Characteristics & Strategy
I Distal to FDS insertion Isolated FDP lacerations (e.g., Jersey finger). Direct repair or bony reinsertion.
II ("No Man's Land") A1 pulley to FDS insertion FDS and FDP within tight sheath. High adhesion risk. Requires 4–6 strand core repair, epitendinous suture, early active motion.
III Distal carpal ligament to A1 pulley Palmar zone. Better vascularity; fewer restrictive pulleys. Excellent outcomes.
IV Within the carpal tunnel Multiple tendons packed closely. High cross-adhesion risk. Transverse carpal ligament may need step-cut lengthening.
V Proximal to carpal tunnel Forearm injuries; often involves musculotendinous junctions and concurrent major nerve lacerations.

Healing Biology & Biomechanics

Tendon healing progresses through three phases:

  1. Inflammatory phase (days 0–5): No intrinsic strength; repair relies entirely on suture material
  2. Fibroblastic phase (days 5–28): Robust but disorganized collagen; repair remains highly vulnerable
  3. Remodeling phase (>28 days): Functional stress induces linear collagen realignment

Modern repair standards:

  • 4–6 strand core suture (non-absorbable braided, 3-0 or 4-0) — biomechanically proven to tolerate early active motion Not specified in source
  • Continuous epitendinous suture — increases tensile strength by up to 50%, reduces cross-sectional area and friction against pulleys Not specified in source
  • Pulley venting: Up to 25% of A2 and entirety of A4 can be vented with minimal functional deficit Not specified in source
  • WALANT technique: Enables intraoperative active motion testing — real-time gap detection and gliding assessment Not specified in source

Rehabilitation: Early active motion (EAM) protocols are now standard, providing statistically superior ROM outcomes by minimizing dense peritendinous adhesions compared to historical Kleinert and Duran passive regimens. Level III

5b. Peripheral Nerve Lacerations

Digital nerve lacerations require precise, tension-free microsurgical repair within 7–14 days for optimal functional recovery. Not specified in source

Critical principles:

  • Tension-free coaptation is absolute: 8% nerve elongation reduces intraneural perfusion by 50%; 15% stretch → complete ischemia Not specified in source
  • Nerve gaps <20 mm: Synthetic/collagen conduits are effective Not specified in source
  • Nerve gaps 20–70 mm: Acellular decellularized nerve allografts Not specified in source
  • Gaps >5 cm or poor vascularity: Autogenous nerve graft (sural, medial antebrachial cutaneous) — gold standard Not specified in source
  • Regeneration rate: ~1 mm/day. Track with advancing Tinel's sign. Not specified in source

Sensory recovery sequence: Sympathetic tone (sweating) → deep pain/temperature → light touch → static two-point discrimination. Not specified in source

6. Acute Foot & Ankle Emergencies

6a. Acute Foot Compartment Syndrome

Typically results from high-energy crush injuries, severe calcaneal fractures, or Lisfranc fracture-dislocations. Rising intracompartmental pressure surpasses capillary perfusion pressure, causing irreversible myoneural ischemia within hours. Not specified in source

The foot has 9 fascial compartments:

  1. Medial (abductor hallucis, flexor hallucis brevis)
  2. Lateral (abductor digiti minimi, flexor digiti minimi brevis)
  3. Superficial central (flexor digitorum brevis)
  4. Deep central / adductor (adductor hallucis, posterior tibial neurovascular bundle)
  5. Calcaneal (quadratus plantae)
  6. Interosseous × 4 (between metatarsals)
Diagnostic Pearl
The presence of palpable pedal pulses does NOT exclude compartment syndrome. Major arterial pressure remains far above capillary occlusion threshold — tissue dies while the macroscopic pulse remains bounding. Not specified in source

Most reliable exam finding: Extreme pain on passive MTP dorsiflexion (stretches ischemic intrinsic muscles). Not specified in source

Manometry: A delta pressure (diastolic BP minus compartment pressure) of < 20–30 mmHg is an absolute indication for emergent fasciotomy. The calcaneal compartment is the best "indicator compartment." Level III

Surgical decompression: Dual dorsal longitudinal incisions:

  • Dorsomedial (medial to 2nd MT) → releases medial, deep central, 1st & 2nd interosseous
  • Dorsolateral (lateral to 4th MT) → releases lateral, superficial/middle central, 3rd & 4th interosseous
  • Additional medial arch incision if calcaneal compartment remains tight Not specified in source

6b. Necrotizing Fasciitis

A highly lethal, rapidly progressive infection characterized by fulminant destruction of fascial planes, causing widespread microvascular thrombosis, skin gangrene, and muscle necrosis. Not specified in source

Table 6. Necrotizing Fasciitis Classification
Type Microbiology Patient Profile
Type 1 (Polymicrobial) Aerobes + anaerobes (incl. Clostridia) + Enterobacteria Immunocompromised: diabetes, obesity, malignancy
Type 2 (Monomicrobial) Group A beta-hemolytic Streptococcus Can strike young, healthy individuals after minimal trauma
  • Hallmark: Severe pain out of proportion to visible pathology Not specified in source
  • LRINEC score assists risk stratification (CRP, WBC, Hb, Na, Cr, glucose) Not specified in source
  • Only life-saving intervention: Emergent, radical surgical debridement + broad-spectrum IV antibiotics (vancomycin + pip-tazo + clindamycin) Not specified in source

6c. Septic Arthritis

A rapidly destructive emergency — bacterial toxins and host enzymes can irreversibly degrade articular cartilage within hours. The ankle is among the most frequently affected joints in adults. Not specified in source

  • Diagnostic criterion: Arthrocentesis showing WBC > 50,000 cells/mm³ is definitively diagnostic (but lower counts do not exclude pathology) Not specified in source
  • Treatment: Urgent surgical irrigation (open or arthroscopic) + culture-directed IV antibiotics + early protected ROM Not specified in source

6d. Open Calcaneus Fractures

In high-energy axial loading, the talus acts as a wedge splitting the calcaneus. Most open fractures manifest medially — the sharp medial cortex lacerates skin from the inside out, often exposing the posterior tibial neurovascular bundle. Not specified in source

Management strategy (temporize → delay → definitive):

  1. Immediate: IV antibiotics, tetanus, OR for irrigation/debridement. Temporize with percutaneous K-wires or external fixator. Place antibiotic bead pouch. Not specified in source
  2. Monitor for the "wrinkle sign" (return of normal skin creasing as edema subsides, typically 10–14 days) — indicates safe skin perfusion for surgical incisions. Not specified in source
  3. Definitive ORIF using CT-based preoperative planning (extended lateral or sinus tarsi approach). Not specified in source

6e. Lisfranc Fracture-Dislocations

The tarsometatarsal (TMT) articulation forms the anatomical keystone maintaining the transverse arch of the midfoot. The Lisfranc ligament (connecting medial cuneiform to 2nd metatarsal base) is the critical stabilizer. Its rupture allows 2nd–5th MTs to displace dorsolaterally. Not specified in source

Diagnostic pearls:

  • Plantar ecchymosis in midfoot arch = pathognomonic Not specified in source
  • Weight-bearing radiographs are essential — look for >2mm diastasis between 1st/2nd MT bases, disrupted colinearity, and the "fleck sign" (avulsed Lisfranc ligament insertion) Not specified in source
  • If equivocal → CT or MRI mandated Not specified in source

Management:

  • Stable injuries (<2mm diastasis): Non-weight-bearing cast 6–8 weeks + rigid orthotics Not specified in source
  • Unstable injuries: Operative. In acute soft-tissue swelling → temporize with percutaneous K-wires, delay definitive surgery 2–3 weeks. Not specified in source
  • ORIF vs. primary arthrodesis: ORIF for bony fracture-dislocations. Level 1 evidence shows primary fusion provides equivalent or superior outcomes for severe comminution or purely ligamentous disruptions, reducing chronic pain and secondary salvage operations. Level I

8. Integration into On-Call Workflow

The successful management of an independent on-call shift requires synthesis of multiple evidence-based tools:

1
Triage: Apply ASSH/ACEP Guidelines to determine immediate consultation vs. transfer vs. safe discharge with outpatient follow-up. Not specified in source
2
Polytrauma Context: Calculate ISS/NISS to determine ETC vs. DCO strategy. Apply MESS for mangled extremities. Level III
3
Open Fractures: Follow AAOS CPG for immediate antibiotics. Classify by Gustilo-Anderson. Debride within 12–24h. Temporize severe injuries with external fixation. Level I
4
Intraoperative Execution: Use AO Surgery Reference for step-by-step surgical approach, classification, reduction technique, and fixation methodology. Not specified in source
5
Multimedia Preparation: Review curated VuMedi/Orthobullets videos for the specific procedure immediately prior to scrubbing. Not specified in source
