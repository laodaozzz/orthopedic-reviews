1. Executive Summary

Lisfranc injuries with concomitant distal metatarsal fractures represent a severe, high-energy traumatic pattern, often resulting in a so-called "floating metatarsal." This injury complex is characterised by exceptional instability at both the tarsometatarsal (TMT) joint and the metatarsal shaft. Level II

⚠️ Key Clinical Principle This injury pattern is an absolute indication for surgical management. Non-operative approaches are associated with high failure rates (conversion to surgery up to 56%[27]) and poor outcomes for displaced injuries. Non-operative management is contraindicated for the "floating metatarsal" pattern.

The diagnostic algorithm for high-energy injuries relies on initial non-weight-bearing (NWB) radiographs and, critically, on computed tomography (CT) for pre-operative planning. CT is essential to define articular comminution and associated fractures, which informs the surgical strategy. Level II

The primary prognostic factor, irrespective of management technique, is the achievement of an anatomic reduction of both the TMT joint and the distal metatarsal fractures to restore the biomechanical arches of the foot. Level III

The central debate in management—Open Reduction and Internal Fixation (ORIF) versus Primary Arthrodesis (PA)—is increasingly resolved in favour of PA for complex, high-energy, or purely ligamentous injuries. A substantial body of evidence, including multiple meta-analyses, demonstrates that PA is associated with: Level II

  • Superior functional outcomes (AOFAS scores: 84.4 vs 75.7)[46]
  • Lower postoperative pain (VAS scores: 1.4 vs 2.0)[46]
  • Significantly lower re-operation rates (17.6% vs 64.1%)[46]

For the specific "floating metatarsal" pattern, a long dorsal plate construct achieving arthrodesis of the TMT joint while simultaneously bridging and fixing the distal metatarsal fracture presents the most biomechanically sound solution. Level III

Long-term outcomes are guarded due to the high-energy nature of the injury. Post-traumatic osteoarthritis (PTOA) is a common, almost inevitable sequela, even with successful surgery. The goal of management is therefore to delay symptomatic arthritis by achieving a stable, plantigrade foot.

2. Pathoanatomy & Biomechanics

2.1 The Lisfranc Joint Complex

The term "Lisfranc joint" is a misnomer; it is not a single articulation but a complex, polyarticular system[1] including the tarsometatarsal (TMT), intermetatarsal, and intertarsal joints.[3] Stability derives from two primary sources: Level V

Osseous Architecture: The Keystone Configuration

The intrinsic bony stability is defined by the "keystone configuration" of the second metatarsal (M2). The base of M2 is recessed into a mortise formed by the medial (C1) and middle (C2) cuneiforms, locking the TMT joint in place.[1] The foot is conceptualised into three columns[3]:

  • Medial column: C1 / M1
  • Middle column: C2–C3 / M2–M3
  • Lateral column: Cuboid / M4–M5 (most mobile)

Ligamentous Stabilizers

The bony architecture is reinforced by a hierarchy of ligaments[3]:

  • Dorsal TMT ligaments — the weakest[2]
  • Plantar TMT ligaments — substantially stronger; their integrity is critical[3]
  • Interosseous ligaments — strongest, most notably the Lisfranc ligament proper (connects C1 to M2 base on its plantar aspect)[3]
🔑 Critical Anatomical Vulnerability There is no transverse ligament connecting the first and second metatarsals.[4] Consequently, the entire stability between the medial and middle columns is dependent upon the Lisfranc ligament complex.[3] This explains why a failure of this single complex leads to catastrophic destabilization of the midfoot.

2.2 Mechanism of Injury: The "Floating Metatarsal" Pattern

While low-energy Lisfranc injuries (sprains) can occur in athletes, the pattern described—a Lisfranc dislocation with a concomitant distal metatarsal fracture—is the hallmark of a high-energy mechanism (motor vehicle collisions, falls from height, crush injuries).[1] Level V

The common injury cascade involves[9]:

  1. Severe axial load applied to a hyper-plantarflexed foot
  2. Tension failure of the weak dorsal ligaments
  3. Rupture of stronger plantar structures or bony avulsion of the M2 base ("fleck sign")[8]
  4. Dorsal displacement of the metatarsals
  5. Force propagation along the metatarsal shaft, causing distal failure

The "floating metatarsal"[11] is a rare and exceptionally unstable injury in which a metatarsal is dislocated at both its proximal (TMT) and distal ends.[12] The biomechanical consequence is the complete loss of longitudinal stability for that ray, leading to collapse of the transverse arch[3] and failure of the forefoot to act as a rigid lever during gait.[15]

3. Diagnostic Algorithm

A high index of suspicion is paramount, as 20% to 50% of all Lisfranc injuries are missed upon initial presentation.[5] Level II

3.1 Clinical Evaluation

History

High-energy trauma (MVC, fall from height) should immediately raise suspicion.[7]

Physical Examination

  • Inspection: Diffuse swelling across the midfoot[3]; the "plantar ecchymosis sign" (bruising in the sole of the midfoot) is highly peculiar and almost pathognomonic.[3] Level III
  • Palpation: Point tenderness along the TMT joints[3]
  • Provocative Tests: Passive pronation-abduction test and midfoot instability test[3]
  • Associated Injuries: A thorough neurovascular exam is mandatory due to the significant risk of acute compartment syndrome.[15]

3.2 Radiographic Assessment

A structured imaging algorithm is required[7]:

Non-Weight-Bearing (NWB) Radiographs — First-Line

Key findings indicating instability[3]:

  • C1-M2 Diastasis: Any widening > 2 mm between the medial cuneiform and M2 base
  • "Fleck Sign": A small avulsion fracture in the C1-M2 space (avulsion of the Lisfranc ligament)
  • Alignment Disruption (AP/Oblique): Break in the continuous line from the medial aspect of M2 to C2, or M4 to cuboid
  • Alignment Disruption (Lateral): Dorsal displacement of the metatarsal bases relative to tarsal bones

Weight-Bearing (WB) Radiographs

Highly context-dependent. For subtle, low-energy injuries with inconclusive NWB films, WB radiographs are "vital"[6] and "more reliable"[25] for unmasking occult instability. In surgically confirmed unstable injuries, 50–96% of NWB films may appear normal.[25] However, for the high-energy "floating metatarsal" injury, the instability is typically obvious on NWB films and the patient is often unable to bear weight, rendering WB films both impractical and diagnostically redundant.

Computed Tomography (CT) — Essential for Pre-operative Planning

CT is the standard for pre-operative planning in high-energy injuries.[3] It is far superior to plain films for identifying non-displaced fractures, the degree of articular comminution, and the full extent of bony involvement.[5] The degree of comminution is a primary factor in the decision between ORIF and primary arthrodesis.[19] Level II

Magnetic Resonance Imaging (MRI) — Gold Standard for Ligaments

MRI is the gold standard for evaluating soft tissue and ligamentous integrity.[19] It has shown 100% accuracy in detecting Lisfranc ligament tears[30] and can identify disruption of the strong plantar C1-M2/M3 ligaments, which is highly predictive of instability.[5] It characterizes the injury as bony vs. purely ligamentous, which informs the ORIF/PA decision.[28] Level II

4. Classification Systems

Several classification systems are used, though their clinical utility varies. There is a growing consensus that traditional classifications are primarily descriptive and offer "zero prognostic value"[35]. The most clinically relevant classification has become a pragmatic binary: "stable" versus "unstable."[19] Level III

4.1 Hardcastle / Myerson Classification

Describes the pattern of TMT dislocation[6]:

  • Type A: Total incongruity — all five metatarsals displaced as a single unit (homolateral)[7]
  • Type B: Partial incongruity — one or more, but not all, articulations remain intact[21] (subtypes B1 medial, B2 lateral)
  • Type C: Divergent displacement — M1 medial, lesser metatarsals lateral[21] (subtypes C1 partial, C2 total)

4.2 Nunley and Vertullo Staging

Used specifically for subtle, low-energy sprains in athletes[19]:

  • Stage I: Lisfranc ligament sprain with no diastasis → stable, non-operative[23]
  • Stage II: Lisfranc ligament rupture with diastasis (> 2 mm) → unstable, requires surgery[19]
  • Stage III: Stage II findings + arch collapse → unstable, requires surgery[19]

4.3 Lawrence and Botte Classification (5th Metatarsal)

Crucial when a fifth metatarsal fracture is present[33]:

  • Zone 1: Tuberosity avulsion fracture (good prognosis)
  • Zone 2: Jones fracture (metaphyseal-diaphyseal junction; watershed blood supply, high nonunion risk)[34]
  • Zone 3: Proximal diaphyseal stress fracture (high nonunion risk)

5. Management Strategies

5.1 Non-Operative Management

Reserved for a very small and select subset[6]:

Patient Selection

  • Stable Injuries: Truly stable, non-displaced (< 2 mm on NWB CT)[27] — corresponds to Nunley–Vertullo Stage I[23]
  • High-Risk Patients: Non-ambulatory or severe comorbidities precluding surgery[3]

Protocol

  1. Strict non-weight-bearing (NWB) immobilization in a cast/boot for 6–8 weeks[3]
  2. Progressive weight-bearing in a boot or with an orthotic for 4–6 weeks[7]
  3. Close radiographic follow-up to detect late displacement[7]
🚫 Contraindication for the "Floating Metatarsal" The high-energy Lisfranc dislocation with a concomitant distal metatarsal fracture is, by definition, unstable, displaced, and involves multiple fractures. Non-operative management is contraindicated. Systematic reviews report conversion-to-surgery rates up to 56%[27], and 54% of non-operatively treated patients developed late displacement requiring surgery.[40] Level II

5.2 Operative Management — The Standard of Care

Indications for Surgical Intervention

Surgical Timing and Soft Tissue Management

A staged approach is often necessary for these high-energy injuries[3]: Level III

  1. Stage 1 (Acute): Emergent closed reduction and temporary stabilization with percutaneous K-wires or an external fixator[3]
  2. Stage 2 (Definitive): Delayed definitive fixation (ORIF or PA) performed 10–15 days later, once the "wrinkle sign" indicates soft tissue recovery[19]

Fixation of Concomitant Distal Metatarsal Fractures

The primary goal is anatomic restoration of metatarsal length, alignment, and rotation[41] to preserve the "metatarsal cascade" — the normal parabolic relationship of the metatarsal heads.[15] Standard techniques include lag screws for oblique fractures, or mini-fragment plates and screws for transverse or comminuted fractures.[41]

6. The Central Debate: ORIF vs. Primary Arthrodesis

6.1 Open Reduction and Internal Fixation (ORIF)

Rationale: Traditional approach aiming to achieve anatomic reduction, stabilize with hardware, and preserve TMT joint motion. Often favoured for bony fracture-dislocations.[3] Typically implies a planned second surgery for hardware removal.[10] Level III

Standard Reduction Sequence[22]:

  1. Restore the cuneiform–navicular relationship
  2. Reduce M2 to C2 (the "keystone")
  3. Reduce M2 to C1 (stabilizing the Lisfranc interval)
  4. Sequentially address TMT joints 1, 3, 4, and 5

Fixation Constructs:

  • Transarticular Screws (TAS): Classic "Lisfranc screw" from C1 to M2[20]
  • Dorsal Bridge Plates: Avoids violating the articular surface[28]
  • Lateral Column (M4–M5): Stabilized with temporary, percutaneous K-wires[19]

6.2 Primary Arthrodesis (PA)

Rationale: Developed in response to high rates of PTOA and chronic pain even after anatomic ORIF.[50] Provides a definitive, stable, fused construct in a single surgery.[3] Increasingly favoured for[19]: Level II

  • Purely ligamentous injuries (poor healing potential)
  • Highly comminuted, non-reconstructible articular fractures

Technique: Meticulous removal of all articular cartilage from the involved joints (typically 1st, 2nd, and 3rd TMT joints), followed by plate and/or screw fixation to create a stable construct for fusion.[3] The lateral column (M4–M5) is not fused.[51]

6.3 Comparative Evidence

📊 The "Fix or Fuse" Evidence The debate features conflicting data, but the overall trend favours PA for complex injuries. Multiple systematic reviews and meta-analyses confirm superior AOFAS scores, lower VAS pain scores, and dramatically lower re-operation rates with PA. Critically, one multicenter study found that fusion (PA) resulted in a better outcome than non-fusion (ORIF) in the treatment of "Lisfranc injuries accompanied by multiple metatarsal fractures."[57] Level II

6.4 Construct Choice for the "Floating Metatarsal"

For the "floating" injury (unstable at both the TMT joint and the distal shaft), the surgical construct must address both points of failure. A long dorsal bridge plate, capable of spanning from the cuneiform, achieving arthrodesis at the TMT joint, and continuing distally to bridge and fix the metatarsal shaft fracture, presents the most logical and biomechanically robust solution.[41] Level III

7. Rehabilitation & Functional Recovery

7.1 Postoperative Protocols

Regardless of ORIF or PA, the protocol is stringent[9]:

  • Strict non-weight-bearing in a cast/boot for 6–8 weeks (some surgeons extend to 8–12 weeks)[9,10]
  • Gradual weight-bearing in a removable boot or supportive orthotic[9]

7.2 Patient-Reported Outcome Measures (PROMs)

  • AOFAS Midfoot Score: Most commonly cited[46]
  • VAS – Pain: Widely used to quantify pain[46]
  • PROMIS: Validated, modern tool used in recent studies[45]
  • VAS-FA: Validated foot/ankle-specific score[37]

7.3 Return to Sport (RTS)

⚠️ "Return to Performance with Pain" While most patients (66–80%) can resume their type of sport, a majority will not return to their preinjury level of performance and will have to manage residual pain and functional deficits.[64,66,67]
  • ORIF: 80% RTS rate at average 29.4 weeks; 67% of those who returned experienced residual pain[66]
  • PA: High satisfaction (97%) and high rate of return to high-impact activities; however, 25–34% reported activities were "more difficult"[32,65]
  • Comparative: A systematic review found no significant difference in the likelihood or timing of RTS between ORIF and PA.[69]

The surgical choice is often driven by patient goals. An elite athlete may choose ORIF to preserve theoretical motion, accepting a planned second surgery.[56] A manual labourer[33] or recreational "weekend warrior"[67] may prefer PA as a single, definitive, and more reliably pain-free procedure.[53]

8. Complications & Long-Term Sequelae

8.1 Acute: Compartment Syndrome

The most feared acute complication is compartment syndrome, a surgical emergency.[18] High-energy mechanisms, crush injuries[15], and multiple metatarsal fractures[15] create a high-risk scenario requiring emergent clinical assessment and, if present, four-compartment fasciotomies.[22]

8.2 Primary Long-Term Complication: Post-Traumatic Osteoarthritis (PTOA)

⚠️ PTOA Is Near-Inevitable PTOA is the most common and "devastating" long-term complication[5], directly related to (1) injury severity and (2) quality of reduction.[3,10] It can develop even after successful surgery.[9] For high-energy, comminuted injuries, the development of arthritis is not a risk but a near inevitability.[10] Level III

This realization reframes the surgical debate: the goal of the index surgery is not to prevent arthritis, but to delay its symptomatic onset by creating a perfectly aligned, stable, plantigrade foot.[19] This provides the ultimate rationale for Primary Arthrodesis — it is, in effect, a "salvage" procedure performed as the primary operation.[46]

8.3 Metatarsal-Specific Complications

  • Malunion: Healing with > 10 degrees of sagittal angulation disrupts the metatarsal cascade[15], leading to transfer metatarsalgia and plantar keratoses[41]
  • Nonunion: Known risk, particularly in vascular watershed zones of the proximal 5th metatarsal (Zones 2 & 3)[33] and in 2nd metatarsal fractures[76]

8.4 Other Complications

  • Hardware Failure/Removal: Symptomatic hardware is common after ORIF; hardware removal is the most common re-operation.[45] Functional outcomes may improve after removal.[56]
  • Complex Regional Pain Syndrome (CRPS): A risk following any significant extremity trauma, leading to chronic disability.[18]

9. Expert Synthesis & Key Takeaways

🎯 Summary of Key Evidence-Based Principles
  1. Mandatory Surgical Management: This injury pattern is an absolute surgical indication. Non-operative management is contraindicated.[3,18,41]
  2. Anatomic Reduction is Paramount: The single most critical prognostic factor is anatomic reduction of both the TMT joint and the distal metatarsal fracture.[17]
  3. Evidence Favours Primary Arthrodesis: Multiple SRMAs confirm PA provides superior AOFAS scores[53], lower VAS pain[46], and dramatically lower re-operation rates.[53] Lisfranc injuries with multiple metatarsal fractures have better outcomes with PA.[57]
  4. Long Dorsal Bridge Plate Construct: For the "floating metatarsal," a long dorsal plate spanning from the cuneiform across the TMT arthrodesis and bridging the metatarsal shaft fracture is the most logical construct.[41]
  5. PTOA Is Near-Inevitable: The goal is to delay symptomatic arthritis by achieving a stable, plantigrade foot.[19]
  6. Return to Sport Is Guarded: While most patients resume activity, a majority experience residual pain and functional deficits.[64,66]

Future Directions

  • Lack of high-quality, prospective RCTs comparing ORIF and PA specifically for high-energy, comminuted fracture-dislocations[27]
  • Role of novel fixation (e.g., suture-button dynamic fixation) for TMT joint in complex bony injuries not well understood[78]
  • Utility of weight-bearing CT as a new diagnostic technology not yet evaluated in this population[5]
