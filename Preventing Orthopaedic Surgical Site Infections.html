Evidence-Based Prevention of Surgical Site Infections in Orthopaedic Surgery

Comprehensive perioperative guidelines synthesis (2024–2026) • Interactive clinical tool

1. Introduction & Epidemiological Burden

Surgical site infections (SSIs) remain one of the most severe, clinically devastating, and economically burdensome complications in modern orthopaedic surgery. Despite continuous advancements in minimally invasive techniques, barrier methods, and operating room sterilization protocols, SSIs account for approximately 20 % of all healthcare-associated infections (HAIs).[1] Level I

In the United States, the CDC estimated 110,800 SSIs associated with inpatient surgeries in 2015. SSIs are the most costly HAI type, driving an estimated annual healthcare cost exceeding $3.3 billion.[1]

At the individual level, an orthopaedic SSI extends hospital stay by a mean of 9.7 days and increases direct hospitalization cost by > $20,000 per admission.[1] In the UK, NICE estimates the additional treatment cost at £3,122.86 per patient.[3] Critically, an SSI is associated with a 2- to 11-fold increase in mortality risk, with 75 % of SSI-related deaths directly attributable to the infection.[1]

⚠ Global Disparity In low- and middle-income countries (LMICs), pooled orthopaedic SSI prevalence reaches 12.3 %—substantially higher than high-income settings. In Africa, up to 20 % of women undergoing caesarean sections contract wound infections. Gram-positive cocci, particularly Staphylococcus aureus, account for 56 % of documented infections in these settings.[4,5]

1.1 Pathogenesis: Biofilm Formation on Implants

The pathogenesis of periprosthetic joint infection (PJI) and fracture-related infection (FRI) is intimately linked to biofilm formation. Biofilms are three-dimensional microbial communities encased in a self-produced extracellular polymeric substance (EPS) that tightly adheres to implant surfaces and devitalized bone.[6] Once mature, bacteria enter a metabolically quiescent state, rendering them highly recalcitrant to host immune responses and systemic antimicrobials.[8]

Infection occurs when the cumulative burden of surgical site contamination—whether from endogenous cutaneous flora, air droplets, or contaminated instruments—exceeds the local immune threshold of host tissues.[10]

2. Evolution of Global Clinical Guidelines (2024–2026)

2.1 CDC / NHSN

The CDC, through HICPAC, mandates standardized surveillance via the National Healthcare Safety Network (NHSN) to track SSI prevalence and generate risk-stratified feedback for surgeons.[1] Superficial incisional SSIs must meet criteria within 30 days; for prosthetic implant procedures, deep/organ-space SSIs are reportable for up to 90 days (and up to 1 year in comprehensive registries).[3] Since April 2004, such surveillance has been mandatory for all English NHS Trusts.[14]

2.2 WHO & NICE

Both WHO Global Guidelines and NICE guidelines (subject to 2025/2026 reviews) strongly recommend:[3,18]

  • Against routine hair removal; if necessary, use electric clippers immediately prior to incision—razors are categorically contraindicated.
  • Alcohol-based chlorhexidine (CHG) solutions for intraoperative skin preparation.
  • Highly specific preoperative decolonization protocols for high-risk S. aureus carriers.

2.3 AAOS Clinical Practice Guidelines (2025/2026)

The AAOS issued updated CPGs addressing SSI prevention following major extremity trauma (open fractures, high-energy closed fractures, degloving injuries, Morel-Lavallée lesions, gunshot wounds, crush injuries, blast injuries).[22,23] Key recommendations:[23,24]

  • Avoid delayed systemic antibiotics in open fractures—initial antibiotics serve dual therapeutic/prophylactic roles.
  • Cautious against indiscriminate/prolonged use to prevent multidrug resistance.
  • Options from emerging evidence: multidisciplinary orthoplastic teams, incisional negative-pressure wound therapy, and debated role of hyperbaric oxygen therapy.

2.4 The 2025 International Consensus Meeting (ICM) on Musculoskeletal Infection

🔬 Landmark Event — Istanbul, May 2025 1,205 international delegates addressed 102 General and 30 Biofilm-specific questions via a two-year Delphi process. The 2025 ICM established highly directive language with explicit evidence grading.[6,26]

Key consensus outcomes:

  • 100 % consensus on the existence of an active immune proteome within the PJI microenvironment (featuring IL-6); research priorities directed at T-cell immunity in biofilm persistence.[6,9]
  • Extending prophylactic antibiotics beyond 24 hours offers no measurable SSI reduction and increases adverse events.[11] Level I
  • Recognized increased risk of revision/infection following TKA in patients with prior ipsilateral knee arthroscopy.[26]
  • Standardized minimum 12-week antibiotic regimen after DAIR for acute PJI.[26]

2.5 Specialized Societal Directives

ASRA Pain Medicine (2025): Discontinue indwelling catheters at earliest infection signs; use bio-occlusive dressings ≥ 24 h; CSF analysis remains diagnostic gold standard for post-neuraxial meningitis; monitor CBC, ESR, CRP when SSI suspected.[28]

ECDC: Maintains cross-continental guideline directory including APSIC and HIS directives.[29]

3. Preoperative Optimization & Decolonization

3.1 Host Optimization: Glycemic Control

Surgical stress induces relative insulin resistance and systemic hyperglycemia via cortisol, epinephrine, and glucagon release. Hyperglycemia impairs leukocyte function—diminishing macrophage phagocytosis, neutrophil chemotaxis, and reactive oxygen species generation.[16]

✓ Glycemic Targets Preoperative HbA1c < 8.0 % for elective clearance. Intra-/postoperative blood glucose < 10 mmol/L (~180 mg/dL) via sliding-scale or continuous insulin.[16]

3.2 Smoking Cessation

Smoking induces peripheral tissue hypoxia, microvascular vasoconstriction, and impaired collagen synthesis. Smoking and BMI > 30 kg/m² are highly significant independent SSI risk factors.[30] Complete cessation for minimum 30 days (ideally 4–6 weeks) pre-surgery significantly lowers SSI risk.[3]

3.3 Cutaneous & Nasal Decolonization

Endogenous flora—primarily MSSA and MRSA—are the leading causative pathogens. The anterior nares are the primary reservoir.[5]

Standard targeted regimen:[32]

  • Intranasal mupirocin 2 % ointment twice daily to each naris for 5 days pre-surgery.
  • Daily chlorhexidine gluconate (CHG) body washes.

4. Intraoperative Environmental & Physiological Controls

4.1 Operating Theatre Standards

  • Positive-pressure ventilation relative to corridors; ≥ 20 ACH (≥ 4 fresh air).[16]
  • Air filtered through HEPA or MERV-16 equivalents.[16]
  • Temperature 20–24 °C; relative humidity 20–60 %.[16]
  • Laminar airflow (LAF) may not significantly reduce SSIs vs. optimized positive-pressure when bundles are maintained.[3]
  • Strict traffic control: limit personnel, keep doors closed, limit movement/talking.[16]
  • "Bare below the elbows": remove watches, jewellery, artificial nails; dedicated theatre footwear; hats mandatory.[14]

4.3 Intraoperative Physiological Homeostasis

🌡️ ASA 2025 Systematic Review[17] Active warming to maintain normothermia (core > 36.0 °C) is essential. Even mild hypothermia triggers peripheral vasoconstriction → ↓ tissue O₂ tension → ↓ neutrophil oxidative burst → impaired killing of surgical contaminants.[3]

Optimize fluid resuscitation for normovolemia to maximize tissue perfusion and antibiotic delivery.[37] While blood transfusions may cause mild immunosuppression, necessary products must never be withheld for SSI concerns—the risk of hemorrhagic shock outweighs infection risk.[16]

5.2 Timing & Redosing

  • Complete infusion within 60 minutes before incision; exceptions for vancomycin/fluoroquinolones: within 120 minutes.[3]
  • Too early (> 2 h): 5-fold SSI increase; too late (after incision): 2-fold increase.[3]
  • With pneumatic tourniquets: antibiotics must be fully infused prior to tourniquet inflation.[3]
  • Redosing mandatory if blood loss > 1,500 mL or procedure exceeds two half-lives (cefazolin: redose every 4 hours).[3]

5.3 Postoperative Duration — The 2025 Paradigm Shift

🚫 2025 ICM Consensus[11] Extending prophylactic antibiotics beyond 24 hours postoperatively yields zero measurable SSI reduction and is actively harmful — associated with ↑ C. difficile infection, acute kidney injury, and multidrug-resistant organisms.[11]

A single preoperative dose (appropriately weight-based and correctly timed) is frequently sufficient and non-inferior to 24-hour regimens. AAHKS still permits up to 24 hours.[3]

6. Advanced Intraoperative Wound Lavage & Local Antimicrobials

6.1 Dilute Povidone-Iodine (PVP-I) Irrigation

Standard protocol: 3-minute tissue soak with 0.35–0.5 % PVP-I solution prior to fascial closure, endorsed by WHO, CDC, and ICM 2025.[40] Level II

Paradoxically, diluting 10 % stock to 0.35 % disrupts the polymer matrix, freeing more active molecular I₂. This targets multiple cellular pathways, eradicating planktonic bacteria and early biofilm matrices while demonstrating significantly less cytotoxicity (avoiding chondrotoxicity/osteoblast inhibition of concentrated betadine).[41]

6.2 Citrate-Based Biofilm-Disrupting Irrigation

Citrate solutions chelate calcium and magnesium cations that cross-link biofilm EPS, causing matrix collapse and exposing protected bacteria.[7]

  • Reduces microbial loads by up to 6 logs (planktonic) and 4–8 logs (mature biofilms).[7]
  • Negligible cytotoxicity → "no-rinse" protocol: extended antimicrobial protection for up to 5 hours post-closure.[7]
  • Secondary benefits: reduced swelling, enhanced ROM, faster opioid weaning.[43]

6.3 PHMB/PLX Irrigation

An Italian Delphi consensus highly recommends polyhexanide + poloxamer as adjunct to mechanical debridement for septic revisions. Offers potent in vitro antibiofilm activity with excellent cytocompatibility. Low-pressure irrigation and short exposure times advised.[44]

7. Postoperative Wound Management & Advanced Dressings

7.1 Closed-Incision Negative Pressure Wound Therapy (ciNPWT)

ciNPWT applies open-cell polyurethane foam over the closed incision, sealed with impermeable drape, connected to a vacuum pump at −125 mmHg.[3]

Mechanisms:

  • Mechanical: splints incision, approximates wound edges, prevents micro-dehiscence.[3]
  • Fluid management: removes exudate, prevents dead-space hematoma, eliminates bacterial nutrition media.[3]
  • Cellular: stimulates angiogenesis, ↑ local microvascular perfusion (aiding antibiotic delivery), clears destructive metalloproteinases while concentrating growth factors.[3]

7.2 Silver-Impregnated Dressings

Silver ions disrupt bacterial membranes, impair metabolism, and bind DNA. Clinical data is highly heterogeneous: broad meta-analyses show RR 0.54, but high-quality RCTs in clean arthroplasty show non-significant RR = 0.62 (CI crossing 1.0).[54] Cost: ~$38–40 vs. $18 for standard dressings.[55] Routine use discouraged; reserved for extreme compound risk factors.

7.3 DACC (Dialkylcarbamoyl Chloride) Dressings

DACC acts via purely physical hydrophobic interaction—bacteria irreversibly bind to the DACC-coated mesh and are removed at dressing change.[56] No chemical cytotoxicity, no resistance risk. Aligns with antibiotic stewardship principles—particularly promising for high-risk, hard-to-heal orthopaedic wounds.

8. Care Bundles, Surveillance & Implementation Science

8.1 Synergistic Bundle Effect

Complex bundles containing ≥ 11 elements spanning pre-, intra-, and postoperative phases yield the greatest impact — up to 44 % SSI reduction vs. unbundled care.[57]

Standard bundle checklist elements include:

  • Preoperative: CHG bathing, nasal decolonization, HbA1c optimization, systemic infection screening.
  • Intraoperative: Weight-based antibiotics within 60 min; active warming; positive-pressure + traffic control; alcohol-CHG prep.
  • Postoperative: Glucose < 10 mmol/L; early catheter removal; advanced bio-occlusive or NPWT dressings.[58]

8.3 Surveillance as the Cornerstone

Successful programs require active tracking via culture-based and non-culture-based methods, rigorous risk-factor data collection, and—most critically—transparent, unblinded, peer-compared SSI rate feedback to surgeons. This has been conclusively shown to alter surgical behavior and drive up bundle compliance.[1]

9. Conclusion & Future Directions

The 2024–2026 guideline updates by WHO, CDC, NICE, AAOS, and the transformative 2025 ICM consensus dictate a decisive movement away from broad, prolonged systemic chemical prophylaxis. Practices such as extending postoperative antibiotics beyond 24 hours are definitively obsolete and actively harmful.

The contemporary standard relies on:

  • Meticulous host optimization (glycemic control, smoking cessation).
  • Aggressive preoperative decolonization of endogenous flora.
  • Precise intraoperative physiological homeostasis.
  • Adoption of localized, cytocompatible technologies (dilute PVP-I, citrate irrigants, ciNPWT).

The future lies in implementation science — closing the gap between evidence and bedside application through audit-feedback surveillance systems and absolute compliance with standardized care bundles.
