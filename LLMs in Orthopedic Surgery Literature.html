Executive Summary LLM research in orthopedics is concentrated in four domains: clinical practice (69%), education (18%), scientific research (12%), and management (1%).[2] The evidence reveals a sharp dichotomy: superhuman performance in text-based administrative and knowledge-retrieval tasks, but critical failures in visual interpretation and autonomous clinical reasoning.

Part 1 · Validated Applications in Clinical Practice

The majority of research (69%) has focused on clinical practice applications.[2] Evidence indicates that while LLMs show promise in data synthesis, their utility is highly dependent on the nature of the task — revealing a critical distinction between knowledge retrieval and nuanced clinical reasoning.

1.1 Clinical Decision Support & Diagnostic Accuracy

Validation in Common Fracture Scenarios

A 2025 pilot study provided ChatGPT-4 with clinical data (history, physical examination, imaging interpretations) for 10 common fractures at a level 1 academic trauma center. The AI was tasked with generating an "assessment and plan" as if it were an orthopedic surgeon.[20]

Key Finding — Preliminary CDS Validation ChatGPT-4 synthesized complex, multimodal data into "safe and reasonable plans" that "included the final clinical outcome of the patient scenario" as determined by the hospital's multispecialty trauma conference.[20] Level III

Guideline-Based Therapy Planning

A 2025 comparative study evaluated four LLMs on adherence to the 2015 IDSA practice guidelines for native vertebral osteomyelitis (NVO).[21] When assessed on 13 standardized questions, ChatGPT-4o achieved 100% "Excellent" accuracy in the Clinical Therapy domain, while Gemini achieved 78% "Excellent" rating.

This suggests that for closed-ended, fact-based queries (e.g., "What is the guideline-approved therapy for NVO?"), advanced LLMs can operate with near-perfect accuracy. Level II

Performance in Differential Diagnosis

A large-scale network meta-analysis (NMA) published in 2024 synthesized 168 studies and 3,063 clinical cases.[13] While ChatGPT-4o (SUCRA=0.9207) and ChatGPT-4 (SUCRA=0.8087) demonstrated strong performance on objective questions, human accuracy remained superior for generating the "top 1 diagnosis" and "top 3 diagnosis." Level I (NMA)

Performance Heterogeneity — Critical Barrier A 2024 systematic review found diagnostic accuracy ranged from 55% to 93%, and GPT-4's disease classification accuracy spanned 2% to 100% across studies.[2] This variability makes autonomous deployment clinically unsafe. The literature strongly supports a "copilot" model.

An RCT validated the copilot model: medical professionals using GPT-4-Turbo showed "better performance in specific research" (e.g., identifying hazardous substances) versus a control group using traditional methods.[25] Level I (RCT)

1.2 Adherence to Clinical Practice Guidelines

A 2024 study directly compared ChatGPT (4o) and Gemini (1.5 Flash) on recommendations for carpal tunnel syndrome, distal radius fractures, and glenohumeral joint osteoarthritis against AAOS CPGs.[11] 174 total recommendations were generated.

~40% Discordance Rate with AAOS Guidelines Overall concordance was only 62.1%, meaning nearly 40% of LLM recommendations were discordant with established standards — described as "unacceptably high."[11] Level II

While accuracy differences were not statistically significant, transparency differed dramatically. Gemini cited sources for 39.1% of recommendations versus only 3.5% for ChatGPT (P<0.0001).[11] This finding has profound implications for clinical adoption: even if ChatGPT is marginally more accurate, its "black box" nature makes it less viable as a clinical tool.

1.3 Critical Limitation: Failure in Image & Radiograph Interpretation

⚠ Critical Failure — 0% Accuracy on Glenoid Classification A 2025 study tested multimodal LLMs on simplified diagrams of the Walch glenoid classification — a fundamental task for shoulder arthroplasty planning. Claude 3.5 Sonnet achieved 0% accuracy (0/16). DeepSeek R1 achieved 44% accuracy (7/16). Both deemed "insufficient for clinical applications."[9]

The nature of the failure is clinically dangerous: models misclassified a decentered A2 glenoid (requiring augments or reverse arthroplasty) as a centered A1 glenoid (standard anatomic replacement).[9] This is not a trivial error — it directly leads to incorrect surgical planning and higher revision rates.

This is corroborated by a 2024 systematic review of 60 studies showing that adding images degrades LLM performance:[10]

Implication: Bifurcation Required Visual diagnostics (X-ray, CT, MRI) must be handled by separate, domain-specific deep learning/computer vision models explicitly trained on medical imaging datasets.[26] General-purpose LLMs are text-based tools only.

Part 2 · Validated Applications in Orthopedic Education

18% of studies focused on education,[2] providing the clearest benchmarks for LLM knowledge by testing against standardized exams used to certify human residents.

2.3 Utility in Surgical Training & Simulation

LLM applications to procedural training remain nascent. Studies propose potential value in "literature synthesis and clinical evaluation" and case-based analysis.[14] The future vision is an integrated training ecosystem combining LLMs with VR/AR platforms.[40] However, this area lacks robust validation — authors explicitly state "further studies, preferably RCTs with larger sample sizes, are required."[41] Level V (Expert Opinion)

Evidence gap: Only discussion of potential rather than performance data available in source. No RCTs identified for surgical simulation applications.

Part 3 · Validated Applications in Administrative & Research Workflows

LLMs have been validated as powerful tools for automating administrative and research tasks — domains where text-generation capabilities reduce the "clerical burden" consuming 34–55% of a physician's day.[42]

3.3 Acceleration of Scientific Research

A 2025 study evaluated ChatGPT's ability to replicate tasks from five published JBJS systematic reviews:[47]

  • Search strategy: Captured 91% of articles — good performance.
  • Abstract screening: Captured 75% — fair performance.
  • In-depth manuscript screening: Captured only 55% — unacceptable for meta-analysis.

A 2024 "academic Turing test" found that neither researchers nor AI-detection software could distinguish LLM-generated abstracts from human-written ones.[50] This validation of capability creates an ethical crisis regarding academic integrity, with mandatory AI-use disclosure proposed as the primary mitigation.[26]

Interpretation: ChatGPT (GPT-4) often shows higher raw accuracy in complex reasoning/exam questions. Gemini appears better suited as a clinical tool, with superior transparency (citations) and readability (patient communication).

4.2 Iterative Improvement: GPT-3.5 vs. GPT-4/4o

The performance leap from GPT-3.5 to GPT-4 is not incremental but a "phase change":[6] [53] [43] [54]

  • OITE: GPT-3.5 at PGY-1 level (54.3%) → GPT-4 at PGY-5 level (73.6%).[6]
  • Diagnostic accuracy: GPT-4.0 "significantly higher" than GPT-3.5 (t=5.753; P<.001).[53]
  • Clinical letters: GPT-4 scored 8.7/10 vs. GPT-3's 7.3/10 (P=0.024).[43]
⚠ Any validation study is a "snapshot" of a rapidly moving target. As noted in the source literature, the CEO of OpenAI has stated "ChatGPT 4.0 is much worse than the new upcoming version 5.0."[22] Continuous re-validation is required.

4.3 Critical Deficiencies & Identified Risks

  1. Image Interpretation Failure — Not validated; 0% accuracy on fundamental classification tasks.[9]
  2. Inconsistency & Hallucinations — Diagnostic accuracy ranges 55–93%; classification accuracy spans 2–100%.[2]
  3. Algorithmic Bias — Models perpetuate societal biases in race, gender, cost predictions.[17]
  4. Governance Gap — Undefined liability,[18] privacy concerns with patient data,[18] and lack of regulatory oversight.[19]
  5. Automation Bias — Human tendency to over-trust automated systems, reinforcing the need for rigorous human oversight.[19]

Part 5 · Synthesis & Future Outlook

Validated Applications (High-Confidence, Human-in-the-Loop)

  • Administrative Automation: Discharge summaries 10× faster; operative note classification +0.10 F1 over human coders.[4] [5]
  • Educational Study Aid: OITE performance at PGY-5 level; RAG-based parity with surgeons (P>0.99).[6] [15]
  • Guideline Retrieval: 100% accuracy on closed-ended therapy queries.[21]
  • Patient Education Simplification: Effective simplification to 6th-grade level maintaining accuracy.[8]
  • Clinical Copilot: Human+AI > Human+Google in RCT.[25]

Validated Failures (Do Not Use Autonomously)

  • Image Interpretation: Clinically unsafe; 0% on Walch classification.[9]
  • Autonomous Clinical Recommendations: ~40% discordance with AAOS CPGs.[11]
  • De Novo Patient Education: Unreadable (11th grade) and lower quality.[12]
  • Autonomous Systematic Reviews: Misses 45% of target publications.[47]

Future Directions

  1. Domain-specific models trained on "robust medical data" and "peer-reviewed medical literature."[57] [6] RAG is the validated first step.[15]
  2. Multimodal integration — genuine fusion of EHR text with imaging data, solving current pattern-recognition deficiencies.[32]
  3. Continuous re-validation against the rapidly, non-linearly improving baseline of LLM capabilities.[22]
Consensus Conclusion LLMs "cannot replace orthopedic professionals in the short term."[2] The validated pathway is as a "copilot" or "auxiliary tool" — with all applications requiring rigorous human oversight to mitigate risks of misinformation, automation bias, and unresolved legal/ethical challenges.[17]
