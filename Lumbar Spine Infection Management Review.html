Comprehensive Review: Postoperative Lumbar Spine Infection Management

An evidence-based educational summary derived exclusively from the provided review document (Shaw JD et al., 2025 and associated references).

Introduction and Epidemiological Context

The increasing utilization of spinal instrumentation has led to a parallel rise in postoperative spinal implant infections (PSII), encompassing superficial/deep SSIs and postoperative discitis (POD). These infections rank as the third most common adverse event following spine surgery, contributing significantly to patient morbidity, prolonged hospitalizations, and complex revision surgeries. The economic burden is substantial, representing a significant fraction of the >$100 billion annual U.S. spine care expenditure.

Reported infection incidence varies widely (1%-12%), depending on procedure invasiveness, patient comorbidities, and diagnostic criteria. Aggregate infection rates for instrumented procedures have increased from approximately 2.2% in 1999 to 5.6% by 2013, correlating with increased fusion volumes.

Pathophysiology

Infections are primarily caused by intraoperative introduction of cutaneous flora or postoperative hematogenous seeding onto hardware. The avascular surfaces of implants (titanium screws, cobalt-chrome rods, PEEK cages) create an immunologically privileged environment that facilitates bacterial biofilm formation.

Biofilm Formation Process: Planktonic bacteria adhere to implants, undergo phenotypic shifts, and secrete an extracellular polymeric substance (EPS) matrix. This matrix physically impedes immune cells and antibiotics, while the hypoxic core forces bacteria into a dormant "persister" state, rendering conventional antibiotics ineffective.

Consensus Definitions & Diagnostics: The 2024-2025 Paradigm

Historical reliance on CDC/NHSN surveillance definitions lacked clinical nuance for complex implant infections. A 2024-2025 EBJIS/MSIS consensus, developed via modified Delphi process with >90% agreement, established a new framework.

Critical Caveat: mNGS's high sensitivity comes with lower specificity (76.8% vs. 94.5%) due to susceptibility to contamination and detection of non-viable nucleic acids. Current guidelines recommend it as an adjunct to, not replacement for, traditional culture.

Hardware Sonication

For explanted hardware, sonication disrupts biofilms and dislodges embedded bacteria into fluid for culture/mNGS. This technique achieves sensitivity >95% and is the gold standard for isolating low-virulence organisms like C. acnes and coagulase-negative staphylococci.

Clinical Practice Guidelines (2020-2025)

IDSA (Medical Management)

Guideline (V) Emphasizes securing microbiological samples before empirical antibiotics. Recommends minimum 2-6 week IV induction for confirmed infections due to frequent bacteremia and need for bone penetration.

NASS (Surgical & Perioperative)

Guideline (V) Focuses on optimizing modifiable risk factors pre-operatively: glycemic control, BMI reduction, smoking cessation. Advocates for broad-spectrum IV prophylaxis and minimally invasive techniques (MISS) to reduce infection risk.

EuroSpine (Structural Quality)

Consensus (V) The SSCoE certification mandates 24/7 multidisciplinary capability (advanced imaging, ICU, emergency revision surgery) to manage catastrophic infections effectively.

Surgical Intervention Strategies

The decision algorithm balances infection source control with biomechanical stability, primarily based on infection chronicity and fusion status.

Early-Onset Infection (<3 months): DAIR Paradigm

Debridement, Antibiotics, and Implant Retention (DAIR) is standard for early infections where biofilms are immature and hardware is stable. Success rates are high when performed early: 87% failure-free survival at 2 years vs. 43% for delayed DAIR.

Post-DAIR therapy: Prolonged antibiotics (typically 6 weeks IV then oral), often continued until radiographic fusion is confirmed.

Late-Onset Infection (>3 months): Hardware Removal/Exchange

Mature biofilms necessitate hardware removal for source control.

  • Complete Removal: Possible if solid bony arthrodesis is present. Antibiotic course typically 6 weeks. Risks include persistent pain (~15%).
  • Implant Exchange: Required if no fusion. Options include single-staged (preferred) or two-staged exchange. Not specified in source Material note: PEEK should be exchanged for titanium due to lower biofilm affinity.

Controversy: Prophylactic Intrawound Vancomycin Powder

Initial retrospective studies suggested dramatic SSI reduction (from 5.6% to 1.4%). However, recent rigorous meta-analyses of RCTs (2023-2025) challenge this.

Current Evidence: Recent data suggest vancomycin powder may not significantly reduce SSIs. Furthermore, high local concentrations inhibit osteoblast proliferation, potentially increasing pseudoarthrosis risk. Use is no longer universal and is reserved for exceptionally high-risk cases.

Biofilm Eradication Strategies

Citrate-Based Irrigation (XPERIENCE™)

Engineered solution with citric acid (chelates EPS), sodium citrate (buffer), and sodium lauryl sulfate (surfactant). Achieves 4-8 log reduction in biofilms via chemical destabilization and mechanical disruption. Features a "no-rinse" residual barrier protocol.

Ultrafine-Grained (UFG) Stainless Steel

Animal study (III) Nanostructured surface topography creates unfavorable physical environment for bacterial adhesion, delaying biofilm maturation in murine models.

Targeted Antibiofilm Pharmacotherapy

For retained hardware, systemic therapy must use agents that penetrate biofilms and kill persister cells.

Key Antibiofilm Agents and Combinations
Agent/Combination Mechanism & Utility
Rifampin + Companion Foundation for Gram-positive infections. Penetrates biofilm, kills dormant cells. Must be combined to prevent resistance.

Local Delivery: Antibiotic-Loaded PMMA

Serves as structural spacer and local delivery system. Exhibits biphasic elution: massive burst release over 24 hours followed by sustained release maintaining supra-MIC levels for 4-8 weeks.

Postoperative Discitis (POD)

A distinct infection following non-instrumented procedures (incidence 0.2-4%). Presentation is insidious: severe back pain with spasms 2-8 weeks post-op.

Diagnostic-Algorithm:
1. Serial CRP/ESR (trends upward)
2. Gadolinium-enhanced MRI (gold standard)
3. CT-guided biopsy before antibiotics (culture positive in 40-80%)

Mainstay of treatment: Conservative management with 6 weeks of targeted IV antibiotics, bracing, and bed rest. Surgery is reserved for failure (intractable pain at 4 weeks, neuro deficits, or instability).

Case series (IV) Systematic review (III) Advanced endoscopic debridement is increasingly suggested for surgical cases to reduce morbidity.
