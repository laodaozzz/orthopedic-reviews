Biomechanical Principles & Clinical Application of Locking Compression Plates (LCP)

🦴 Orthopaedic Trauma 📚 Evidence-Based Review

1. Executive Summary

The landscape of orthopaedic trauma surgery has undergone a paradigm shift over the last three decades, transitioning from a purely mechanical philosophy centered on absolute stability and anatomical reduction to a more biological approach that prioritizes the preservation of blood supply and the optimization of the healing environment. Central to this evolution is the Locking Compression Plate (LCP), a hybrid implant system that integrates two distinct mechanical principles: the friction-based compression of conventional plating and the fixed-angle stability of the internal fixator. This report provides a comprehensive, expert-level analysis of the biomechanical differences between locking and non-locking screws within the LCP system. It synthesizes data from major orthopaedic literature to establish a rigorous clinical decision-making framework for screw selection, addressing the nuances of fracture pattern, bone quality, and the complex mechanics of hybrid fixation. Furthermore, it examines the operational pitfalls associated with these advanced constructs, providing evidence-based strategies to mitigate complications such as stress risers, loss of reduction, and implant failure.

2. Historical Evolution and Theoretical Foundations

To fully appreciate the biomechanical distinctions of the LCP system, one must understand the historical trajectory of internal fixation. The AO Foundation (Arbeitsgemeinschaft für Osteosynthesefragen) originally championed the concept of absolute stability. The early Dynamic Compression Plates (DCP) were designed to achieve rigid fixation through high interfragmentary compression. Ref [1]

The biological cost of this stability was significant; the plates required extensive soft tissue stripping for application and relied on heavy friction against the periosteum, leading to localized bone necrosis underneath the plate—a phenomenon colloquially termed "plate disease." Ref [2]

The recognition of these biological downsides led to the development of the Limited Contact–Dynamic Compression Plate (LC-DCP), which reduced the plate-to-bone contact area. However, the mechanical principle remained friction-based. The true revolution occurred with the advent of the Point Contact Fixator (PC-Fix) and the Less Invasive Stabilization System (LISS), which introduced locking screws. Ref [4] These systems functioned as internal fixators, mechanically decoupling the plate's stability from its contact with the bone. The LCP system represents the synthesis of these two lineages, incorporating the "Combi-hole" to allow surgeons to utilize standard compression techniques, locking fixator techniques, or a hybrid of both within the same construct.

3. Fundamental Biomechanics of Screw Fixation

The distinction between locking and non-locking screws is not merely a variation in thread design but represents a divergence into two fundamentally different physical systems for load transfer.

3.1 Friction-Based Stability: The Non-Locking Screw

Conventional plating relies on the mechanics of friction to stabilize fracture fragments. When a non-locking screw (cortex or cancellous) is inserted and tightened, it converts torque into axial tension (preload) along the screw shaft. Ref [4]

Physics of the Friction Interface

The stability of the construct is governed by the Coulomb friction equation:

Coulomb Friction Model
Ff ≤ μ · FN

Where:
Ff = frictional force resisting shear between plate and bone
μ = coefficient of friction (metal plate / cortical bone)
FN = normal force generated by screw torque (preload)

In this system, the screw functions primarily as a clamp. The plate is pressed firmly against the bone, and the load is transferred from the bone to the plate via this frictional interface. Ref [3]

🔑 Key Implications of Friction-Based Fixation
  1. Dependence on Screw Purchase: The generation of the normal force (FN) is entirely dependent on the screw's ability to hold against the bone threads. In osteoporotic bone, the screw threads may strip before sufficient normal force is generated. Ref [7]
  2. Sequential Failure Mechanism: Loads are not shared equally. The screw closest to the fracture site sees the highest stress. If overloaded, it toggles loose, shifting load to the next screw—a "zipper-like" sequential failure. Ref [7]
  3. Biological Compression: The high compressive force compresses the periosteum, occluding capillary blood flow, potentially delaying revascularization. Ref [3]

3.2 Internal Fixator Concept: The Locking Screw

Locking screws operate on the principle of the "fixed-angle construct." The head of the locking screw features a thread that mates with a corresponding thread in the plate hole. Ref [3]

Physics of the Locked Interface

When a locking screw is tightened, it locks into the plate, forming a single rigid frame. Stability depends on the stiffness of this frame rather than friction between plate and bone. Ref [4]

✅ Key Properties of Locked Fixation
  1. Direct Load Transfer: Bone → Screw → Plate. The plate-bone interface is mechanically bypassed. The plate does not need to touch the bone. Ref [3]
  2. Resistance to Toggling: The screw cannot change its angle relative to the plate. Resistance is governed by bending strength, not pullout strength. Ref [7]
  3. Parallel Failure: All screws resist load simultaneously. The entire bone block must fail for the construct to fail. This results in a significantly higher failure load in weak bone. Ref [7]

3.3 Comparative Biomechanical Performance

Research comparing these two systems highlights distinct performance profiles depending on the substrate:

  • In Normal Bone: Locked constructs are actually 6–31% less stiff than non-locked constructs in normal diaphyseal bone. There is no mechanical advantage to using locking screws in healthy, simple diaphyseal fractures. Ref [7]
  • In Osteoporotic Bone: Locked constructs are 21–74% stiffer and require significantly higher force to fail (e.g., 260 lbf vs. 223 lbf in axial loading). Ref [7]

4. The LCP System and the Mechanics of the "Combi-Hole"

The engineering breakthrough of the LCP system is the "Combi-hole," a figure-eight shaped hole that accommodates both screw types, allowing the surgeon to modulate the mechanical environment of the fracture. Ref [1]

4.1 Anatomy of the Combi-Hole

The Combi-hole consists of two distinct, overlapping geometries:

  1. The Dynamic Compression Unit (DCU): This non-threaded section utilizes the sloping ramp geometry of the LC-DCP. When a standard cortex or cancellous screw is inserted eccentrically, the spherical screw head engages the ramp. As the screw is tightened, the vertical descent of the head is converted into horizontal translation of the bone fragment, creating interfragmentary compression. Ref [2] This section allows for longitudinal angulation (up to 40°) and transverse angulation (7°). Ref [12]
  2. The Threaded Locking Section: This section features a conical or cylindrical thread that accepts the locking screw head. The conical design allows for secure locking even if the screw is slightly off-axis, though manufacturer guidelines stress coaxial insertion to avoid cross-threading. Ref [2]
⚠️ Mechanical Exclusion Principle

A single Combi-hole can function in compression mode OR locking mode, but not both simultaneously. If a locking screw is inserted, the plate is fixed in space relative to that bone segment. The "gliding" required for dynamic compression in the DCU section is physically impossible once a locking screw has engaged. Ref [1]

5. Hybrid Fixation: Mechanics, Sequence, and Stiffness

Hybrid fixation—the mixing of locking and non-locking screws within a single construct—is a powerful but complex technique. It allows the surgeon to combine the reduction capabilities of conventional screws with the stability of locking screws. Ref [14]

5.1 The Correct Sequence of Insertion

The sequence of screw insertion is dictated by the mechanics of the Combi-hole and the reduction goals:

Step 1 — Reduction and Compression (Non-Locking): Non-locking screws must be inserted first. These screws are used to "lag" the bone to the plate or to compress the fracture via the DCU ramp. This phase relies on friction and absolute stability principles. Ref [3]

Step 2 — Protection and Stabilization (Locking): Once reduction and compression are achieved, locking screws are inserted into the remaining holes. These screws "lock in" the construct, protecting the non-locking screws from loosening. Ref [16]

🚨 The "Reverse Sequence" Error

If a surgeon inserts a locking screw first, the plate is fixed at a set distance from the bone. Subsequent attempts to use a lag screw or compression screw will fail because the plate cannot move to compress the fracture. This results in a non-compressible gap, a recipe for non-union in simple fractures. Ref [3]

5.2 Stiffness Modulation and Stress Risers

A fully locked construct is extremely stiff. In osteoporotic bone, the transition from this rigid metal construct to the flexible porotic bone creates a significant stress riser at the end of the plate. Ref [17]

The "Soft End" Technique

Biomechanical studies have demonstrated that hybrid constructs can mitigate this risk. Replacing the outermost locking screw with a bicortical non-locking screw creates a "stiffness gradient."

  • Mechanism: The non-locking screw allows for a minute amount of toggle (elastic deformation) that a locking screw does not. This compliance smooths the transfer of bending loads from the rigid plate to the bone.
  • Evidence: In osteoporotic diaphyseal models, constructs with a non-locking end screw showed a 40% increase in bending strength compared to all-locked constructs. Ref [17]

Torsional Rigidity

Hybrid constructs exhibit superior torsional performance. The addition of even a single locking screw on either side of a fracture significantly increases resistance to rotation. Studies show that hybrid constructs are 42% stronger in torsion than all-non-locked constructs in osteoporotic models. Ref [15]

6. Clinical Decision-Making Framework

The choice between locking and non-locking screws should be driven by an algorithmic assessment of Fracture Pattern (biology) and Bone Quality (mechanics).

6.1 Decision Based on Fracture Pattern

Simple Fractures (Transverse, Short Oblique)

🎯 Goal: Anatomical Reduction + Primary Bone Healing

Requirement: Interfragmentary compression to abolish strain at the fracture site.

Screw Selection: Non-Locking Screws (Compression).

Rationale: Locking screws cannot generate interfragmentary compression. Bridging a simple fracture with a locking plate leaves a small gap. Due to the high stiffness of the locking plate, the strain across this small gap will be extremely high. According to Perren's strain theory, tissue cannot differentiate into bone under high strain, leading to non-union. Ref [8]

Comminuted / Multifragmentary Fractures

🎯 Goal: Restoration of Axis, Length, Rotation + Secondary Bone Healing

Requirement: Relative stability (flexible fixation) to stimulate callus.

Screw Selection: Locking Screws (Bridging).

Rationale: Attempting to compress a comminuted zone disrupts fragment vascularity and shortens the bone. The correct strategy is Bridge Plating: the plate spans the comminution without touching intermediate fragments. Ref [3]

Crucial Consideration — Working Length: To prevent the construct from being too stiff (suppressing callus), maximize the working length by leaving 2–3 holes empty over the fracture zone. Ref [19]

6.2 Decision Based on Bone Quality

Normal Bone (Young, Healthy Patients)

Indication: Non-Locking Screws. Healthy bone provides excellent thread purchase. Locking constructs offer no stiffness advantage in normal diaphyseal bone. Given the significantly higher cost of locking screws (often 5–10× the price), their routine use in simple fractures in healthy patients is not cost-effective. Ref [7,8]

Osteoporotic Bone (Elderly, Metabolic Bone Disease)

Indication: Locking Screws. In porous bone, the shear strength of the thread interface is the weak link. Locking screws bypass this weak link, relying on the much larger compressive strength of the surrounding bone block. They are the "gold standard" for geriatric fractures. Ref [7]

7. Specific Anatomical Scenarios and Surgical Techniques

7.1 Periarticular Fractures (Proximal Humerus, Distal Femur, Proximal Tibia)

These regions present a unique challenge: a small articular segment (often osteopenic) subjected to high joint reaction forces.

  • Strategy: The locking plate functions as a "raft" or blade plate. Multiple locking screws are placed in the meta-epiphyseal block to prevent varus/valgus collapse.
  • Surgical Nuance: It is critical to reduce the articular surface before applying locking screws. Once locked, the screws will not pull fragments together. "Whirlybird" or reduction clamps must be used to restore the joint surface first. Ref [6]

7.2 Periprosthetic Fractures (Vancouver B1)

Fractures around a stable hip stem (Vancouver B1) prevent the use of bicortical screws due to the metal implant in the canal.

  • Role of LCP: The LCP system is uniquely suited via Locking Attachment Plates (LAP) or offset locking screws.
  • Mechanism: Monocortical non-locking screws have insufficient pullout strength. However, monocortical locking screws are viable because the screw head is fixed to the plate. This allows stable fixation despite the stem. Ref [22]

7.3 Minimally Invasive Plate Osteosynthesis (MIPO)

MIPO involves sliding a plate submuscularly to bridge a fracture without opening the fracture site.

⚠️ MIPO Requirement

Because the fracture is not exposed, the plate cannot be perfectly contoured to the bone surface. Locking screws are mandatory. Using a non-locking screw in a MIPO construct will pull the bone toward the plate (likely sitting a few millimeters off the bone), causing primary loss of reduction. Locking screws maintain the "stand-off" position, preserving alignment. Ref [3]

8. Pitfalls, Complications, and Risk Mitigation

8.1 The "Too Stiff" Construct (Locked Plate Non-Union)

A common error is creating a construct that is too rigid for secondary healing. If a surgeon bridges a comminuted fracture with a locking plate and fills every hole with a locking screw, the construct suppresses interfragmentary motion almost completely, resulting in atrophic non-union. Ref [19]

🚨 Mitigation: The Rule of Screw Density

When bridging, screw density should be low (ideally < 0.5, meaning fewer than half the holes are filled). Leave 2–3 holes empty directly over the fracture gap to increase the elastic working length of the plate.

8.2 Primary Loss of Reduction

Scenario: A surgeon reduces a fracture with clamps, then inserts a non-locking screw into a plate slightly elevated off the bone. The screw pulls the bone to the plate, disrupting the reduction.

Mitigation: In MIPO or bridging, use locking screws to "freeze" the reduction. If a non-locking screw must be used to pull the plate to the bone, ensure it is done before final reduction is confirmed, or ensure the plate is perfectly contoured. Ref [3]

8.3 Cold Welding and Extraction Issues

Titanium locking screws are prone to galling ("cold welding") if over-tightened. The screw head fuses to the plate, making removal impossible with standard screwdrivers.

⚠️ Mitigation

Always use a Torque-Limiting Screwdriver (typically 1.5 Nm or 4.0 Nm depending on system) for final tightening. Never use power tools to drive locking screws to their final seated position. Clean blood and debris from plate threads before insertion. Ref [12]

8.4 Stress Risers and Periprosthetic Fracture

An abrupt end to a rigid locking construct concentrates stress at the plate terminus.

Mitigation: Avoid ending a construct with a unicortical locking screw in the diaphysis. Use a bicortical locking screw, or ideally, a bicortical non-locking screw at the plate ends to graduate the stiffness. Ref [17]

9. Conclusion

The Locking Compression Plate represents a sophisticated toolkit rather than a simple implant. It demands that the surgeon move beyond a "one-screw-fits-all" mentality to a nuanced, biomechanically driven selection process. The decision to use a locking or non-locking screw fundamentally alters the mechanics of the fixation—from friction-based compression to fixed-angle bridging.

✅ Core Principles of LCP Use
  1. Respect Biology: Use non-locking compression for simple fractures (absolute stability) and locking bridge plating for comminuted fractures (relative stability).
  2. Respect Mechanics: Use locking screws in osteoporotic bone and non-locking screws in healthy diaphyseal bone.
  3. Master the Hybrid: Sequence non-locking screws first for reduction, then locking screws for stability. Avoid overly rigid constructs and utilize non-locking screws at plate ends to prevent stress risers.

By integrating these biomechanical principles with careful surgical technique, the LCP system allows for the successful management of increasingly complex fracture patterns and patient populations, bridging the gap between mechanical rigidity and biological healing.
