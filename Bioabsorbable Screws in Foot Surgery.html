Bioabsorbable Fixation in Foot & Ankle Surgery

Expert Consensus, Guidelines & Systematic Evidence (1995–2025)
Evidence-Based Review

1. Executive Summary

Orthopaedic management of foot and ankle pathology has historically relied on metallic osteosynthesis—utilizing stainless steel and titanium—to achieve the mechanical stability required for bone union. However, the permanence of these materials often necessitates secondary surgical interventions for hardware removal due to soft tissue irritation, pain, or the need to accommodate future growth in pediatric patients. This report provides an exhaustive analysis of bioabsorbable and biodegradable fixation devices, synthesizing data from 1995 to 2025, grounded in expert consensus from the American Orthopaedic Foot and Ankle Society (AOFAS), technical guidelines from the AO Foundation, and contemporary systematic reviews.

Current evidence suggests a definitive paradigm shift toward bioabsorbable fixation in specific, low-load clinical scenarios. Third-generation magnesium (Mg) alloys and improved poly-L-lactic acid (PLLA) copolymers have largely mitigated the historical complications of sterile abscess formation associated with early polyglycolic acid (PGA) implants. Systematic reviews consistently demonstrate that for distal tibiofibular syndesmotic fixation, pediatric physeal fractures, and distal metatarsal osteotomies, bioabsorbable fixation offers clinical non-inferiority to metallic implants regarding union rates and functional scores (AOFAS, OMAS), while significantly reducing the rate of secondary procedures. Level I–II

🔬 Paradigm Shift While metallic implants are biologically inert but mechanically persistent, bioabsorbable implants are mechanically transient but biologically active. The degradation products—whether acidic monomers from polymers or hydrogen gas from magnesium—interact with local host tissue, necessitating precise patient selection.

2. The Clinical Imperative for Biodegradable Fixation

The foundation of modern fracture care is built upon the principles of anatomical reduction and stable internal fixation, a philosophy championed by the AO Foundation since the mid-20th century. Traditionally, this has been achieved using stainless steel or titanium implants. While effective, these materials possess a modulus of elasticity significantly higher than human cortical bone—approximately 110–220 GPa for metals versus 10–30 GPa for bone.[1] This mismatch leads to "stress shielding," where the rigid implant bears the mechanical load intended for the bone, potentially leading to localized osteopenia and weakening of the healed fracture site.

Furthermore, the superficial nature of the foot and ankle anatomy—with minimal soft tissue coverage over the medial malleolus, dorsal midfoot, and distal fibula—renders metallic hardware frequently symptomatic. Patients often report pain, cold sensitivity, and difficulty with shoe wear, driving a high rate of hardware removal operations. Systematic reviews indicate that elective removal of metallic screws in hallux valgus surgery occurs in up to 26.9% of patients,[4] while routine removal of syndesmotic screws remains a common, albeit debated, practice.[5]

⚠️ Historical Limitation The ideal bioabsorbable implant, as defined in AOFAS literature, maintains mechanical strength during the critical phase of bone healing (typically 8–12 weeks) and then gradually degrades, transferring load back to the bone to stimulate remodeling, before disappearing completely without inducing a toxic or inflammatory response.[3]

3. Biomaterials Science & Degradation Mechanisms

3.1 First & Second Generation Polymers (PGA, PLA, PLLA, PLGA)

The majority of clinical data from the 1990s through the 2010s relies on alpha-hydroxy polyesters. Degradation occurs via hydrolysis, where water molecules cleave polymer chains into monomers (lactic acid or glycolic acid), which are then metabolized via the Krebs cycle.[3]

  • Polyglycolic Acid (PGA): The earliest bioabsorbable material. PGA is characterized by high initial mechanical strength but very rapid degradation (complete absorption in 6–12 weeks). This rapid breakdown releases a high concentration of glycolic acid, frequently overwhelming local tissue clearance capacity, causing sterile sinus formation in up to 40% of early cases.[3] Level III
  • Poly-L-Lactic Acid (PLLA): Developed as a hydrophobic, highly crystalline polymer to counteract PGA's rapid degradation. PLLA retains mechanical strength for up to 36 weeks and degrades over 15–60 months. While this solved acute inflammation, long-term crystalline debris has been linked to late-onset osteolysis and foreign body reactions occurring years after surgery.[3] Level III
  • Poly-L-Lactide-co-Glycolide (PLGA): Second-generation copolymers that balance properties. By combining L-lactide and glycolide, manufacturers can tune the degradation rate. PLGA implants demonstrate better biocompatibility than pure PLLA, with reduced rates of encapsulation and foreign body reactions.[11] Level III

3.2 Third Generation: Magnesium (Mg) Alloys

The most significant recent advancement (2015–2025) is the introduction of biodegradable magnesium alloys (e.g., Mg-Zn-Ca).

  • Corrosion vs. Hydrolysis: Unlike polymers, magnesium degrades via electrochemical corrosion, releasing magnesium ions (Mg²⁺), hydroxide ions (OH⁻), and hydrogen gas (H₂).[1]
  • Mechanical Superiority: Magnesium alloys possess a Young's modulus (41–45 GPa) remarkably similar to cortical bone. This biomechanical compatibility minimizes stress shielding, theoretically improving healed bone quality compared to titanium fixation.[1]
  • Osteoconductivity: A critical distinction—magnesium degradation creates an alkaline environment (due to OH⁻ release) and releases Mg²⁺ ions, which upregulate calcitonin gene-related peptide (CGRP) in periosteal neurons, actively promoting osteoblast differentiation and bone formation.[1] This makes Mg implants bioactive, not merely biodegradable. Level II

4. Expert Consensus & Clinical Practice Guidelines

4.1 AOFAS Position Statements

The American Orthopaedic Foot and Ankle Society has tracked the evolution of bioabsorbable fixation for decades.

  • Historical Context: In early position papers (circa 1995), the AOFAS acknowledged theoretical benefits but urged caution, emphasizing that absorbable screws generally possess lower torsional and shear strength than metallic equivalents, recommending them primarily for fractures where rotational forces could be neutralized by casting.[3]
  • Current Stance: Modern AOFAS literature (Foot & Ankle International, Foot & Ankle Surgery) supports bioabsorbable screws for: syndesmotic fixation, pediatric fractures, and distal metatarsal osteotomies. However, bioabsorbable screws are not recommended for patients over 70 years of age, or those with advanced osteoporosis or rheumatoid disease.[3] Level III

4.2 AO Foundation Trauma Guidelines

The AO Foundation guidelines are nuanced, prioritizing the principle of fixation over the material.

  • Lag Screw Principle: For medial malleolar fractures, the core requirement is the "lag screw principle"—threads must be entirely within the distal fragment (epiphysis) and not cross the fracture line.[14]
  • Material Agnosticism: While standard AO teaching references stainless steel and titanium, the Technical Commission has approved bioabsorbable solutions (PLGA, Mg) for specific indications where they meet mechanical requirements. For ankle fractures, bioabsorbable implants are cited as valid alternatives to reduce imaging artifacts and avoid removal.[15]
  • Pediatric Specifics: AO Pediatric guidelines are more explicit: for Salter-Harris type fractures (distal tibia), bioabsorbable screws are highlighted as a solution that eliminates the removal decision entirely.[17] Level III

4.3 Absolute Contraindications

🚫 Absolute Contraindications (AOFAS & AO Consensus)
  1. Active or Latent Infection: Risk of bacterial colonization of the degrading porous scaffold.
  2. High-Load Instability: Unstable, comminuted fractures requiring load-bearing fixation are unsuitable for bioabsorbable screws alone.
  3. Poor Vascularity: Areas with compromised vascular supply may not clear degradation products efficiently, increasing risk of sterile abscess.

5. Systematic Review of Clinical Applications

5.1 Tibiofibular Syndesmosis Fixation

The syndesmosis is the most extensively studied indication for bioabsorbable screws. The physiological requirement is to heal in a reduced position (6–8 weeks) and then regain mobility. Rigid metal screws inhibit this late mobility unless removed.

Bioabsorbable vs. Metallic Screws

  • Functional Outcomes: A meta-analysis of RCTs found no statistically significant difference in AOFAS Ankle-Hindfoot scores (Mean Difference = 2.65, p=0.27) or VAS pain scores between groups.[4] Level I
  • Range of Motion: Both groups achieve comparable ROM. Bioabsorbable screws theoretically allow earlier restoration of physiological micromotion.[18]
  • Complication Rates: One meta-analysis noted that while the rate of complications was not statistically different, the absolute number was higher in the bioabsorbable group (23.4%) compared to the metallic group (5.7%).[18] These were primarily minor wound issues and sterile effusions. However, this must be balanced against the complication rate of routine metal screw removal.
  • Effectiveness: In a prospective study of 75 patients, 93% returned for final evaluation with a mean AOFAS score of 90/100. No patients showed radiographic evidence of late syndesmotic instability or osteolysis.[19] Level II

The Suture Button Confounder

The debate is no longer binary. Suture button devices (e.g., TightRope) have emerged as a third option. Meta-analyses suggest dynamic suture fixation may be superior to rigid screw fixation—associated with higher AOFAS scores, lower rates of malreduction, and quicker return to work.[21,22] Consensus suggests bioabsorbable screws as the preferred rigid fixation option, but dynamic fixation may be the overall preferred standard in suitable patients.

5.2 Pediatric Physeal Fractures (Salter-Harris)

This indication represents perhaps the strongest consensus for bioabsorbable use.

  • Clinical Rationale: Metallic screws crossing the physis must be removed to prevent growth tethering. Bioabsorbable screws eliminate this requirement entirely.
  • Outcomes: A study comparing metal vs. bioabsorbable screws in 67 pediatric distal tibia fractures found no significant difference in growth incidence of arrest or Oxford Ankle and Foot Questionnaire scores.[23] Level II
  • Economic Impact: The bioabsorbable group had significantly shorter total hospital stays (2.6 vs. 5.2 days) and lower overall medical costs ($397 savings per patient) due to elimination of the removal procedure.[23]
  • Safety: Long-term follow-up (12–30 months) confirmed that PLGA implants do not disturb the growth plate.[25]

5.3 Hallux Valgus & Forefoot Osteotomies

Recent systematic reviews have focused on Magnesium (Mg) screws for distal chevron osteotomies.

  • Radiographic Equivalence: Studies comparing Mg to Ti screws found no significant difference in Hallux Valgus Angle (HVA) or Intermetatarsal Angle (IMA) correction (p=0.226 and p=0.712).[4] Level II
  • Healing: Osteotomy union was achieved significantly faster in the Mg group in some cohorts, supporting magnesium's osteoconductive properties.[28]
  • Complications: While Mg screws showed higher rates of radiographic "hardware failure" (screw breakage), this did not translate to clinical failure—union and correction were maintained.[28]

5.4 Medial Malleolar Fractures

For medial malleolar fractures, AO principles dictate lag screw fixation.

  • Efficacy: A retrospective review comparing Mg vs. Ti screws showed similar AOFAS scores (p=0.191) and union rates (100% in both groups).[2] Level II
  • Removal Rates: In the titanium group, a significant portion required removal due to pain and difficulty wearing shoes. In the Mg group, removal was zero.[30]

6. Safety Profile & Complications

6.1 Foreign Body Reactions & Osteolysis

The degradation of polymer-based screws releases acidic monomers.

  • Sterile Abscesses: First-generation PGA screws had fluid accumulation rates up to 40%. Current PLGA and SR-PLLA implants have significantly lower rates (0–4%). These present as painless, fluctuant masses months to years after surgery.[3] Level III
  • Osteolysis: The acidic environment can cause local bone resorption, visible in a significant number of patients but rarely symptomatic.[10]
  • Copolymer Composition Matters: PDLLA induced marked foreign body reactions in animal models, whereas PLGA did not.[11]

6.2 Magnesium Gas Formation

💨 Radiographic Cysts — A Benign Finding As Mg corrodes, hydrogen gas is released, resulting in radiolucent "cysts" or cavities around the screw. These are generally benign, do not cause pain or instability, and resolve spontaneously. However, they can mimic osteolysis or infection on imaging, potentially leading to unnecessary alarm if the surgeon is unfamiliar with Mg biodegradation.[1]

6.3 Mechanical Limitations

  • Torsional Strength: Bioabsorbable screws have lower torsional rigidity. If a screw binds during insertion, the head can shear off. AO guidelines emphasize tapping the entire drill hole to reduce insertion torque.[3]
  • Bending Failure: In TMT-1 arthrodesis models, bioabsorbable screws failed at lower loads (85.1 N) compared to titanium (120.6 N), often via bending. This restricts their use to compression-dominant constructs.[9]

7. Economic Analysis & "Choosing Wisely"

The economic justification relies on the "total cost of care" rather than implant cost alone.

  • Implant vs. Procedure Cost: Bioabsorbable screws are individually more expensive, but the cost of a single removal surgery (anesthesia, facility fees, surgical time) far exceeds the price difference.
  • Finnish Ankle Fracture Study: Total cost of treating fractures with SR-PGA screws was £3,503 vs. £4,514 with metallic implants when removal costs were factored in.[34] Level II
  • Pediatric Savings: One study documented savings of approximately $400 per patient in the bioabsorbable group, alongside 50% reduction in total hospital days.[23]
💡 Choosing Wisely Application If a surgeon's practice is to never remove asymptomatic metal hardware, the economic argument weakens. However, given that 20–50% of ankle fracture patients eventually request removal due to pain, bioabsorbable screws act as "insurance policy" against future surgery. The most high-value application is in subcutaneous areas (medial malleolus, dorsal foot) in thin or active patients.

9. Conclusion & Key Takeaways

The integration of bioabsorbable fixation into foot and ankle surgery represents a mature and evidence-based advancement. Expert consensus from AOFAS and AO guidelines supports these devices as safe and effective alternatives in specific, low-load indications.

1

Strongest Indications

Pediatric physeal fractures, syndesmotic fixation, and distal metatarsal osteotomies have the highest level of evidence supporting bioabsorbable use.

2

State-of-the-Art Materials

Magnesium alloys offer osteoconductive properties and mechanical similarity to bone. PLGA copolymers remain reliable for pediatric applications.

3

Economic Advantage

Higher initial implant cost is offset by elimination of secondary removal surgeries, reducing total cost of care by up to £1,000 per patient.

4

Safety Vigilance

Strict adherence to contraindications (infection, instability, poor vascularity) is paramount. Sterile abscesses are rare but require monitoring.

For a young, active patient with a medial malleolar fracture or a child with a Salter-Harris injury, bioabsorbable fixation offers a distinct advantage by providing a "one and done" surgical solution, effectively mitigating the physical, psychological, and financial burden of secondary orthopedic interventions.
