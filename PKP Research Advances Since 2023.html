1. Introduction: The Evolving Landscape of Spinal Restoration

The management of vertebral compression fractures (VCFs) has undergone a profound transformation. Originally conceived as a purely mechanical intervention to stabilize collapsed vertebrae and alleviate pain, Percutaneous Kyphoplasty (PKP) has evolved into a sophisticated, multidisciplinary procedure at the intersection of materials science, biomechanical engineering, and oncology.1

The period between 2023 and 2025 marks a transition from the "inert filling" paradigm—where the primary goal was simply to occupy space with polymethylmethacrylate (PMMA)—to a "biological restoration" paradigm. This new era is characterized by:

  • Bioactive materials that participate in bone healing rather than merely occupying space
  • Digital technologies (robotics, AI, navigation) that enhance surgical precision and predict complications
  • Rigorous evidence-based re-evaluation of clinical indications and technical nuances

Osteoporotic Vertebral Compression Fractures (OVCFs) represent a growing epidemic mirroring global population aging. VCFs affect millions annually, leading to debilitating pain, progressive kyphotic deformity, pulmonary compromise, and a cascade of functional decline that significantly increases mortality.1 While traditional procedures like vertebroplasty (PVP) and balloon kyphoplasty (PKP) have demonstrated efficacy in providing immediate pain relief, they have been limited by cement leakage, lack of biological integration, and potential adjacent segment fractures due to biomechanical mismatch.3

Scope of This Review

This report synthesizes the latest research advances in PKP since 2023 across four critical domains: (1) the material science revolution in bone cements, (2) the technological leap in surgical navigation and robotics, (3) resolution of clinical controversies through high-level evidence, and (4) expanded indications redefining minimally invasive spine surgery.

2. Novel Bone Cement Materials: The Bioactive Revolution

For nearly four decades, Polymethylmethacrylate (PMMA) has served as the gold standard for vertebral augmentation due to its ease of handling, rapid setting time, and high compressive strength. However, PMMA is bioinert—it acts as a permanent foreign body that never integrates with host bone. Its high stiffness (Young's modulus) often exceeds that of osteoporotic trabecular bone, creating stress risers that predispose adjacent levels to fracture. Furthermore, exothermic polymerization can cause thermal necrosis to adjacent neural tissues.4

The 2023–2025 research landscape is defined by the quest for bioactive, biodegradable, and functionalized cements engineered to actively modulate the local biological environment, promote osteointegration, and deliver therapeutic agents.

2.1. Magnesium Phosphate Cements (MPCs): Orchestrating Bone Homeostasis

Perhaps the most significant breakthrough involves Magnesium Phosphate Cements (MPCs). Unlike traditional Calcium Phosphate Cements (CPCs), which exhibit slow degradation and inadequate mechanical strength, MPCs offer a degradation profile that can be tuned to match the rate of new bone formation—"isochronous degradation."6

The pTMPC-SMA Cement: Dual-Action Mechanism

A landmark 2025 study introduced a premixed magnesium phosphate bone cement loaded with strontium ranelate and bioglass microspheres grafted with alendronate sodium (pTMPC-SMA).6 This material targets the RANKL/OPG pathway:

  • Strontium ranelate is a dual-action agent that promotes osteoblast differentiation (bone formation) while inhibiting osteoclast differentiation (bone resorption). It enhances OPG expression, which binds RANKL and prevents osteoclast maturation.6
  • Alendronate-grafted bioglass provides sustained, local release of a potent bisphosphonate that directly induces osteoclast apoptosis, bypassing systemic side effects of oral bisphosphonates.6

In Vivo Performance

Preclinical osteoporotic rabbit vertebroplasty models demonstrated excellent injectability and anti-washout properties. Histological analysis revealed progressive replacement by new viable bone trabeculae ("creeping substitution") as the material degraded—resulting in biologically restored rather than permanently filled vertebral bodies.6

Clinical Significance

The restoration of trabecular architecture potentially restores natural spine biomechanics, reducing the modulus mismatch implicated in adjacent segment fractures. MPCs may offer a true regenerative solution for OVCFs.

2.2. Modified Calcium Phosphate and Composite Cements

Starch/BaSO4-Enhanced CPCs

A 2024 formulation (CPB) incorporated starch and Barium Sulfate into the calcium phosphate matrix. Starch acts as both a rheological modifier (improving injectability and preventing phase separation) and a pore-forming agent—creating macropores upon degradation that facilitate blood vessel and osteoprogenitor cell infiltration. BaSO4 provides essential radiopacity.5

Mineralized Collagen/PMMA Composites

Studies from 2023–2024 investigated PMMA composited with bone-mineralized collagen, reducing the elastic modulus closer to human cancellous bone. This mitigates "stress shielding" and adjacent fracture risk while improving biocompatibility over pure PMMA.8

2.3. Functionalized Cements: Drug Delivery and Magnetic Guidance

Antibiotic-Loaded Cements

Newer standardized preparations of biodegradable calcium sulfate/phosphate beads loaded with gentamicin provide high local antibiotic concentrations for spondylodiscitis while serving as bone repair scaffolds—beyond the unpredictable hand-mixing of antibiotic powder with PMMA.9

Magnetic Nanoparticle Guidance

"Magnetic Kyphoplasty"—developed in a porcine model—uses magnetic bone cement as a "homing beacon" for intravenously injected magnetite nanoparticles loaded with chemotherapy. This maximizes local drug concentration for spinal metastases while minimizing systemic toxicity.10

3. Technological Advances: The Era of Digital Spine Surgery

Traditional PKP relies on 2D fluoroscopy and surgeon tactile feedback. This manual approach carries significant radiation exposure, variability in cannula placement accuracy, and unpredictable cement leakage risk. The 2023–2025 period has seen rapid maturation of digital technologies that transform PKP from a "see-and-do" to a "predict-and-execute" workflow.

3.1. Robotic-Assisted Kyphoplasty (RAK)

A major 2025 retrospective analysis (240 patients, 120 per arm) provided compelling evidence for RAK superiority over conventional fluoroscopy-guided kyphoplasty (FK):11

Key RAK Outcomes (Level II Evidence)
  • Cement leakage: 5.8% vs. 19.2% (p=0.002) — dramatic reduction from precision cannula placement
  • Height restoration: 68.00% vs. 64.38% (p=0.004) — optimized by 3D trajectory planning
  • Procedure time: 50.91 min vs. 86.40 min (p<0.00001) — "one-pass" accuracy eliminates repeated adjustments
  • Patient radiation: 18.76 mGy vs. 22.69 mGy (p<0.00001) — surgeon controls from console outside radiation field

Crucially, RAK maintained its performance regardless of anatomical level. While thoracic vertebrae increased difficulty for fluoroscopic techniques, the robotic system acted as a "skill equalizer," standardizing outcomes across spinal levels.11 Further support came from a 2025 study showing that robotic precision allowed unilateral approaches to achieve bilateral-equivalent biomechanical outcomes with less trauma.13

3.2. Artificial Intelligence and Deep Learning

Predicting Bone Cement Leakage (BCL)

Deep learning models based on Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) now analyze radiomic features imperceptible to the human eye—including micro-breaks in the cortical shell, intravertebral vacuum cleft morphology, and trabecular density patterns. A 2024 multimodal model integrates CT and MRI data to predict both the risk and type of leakage (intradiscal vs. paravertebral venous).14

This allows true preventative medicine: high-risk patients can receive altered surgical plans preoperatively (higher-viscosity cement, adjusted injection volume, modified cannula trajectory).

Outcome Prediction

A 2025 Random Forest model predicted Distal Lumbosacral Pain (DLP) following PKP, identifying sarcopenia and preoperative Cobb angle as critical risk factors—enabling targeted rehabilitation.19

3.3. Intraoperative Navigation and Augmented Reality

O-Arm Navigation

O-arm (3D intraoperative CT) navigation provides real-time 3D visualization superior to 2D fluoroscopy. A 2024 study quantified median radiation: 838 mGycm (O-arm) vs. 1,108 mGycm (control) (p<0.0001).20 O-arm is particularly valuable in Kummell's disease where standard landmarks are obscured by sclerosis and collapse.21

Augmented Reality (AR)

AR systems (e.g., HoloLens, Augmedics xVision) overlay 3D anatomy onto the surgeon's field of view, achieving needle placement accuracy of 93–100%.23 AR reduces cognitive load and radiation exposure by minimizing live fluoroscopy needs, though high costs remain a barrier.25

4. Clinical Controversies & Evidence-Based Resolution

4.1. Unilateral vs. Bilateral Approaches

Recent evidence overwhelmingly supports the unilateral approach as the standard of care for routine osteoporotic fractures.26

Level I
  • Clinical equivalence: A 2025 meta-analysis of 35 RCTs confirmed statistically indistinguishable VAS and ODI scores between techniques.26
  • Procedural superiority: Unilateral PKP was associated with shorter operative times, lower cement dosage, and reduced radiation exposure.26
  • Safety profile: Unilateral demonstrated a lower rate of cement leakage (P<0.0001) and overall complications.27
  • Adjacent fractures: No significant difference in postoperative adjacent-level fracture rates.26
Consensus Recommendation

For routine OVCFs, unilateral PKP should be the preferred technique due to superior safety, efficiency, and cost-effectiveness. Bilateral approaches may be reserved for severe vertebra plana or technically insufficient unilateral access.26,27,28

4.2. Stentoplasty (VBS) vs. Balloon Kyphoplasty

Vertebral Body Stenting (VBS) involves placing a metal stent that maintains height after balloon deflation, preventing "elastic recoil."

Level I
  • Radiographic outcomes: VBS achieved better Cobb angle correction (SMD = −1.00, p<0.0001).30
  • Clinical outcomes: Pain (VAS) and functional (ODI) scores were comparable or only marginally better with VBS.30
  • Safety: No significant difference in cement leakage rates.30
  • Cost/Time: VBS associated with longer surgical times and significantly higher material costs.30
Consensus Recommendation

VBS is best reserved for specific indications: severe kyphosis, fresh traumatic fractures requiring maximum height restoration, or Kummell's disease with substantial necrotic voids where intra-operative re-collapse risk is high.33

4.3. Adjacent Vertebral Fractures (AVFs) & Prophylactic Vertebroplasty

Level II

Meta-analyses (2024) found no significant difference in AVF rates between kyphoplasty and vertebroplasty, nor between unilateral and bilateral techniques—suggesting the natural history of osteoporosis, rather than the procedure itself, is the primary driver.29

A notable 2025 retrospective study identified a specific subgroup—patients with "biendplate-involvement" fractures (both superior and inferior endplates compromised)—who significantly benefited from prophylactic vertebroplasty: recurrent fracture rate decreased from 28.6% to 7.7%.35

Consensus Recommendation

Routine prophylactic vertebroplasty is not supported. Consider it for high-risk patients with biendplate fractures or severe local kyphosis where mechanical stress on adjacent levels is extreme.35

4.4. Kummell's Disease: Managing the Vacuum Cleft

Kummell's disease (post-traumatic vertebral osteonecrosis) features an intravertebral vacuum cleft (IVC) and pseudarthrosis, carrying high cement leakage risk with standard PKP.21

Level IV
  • O-arm navigation is emphasized for safe, precise instrument placement into the cleft, overcoming the difficulty of sclerotic bone and deformed anatomy.21
  • "Mesh-hold" bone filling containers allow cement interdigitation with surrounding bone while preventing gross leakage, transforming mobile non-union into a solid fusion block.36

5. Expanded Indications: Beyond Simple Osteoporosis

5.1. Spinal Metastases: Combination Therapy Standard

Treatment has moved from palliative radiotherapy alone to a multimodal, minimally invasive approach combining ablation technologies with PKP.37

Level I
  • Radiofrequency/Microwave Ablation + PKP is clinically superior to PKP alone for painful metastases.37
  • Synergistic mechanism: Ablation physically destroys tumor cells and disrupts periosteal nerve fibers; subsequent cement stabilizes the lytic defect.
  • Local tumor progression reduced to 5% at 1-year follow-up; significantly better pain relief (Mean Difference 4.82 VAS).37

5.2. Multiple Myeloma (MM)

Level II

PKP in MM patients improves Quality of Life and is safe alongside systemic chemotherapy/immunotherapy. Follow-up data extending 4 years shows sustained pain relief, making it a durable palliative solution as MM patients live longer due to advances in systemic therapies.40,41

5.3. Traumatic Burst Fractures

Traditionally contraindicated due to fear of pushing retropulsed fragments into the canal, recent case series (2024–2025) suggest stand-alone PKP can be safe for select burst fractures in neurologically intact patients.42

Level IV
  • Key safety principle: Use balloon tamp for indirect reduction via ligamentotaxis or anterior column stabilization.
  • In carefully selected patients (TLICS score <4, poor surgical candidates), PKP avoided open fixation—reducing morbidity, blood loss, and recovery time.44
Source limitation: Burst fracture evidence is based on case series (Level IV). The source document notes this indication requires careful patient selection and is not yet standard of care.

5.4. Aggressive Vertebral Hemangiomas

Level II

Systematic reviews (2025) indicate PKP/vertebroplasty achieves symptom resolution in 82.5% of aggressive hemangiomas without cord compression—superior to conservative management or radiation alone. The cement acts as an embolizing agent and provides structural reinforcement.45

6. Conclusion & Key Takeaways for the 2025 Clinician

The field of PKP has undergone profound maturation between 2023 and 2025, transitioning from the "plumbing" model of filling a void with cement to an era of refinement and diversification.

2025 Clinical Standards — Key Takeaways
  1. Adopt Unilateral PKP as the default for standard OVCFs to minimize risk, cost, and radiation.26
  2. Prioritize Bioactive Cements (MPCs or modified CPCs) for younger osteoporotic patients to promote long-term vertebral health and reduce refracture risks.6
  3. Integrate Preoperative AI/Planning to identify high-risk leakage patterns and tailor the surgical approach.14
  4. Embrace Combination Therapies (Ablation + Cement) for all painful metastatic lesions to achieve both local control and stability.37
  5. Consider Robotics or Navigation if high-volume practice justifies capital cost, particularly for radiation safety and complex anatomy management.11,20

Looking toward 2030, the integration of these elements suggests a future where "Vertebral Augmentation" will be redefined as "Vertebral Regeneration"—the injection of a material that not only supports the spine mechanically but restores it biologically, guided by the steady hand of a robot and the predictive eye of artificial intelligence.
