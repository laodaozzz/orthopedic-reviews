1. Foundational Principles of Damage Control Orthopedics (DCO)

The management of a polytraumatized patient with multiple fractures in a resource-limited setting demands a paradigm shift from conventional orthopedic care. The principles of Damage Control Orthopedics (DCO) provide the essential framework, prioritizing the patient's overall physiological stability and survival over immediate, definitive fracture fixation. Initial management focuses not on anatomical perfection but on rapid, effective stabilization to mitigate hemorrhage, control pain, and prevent the systemic consequences of severe trauma. LOE II

1.1 Initial Assessment and Resuscitation Priorities

The approach to any severely injured patient must adhere strictly to Advanced Trauma Life Support (ATLS®) protocols. All orthopedic interventions are secondary to the primary and secondary surveys. LOE II The non-negotiable priorities are assessment and management of Airway, Breathing, Circulation, Disability, and Exposure. LOE II

Orthopedic injuries are addressed only after immediate life threats have been managed, except for pelvic fractures, which are a direct component of circulatory resuscitation. LOE II Clinical signs of hemodynamic compromise—tachycardia, hypotension, prolonged capillary refill, and altered mental status—mandate a focus on life preservation above all else. LOE II

1.2 The "Second Hit" Phenomenon

The DCO philosophy is rooted in avoiding the "second hit". LOE II The initial trauma constitutes the "first hit," triggering systemic inflammatory response syndrome (SIRS) and coagulopathy. A prolonged, complex surgical procedure on an unstable patient acts as a "second hit," potentially leading to:

  • Multiple Organ Dysfunction Syndrome (MODS)
  • Acute Respiratory Distress Syndrome (ARDS)
  • Death
Core DCO Principle
Perform the minimum necessary intervention in the acute phase using minimally invasive techniques to control bleeding, reduce pain, prevent soft tissue damage, and facilitate nursing care and transport. Definitive fixation is delayed until physiological stability is achieved (staged treatment). LOE II

In austere environments, DCO extends beyond physiology to critical resource management. During mass casualty incidents, DCO allows a single surgical team to manage a larger number of critically injured patients by rapidly stabilizing one patient and freeing the operating room for another. LOE III The decision to employ DCO is based on the patient's hemodynamic status, Injury Severity Score (ISS), casualty load, and available resources. LOE II

1.3 Triage Hierarchy for Orthopedic Injuries

Within the DCO framework, orthopedic injuries are triaged based on their potential to cause death, limb loss, or irreversible disability:

1

Immediate Life-Threatening Hemorrhage

Unstable pelvic ring fracture in a hemodynamically unstable patient — the highest orthopedic priority. Stabilization with a pelvic binder is a resuscitative procedure. LOE II

2

Limb-Threatening Vascular Compromise

Fracture or dislocation with absent distal pulses or limb ischemia — requires immediate realignment and splinting to restore perfusion. LOE II

3

Open Fractures

All open fractures are contaminated — require urgent IV antibiotics, tetanus prophylaxis, and formal surgical debridement when the patient is stable. LOE II

4

Closed, Stable Long Bone Fractures

Management can be deferred until life-threatening injuries are addressed. AO/ICRC/WHO consensus: treat with closed reduction and non-operative methods to minimize iatrogenic infection risk. LOE III

2. Emergency Stabilization of Pelvic Ring Injuries

Unstable pelvic ring fractures are among the most immediate orthopedic threats to life. The pelvic ring houses a rich venous plexus and major arteries; disruption can lead to rapid exsanguination of several liters of blood into the retroperitoneal space. LOE II The primary goal is to reduce pelvic volume, creating a tamponade effect to control hemorrhage.

2.1 Clinical Recognition

High-energy mechanisms (MVC, pedestrian-vehicle, falls >3 m, crush injuries) should raise immediate suspicion. LOE II Clinical signs include:

  • Bruising/swelling over iliac crests, pubis, or sacrum
  • Deformity or asymmetry of the pelvis
  • Leg length discrepancy
  • Scrotal hematoma or blood at urethral meatus
  • Hemodynamic instability with high-energy mechanism — assume unstable pelvic fracture until proven otherwise
⚠ Critical Examination Rule
Gentle compressive pressure to the iliac crests should be performed only once, by the most senior clinician. The practice of "springing" or rocking the pelvis is contraindicated — it can dislodge forming clots and worsen hemorrhage. LOE II In patients with obvious deformity or profound shock, defer examination entirely and apply a binder immediately.

2.2 Pelvic Binder Application

Principle: apply circumferential compression to close the pelvic ring, reducing volume and facilitating clot formation. LOE II

⚠ Most Critical Anatomical Detail
The binder must be centered over the greater trochanters of the femurs, NOT the iliac crests. Placing the binder over the iliac crests can cause the fractured hemipelvis to externally rotate and open further, increasing pelvic volume and worsening hemorrhage. LOE II

Improvised Sheet Wrap Technique

In the absence of a commercial device (SAM Pelvic Sling™, T-POD™), an effective binder can be improvised from a standard bed sheet: LOE III

  1. Preparation: Fold a standard bed sheet lengthwise to a width of approximately 20–30 cm.
  2. Placement: Slide the sheet under the patient, centered at the level of the greater trochanters. Minimize pelvic movement.
  3. Application: Two rescuers grasp the ends, pull across the front over the pubic symphysis, applying firm, continuous, opposing traction.
  4. Securing: While maintaining tension, secure by twisting ends and clamping (trauma shears, hemostats, zip ties) or tying a secure knot. Use a windlass (stick twisted into the knot) if a single rescuer.

2.3 Adjunctive Maneuvers

After binder application, if no associated femur/tibia/ankle fractures are present, bring the lower limbs together and internally rotate. Tie the knees and/or ankles in a figure-of-eight bandage. This uses the femurs as levers to help close the pelvic ring. LOE II

Post-Application Monitoring
The pelvic binder is a temporary, resuscitative measure that must remain in place during all patient movements, including transport. If the patient remains unstable despite adequate fluid resuscitation and a correctly applied binder, ongoing arterial bleeding or another hemorrhagic source must be considered. LOE II

3. Temporary Stabilization of Lower Limb Fractures

3.1 Femoral Shaft Fractures

Femoral shaft fractures are severe injuries with blood loss of up to 1.5 liters into the thigh compartments. LOE III Powerful surrounding muscles cause shortening and angulation, necessitating traction.

3.1.1 Skeletal Traction

Skeletal traction provides continuous, controlled longitudinal pull directly on the bone, effective for overcoming strong muscle forces over longer periods. LOE II

  • Indications: Unstable, displaced femoral shaft fractures in adults where stabilization is needed for >48–72 hours. Also for certain acetabular/pelvic fractures. LOE III
  • Equipment: Steinmann pin or K-wire; Kirschner/Bohler bow; sterile supplies; traction bed frame with pulley, rope, and weights (10–15% body weight, typically 5–9 kg). LOE III
  • Pin site: Proximal tibia — approximately 2 cm distal and 2 cm posterior to the tibial tuberosity. Insert lateral to medial to avoid common peroneal nerve. LOE III
  • Counter-traction: Elevate foot of bed on blocks using patient's own body weight. LOE III

3.1.2 Skin Traction and Thomas Splint

The Thomas splint is a non-invasive, reusable device that dramatically reduced femoral fracture mortality from 80% to under 8% during World War I. LOE III

  • Components: Padded ring against ischial tuberosity + two metal side bars extending beyond the foot. Skin traction applied to lower leg with ~3 kg force. LOE III
  • Support: Thigh and leg supported by soft slings or Kramer wire ladder splint to prevent posterior bowing.
  • Contraindications: Associated fractures of pelvis, hip, tibia, or ankle on the same side. LOE III
  • Complications: Pressure sores (groin), peroneal nerve palsy (fibular head), compartment syndrome (tight bandages). LOE III

3.1.3 Improvised Traction Splints

Axillary Crutch Method: An aluminum axillary crutch can be converted into an effective traction splint. The padded axillary bar rests against the ischial tuberosity; skin traction cord is attached to the telescopic distal end; traction is applied by extending the crutch. Aluminum components are lightweight and radiolucent. LOE III

Ski Pole / Stick Method: Any rigid pole with a proximal padded anchor around the upper thigh and a distal ankle hitch. A pulley system (e.g., trucker's hitch) applies traction between the ankle hitch and the pole's distal end. LOE IV

3.2 Tibial Shaft Fractures

3.2.1 POP Back Slab (Primary Tool)

A non-circumferential POP back slab is the safest and most effective method for temporarily immobilizing the majority of tibial shaft fractures. LOE II It provides stability while allowing swelling, minimizing compartment syndrome risk.

A long-leg posterior slab immobilizing both the knee and ankle is required to control rotational forces. LOE II

  1. Padding: Stockinette + 2–3 layers of soft roll from toes to upper thigh. Extra padding over bony prominences (heel, malleoli, fibular head, patella). LOE III
  2. Plaster: 12–15 layers of POP bandage, from metatarsal heads to gluteal fold. Submerge in tepid water, squeeze excess, smooth onto posterior leg. LOE III
  3. Molding: Use palms only (no fingertips) to avoid pressure points. Ankle in neutral 90°, knee in 10–20° flexion until set. LOE III
  4. Securing: Elastic bandage from distal to proximal, not too tightly. LOE III
Why POP over fiberglass?
AO/ICRC guidelines recommend POP for austere settings because it can be removed by soaking in water, whereas fiberglass requires an oscillating saw — which may be unavailable or rendered useless by power failures. LOE III

3.2.2 Calcaneal Skeletal Traction

For highly unstable proximal/distal tibial fractures, calcaneal traction may be considered. However, studies comparing calcaneal traction to short leg splinting for unstable ankle fracture-dislocations found traction was associated with significantly higher rates of skin necrosis and loss of reduction. LOE II A well-molded POP slab remains the preferred initial treatment for most tibial fractures.

4. Temporary Stabilization of Upper Limb Fractures

Upper limb fractures are generally not life-threatening but are critical for preserving function. The primary goal is pain control, prevention of further nerve/vessel injury, and maintaining a position that facilitates recovery. For the upper limb, mobility is the ultimate goal. LOE III

4.1 Principles of Immobilization

Upper Limb Fracture Stabilization Methods
Injury Location Recommended Splint Key Details Evidence
Humeral Shaft Coaptation splint (U-slab) or Velpeau bandage U-shaped slab from axilla, around elbow, up lateral arm. Velpeau secures arm to chest with axillary padding. LOE III
Elbow / Forearm Long-arm posterior splint or Sugar-tong splint Elbow at 90°; sugar-tong provides superior rotational control (volar wrist → around elbow → dorsal hand). LOE III
Wrist (Distal Radius) Volar forearm splint or Sugar-tong splint Immobilizes wrist while leaving fingers free. Wrist in 20–30° extension ("position of function"). LOE III
Consider Nursing Burden
A patient with lower limbs in traction and the uninjured arm completely immobilized in a bulky Velpeau dressing loses all ability to self-care (use trapeze, feed self, assist with hygiene). This near-total dependency places significant strain on limited nursing staff. Consider a less restrictive method that allows some shoulder and hand movement where clinically safe. LOE IV — expert opinion

Upper limb POP splints typically require only 8–10 layers of plaster. After any splint application, a thorough neurovascular check of the hand and fingers is mandatory and must be documented. LOE III

5. Management of Open Fractures

Open fractures are a surgical emergency, particularly in austere settings where contamination risk is high and infection treatment resources are scarce. LOE II

Cardinal Rule
DO NOT CLOSE THESE WOUNDS PRIMARILY. Primary closure of a contaminated traumatic wound creates a closed space ideal for bacterial proliferation and severe infections (gas gangrene, osteomyelitis). LOE II

5.1 Wound Management Protocol

  1. Field / ED Care: Control hemorrhage, cover wound with sterile saline-soaked dressing, splint the limb. Avoid aggressive irrigation (may drive contaminants deeper). LOE II
  2. Surgical Debridement (I&D): As soon as the patient is hemodynamically stable. Wound surgically extended for full visualization. Meticulous excision of all devitalized/contaminated tissue. Bone fragments completely detached from periosteum are removed. Copious irrigation with several liters of sterile saline. LOE II
  3. "Second Look": Many high-energy wounds require a second debridement 24–48 hours later for tissue whose viability was initially uncertain. LOE II
AO/ICRC/WHO Guidelines
Primary internal fixation (plates, screws, IM nails) for open fractures is expressly prohibited in disaster/conflict settings. Foreign body introduction into a contaminated wound carries unacceptably high risk of deep, intractable infection and biofilm formation. LOE III

5.2 Antibiotic and Tetanus Protocols

Administration should begin as soon as possible — delays beyond 3 hours significantly increase infection rates. LOE II

Antibiotic Protocol for Open Fractures in Austere Settings
Gustilo-Anderson Type Wound Characteristics Recommended Antibiotics Evidence
Type I Wound <1 cm, minimal contamination 1st-gen cephalosporin (Cefazolin 2 g IV q8h) LOE II
Type II Wound 1–10 cm, moderate soft tissue damage 1st-gen cephalosporin (Cefazolin 2 g IV q8h) LOE II
Type III Wound >10 cm, extensive damage, high contamination Cephalosporin + Aminoglycoside (Gentamicin 5 mg/kg IV q24h) LOE II
High Contamination Farm injuries, fecal/soil contamination Add high-dose Penicillin (or Clindamycin if allergic) for Clostridium coverage LOE II

Tetanus prophylaxis is mandatory for all open fractures. Tetanus toxoid and, if available, tetanus immune globulin should be administered per standard protocols. LOE II

5.3 Stabilization Options for Open Fractures

  • Skeletal Traction: Pin placed far from the zone of injury — provides robust stabilization while leaving the wound completely accessible. LOE II
  • Cast with a Window: POP cast applied, then a window cut directly over the wound for inspection/dressing changes. Pad window edges; retain the plaster piece to prevent "window edema." LOE III
  • Bivalved Cast: Full cast immediately bivalved (two longitudinal cuts). Held together with elastic wrap — easily opened for wound inspection or swelling accommodation. LOE III

6. Comparative Analysis, Risk Mitigation, and Nursing Care

6.1 Splint vs. Cast — The Compartment Syndrome Imperative

In the acute phase (first 24–72 hours), the risk of acute compartment syndrome dictates the choice between a splint and a circumferential cast. Compartment syndrome occurs when swelling within a closed fascial compartment compromises tissue perfusion, leading to muscle/nerve ischemia and necrosis. LOE II

Table 1: Non-Circumferential Splint vs. Circumferential Cast
Feature Non-Circumferential Splint (Back Slab) Circumferential Cast
Swelling Accommodation Excellent — allows natural swelling Poor — rigid, non-yielding space
Compartment Syndrome Risk Very Low — safest option in acute phase High — major documented risk within 48–72 h
Ease of Application High — faster and easier Moderate — more technically demanding
Immobilization Quality Good — adequate for most acute fractures Excellent — superior rigid immobilization
Ease of Removal / Wound Inspection High — unwrap elastic bandage Low — requires cast saw or shears
Use in Acute Polytrauma STRONGLY RECOMMENDED CONTRAINDICATED until swelling resolved
Compartment Syndrome Recognition
Cardinal symptom: severe pain out of proportion to injury, worsened by passive stretching. Later signs (paresthesia, pallor, paralysis, pulselessness — the "Five P's"). Pulselessness is a very late sign — irreversible muscle damage occurs long before pulses are lost. Polytrauma patients (obtunded, sedated, distracted) are at highest risk because they cannot report escalating pain. LOE II

Protocol Frequency Key Actions
Traction System Check Every shift Weights hanging freely; ropes in pulley grooves; correct patient position for line of pull and counter-traction. If mechanical traction released, maintain manual traction continuously.

Context-Dependent Decision Making
The "optimal" stabilization method is context-dependent, determined by the anticipated duration of stabilization and the reliability of follow-up care. A biomechanically superior method requiring intensive monitoring that cannot be provided is more dangerous than a less perfect method that fails safely. LOE IV — expert opinion

7. Patient Transport and Evacuation

Safe transport of a polytrauma patient with temporarily stabilized fractures is a significant challenge. Every movement risks disrupting stabilization. LOE III

7.1 General Transport Principles

  • All movements coordinated with one designated leader
  • Injured limbs manually supported and moved in unison with the body
  • Pelvic fractures: Scoop stretcher preferred over log-roll to minimize pelvic movement
  • Pre- and post-move checks of all splints/bandages and distal neurovascular status

7.2 Transporting Patients in Skeletal Traction

⚠ Critical Limitation
Transporting a patient in bed-based skeletal traction is exceptionally difficult and should be avoided whenever possible. The frame, pulley, and weight system cannot be safely moved with the patient. LOE III

The only solution is to temporarily replace mechanical traction with manual traction — a dedicated, trained provider must maintain continuous longitudinal pull throughout the entire transport, from bed to vehicle to new bed. This is physically demanding and high-risk for loss of reduction. LOE III

This limitation is a powerful argument for using transportable methods (Thomas splint, POP slab) for any patient likely to require transfer. Applying skeletal traction may effectively "ground" a patient at a lower-level facility, while a Thomas splint preserves the option of evacuation. LOE IV — expert opinion

7.3 Preparing for Transfer to Higher Echelon

  • Documentation on the patient: Write directly on the splint/cast with permanent marker — name, injuries, date/time of injury and splint application, most recent neurovascular exam, last antibiotic/analgesic dose, tetanus status. LOE III
  • Pre-transport cast preparation: Any circumferential cast must be bivalved (split on both sides to skin) before departure to accommodate swelling and prevent compartment syndrome. LOE III
  • Limb elevation: Upper extremity in sling or on abdomen; lower extremity propped on pillows/blankets. LOE III

Conclusion: Decision-Making Framework

The management framework is built upon life-saving priorities in a clear hierarchy:

  1. Immediate hemorrhage control — pelvic binder for unstable pelvic fractures
  2. Urgent infection prevention — antibiotics and debridement for open fractures
  3. Safe temporary stabilization — non-circumferential POP splints for most acute fractures
  4. Femoral shaft management — non-invasive traction splints for transport; skeletal traction reserved for prolonged stabilization with adequate nursing support
  5. Decision context — the "optimal" technique is inextricably linked to the anticipated duration of care and logistical feasibility of transport
Paradigm Shift Required: Success demands a fundamental shift from the pursuit of anatomical perfection to physiological stabilization and damage containment. The objective is to stabilize the patient and limb in the fastest, least invasive, and most resource-efficient manner possible, accepting a temporary, less-than-perfect reduction to preserve life and limb.
