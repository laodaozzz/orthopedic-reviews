Comprehensive Review: Pediatric Ankle Fractures

Classification Systems · Pathophysiology · Management Strategies — An Evidence-Based Educational Summary

1. Introduction: The Unique Landscape of Pediatric Ankle Trauma

Pediatric ankle fractures represent a distinct and complex subset of orthopedic trauma, fundamentally diverging from adult counterparts due to the presence of the physis (growth plate) and the unique biomechanical properties of the immature skeleton. Unlike the adult ankle—where bony architecture and ligamentous complexes are static—the pediatric ankle is a dynamic, evolving organ system. The physis introduces a plane of mechanical weakness that alters fracture propagation, dictates classification, and fundamentally changes management priorities.[1]

1.1 Epidemiology & Incidence Trends

Ankle fractures account for approximately 5% of all pediatric fractures and 9–18% of all physeal injuries.[1] The estimated incidence is 187 per 100,000 inhabitants annually, with a marked predilection for the adolescent demographic (>10 years).[1] The distal tibia is the second most common site of physeal injury after the distal radius.[5]

  • Gender: Historical male predominance, though the gap is narrowing with increased female athletic participation.[1]
  • Peak Age: 11–12 years (girls) and 13–14 years (boys)—aligning with the adolescent growth spurt and "adolescent awkwardness."[6]

1.2 The "Weak Link" Phenomenon: Biomechanical Divergence

In the skeletally immature patient, the hypertrophic zone of the physis is biomechanically weaker than the robust ligamentous complexes.[6] Mechanisms that produce severe ankle sprains in adults—such as supination-inversion—frequently result in physeal fractures (Salter-Harris I–IV) in children.[8]

⚠ Clinical Pearl: The diagnosis of "ankle sprain" in a child with open physes is a diagnosis of exclusion and should be viewed with high suspicion. Predominantly, these injuries represent non-displaced Salter-Harris I fractures.[8] Misdiagnosis can lead to growth arrest or deformity.

Periosteal Interposition: The pediatric periosteum is thicker, more vascular, and more osteogenic than in adults. While this aids rapid healing, it can become entrapped within the fracture gap—particularly in distal tibial injuries—a frequent cause of failed closed reduction.[7]

2. Developmental Anatomy & Physiology of the Distal Tibia

2.1 Ossification & Growth Dynamics

  • The distal tibial physis contributes 3–4 mm/year of longitudinal growth, accounting for 15–20% of total tibial length.[9]
  • The secondary ossification center appears between 6–24 months, forming the medial malleolus by age 7–8.[11]
  • The distal fibular physis appears later (9–24 months) and typically closes 1–2 years after the tibial physis.[9]
  • Fractures propagate through the hypertrophic zone, where chondrocytes are large and the extracellular matrix is sparse.[12]

2.2 The Sequence of Physeal Closure: Kump's Bump Pattern

The distal tibial physis undergoes a gradual, asymmetrical closure over an 18-month period during adolescence (girls ≈ 12–14 yrs; boys ≈ 15–17 yrs).[13]

3.2 The Lauge-Hansen Classification (Adult Paradigm)

Developed in 1950 from cadaveric studies, Lauge-Hansen classifies adult fractures by foot position and force direction, predicated on sequential ligament/bone failure.[4]

Table 2 – Lauge-Hansen Classification (Adult)
PatternFrequencyFailure Sequence
SER (Supination–Ext. Rotation)40–75%Ant. syndesmosis → fibula (spiral) → post. syndesmosis → medial malleolus[4]
SAD (Supination–Adduction)10–20%Lateral ligament tension → medial shear fracture[4]
PER (Pronation–Ext. Rotation)Less commonDeltoid rupture → syndesmosis → high fibula (Maisonneuve)
PAB (Pronation–Abduction)Less commonMedial failure → lateral cortex compression
Limitation: The Lauge-Hansen system assumes ligamentous failure precedes bony failure—a pattern not consistently observed in children due to relative ligament strength vs. physis weakness.[7]

3.3 The Dias-Tachdjian Classification (Pediatric Adaptation)

Introduced in 1978, this system adapts Lauge-Hansen mechanistic principles to the pediatric skeleton, recognizing that forces causing ligamentous tears in adults cause physeal fractures in children.[4] It is considered the most robust system for predicting fracture patterns in skeletally immature patients.

3.3.1 Supination-Inversion (SI) — Most Common (up to 61%)[4]

  • Stage I: Traction injury laterally → distal fibular physis separation (SH I/II) or epiphyseal avulsion.[2]
  • Stage II: Talus impacts medial malleolus → shear fracture of distal tibial physis (SH III/IV). Unstable, high-risk.[2]
vs. Adult SAD: In adults, the medial malleolus fractures vertically through bone. In children, the fracture line travels through physis/epiphysis (SH III/IV), risking medial growth arrest → varus deformity.

3.3.2 Supination-External Rotation (SER) — 20–30%[4]

  • Stage I: External rotation → SH II fracture of distal tibia (long spiral metaphyseal fracture, anterolateral → posteromedial).[2]
  • Stage II: Continued rotation → fibula fracture. Unlike adults, this is often a high spiral fracture of the fibular shaft or SH I/II of distal fibula.[2]
Key Insight: In Dias-Tachdjian SER II, the fibula fracture may occur much higher than in adults due to force transmission through the interosseous membrane. Always image the entire lower leg.

3.3.3 Pronation-Eversion-External Rotation (PEER)

Tension medially → SH I/II fracture of medial malleolus. Talus rotates externally → short oblique/transverse fibula fracture above the syndesmosis.[2] High fibular fracture should raise suspicion for syndesmotic injury.

3.3.4 Supination-Plantar Flexion (SPF)

Foot supinated and plantar flexed → SH I/II fracture of posterior distal tibia as talus is driven posteriorly.[2]

Limitation of Dias-Tachdjian: The system struggles to classify Transitional Fractures (Tillaux and Triplane), which depend on the state of physeal fusion rather than strictly force vectors.[14]

4. Pathophysiology of Transitional Fractures

Transitional fractures are unique to adolescents and occur exclusively during the ~18-month window of distal tibial physeal closure. They are characterized by articular involvement and complex multiplanar geometry.[13]

4.1 Juvenile Tillaux Fracture

  • Definition: Isolated Salter-Harris III fracture of the anterolateral distal tibial epiphysis.[13]
  • Demographics: Girls 12–14, Boys 15–18. Occurs after medial physis closure but before anterolateral fusion.[13]
  • Mechanism: Supination-External Rotation. The AITFL (inserting on the Tubercle of Chaput) is stronger than the remaining open physis → avulsion of the anterolateral epiphyseal fragment.[19]
  • Diagnosis: Can be subtle on AP radiographs. CT is mandatory for assessing displacement; plain films often underestimate the articular step-off.[10]
  • Critical Threshold: Displacement >2 mm = surgical intervention.[10]

4.2 Triplane Fracture

  • Definition: Complex Salter-Harris IV injury spanning three planes—effectively combining SH II (metaphysis) with SH III (epiphysis).[14]
  • Planes:
    • Sagittal: Fracture through epiphysis (seen on AP)
    • Axial: Fracture through physis (seen on CT)
    • Coronal: Fracture through posterior metaphysis (seen on lateral)[14]
  • Variants:
    • 2-Part: Most common. Medial tibia (intact) vs. lateral fragment (epiphysis + metaphysis).
    • 3-Part: Separate anterior epiphyseal and posterior metaphyseal fragments.
    • 4-Part: Includes separate medial malleolar fragment.[14]
  • "Mercedes-Benz" Sign: On axial CT cuts, fracture lines in the epiphysis resemble the Mercedes logo, indicating a 3-part injury.[23]

5. Management Strategies

Management has evolved with a distinct trend toward aggressive management of articular congruity in transitional fractures, balanced by a desire to minimize iatrogenic physeal damage in younger children.[12],[25]

5.1 Conservative Management

Indications:

  • SH I and II fractures of the distal fibula (low risk)
  • Non-displaced SH I and II of the tibia
  • Transitional fractures (Tillaux/Triplane) with <2 mm of displacement[10]

Protocol:

  • Immobilization: Long-leg cast initially (flexing the knee relaxes the gastrocnemius, neutralizing deforming forces).[13]
  • Duration: 3–4 weeks in long-leg cast → transition to short-leg walking cast/boot for 2–4 additional weeks.[19]
  • Monitoring: Weekly radiographs for the first 3 weeks to detect loss of reduction.[13]

5.2 The "2 mm Rule" for Surgical Intervention

Universal Threshold: The decision dichotomy between conservative and surgical management for intra-articular fractures (SH III, IV, Tillaux, Triplane) is >2 mm of residual displacement after closed reduction.II[10],[21]

Evidence: Articular step-offs >2 mm lead to significantly higher rates of post-traumatic osteoarthritis and poor functional outcomes (AOFAS scores).[10] In SH III/IV injuries, anatomic reduction also realigns the physeal layers to prevent bony bar formation.[12]

5.3 Surgical Techniques

5.3.1 Closed Reduction and Percutaneous Pinning (CRPP)

Used for unstable extra-articular fractures (SH II) or minimally displaced intra-articular fractures. Reduction involves reversing the mechanism of injury. Fixation with K-wires or cannulated screws. In younger children (>2 years growth remaining), smooth K-wires preferred; avoid crossing physis or cross centrally with minimal passes.[18]

5.3.2 Open Reduction and Internal Fixation (ORIF)

Required when closed reduction fails (often due to interposed periosteum) or for complex articular fractures exceeding the 2 mm threshold.[7]

  • Anterolateral Approach: For Tillaux and lateral Triplane fractures.
  • Anteromedial Approach: For medial malleolus fractures or medial triplane variants.
  • Screw Trajectory in Triplane Fractures:
    • Parallel to physis (traditional AO): Avoids crossing physis.
    • Perpendicular to fracture: Maximizes compression; acceptable in transitional fractures (near skeletal maturity).[21]
  • Periosteal Management: Critical step—extricate entrapped periosteal flap to allow anatomic reduction.[7]

5.3.3 Arthroscopically Assisted Reduction

An emerging standard for Tillaux and Triplane fractures.[28],[30]

  • Benefits: Direct articular visualization superior to fluoroscopy for judging <2 mm step-off; diagnosis/treatment of associated intra-articular pathology (loose bodies, chondral injuries).[28]
  • Outcomes: Excellent functional scores (AOFAS >90), reduced postoperative stiffness, potentially lower long-term arthritis rates.[29]

6. Complications

The complication profile shifts with age: in young children (Dias-Tachdjian patterns), the primary risk is growth arrest; in adolescents (transitional patterns), the primary risk is post-traumatic arthritis.

6.1 Premature Physeal Closure (PPC)

  • Incidence: 14–30% depending on fracture type.III[33]
  • Risk Factors:
    • SH III, IV, V injuries (highest risk)[12]
    • Displacement: every 1 mm of initial displacement increases PPC risk by ~15%[18]
    • Multiple forceful reduction attempts (damage germinal layer)[34]
  • Consequences:
    • Complete arrest → Limb Length Discrepancy (LLD)
    • Partial arrest → Angular deformity (medial arrest → varus; lateral → valgus)[18]
  • Transitional Fractures: PPC is rarely clinically significant (0–8%) because the physis is already closing.[10]

6.2 Post-Traumatic Osteoarthritis

  • Prevalence: Radiographic OA in up to 50% of intra-articular fractures at 10+ years, particularly those with residual displacement.III[35]
  • Prevention: The only modifiable risk factor is quality of reduction. Anatomic reduction (<2 mm) is the strongest predictor of joint survival.[26] Arthroscopic assistance has been shown to reduce OA rates.[28]

7. Postoperative Management & Rehabilitation

Phase I (Weeks 0–4): Protection & Edema Control

  • Strict Non-Weight Bearing (NWB)
  • Long-leg cast or splint immobilization
  • Elevation; no ROM exercises[24]

Phase II (Weeks 4–6): Mobilization

  • Transition to CAM boot or short-leg cast
  • Partial Weight Bearing (PWB) if radiographic callus visible
  • Active dorsiflexion/plantarflexion ROM; avoid inversion/eversion
  • "Toe crunches" for intrinsic foot strength[40]

Phase III (Weeks 8–12): Strengthening & Return

  • Full Weight Bearing (FWB) in shoe
  • Calf raises, Theraband resistance, proprioception (BAPS board, single-leg stance)
  • Return to sport at 3–4 months pending functional clearance (single-leg hop without pain)[41]

8. Conclusion

The management of pediatric ankle fractures requires a nuanced understanding of the interplay between injury mechanism, skeletal maturity, and classification. The Dias-Tachdjian system remains the gold standard for skeletally immature children, while transitional fracture patterns (Tillaux and Triplane) require a distinct diagnostic and therapeutic approach during the adolescent window of physeal closure.

Current evidence strongly supports:

  • The "2 mm rule" as the surgical threshold for intra-articular injuries[10]
  • A definitive trend toward arthroscopically assisted reduction[28],[30]
  • Increasing adoption of bioabsorbable fixation, offering equivalent outcomes while eliminating removal surgeries[25],[31]
Core Principle: In the young child, the priority is preserving the physis to prevent deformity. In the adolescent, the priority is restoring the articular surface to prevent osteoarthritis. Accurate classification using Dias-Tachdjian and recognition of transitional patterns are the cornerstones of achieving these outcomes.
