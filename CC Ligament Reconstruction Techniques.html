An Exhaustive Evidence-Based Analysis of Suspensory Button Fixation — Interactive Educational Summary

1. Introduction: The Evolution of Acromioclavicular Joint Stabilization

The acromioclavicular (AC) joint functions as the primary strut connecting the axial skeleton to the upper extremity. Injuries are ubiquitous among young, active populations in contact sports or high-velocity trauma. While low-grade injuries (Rockwood Types I–II) are managed non-operatively, and high-grade injuries (Types IV–VI) mandate surgical intervention, Type III injuries remain one of the most contentious topics in modern sports medicine.[1]

Legacy Techniques and Their Limitations

The Weaver-Dunn procedure, long considered the gold standard, involved coracoacromial (CA) ligament transfer. However, biomechanical analyses demonstrated the CA ligament possesses only ~25% of the stiffness and strength of the native CC complex, leading to graft elongation and recurrent deformity.[2]

The clavicular hook plate addressed stability by leveraging the subacromial space but introduced iatrogenic pathology: subacromial impingement, rotator cuff tearing, acromial osteolysis, and obligatory second surgery for removal.[4]

Paradigm Shift Contemporary orthopaedic surgery has coalesced around suspensory fixation—devices such as the TightRope (Arthrex) and Dog Bone button system utilizing ultra-high-molecular-weight polyethylene (UHMWPE) sutures and titanium cortical buttons to recreate superior-inferior stability.[7]

2. Anatomical and Biomechanical Imperatives

2.1 The Coracoclavicular Ligament Complex

The CC ligament complex is a distinct bipartite system:[6]

  • Conoid Ligament: Originates from the posteromedial coracoid base, inserts onto the conoid tubercle. Its vertical orientation makes it the primary restraint against superior clavicular migration. It is the stronger bundle, failing at higher loads. LOE II
  • Trapezoid Ligament: Situated anterolateral to the conoid, inserts onto the trapezoid line. Its horizontal trajectory resists AC joint compression and posterior clavicular translation during axial loading. LOE II
Biomechanical Benchmarks Native CC complex load-to-failure: 500–800 N. Modern suspensory constructs with heavy suture tapes: 1,000–1,300 N — supraphysiological strength serving as an internal splint during biologic healing.[7]

2.2 The AC Capsule and Horizontal Instability

A critical insight from biomechanical literature: the AC ligaments (superior, inferior, anterior, posterior) are the primary restraints to horizontal translation, particularly in the anteroposterior plane.[8] Neglecting the AC capsule leads to persistent dynamic posterior instability — a well-reduced joint on static AP radiographs may show significant posterior clavicular translation during cross-body adduction.

Expert consensus is moving toward "hybrid" reconstructions combining suspensory CC fixation (vertical) with AC cerclage or capsular repair (horizontal).[1] LOE III

2.3 The "Safe Zone" for Coracoid Drilling

⚠ Critical Surgical Constraint The safe zone is at the coracoid base, 25–30 mm posterior to the anterior tip.[10] Safe drill diameter: <7 mm (males), <6 mm (females).[12] Drill tunnels must be kept small (2.4–3.0 mm) and perfectly centered. Eccentric drilling or large-diameter drills (≥4.0 mm) create stress risers predisposing to catastrophic coracoid fracture.[9]

3. Surgical Technique: Suspensory Button Fixation

3.1 Preoperative Planning

  • Zanca view (10–15° cephalic tilt) is mandatory to visualize the AC joint without scapular overlap.[14]
  • Bilateral comparative views (weighted/unweighted) for CCD measurement — side-to-side difference >100% indicates Type V injury.
  • Axillary lateral views critical for assessing Type IV (posterior) displacement.

TimingTissue StatusRecommended TechniqueGraft Augmentation

Chronic (>3 weeks)Scarred / resorbedSuspensory + biologic augmentationRequired (semitendinosus or tibialis anterior allograft)[8]

3.2 Patient Positioning & Anesthesia

  • General anesthesia + interscalene nerve block.[2]
  • Beach-chair position — gravity assists inferior translation of the arm for reduction.
  • Pneumatic arm holder (SPIDER or similar) for dynamic manipulation.

3.3 Diagnostic Arthroscopy

  1. Posterior viewing portal: ~2 cm inferior and 1 cm medial to posterolateral acromion corner.
  2. Anterior working portal: High in the rotator interval for instrumentation and coracoid visualization.[10]
  3. Diagnostic sweep to rule out concomitant pathology (labral tears, rotator cuff injuries present in 18–30% of high-grade AC injuries).[4]

3.4 Coracoid Preparation

🔑 Pearl: "Mediolateral Defining" Visualize the medial and lateral borders of the coracoid to place the drill guide perfectly centered. Failure to clear the base adequately is the primary cause of eccentric drilling and subsequent fracture.[10] Use a 70° arthroscope to peer "around the corner" of the coracoid base.[15]

Neurovascular Warning: The musculocutaneous nerve typically enters the conjoint tendon 3–5 cm distal to the coracoid tip. Care must be taken medially to avoid the neurovascular bundle.[10]

3.5 Clavicular Approach

  • 2–3 cm incision over the distal clavicle, centered ~25–30 mm medial to the AC joint (conoid tubercle).
  • Subperiosteal elevation of the deltotrapezial fascia for robust flaps — this fascia is crucial for AC stability and must be repaired meticulously.[15]
  • Small Hohmann retractors anterior and posterior for neurovascular protection.[17]

3.6 Drill Tunnel Creation

⚠ Highest-Risk Step Margin for error measured in millimeters. Follow the "Safe Zone" protocol rigorously.
  1. Guide placement: Specialized AC drill guide hooked beneath coracoid base through anterior portal.[10]
  2. Fluoroscopic verification (MANDATORY): True AP view with 15–30° cephalic tilt to superimpose coracoid margins. Pin must bisect coracoid width.[19]
  3. Drilling: 2.4 mm cannulated drill (4.0 mm+ drills abandoned due to fracture risk).[9] "Peck" drill to prevent thermal necrosis. Stop immediately upon breaching inferior coracoid cortex.

3.7 Implant Passage and Fixation

  1. Shuttling: Suture shuttle passed through tunnel, retrieved from subcoracoid space via anterior portal.[20]
  2. Seating inferior button: Pull superiorly; visually confirm button deployed flush on cortical bone with arthroscope. Soft tissue entrapment causes "bouncing" and early failure.
  3. Reduction & superior fixation: Manual clavicle reduction with axial load at elbow. Fluoroscopic confirmation of anatomic reduction.
  4. Tensioning:
    • Knotless systems: Self-locking mechanism engages at "hard stop."
    • Knotted systems: Sliding locking knot + static half-hitches under reduction tension.[7]

3.8 Biologic Augmentation (Chronic/Revision)

Semitendinosus allograft is most common. Graft is typically passed around the coracoid (loop technique) to avoid a second tunnel. The TightRope/Dog Bone construct protects the graft during the 12-week integration phase.[3] [8]

5.2 Training Recommendations

  • Cadaveric labs: Essential for 3D understanding of drill guide orientation relative to neurovascular structures.
  • Mentorship: Experienced surgeon scrubbing for first 5–10 cases.
  • Patient selection: Avoid small coracoids or poor bone quality during learning phase.[34]

6. Pitfalls and Complications: The "Fracture Crisis"

6.1 Coracoid Fracture: Mechanisms and Prevention

Historical fracture rates reached 20% with large (4.0–6.0 mm) or multiple tunnels.[9] The stress concentration factor (Kt) increases significantly as hole diameter to bone width ratio increases.

The "Safe Zone" Protocol for Prevention
  1. Drill size: Limit to 2.4 mm or 3.0 mm[9]
  2. Target: Base of coracoid, 25–30 mm posterior to tip[10]
  3. Visualization: 70° scope for centering; fluoroscopy (True AP) before reaming
  4. Single tunnel: Avoid double-tunnel techniques in the coracoid[17]

6.2 Management of Intraoperative Fracture

Bailout Technique If the coracoid fractures: do not attempt tunnel salvage. Switch immediately to a coracoid loop (cerclage) technique — pass sutures/graft around the entire coracoid base. Outcome is biomechanically comparable to tunnel fixation.[3]

6.3 Postoperative Fracture

Non-operative management is often successful if the suspensory fixation holds reduction. Case reports demonstrate excellent functional outcomes and return to military duty/sports.[38]

6.4 Other Complications

  • Button subsidence ("windshield wiper" effect): The Dog Bone button was designed to increase contact surface area, distributing load more effectively.[7]
  • Infection: Rare deep infection (~3.8%) requires irrigation and debridement. Graft removal if infected; hardware may be retained in acute settings.[30]
  • Hardware irritation: 26.7% rate; superior knot stack prominence may require removal under local anesthesia.[14]

7. Postoperative Rehabilitation Protocol

Phase 1: Protection (Weeks 0–6)

Sling at all times (except hygiene). Passive ROM only — pendulum exercises. No active elevation, no cross-body adduction, no lifting >1–2 lbs.[18]

Phase 2: Activation (Weeks 6–12)

Discontinue sling. Active-assisted → Active ROM. Focus on scapular mechanics (retraction/protraction). No heavy lifting; avoid bench press/dips.

Phase 3: Strengthening (Months 3–6)

Rotator cuff and periscapular strengthening. Light throwing/jogging. Goal: full ROM and symmetric strength.

Phase 4: Return to Sport (Month 6+)

Criteria: full painless ROM, >90% strength vs. contralateral side, radiographic stability. Contact sports typically allowed after 6 months.[18]

10. Conclusion

Arthroscopic coracoclavicular ligament reconstruction using suspensory button devices represents a mature, evidence-based solution to high-grade AC joint instability. By adhering to principles of anatomic restoration, surgeons can provide outcomes functionally superior to rigid fixation methods while avoiding mandatory hardware removal morbidity.

However, the technique is technically demanding. The margin for error during coracoid drilling is unforgiving. Success is contingent upon disciplined adherence to the "Safe Zone" protocol — small-diameter (2.4 mm) drills, rigorous fluoroscopic confirmation, and precise anatomical knowledge. For the novice surgeon, the learning curve is real, with the first 20 cases carrying heightened risk. Yet with appropriate training and mentorship, this procedure offers a robust, reproducible, and biomechanically sound solution for one of the shoulder's most challenging injuries.
