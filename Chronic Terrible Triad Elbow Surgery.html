1. The Pathologic Evolution: From Acute Instability to Chronic Stiffness

The "terrible triad" injury (TTI) of the elbow is a complex fracture-dislocation pattern defined by the constellation of an elbow dislocation, a radial head or neck fracture, and a fracture of the ulnar coronoid process. Level II The injury mechanism is typically a fall onto an extended, supinated forearm, which generates a destructive combination of axial, valgus, and posterolateral rotatory forces.

This force application creates a predictable, circular cascade of soft-tissue and osseous failure — the "Horri circle" — which progresses from lateral to medial. The primary lesion is disruption of the lateral collateral ligament (LCL) complex, specifically the lateral ulnar collateral ligament (LUCL). This is followed by disruption of the anterior and posterior capsules and, with sufficient energy, final-stage failure of the medial collateral ligament (MCL). The associated fractures of the radial head (a key secondary valgus and posterolateral stabilizer) and the coronoid process (the primary anterior and varus buttress) create a profoundly unstable joint.

⚠️ Key Conceptual Shift: Neglected vs. Acute TTI

The primary goal of acute TTI surgery is to restore stability specifically to allow early mobilization. A "neglected" or "chronic" TTI, operationally defined as one untreated for more than 3 to 4 weeks, represents a complete pathologic transformation. In this state, the primary clinical problem is no longer acute instability, but rather a profound, functionally limiting joint contracture.

This post-traumatic stiffness — which renders the elbow "notoriously unforgiving" — is a primary cause of functional impairment and a frequent indication for reoperation. The pathology of this contracture is multifactorial and is classified as:

  • Extrinsic Causes (Extra-articular): Capsular contracture (arthrofibrosis) and heterotopic ossification (HO). The joint capsule and collateral ligaments become thickened and fibrotic, with histological analysis revealing increased collagen cross-linking and proliferation of highly contractile myofibroblasts. Concurrently, HO creates a non-anatomic, extrinsic bony block to motion.
  • Intrinsic Causes (Intra-articular): Sequelae of the original trauma. Unhealed, malunited, or nonunited radial head and coronoid fragments create an incongruous joint surface, acting as an intrinsic bony block. This incongruity rapidly accelerates post-traumatic joint arthrosis, leading to formation of impinging osteophytes.
  • Mixed Type: Most commonly seen in neglected TTI. The initial trauma, malunion, and instability (intrinsic) necessitate prolonged immobilization. This immobilization and soft-tissue trauma cause the extrinsic capsular contracture and provide the inflammatory nidus for HO formation. The extrinsic block then locks the elbow, accelerating destructive forces on the malunited (intrinsic) joint — a vicious, feed-forward loop.

2. Preoperative Stratification and Surgical Philosophy

2.1 Clinical and Radiographic Evaluation

A thorough clinical assessment of active and passive range of motion (ROM) in both the flexion-extension and pronation-supination arcs is paramount. A meticulous neurovascular examination, with special attention to the ulnar nerve, must be performed. Any pre-existing sensory deficits or a positive Tinel's sign over the cubital tunnel are critical findings that bias the surgeon toward nerve identification and transposition.

While standard radiographs are necessary, a Computed Tomography (CT) scan is indispensable for surgical planning in a chronic, stiff elbow. In this setting, the anatomy is grossly distorted by malunion and HO, obliterating normal surgical intervals. The CT scan functions as the central "surgical road map," allowing the surgeon to:

  1. Define the precise location and extent of heterotopic ossification.
  2. Assess the status of the original fractures, including radial head malunion or coronoid deficiency.
  3. Evaluate for joint incongruity, intra-articular loose bodies, and the extent of post-traumatic arthrosis.
  4. Identify neurovascular structures that may be encased within channels of heterotopic bone, allowing for a safer planned "normal-to-abnormal" dissection.

2.2 Patient Selection and Surgical Goals

Surgical intervention is indicated for functional impairment that limits activities of daily living (ADLs). The primary goal of this complex, single-stage reconstructive salvage is to restore a functional, painless arc of motion, defined as:

✅ Target Functional Arc
  • Flexion-Extension: 30° to 130° (a 100° arc)
  • Pronation-Supination: 50° of pronation and 50° of supination (a 100° arc)

Achieving this arc intraoperatively is the prerequisite for success, as it enables aggressive, immediate post-operative mobilization protocols essential to inhibit the reformation of arthrofibrosis and recurrent contracture.

3. Operative Management, Step 1: Arthrolysis for Joint Release

The essential first step is arthrolysis — the liberation of the joint from its "tether" of fibrotic soft tissue. This must be accomplished to regain passive motion and to physically access the underlying osseous pathology.

3.1 Open Contracture Release

For a neglected TTI with extensive stiffness, open contracture release is the workhorse approach, as it is more robust and allows for subsequent reconstructive steps. The release must be circumferential:

  • To gain extension: Release all anterior tethers, including the thickened anterior capsule.
  • To gain flexion: Release the posterior capsule and contracted posterior band of the MCL.

This is often performed via a "column procedure," utilizing combined medial and lateral approaches. The lateral approach (Kocher interval) provides access to the anterior capsule and posterior joint. The medial approach is essential for identifying and transposing the ulnar nerve — strongly recommended in all cases with pre-operative nerve dysfunction and in any case where a significant correction in the flexion-extension arc is anticipated.

3.2 Arthroscopic Contracture Release

Arthroscopic arthrolysis involves using standard portals to debride fibrotic tissue and release the capsule. Studies on post-traumatic contracture demonstrate an average gain of 63° in flexion/extension and 38° in pronation/supination. Level III It offers improved joint visualization and lower surgical morbidity.

3.3 Comparative Analysis: Open vs. Arthroscopic

🔴 Critical Distinction for Neglected TTI

Arthroscopic release is relatively contraindicated in the setting of "extensive heterotopic ossification, severe arthritis, or marked muscular contracture" — the very definition of a neglected TTI. Furthermore, arthroscopy alone cannot perform the essential reconstructive steps of radial head replacement or coronoid grafting. Open arthrolysis is a necessity for the definitive reconstructive procedure.

4. Operative Management, Step 2: Addressing Osseous Blocks

4.1 Surgical Excision of Heterotopic Ossification (HO)

Open excision remains the gold standard for removing motion-limiting HO. The key principle is to identify normal anatomic planes first and then dissect from this "normal" tissue toward the "abnormal" heterotopic bone. All HO limiting flexion, extension, or rotation must be excised. The olecranon fossa must be thoroughly debrided, which may require excision of the terminal 1–1.5 cm of the olecranon tip if impingement persists.

📌 Critical Debate: Timing of HO Excision

Traditional (Delayed) View: Wait for HO maturation (radiographic consolidation, typically 6–12 months post-injury) before excision, fearing early intervention would lead to high recurrence.

Modern (Early) View: Evidence now demonstrates this delay is detrimental. Waiting allows formation of secondary, irreversible soft-tissue contractures and muscle atrophy. A review of the literature found no significant difference in recurrence rates between early excision (average 6–7 months) and delayed excision (average 23–33 months), but patients in the early excision group had significantly better restoration of elbow ROM. 4.2 Management of Ulnohumeral Impingement (Arthrosis)

The Outerbridge-Kashiwagi (O-K) procedure is an elegant and effective technique for addressing the intrinsic bony block from post-traumatic arthrosis.

  • Technique: Performed through a posterior, triceps-splitting approach. The surgeon creates a window (fenestration) in the floor of the olecranon fossa.
  • Mechanism: This single posterior window allows for debridement of the posterior compartment (olecranon osteophytes) and provides a direct portal to reach through and debride the anterior compartment (coronoid osteophytes and coronoid fossa).
  • Technical Pearl: The window should be created by first making circumferential fenestrations with a 2-mm drill, then completing the osteotomy with an osteotome. High-speed burr use is specifically avoided to decrease the theoretical risk of HO formation.

Outcomes for ulnohumeral arthroplasty are favorable, with significant pain relief and modest but functional ROM gains, and good to excellent results reported in approximately 80% of patients. Level IV

5. Operative Management, Step 3: Reconstruction of the Triad

This is the definitive and most critical phase. After liberating the joint from its soft-tissue and osseous blocks, the surgeon must reconstruct the original, now-malunited components of the TTI. Failure to restore stability will lead to recurrent subluxation, preventing early mobilization and guaranteeing the return of a stiff, painful, and arthritic elbow.

5.1 Management of the Radial Head

In a neglected TTI, the original radial head fracture is now a symptomatic malunion or nonunion. Radial Head Arthroplasty (RHA) is the treatment of choice. The malunited/nonunited native radial head is excised and replaced with a metallic prosthesis.

🚫 Strong Contraindication

Radial head excision without replacement is strongly contraindicated. In the setting of a TTI with its associated ligamentous injuries, simple excision leads to disastrous instability, proximal radial migration, and rapid-onset arthrosis. RHA restores the radiocapitellar articulation and properly tensions the LCL, restoring varus stability.

5.2 Reconstruction of Chronic Coronoid Deficiency

The coronoid process is the primary anterior and varus buttress of the ulnohumeral joint. In a neglected state, the original fragment is often malunited, resorbed, or nonunited, resulting in a "coronoid deficiency" that predisposes to chronic instability. A chronic, deficient, unstable coronoid must be reconstructed.

The most elegant and efficient solution is to use a fragment of the excised native radial head (being removed for the RHA) as an osteochondral autograft to rebuild the coronoid. This approach connects two steps of the procedure, avoids all donor site morbidity, and has been used successfully for defects as large as 75% of the coronoid.

5.3 Late Reconstruction of the Collateral Ligaments

  • Lateral Collateral Ligament (LCL) Complex: This is the primary soft-tissue lesion in a TTI. In the chronic state, the attenuated and scarred ligament leads to symptomatic Posterolateral Rotatory Instability (PLRI). This must be reconstructed, typically using the O'Driscoll technique with an autograft. Long-term studies show a 97% success rate, resolution of instability, and excellent functional scores (mean MEPS 96). Level IV
  • Medial Collateral Ligament (MCL) Complex: Typically managed with benign neglect. If the primary static stabilizers (coronoid, RHA, and LCL) are fully reconstructed, the elbow will be stable. Late MCL reconstruction is reserved only for the rare case of persistent valgus instability after all other components have been definitively addressed.

6. Salvage Pathway: Total Elbow Arthroplasty (TEA)

In some cases, the neglected TTI is so advanced — with extensive callus, ankylosis, and destructive end-stage post-traumatic arthritis — that the joint is no longer reconstructible. For these patients, Total Elbow Arthroplasty (TEA) is the final salvage procedure. The primary goals of TEA are pain relief and restoration of a functional ROM.

⚠️ The "Faustian Bargain" of TEA in Young Patients

TEA is rarely preferred in patients under 50 years old. The procedure is associated with very high rates of long-term mechanical failure. Studies report complication rates of 25% and reoperation rates of 22% at 10-year follow-up in patients <40 years. Long-term failure rates as high as 82% have been cited in patients <50. This trades long-term durability for immediate function.

7. Postoperative Management and Complication Mitigation

7.1 Prophylaxis of Recurrent Heterotopic Ossification

HO is a primary driver of recurrent stiffness. Prophylaxis after HO excision is essential.

  • NSAIDs (Indomethacin): Routine use is common practice but is not supported by high-level evidence for elbow trauma. A 2023 Level I Randomized Controlled Trial comparing indomethacin versus placebo found no significant difference in HO incidence (49% vs. 55%, P=.52) or in ROM/functional scores. Level I
  • Radiation Therapy (RT): External beam radiation appears to be an effective strategy. A large retrospective review of high-risk patients receiving a single fraction of post-operative RT (typically 7–8 Gy) found the protocol to be safe and efficacious in preventing clinically significant HO. While 48% developed radiographic HO, in all cases it was "small and not functionally significant." Level III
✅ Evidence-Based Recommendation

Surgeons should move away from ineffective NSAID prophylaxis and toward single-dose radiation therapy (7–8 Gy) for high-risk patients undergoing HO excision.

7.2 Management of Iatrogenic Nerve Palsy

Delayed-Onset Ulnar Neuritis (DOUN) occurs in up to 11% of arthroscopic releases and is a surgical emergency.

  • Risk Factors: Diagnosis of HO and pre-operative neurological symptoms.
  • Presentation: The "rapidly progressive" form (58% of DOUN cases) presents within the first week with increasing sensorimotor ulnar neuropathy and, counter-intuitively, a rapidly deteriorating range of motion. This loss of motion is often misdiagnosed as "stiffness."
  • Treatment: Urgent ulnar nerve transposition.

7.3 Postoperative Rehabilitation Protocol

The rehabilitation protocol is dictated by the surgeon's intraoperative assessment of stability:

  • If Stable (RHA, stable coronoid, capsule release only): Custom posterior orthotic at 90° flexion and neutral rotation. The patient removes the splint 5 times daily starting on postoperative day 1 for immediate active and active-assisted ROM exercises.
  • If Unstable (LCL Reconstruction): A hinged elbow brace locked at 60° of extension to protect the LCL repair. This block is gradually removed, allowing an extra 30° of extension every 2 weeks to achieve full extension by 6 weeks.
  • Strengthening exercises (biceps, triceps, grip) are initiated at 6 weeks, post-radiographic confirmation of healing.

8. Functional Outcomes and Synthesis Algorithm

The expected outcomes for the chronic, neglected population are more guarded than for acute TTI repair. While systematic reviews of acute repair show excellent mean MEPS of 78–95 and DASH scores of 9–31, a more realistic expectation for motion gain is the 35–40° in total arc reported for complex contracture releases. Patient-reported outcomes for surgical release are high, with significant pain reduction and satisfaction.

8.1 Sequential Salvage Algorithm for the Neglected TTI

Step 1 — Pre-op: CT scan to map pathology. Counsel patient on high complication rates and risk of nerve injury.
Step 2 — Exposure/Nerve: Open approach; identify and transpose the ulnar nerve.
Step 3 — Release: Perform extensive open arthrolysis (anterior and posterior capsular release).
Step 4 — Resect: Perform early surgical excision of all motion-limiting HO. If arthrosis is present, perform Outerbridge-Kashiwagi procedure.
Step 5 — Reconstruct: (a) Excise malunited radial head → perform RHA. (b) Reconstruct coronoid deficiency (e.g., with excised radial head fragment). (c) Perform late LCL reconstruction.
Step 6 — Assess MCL: Assess for valgus instability. Only repair the MCL if instability persists after all other components are addressed.
Step 7 — Prophylaxis: Order single-dose post-operative RT (7–8 Gy) to prevent HO recurrence. Do not rely on Indomethacin.
Step 8 — Rehab: Institute immediate, aggressive AROM in a splint, or hinged brace if LCL was reconstructed.
Salvage — If Failed or Non-Reconstructible: Proceed to TEA. Counsel patient on high long-term failure rates in young patients.
