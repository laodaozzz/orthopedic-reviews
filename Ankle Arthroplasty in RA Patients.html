Total Ankle Arthroplasty in Rheumatoid Arthritis

Outcomes, Complications, and Perioperative Management — Evidence-Based Interactive Summary

Executive Summary & Historical Context

The management of end-stage ankle arthritis in rheumatoid arthritis (RA) represents one of the most complex challenges in modern orthopaedics. Up to a substantial proportion of RA patients eventually develop symptomatic ankle involvement II. The pathophysiology involves synovial proliferation, cartilage degradation, and distinctive periarticular erosions destabilising the talocrural joint [1].

Historically, ankle arthrodesis (fusion) served as the gold standard, providing mechanical reliability in a population with poor bone stock and compromised soft tissues III [5]. However, fusion places supraphysiologic stress on adjacent subtalar and talonavicular joints—joints often already ravaged by RA. Long-term follow-up reveals cascading adjacent-segment disease, progressive disability, and the need for further extensive fusions [7, 8].

Total ankle arthroplasty (TAA) emerged as a function-preserving alternative. Early first-generation implants (1970s–1980s) were disastrous, with catastrophic aseptic loosening rates within five years III [5, 12]. These failures were particularly pronounced in RA patients due to poor bone quality and active osteolysis [12]. It was not until the advent of third-generation, porous-coated, bone-sparing implants that TAA became viable for this demographic II [6].

🔑 Key Insight Contemporary data suggest that properly selected RA patients can achieve survivorship and functional outcomes comparable to, and in some metrics superior to, patients with primary osteoarthritis (OA). This success is contingent upon sophisticated perioperative medical management and surgical strategies designed to mitigate infection and osteoporotic subsidence risks [1, 15].

Pathophysiology of the Rheumatoid Ankle

The Osteoporotic Substrate

Bone quality in RA is compromised by two synergistic forces: the disease itself and its treatment. Systemic inflammation driven by TNF-α and IL-6 upregulates the RANKL pathway, stimulating osteoclast activity while suppressing osteoblast-mediated bone formation I [1]. This results in generalised osteopenia and focal juxta-articular osteoporosis, creating a "soft" bony bed for implant fixation [1].

This is exacerbated by chronic glucocorticoid therapy, the leading cause of secondary osteoporosis I [4]. Corticosteroids induce rapid apoptosis of osteocytes and osteoblasts, leading to profound uncoupling of bone remodelling. The risk of nontraumatic osteonecrosis—particularly of the talus—is significantly elevated in long-term steroid users [4]. For the TAA surgeon, this presents a critical biomechanical challenge: the talar component must resist substantial forces during gait, yet it relies on a talar dome that may be structurally hollow or necrotic [10].

The Compromised Soft Tissue Envelope

The anterior ankle soft tissue envelope is notoriously thin, consisting of little more than skin, subcutaneous fat, and the extensor retinaculum II [20]. In RA patients this envelope is frequently atrophic due to chronic steroid use inhibiting collagen synthesis and fibroblast proliferation, combined with RA-associated vasculitis impairing microvascular perfusion [3, 21]. This creates a "perfect storm" for wound complications [20].

⚠ Clinical Pearl The RA cohort consistently demonstrates a higher risk of major wound complications and subsequent deep infections compared to OA cohorts II [9]. The soft tissue envelope is the primary mode of early failure.

Preoperative Evaluation

Predictive Value of ESR & CRP

Elevated preoperative ESR and CRP are traditional red flags for latent infection in joint arthroplasty II [23]. However, applying these thresholds to RA patients is fraught with difficulty due to the "noise" of systemic inflammation [24]. Research indicates that RA patients often have baseline elevations that do not correlate with surgical site infection risk in the same linear fashion as in OA patients [24]. One TKA study found no statistically significant link between modest preoperative CRP/ESR elevations and postoperative wound complications or PJI [23].

The nuance lies in distinguishing between controlled systemic inflammation and uncontrolled flare. Composite scores such as the Disease Activity Score 28 (DAS28) provide more granular assessment II [28]:

  • DAS28 < 2.6: Remission
  • DAS28 2.6–3.2: Low disease activity
  • DAS28 3.2–5.1: Moderate disease activity
  • DAS28 > 5.1: High disease activity [29]
📊 Evidence Update A pivotal study comparing outcomes in patients with active inflammatory arthritis versus quiescent disease found no significant difference in implant survivorship at intermediate follow-up II [14]. Similarly, a systematic review found no significant differences in early perioperative complication rates between well-controlled RA patients and OA patients II [2].

Conclusion: The diagnosis of RA is less important than the physiologic status of the patient at the time of surgery. If the patient is medically optimised, the "rheumatoid factor" alone does not condemn the implant to failure [1].

Perioperative Pharmacological Management

The 2022 American College of Rheumatology (ACR) and American Association of Hip and Knee Surgeons (AAHKS) guidelines provide the most authoritative framework II [15].

Traditional DMARDs

The most significant shift is the permissive stance toward non-biologic DMARDs. Historically agents like methotrexate were stopped weeks before surgery; current consensus, supported by multiple meta-analyses, is that the risk of flare upon discontinuation outweighs the negligible infection risk associated with stopping them [15].

✅ 2022 ACR/AAHKS Recommendation Conditionally recommended to CONTINUE methotrexate, leflunomide, hydroxychloroquine, sulfasalazine, and apremilast through the perioperative period II [15].

Biologic Agents & Targeted Synthetic DMARDs

The overarching principle is to time surgery to correspond with the nadir of the drug's serum concentration [15]:

  • TNF Inhibitors (Adalimumab, Etanercept, Infliximab): Withhold for one dosing cycle prior to surgery. Schedule surgery when the next dose is due.
  • JAK Inhibitors (Tofacitinib, Baricitinib, Upadacitinib): Withhold at least 3 days prior to surgery. Carry FDA black box warning for VTE—surgeon must be hyper-vigilant regarding DVT prophylaxis [39].
  • Rituximab: Plan surgery at least 4–6 months after last infusion, or optimally in the last month of the dosing cycle [15].
  • IL-17 / IL-12/23 inhibitors (Secukinumab, Ustekinumab): Withhold one dosing cycle Conditional [15].

Restarting Medications

Restart biologic and targeted synthetic DMARDs approximately 14 days postoperatively, contingent upon evidence of wound healing, suture removal, and absence of clinical signs of infection [15].

Perioperative Glucocorticoid Management

The historical practice of massive "stress dosing" is now considered excessive for most orthopaedic procedures II [43]. The HPA axis is generally suppressed if a patient has taken ≥ 5 mg/day of prednisone for ≥ 3 weeks or has a Cushingoid appearance [43].

💊 Stress Dosing for TAA (Moderate Stress)
  • Continue daily maintenance dose on the day of surgery
  • If HPA suppression suspected: 25 mg Hydrocortisone IV at induction
  • Follow with 100 mg Hydrocortisone (divided) over next 24 hours
  • Rapidly taper back to home dose [44, 46]

Surgical Strategies in the Osteoporotic Patient

Implant Selection

Modern three-component mobile-bearing designs (e.g., STAR prosthesis) and fixed-bearing designs (e.g., Salto Talaris, Infinity) rely on press-fit fixation II [13]. In the osteoporotic patient, however, standard press-fit may be insufficient; the "soft" cancellous bone can yield under load, leading to early subsidence [17].

Stemmed implants are increasingly favoured for compromised bone quality:

  • INBONE II System: Modular intramedullary tibial stem bypasses metaphyseal bone and engages stronger diaphyseal cortical bone III [17].
  • INFINITY System: Lower-profile implant; some series show higher early revision rates when used in challenging bone [51].
  • INVISION System: Revision-specific, offers wider talar components to bridge defects—salvage option for severe osteonecrosis or subsidence [49].

Biocementation: A Hybrid Solution

For "eggshell" bone, biocementation uses injectable, resorbable calcium sulfate/phosphate composite at the bone-implant interface IV [52]. It provides immediate compressive strength (grout effect), then resorbs and is replaced by host bone, theoretically allowing eventual biological fixation [52].

Osteoporosis & Reoperation Rates

📈 Surprising Evidence A 2025 retrospective cohort study of 270 osteoporotic TAA patients found no statistically significant difference in reoperation (5.9% vs 5.6%) or periprosthetic fracture (6.3% vs 4.1%) at 3 years compared to non-osteoporotic controls II [51]. A diagnosis of osteoporosis alone should not exclude a patient from TAA, provided specific surgical adaptations are made [54].

Wound Management & Soft Tissue Complications

Technical Prevention Strategies

  1. Full-Thickness Flaps: Raise fascia and extensor retinaculum with the skin flap to preserve the subdermal plexus III [20].
  2. No Retraction Injury: Self-retaining retractors used with extreme caution or released periodically [20].
  3. Extensor Retinaculum Repair: Meticulous closure provides a vascularised bed over the implant [20].

Advanced Wound Care Modalities

  • Incisional Negative Pressure Wound Therapy (iNPWT): Prophylactic NPWT (e.g., PICO, Prevena) in high-risk patients showed significant decrease in wound complications II [55].
  • Aperture Pad Dressing: Modified Sir Robert Jones compressive dressing with cutout for pressure relief over incision; reduced wound complications threefold III [56].
  • Tranexamic Acid (TXA): Reduces postoperative hematoma, a potent culture medium for bacteria, significantly lowering infection rate II [55].

Complications: Periprosthetic Joint Infection

PJI remains the most feared complication in TAA, and RA patients are at elevated risk II [9]. Diagnosis is notoriously difficult due to the "Seronegative Infection" phenomenon [23].

The Diagnostic Dilemma

  • False Positives: Baseline systemic inflammation can keep ESR/CRP chronically elevated [25].
  • False Negatives: Potent immunosuppression (e.g., Tocilizumab, an IL-6 inhibitor) can completely blunt the CRP response, masking infection III [38].
🚨 Critical Recommendation In an RA patient with a painful TAA and elevated markers, aspiration is mandatory. Synovial fluid analysis is the cornerstone of diagnosis. Alpha-defensin is less affected by systemic inflammatory diseases and antibiotics, showing superior diagnostic performance in RA patients II [24, 25, 58]. Relying on ESR/CRP alone is negligent.

Outcomes & Survivorship: The Final Verdict

Despite the myriad challenges, outcomes of TAA in RA patients are remarkably resilient:

  • Survivorship: Modern registry data indicates RA is not an independent risk factor for revision when accounting for other variables. Survivorship at 10 years is comparable to OA cohorts II [1].
  • Function: While RA patients may not achieve high-impact sports function, their reported satisfaction is often higher due to the polyarticular nature of their disease—relieving severe ankle pain provides a massive relative improvement in global quality of life II [2, 22].
  • Safety: A systematic review confirmed no significant differences in perioperative complication rates between RA and OA groups when modern protocols are followed II [2].

Conclusion & Key Clinical Takeaways

📋 Four Pillars of Successful TAA in RA
  1. Do Not Fear the Markers: Elevated preoperative ESR/CRP are not absolute contraindications. Differentiate between active local infection and systemic disease activity. Aspiration is the arbiter.
  2. Follow the Cycle: Time surgery to the nadir of biologic drug levels. Continue methotrexate. Do not stress-dose steroids unless truly HPA-suppressed.
  3. Stem the Tide: In severe osteoporosis, favour stemmed or modular implants (INBONE/INVISION) over standard low-profile designs.
  4. Protect the Envelope: Use prophylactic negative pressure wound therapy and aperture dressings. The soft tissue is the primary mode of early failure.

When these principles are rigorously applied, TAA transforms from a high-risk gamble into a reliable, limb-salvaging procedure that restores mobility to the rheumatoid patient.
