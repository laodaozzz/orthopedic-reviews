The Interosseous Talocalcaneal Ligament in Pediatric Subtalar Arthroereisis

Biomechanical Roles, Surgical Management, and Stability Implications — Evidence-Based Educational Summary

1. Introduction: The Biomechanical Imperative

The management of symptomatic pediatric flexible flatfoot (pes planovalgus) remains debated in orthopedics, particularly regarding the balance between conservative management and surgical intervention. The subtalar joint (STJ)—the "coxa pedis"—is a complex mechanical linkage critical for transferring rotational forces from the lower limb to the foot [1].

While osteotomies and arthrodesis represent traditional reconstructions, subtalar arthroereisis (SA) has emerged as a minimally invasive alternative aimed at correcting deformity by limiting excessive subtalar eversion through sinus tarsi implant insertion [2].

⚠ The Surgical Paradox To insert a mechanical device that limits pronation, the surgeon must often encroach upon, displace, or section the interosseous talocalcaneal ligament (ITCL)—the very biological structure that naturally limits pronation. Does this compromise hindfoot stability? This report synthesizes evidence from cadaveric studies, finite element analysis (FEA), and clinical outcome data to answer this question [3,4].

2. Anatomical Architecture of the Subtalar Complex

2.1 The Peritalar Complex and "Coxa Pedis"

The subtalar joint is functionally divided into two distinct articulation systems [1]:

  • Posterior Subtalar Joint: Formed by the concave posterior facet of the talus on the convex posterior facet of the calcaneus. The surface geometry dictates the primary axis of rotation.
  • Anterior Complex (Coxa Pedis): Includes the talar head articulating with the middle and anterior calcaneal facets and the navicular bone. Functions analogously to the hip joint, distributing weight and torque across the midfoot.

2.2 Sinus Tarsi vs. Canalis Tarsi

The space between talus and calcaneus is a funnel-shaped canal divided into two distinct zones [6]:

Sinus Tarsi (Lateral)

Wide, conical opening. Contains the cervical ligament, inferior extensor retinaculum roots, and adipose tissue.
Type I implants (e.g., MBA, Giannini) occupy this space.

Canalis Tarsi (Medial)

Narrow medial tunnel connecting sinus tarsi to medial hindfoot. Anatomical home of the ITCL.
Type II implants (e.g., HyProCure) penetrate deep into this zone [7].

2.3 The Interosseous Talocalcaneal Ligament (ITCL)

The ITCL is the cornerstone of subtalar stability: a wide, flat, bilaminar band within the canalis tarsi, separating the posterior joint from the anterior complex.

2.3.1 Morphology and Orientation

Anatomical studies describe the ITCL as predominantly a band-type ligament (92.5%), with fan-type and multiple-band variants [9,15]. Fibers run obliquely from the sulcus tali (talus) inferiorly and laterally to the sulcus calcanei (calcaneus). As the calcaneus everts, insertion points move apart, tensioning the ligament—it acts as a "check rein" against excessive eversion.

2.3.2 The "Cruciate Ligament" of the Foot

Functionally, the ITCL is compared to the ACL of the knee. Tochigi et al. confirmed the ITCL is the primary restraint against axial distraction and a critical stabilizer against anterior talar translation [11,12].

2.3.3 Innervation and Proprioception

Immunohistochemical studies identified a high density of mechanoreceptors (Pacinian corpuscles, Ruffini endings, Golgi tendon organs) within the ITCL [13]. Tension on the ITCL triggers a reflex contraction of foot invertors (e.g., tibialis posterior) for dynamic arch stabilization. Sectioning the ITCL disrupts this afferent pathway, raising concern about sensorimotor instability post-arthroereisis [14].

2.4 The Cervical Ligament (CL)

Located lateral to the ITCL in the sinus tarsi proper, the CL primarily resists supination (inversion). In flatfoot (a pronation deformity), the CL is less mechanically relevant than the ITCL [1].

3. Pathomechanics of the Flexible Flatfoot

3.1 Talar Displacement — "Peritalar Subluxation"

In pathologic flatfoot, the talus slides anteriorly, inferiorly, and medially relative to the calcaneus under weight-bearing. This forces the calcaneus into valgus and the navicular into abduction. This implies pre-existing failure or attenuation of the ITCL [5,16].

💡 Key Argument for ITCL Sectioning Proponents argue that in symptomatic flatfoot, the ITCL is already functionally incompetent, stretched, or atrophic, and thus its preservation is not structurally prioritized [5].

3.2 The "Unlocked" Foot

During normal gait, the STJ supinates at heel rise, converting the midfoot from a flexible shock absorber into a rigid lever ("locking" of the midtarsal joint). In flatfoot, the ITCL fails to guide supination—the joint remains pronated ("unlocked") throughout stance, leading to inefficient gait, muscle fatigue, and medial soft tissue strain [16].

3.3 Strain on Medial Structures

The failure of the central pivot transfers excessive load to the spring ligament and tibialis posterior tendon. Biomechanical models confirm that untreated flatfoot significantly increases strain on these structures, predisposing to posterior tibial tendon dysfunction (PTTD) in adulthood [16,17].

4. Biomechanical Function of the ITCL: Evidence from Sectioning Studies

4.1 Tochigi et al.: The Role of Apposition Level V — Cadaveric

Methodology: Fresh-frozen cadaveric specimens mounted in a servohydraulic testing system with axial distraction and transverse drawer tests [11,12].

Key Findings:

  • The ITCL is the primary restraint maintaining apposition of the subtalar joint.
  • ITCL sectioning produced a significant increase in joint laxity, specifically allowing the talus to lift off the calcaneus.
  • ITCL failure results in anterolateral rotatory instability—mirroring the talar displacement seen in flatfoot, confirming the ITCL as the primary check against the deformity mechanism.

4.2 Kjaersgaard-Andersen et al.: Range of Motion Level V — Cadaveric

This sectioning study demonstrated that ITCL sectioning resulted in a significant increase in subtalar eversion ROM [1]. While the calcaneofibular ligament (CFL) is the primary stabilizer in inversion, the ITCL becomes the dominant stabilizer when the CFL is lax (i.e., in pronation)—the "last line of defense" against severe pronation.

4.3 The "Neutral Zone" Concept

ITCL sectioning significantly widens the "neutral zone"—the ROM range where little passive resistance exists. Without the ITCL, the subtalar joint becomes "wobbly" in mid-range, relying entirely on muscular control or the implant block [12].

5.1 Type I Implants: The "Spacer" Approach

Surgeons using Type I implants (e.g., MBA) often attempt ITCL preservation. The implant acts as a plug lateral to the ligament. However, if oversized or placed too deep, impingement upon the ITCL causes sinus tarsi pain (sinus tarsitis)—the most common complication [19,20]. An intact, taut ITCL can also create a "recoil" force causing lateral implant extrusion [4].

5.2 Type II Implants: The "Axis-Altering" Approach

HyProCure and similar devices are placed deep into the canalis tarsi. Since this is the anatomical home of the ITCL, it is impossible to place a rigid stent without displacing the ligamentous tissue. The surgical technique explicitly requires "decompression" or sectioning using tenotomy scissors [5,7].

6. The Surgical Dilemma: To Cut or Not to Cut?

6.1 Rationale FOR Sectioning the ITCL

6.1.1 Correcting the Deformity

In chronic severe flatfoot, soft tissues within the sinus tarsi become contracted and fibrotic, holding the foot in deformed position. Releasing the contracted ITCL mobilizes the STJ and allows talar reduction. The Giannini technique mandates "complete sectioning of the deep interosseous talocalcaneal ligament" for spacer insertion [4].

6.1.2 Preventing Implant Extrusion

A taut ITCL acts as a spring exerting constant lateral force on the device. Sectioning eliminates this recoil, allowing passive implant seating [5].

6.1.3 Denervation for Pain Relief

Given the high nociceptor density in the ITCL, "decompressing" it may denervate the painful structure, potentially reducing postoperative sinus tarsitis [13,14].

6.2 Risks of Sectioning the ITCL

⚠ The "Giannini Warning" — Critical Evidence A study of 45 feet treated with the smooth Giannini spacer (protocol requiring ITCL sectioning) reported a 22% rate of spacer dislocation (subluxation or extrusion) [4]. The smooth, non-threaded device had no biological tether or mechanical retention once the ITCL was cut. This proves that ITCL sectioning without adequate implant retention causes instability and failure.

6.2.1 Loss of Proprioception

Sectioning the ITCL removes afferent feedback protecting the ankle from sprains. While no specific clinical study has quantified post-arthroereisis proprioceptive deficit, theoretical biomechanics suggest patients may rely more heavily on visual or vestibular cues [13].

7. The "Healing" Hypothesis: Fibrosis as the New Stabilizer

If ITCL sectioning causes instability (as in the Giannini study), why do modern threaded implants generally succeed? The answer: fibrosis and neoligament formation [2,5].

7.1 Mechanism of "Neoligament" Formation

  • Fibrotic Anchoring: The inflammatory response to tenotomy and the titanium foreign body triggers fibroblast proliferation and dense scar tissue formation [5].
  • Thread Engagement: Unlike smooth spacers, modern implants have aggressive threads. Scar tissue grows into these threads, biologically locking the implant [2].
  • The "Neoligament": Over 12–24 months, scar tissue matures into a fibrous capsule mimicking the original ligament's function, encapsulating the device for long-term stability.

7.2 Evidence from Implant Removal

Studies show that in pediatric patients, even after implant removal (typically after 2–4 years), flatfoot correction is often maintained [2]. This suggests soft tissues have remodeled in the corrected position, providing structural integrity independent of hardware—consistent with a syndesmotic screw analogy [22].

💡 Syndesmotic Screw Analogy Just as a syndesmotic screw allows disrupted ligaments to heal in reduced position—and remains stable after screw removal—the arthroereisis implant acts as an internal splint, holding the STJ in alignment while the cut ITCL heals at a physiologically advantageous length [22].

8. Biomechanical Stability: Implant vs. Native Ligament

8.1 The "Super-Stable" Joint

A rigid titanium implant is infinitely stiffer than a biological ligament. When in place, the STJ is often stiffer and more restricted than normal—the implant creates a hard mechanical block to eversion. "Instability" (floppiness) is not the clinical problem while the implant is in situ; the concern is excessive rigidity [18].

8.2 Stress Concentrations

FEA models indicate a massive increase in von Mises stress at the implant-talus contact point (sulcus tali). Without the ITCL, the implant bears the entire load of deceleration during heel strike [23]. This explains why talar neck cysts or localized osteopenia are sometimes seen around implants on X-rays.

9. Clinical Outcomes and Complications

9.1 Correction of Deformity

Implants placed deep in the canalis (requiring ITCL manipulation) achieve better radiographic correction [7,24]:

  • Meary's Angle: HyProCure (Type II) reduced from ~13.8° to ~6.3°, superior to sinus-only implants [24].
  • Talar Declination: Correction is superior when the implant engages the central pivot zone [7].

9.2 Complication Rates

  • Sinus Tarsi Pain: Up to 46% in some series (typically 10–15%) [20]. Studies do not conclusively show that ITCL preservation reduces pain—impingement from preserving the ligament can increase pain.
  • Implant Removal: 7–40% [19]. Primary reason: pain or intolerance. Extrusion rates are significantly lower in threaded, deep-seated implants versus historic smooth spacers [4].

9.3 Pediatric Remodeling

In children, implant presence for 2–4 years allows osseous remodeling—talus and calcaneus grow into the corrected relationship. This "guided growth" (hemiepiphysiodesis effect) is the ultimate safety net mitigating ITCL loss [2].

10. Conclusion and Synthesis

The ITCL plays a dual role in arthroereisis: it is both the native stabilizer that has failed and the anatomical obstacle to its mechanical replacement.

Key Conclusions (from source document analysis)
  1. Necessity of Sectioning: For axis-altering implants (Type II/canalis tarsi), controlled sectioning is necessary. The canalis anatomy does not permit a rigid stent and taut ligament simultaneously [5,7].
  2. Stability Compromise: Cutting the ITCL does not compromise stability provided the implant is correctly sized with retention features (threads). The implant acts as a rigid surrogate [2,5].
  3. Retention Mechanism: Stability relies on fibrosis ("neoligament") formation around the implant over 12–24 months [2].
  4. The Giannini Warning: Sectioning without threaded anchoring (smooth spacer) leads to a "loose" joint with implant ejection and 22% dislocation rate [4].

Decision Algorithm

1
Assess flatfoot severity — Meary's angle, talar declination, clinical flexibility.
2
Select implant type: If axis correction needed → Type II (canalis tarsi). If simple lateral block → Type I (sinus tarsi).
3
If Type II selected: Controlled ITCL decompression is required. Ensure threaded implant for fibrotic anchorage.
4
If Type I selected: Attempt ITCL preservation. Avoid oversizing to prevent impingement. Use threaded devices to prevent extrusion.
5
Post-operative: Rely on fibrotic encapsulation (12–24 months) and pediatric bony remodeling (2–4 years) for definitive stabilization.
