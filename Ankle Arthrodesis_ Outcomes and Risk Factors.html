1. Introduction & Historical Context

Ankle arthrodesis — the surgical fusion of the tibiotalar joint — remains the enduring "gold standard" for the management of end-stage ankle arthritis (ESAA), particularly in cases where joint-preserving osteotomies are insufficient and total ankle arthroplasty (TAA) is contraindicated.Review[1]

The procedure's primary objective is to alleviate debilitating pain by eliminating motion at the degenerated articular interface, converting a painful, stiff joint into a painless, rigid one that allows for a functional plantigrade gait.[4] While third-generation total ankle prostheses represent a viable motion-preserving alternative, arthrodesis remains the definitive solution for:

  • Young, high-demand laborers
  • Patients with significant deformity
  • Those with poor soft-tissue envelopes or neuromuscular compromise[5]

Historical Evolution

The historical trajectory reflects a continuous struggle between biological requirements for osseous union and mechanical necessity of rigid fixation:

  • Early era (Charnley): External compression techniques — plagued by pin-tract infections and delayed unions.[6]
  • Modern era: Paradigm shift toward internal fixation — initially with open techniques, more recently with minimally invasive arthroscopic approaches that preserve the periosteal blood supply.[7]
Key Biomechanical Principle
The ankle mortise is not merely a hinge — it is a complex load-bearing structure subject to forces up to 5× body weight during the stance phase of gait. The fused ankle must withstand immense shear and rotational torque. Failure to achieve a stable, biologically active fusion site results in non-union, occurring in 5% to 41% of cases depending on the host risk profile and fixation method.[3]

2. Indications & Patient Selection

2.1 Post-Traumatic Osteoarthritis (PTOA)

PTOA remains the most common indication for ankle arthrodesis, often resulting from prior malleolar fractures, pilon fractures, or chronic ligamentous instability.[3] The goal is not merely fusion but the restoration of limb alignment. Varus or valgus malalignment locked into a fusion will inevitably accelerate degeneration of the knee and subtalar joints due to altered load transmission vectors.[11]

2.2 Inflammatory & Neuropathic Arthropathies

Patients with rheumatoid arthritis often present with osteopenia and soft tissue compromise. Arthrodesis is preferred when bone stock is insufficient to support an implant or when severe deformity exists.[12]

Charcot neuroarthropathy represents a distinct and challenging subset. The neuropathic patient lacks protective sensation, meaning the fusion construct must withstand uninhibited loading forces. In these "Type B" hosts, standard fixation often fails, necessitating robust constructs such as tibiotalocalcaneal (TTC) nailing or supplemental beaming screws.[14]

2.3 Salvage of Failed Total Ankle Arthroplasty (TAA)

Converting a failed TAA to an arthrodesis is technically demanding due to massive bone volume loss. These cases frequently require structural allografts or specialized techniques like the "Bamboo Hut" method to bridge the void between the tibia and the talus (or calcaneus).[16]

2.4 Avascular Necrosis (AVN) of the Talus

AVN poses a significant risk for non-union (Odds Ratio: 13.16) because the talar body acts as a necrotic sequestrum rather than a vascularized bed for fusion.[9] In Hawkins III/IV collapse, surgical strategies must include resection of necrotic bone and compression against viable bleeding bone — often requiring a Blair fusion or TTC fusion.[19]

3. Surgical Approaches: Comparative Analysis

3.1 Arthroscopic Ankle Arthrodesis (AAA)

AAA utilizes standard anteromedial and anterolateral portals. The primary advantage lies in preservation of the soft tissue envelope — by avoiding extensive stripping, the extra-osseous blood supply derived from the periosteal network remains intact, fostering a more robust biological environment for union.[1]

Fusion Rates & Clinical Outcomes

Systematic reviews and meta-analyses consistently demonstrate statistically superior fusion rates for AAA compared to OAA:

  • AAA fusion rates: 88.7% to 97%
  • OAA fusion rates: 78.5% to 85.0%
  • Odds ratio: 2.0–3.32 favoring arthroscopyLevel I Meta-analysis[2]

Bai et al. analyzing 1,102 patients reported fusion rates of 95.1% for AAA versus 83.2% for OAA (OR 3.32).[7] This discrepancy is attributed to the superior visualization of posterior recesses and gutters, allowing for more complete debridement.[21]

Complication Profile

The biological advantages translate into a significantly lower complication profile:

  • Infection rates: AAA 6–10% vs OAA 13–18.5%Meta-analysis[7]
  • OR for complications: 0.47–0.60 favoring arthroscopy[2]
  • Hospital stay: Shortened by 1.2–1.8 days[7]

Deformity Correction Limits

Critical Threshold
While experienced surgeons can correct coronal plane deformities of up to 10–15° via arthroscopic bone resection and tensioning, severe varus/valgus deformities (>15°) or significant sagittal malalignment generally mandate an open approach.[3]

3.2 Open Ankle Arthrodesis (OAA)

OAA remains indispensable for complex cases, revision surgeries, and significant deformities.

  • Anterior Approach: Interval between tibialis anterior and EHL. Broad exposure; higher wound dehiscence rates.[4]
  • Transfibular (Lateral) Approach: Distal fibula osteotomy/resection. Unparalleled visualization; significantly more effective for correcting tibial-talar angles >10°.[4,23]
  • Posterior Approach: Interval between FHL and peroneal tendons. Reserved for posterior plating or compromised anterior soft tissues.[3]

4. Fixation Biomechanics & Implant Technology

Achieving solid arthrodesis requires absolute stability. Any micromotion in excess of 150 microns disrupts bridging trabeculae, leading to fibrous non-union.[25]

4.1 Screw Fixation Constructs

Two vs. Three Screws

Modern biomechanical data strongly supports the use of three screws. A third screw significantly increases stiffness and resistance to rotational torque — the primary disruptive force during the rolling phase of gait.Cadaveric[25]

Surface Area vs. Stability Trade-off
Adding a third 7.0mm screw increased joint surface area lost from 5.91% to 9.51%. Despite this loss, the substantial gain in mechanical stability justifies the third screw, particularly in osteopenic bone where screw purchase is the limiting factor.[25,26]

Crossed vs. Parallel Configuration

Finite Element Analysis (FEA) studies indicate that crossed screw configurations offer superior resistance to torsion and shear compared to parallel screws. Specifically, two crossed screws combined with an anterior plate demonstrated the smallest maximum displacement under dorsiflexion, neutral vertical load, and torsion.FEA[27]

The "Home Run" Screw

The "home run" screw is inserted from the posterior distal tibia directed anteriorly and distally into the talar neck and head. This trajectory directly counters the pull of the Achilles tendon, which generates a powerful plantarflexion moment that tends to "gap" the anterior fusion site.[30]

Biomechanical simulations favor a posteromedial entry point: it minimizes stress concentration within the talus and reduces micromotion (mean 4.88 μm vs 5.19 μm) compared to a posterolateral entry.[32]

4.2 Plating Systems

Anterior tension band plating converts distraction force into compressive force at the posterior interface.[22] Biomechanical comparisons confirm that an anterior locking plate with a compression screw provides significantly greater bending stiffness than 2- or 3-screw constructs alone.[25]

Double plating (medial and lateral) is 1.5–5× stiffer than single plate constructs, although this requires extensive soft tissue stripping.[25]

4.3 Intramedullary Fixation (TTC Nailing)

Retrograde intramedullary nailing provides a load-sharing central axis of fixation. The "Bamboo Hut" technique utilizes autologous fibular strut grafts arranged circumferentially around the nail (at 12, 3, and 6 o'clock positions). The nail acts as the central pole and struts are compressed by the locking mechanism. Small series show 100% union rates for massive bone defects.[16]

5. Joint Preparation: The Biology of Fusion

While fixation provides stability, biological preparation dictates whether cellular fusion will occur. The objective is to expose vascular cancellous bone and maximize the surface area for osteogenic bridging.[20]

5.1 Surface Preparation Techniques

  • Subchondral Drilling: Creates perforations (2.0–2.5mm drill) into the sclerotic subchondral plate, allowing marrow elements and mesenchymal stem cells to egress — creating "spot welds" of healing.[21]
  • Fish Scaling / Feathering: Uses an osteotome or sagittal saw to create shingled, scale-like cuts. Dramatically increases macroscopic surface area while maintaining structural integrity of the subchondral plate. In triple arthrodesis non-unions, curettage combined with fish scaling was the most efficacious technique.III[34,36]
  • Petalling (Judet Technique): Lifts thin osteoperiosteal or cortical flakes ("petals") without detaching them, creating a biologically active "artificial callus" environment. Particularly valuable in sclerotic, avascular bone.[37]

5.2 Biological Augmentation

For "Type B" hosts (compromised biology):

  • rhPDGF-BB / β-TCP (Augment®): A Level I randomized controlled trial demonstrated this synthetic graft was non-inferior to autograft for ankle and hindfoot fusions, without donor site morbidity.Level I RCT[10]
  • Bone Marrow Aspirate (BMA): Provides osteoprogenitor cells — often mixed with allograft to supply the osteoinductive component to a structural scaffold.[40]

6. Non-Union: Risk Factors & Prevention

Non-union is the primary failure mode. Rates vary from <5% in healthy cohorts to >40% in high-risk populations.[9]

6.2 Mechanistic Insights

  • Smoking (OR 2.89): Nicotine-induced vasoconstriction and CO-mediated hypoxia inhibit osteoblast function. Cessation for ≥6 weeks preoperatively is standard.[9]
  • Prior Infection (OR 2.40): Residual biofilm and compromised vascularity impair delivery of immune cells and osteoprogenitors.[9]
  • Diabetes (OR 1.71): Microangiopathy, impaired collagen synthesis, and neuropathy leading to unsensed micromotion.[9,13]
  • AVN (OR 13.16): Fusing a tibia to a necrotic talar dome is biologically futile without resection of dead bone.[9]

6.3 Management of Established Non-Union

  • Structural Revision: Conversion from screw fixation to rigid anterior plating or TTC nailing.
  • Biological Revision: "Bamboo Hut" technique or femoral head allografts with rhPDGF-BB to restore bone stock.[16]

7. Adjacent Segment Disease & Subtalar Joint

7.1 Adjacent Segment Disease (ASD)

By eliminating sagittal plane motion at the ankle, arthrodesis forces the subtalar (ST), talonavicular (TN), and calcaneocuboid (CC) joints to compensate, predisposing them to osteoarthritis.[42]

  • Radiographic arthritis: Pervasive — occurring in 57% to 100% of patients at 10–20 year follow-up.[42]
  • Symptomatic ASD: Only approximately 28–30% develop symptoms severe enough to require secondary fusion.[43]
Alignment Is Key
Optimal fusion position — neutral dorsiflexion, 5° valgus, 5–10° external rotation — allows the midfoot to remain unlocked during stance, preserving adjacent joint function. Fusing in varus "locks" the transverse tarsal joints, accelerating TN arthritis.[3]

7.2 Tibiotalocalcaneal (TTC) Arthrodesis

TTC is indicated for combined ankle and subtalar arthritis, severe deformity, Charcot neuroarthropathy, and AVN.[12]

Key Finding
Comparative studies show no significant difference in patient satisfaction, pain scores, or functional outcomes between isolated ankle arthrodesis and TTC arthrodesis in patients with severe pathology.[11] Gait parameters (cadence, velocity) are comparable. This empowers surgeons to address hindfoot pathology aggressively rather than preserving a compromised subtalar joint.

8. Conclusion & Future Directions

Ankle arthrodesis remains a resilient and reliable intervention for end-stage ankle pathology. Key takeaways:

  1. Approach: AAA has established itself as the standard of care for isolated arthritis with minimal deformity (OR ~3.32 for fusion, OR ~0.5 for complications vs OAA).[2,7]
  2. Fixation: Three-screw constructs with a posteromedial "home run" screw and hybrid plating systems provide the necessary rigidity.[25,27,32]
  3. Biology: Fish scaling and petalling maximize osteogenic surface area. rhPDGF-BB is non-inferior to autograft.[10,34,37]
  4. Long-term: ASD is radiographically predictable but not inevitably clinically symptomatic. TTC fusion offers comparable functional outcomes to isolated ankle fusion in appropriate candidates.[11,42,43]
