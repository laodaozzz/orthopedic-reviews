Introduction & Epidemiology

Geriatric distal femur fractures represent a growing orthopedic crisis with outcomes paralleling or exceeding proximal femur fractures. These injuries typically result from low-energy trauma in osteoporotic bone and present formidable challenges due to systemic frailty and compromised bone stock.

🔍 Key Insight

Management goals have shifted from perfect anatomical reconstruction to facilitating immediate, unrestricted postoperative mobilization to prevent the devastating cascade of immobility-related complications.

Fracture Classification & Osteoporotic Substrate

The AO/OTA classification remains the gold standard. In geriatric osteoporotic bone, even low-energy trauma can cause severely comminuted fractures due to the "eggshell" architecture of depleted metaphyseal bone.

⚠️ Critical Biomechanical Principle

When the medial cortical buttress is pulverized, the entire axial load transfers to the lateral fixation construct, creating high risk for varus collapse in osteoporotic bone.

Working Length Optimization in Bridge Plating

According to Perren's strain theory, optimal working length (75-90% of femoral shaft) with low screw density (<0.5) reduces strain to 2-10%, promoting secondary bone healing. However, medial fracture gaps >5mm serve as independent predictors of non-union regardless of plate optimization.

Primary Distal Femur Replacement (DFR)

DFR has emerged as a paradigm shift for fractures exceeding fixation limits. Validated indications include:

  • Severe intra-articular comminution (AO/OTA 33-C3)
  • Incompetent cortical bone stock ("eggshell" bone)
  • Pre-existing severe osteoarthritis
  • Periprosthetic fracture with loose implant (Rorabeck Type III)
  • Contralateral limb impairment requiring immediate weight-bearing
