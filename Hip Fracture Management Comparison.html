Clinical Evidence & Surgical Management: Concomitant Ipsilateral Femoral Neck and Subtrochanteric Fractures in the Elderly

Executive Summary

The management of concomitant ipsilateral femoral neck and subtrochanteric fractures presents a significant biomechanical and biological challenge in the elderly population. This dual-injury pattern, often termed a "segmental proximal femoral fracture," requires a strategy that addresses the high-risk intracapsular neck fracture (necessitating arthroplasty to avoid avascular necrosis) and the extracapsular subtrochanteric fracture (demanding rigid fixation to withstand massive deforming forces). Level III

Two primary surgical interventions are analyzed: Total Hip Arthroplasty (THA) with adjuvant internal fixation versus Long-stem (revision-type) Hip Arthroplasty. Evidence suggests long-stem arthroplasty offers immediate biomechanical stability but carries higher risks of intraoperative fracture, blood loss, and fat embolism. Conversely, THA with adjuvant plating provides a lower physiological insult and preserves bone stock, relying on modern locking plate technology for stable fixation.

1. Clinical and Pathological Landscape

1.1 Epidemiology and Injury Mechanism

While historically rare and associated with high-energy trauma in young adults, this injury pattern is increasingly seen in the elderly following low-energy falls onto osteoporotic bone. The mechanism often involves a torsional failure of the femoral neck combined with a bending failure of the subtrochanteric shaft. Epidemiological Data not specified in source

Diagnostic Pitfall: Delayed diagnosis of the femoral neck component is common due to the distracting deformity of the subtrochanteric fracture. This worsens prognosis and often mandates arthroplasty. Level IV

1.2 Anatomical Challenges and Deforming Forces

The subtrochanteric region is a zone of immense biomechanical stress. The deforming forces create a classic deformity:

  • Proximal Fragment: Flexion, abduction, and external rotation (due to iliopsoas and gluteus medius/minimus pull).
  • Distal Fragment: Shortening and varus angulation (due to adductor magnus pull).

A concomitant neck fracture creates a "floating" proximal segment, rendering standard reduction maneuvers ineffective. Level V

1.3 The Geriatric Physiological Context

The primary imperative is immediate stability for mobilization, as prolonged bed rest carries high mortality. Osteopenic bone complicates fixation due to reduced screw pull-out strength and brittle cortices, increasing iatrogenic fracture risk. Level IV

2. Surgical Modality I: Long-Stem (Revision-Type) Hip Arthroplasty

2.1 Philosophy and Implant Design

This approach converts the segmental injury into a single, stable reconstruction by bypassing the fracture site. Workhorse implants are fluted tapered stems (e.g., Wagner SL, Arcos), which rely on distal "scratch fit" fixation in the intact diaphysis. Modularity allows adjustment of leg length, offset, and version independent of proximal anatomy. Level III

2.2 Clinical Outcomes

Studies report high rates of union (87-96%) and excellent implant survivorship (93% at 11 years). Functional scores (Harris Hip Score) improve significantly, enabling early weight-bearing. Level III

2.3 Complication Profile

Major Risk: Intraoperative Femoral Fracture. The incidence is alarmingly high, reported at 14.8-17.9%. This occurs due to mismatch between the straight, stiff stem and the bowed geriatric femur, leading to cortical perforation or "burst" fracture. This complication often negates the benefits of the procedure. Level III

Other significant burdens include:

  • Increased Blood Loss: Mean ~678 ml vs. ~436 ml for standard stems.
  • Longer Operative Time: Mean ~137 min vs. ~113 min.
  • Fat Embolism Syndrome (FES): High risk due to intramedullary pressurization during canal preparation and stem insertion.
  • Stress Shielding: Can lead to a 24% reduction in proximal femoral bone mineral density. Level I

3. Surgical Modality II: THA with Adjuvant Internal Fixation

3.1 Rationale and Technique

This "hybrid" approach combines a standard primary stem for the neck fracture with a locking compression plate (LCP) for the subtrochanteric fracture. It minimizes distal canal violation, preserves biology, and avoids the risks of inserting a long stem into a curved canal. A minimally invasive plate osteosynthesis (MIPO) technique is often used. Level III

3.2 Clinical Outcomes

Union rates are excellent, reported at approximately 96.9%. Functional outcomes (HHS) are comparable or superior to long-stem cohorts, potentially due to less invasive surgery and preservation of the abductor mechanism. Level III

3.3 Complication Profile

This approach avoids the specific high risks of long stems but introduces others:

  • Fixation Failure: Relies on fracture healing. A critical stress riser can occur at the junction between the stem tip and plate if overlap is insufficient. Modern LCPs with cables are essential in osteoporotic bone to prevent screw pullout.
  • Infection Risk: Theoretical increase due to extensive soft tissue dissection and more hardware, though comparative data often shows no significant difference. Level III

4. Biomechanical & Systemic Comparisons

4.1 Construct Stiffness and Stress Shielding

Long stems provide high axial/torsional stiffness but cause proximal stress shielding and bone resorption. Plated constructs with modern LCPs offer comparable stiffness while distributing load more physiologically. Level V (Biomechanical Studies)

4.2 Fat Embolism Syndrome (FES)

FES is a potentially lethal risk associated with intramedullary pressurization. Long-stem arthroplasty carries a significantly higher risk compared to plating, which involves no canal reaming. This is a critical consideration in frail patients with limited cardiopulmonary reserve. Level III

5. Rehabilitation and Functional Recovery

5.1 Weight-Bearing Protocols

Long-Stem: Typically allows Immediate Weight-Bearing As Tolerated (WBAT). THA + Plate: Traditionally prescribed Protected Weight-Bearing (PWB), though modern evidence supports "permissive weight-bearing" for stable constructs, as the risks of immobility often outweigh hardware concerns. Level II

7. Conclusion and Recommendation

The choice is not binary but nuanced, based on patient anatomy, bone quality, and surgical risk tolerance. Level III

Long-Stem Arthroplasty is indicated for severe osteoporosis, pathological bone, or non-union conversions where biological healing cannot be trusted. It offers immediate mechanical stability but carries significant surgical insult.

THA with Adjuvant Locking Plate is increasingly favored for the "standard" elderly trauma patient. It is safer physiologically, avoids the risk of catastrophic intraoperative fracture, and preserves bone stock. It is the superior strategy when femoral bowing is present or when minimizing surgical trauma is paramount.
