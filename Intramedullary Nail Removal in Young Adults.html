1. Clinical Context: The Healed Intertrochanteric Fracture in the Young Adult

1.1 Etiology & Fracture Patterns in High-Energy Trauma

Intertrochanteric fractures in young adults stand in stark contrast to the low-energy fragility fractures commonly observed in the elderly population with osteoporosis. Evidence I In younger individuals, these injuries are almost invariably the result of high-energy trauma, such as motor vehicle collisions or falls from a significant height. This etiological difference has profound implications for both the initial fracture management and the long-term considerations of implanted hardware. The high-energy mechanism often results in more complex and unstable fracture patterns, characterized by significant comminution, particularly of the posteromedial cortex, displacement of the lesser trochanter, and potential subtrochanteric extension.

A critical determinant for the choice of fixation is the integrity of the lateral femoral wall. Fractures involving compromise of this structure are considered an indication for intramedullary nailing. The inherent instability of these high-energy fracture patterns makes extramedullary devices, such as the sliding hip screw (SHS), biomechanically less favorable. Therefore, the decision to use an IM nail in a young adult is often not elective but a necessity dictated by the fracture's personality.

1.2 Rationale & Controversies in Intramedullary Nailing

The primary rationale for using an intramedullary nail in unstable intertrochanteric fractures is its superior biomechanical profile. Functioning as an internal splint, the nail is a load-sharing device positioned closer to the mechanical axis of the femur. This reduces the bending moment and lever arm acting on the implant, providing a more stable construct compared to an extramedullary plate, especially in the presence of medial cortical comminution. This stability facilitates early mobilization, which is crucial for reducing the morbidity associated with prolonged recumbency.

Controversy — Expanding IMN Use for Stable Fractures A large multicenter randomized clinical trial (INSITE, n = 850) found no significant difference in patient-reported health-related quality of life at one year between those treated with a more costly IMN and those treated with a standard SHS for stable fractures. This suggests that the expanding use of IMNs for stable fractures may be unwarranted, potentially exposing a larger population to the unique long-term risks associated with these implants without a clear clinical benefit.

Further controversies surround the specifics of the implant itself: the choice between a short or long nail, the necessity of distal interlocking screws, and the design of the proximal femoral neck screw(s). These design variables are not merely academic; they directly influence the surgical procedure and can significantly impact the difficulty and risks of a subsequent removal operation.

1.3 Long-Term Implant Biomechanics & Host Response in Active Patients

The long-term presence of a rigid metallic implant within the femur of a young, active adult initiates a complex biological and biomechanical cascade. A primary concern is stress-shielding, where the stiff implant bears a disproportionate amount of physiological load, thereby reducing the stress experienced by the surrounding bone. According to Wolff's law, this can lead to localized osteopenia, particularly in the proximal femur, making the bone highly susceptible to fracture once the implant is removed.

Simultaneously, modern implants—particularly those made of titanium alloys—promote osteointegration, where bone grows directly onto the implant surface. While beneficial for initial fracture stability, this process becomes a major obstacle during removal. Over months and years, bone can grow into the slots and cannulations of the nail and form a dense cap over its proximal end, effectively incarcerating the implant.

Clinical Paradox The very factors that make a young adult an ideal candidate for rapid and robust fracture healing—a high metabolic rate and potent osteogenic potential—are the same factors that increase the likelihood of a technically demanding and morbid hardware removal due to extensive bony ingrowth.

2. Decision-Making Framework for IM Nail Removal

The current body of evidence overwhelmingly supports a selective, symptom-based approach rather than routine removal of asymptomatic hardware. The potential for serious complications is substantial and often outweighs the benefits in patients who are not experiencing implant-related problems.

2.1 Evidence-Based Indications for Removal

Absolute Indications

  • Deep Infection / Osteomyelitis: The implant acts as a biofilm-colonized foreign body that perpetuates infection, making eradication impossible without its removal. Evidence II
  • Implant Failure: Mechanical failure (nail or screw breakage, loosening) resulting in pain, instability, or loss of fixation necessitates removal and often revision surgery.
  • Nonunion / Malunion: Removal of the original implant is a prerequisite for corrective surgery, such as exchange nailing or osteotomy.

Relative Indications

  • Hardware-Related Pain or Irritation: The most frequent reason for elective removal. Pain may arise from prominence of the proximal nail end or locking screws irritating the gluteal musculature or iliotibial band, or lateralization of a cephalomedullary lag screw during fracture impaction. Evidence II
  • Patient Request (Asymptomatic): Valid consideration but must be met with a thorough discussion of risks, as the procedure may not resolve symptoms.
  • Preparation for Future Arthroplasty: A femoral nail is an absolute impediment to performing a total hip arthroplasty (THA). Removal is a necessary preliminary step.
  • Return to High-Impact Activities: In elite athletes, symptomatic hardware can be a barrier to peak performance, though the removal surgery itself will significantly delay return to sport.
Critical Diagnostic Step A thorough diagnostic workup is essential before proceeding with removal. This should include precise physical examination to localize tenderness over a prominent part of the implant and, crucially, diagnostic injections of local anesthetic around the suspected hardware to see if it temporarily resolves the pain. A positive response provides much stronger evidence that the hardware is the culprit.

2.2 The Controversy: Routine vs. Selective Removal

The consensus in the orthopedic literature is firmly against the routine removal of asymptomatic IM nails. Complication rates for elective removal are reported to be in the range of 10–13%, with some series noting even higher numbers. Furthermore, the primary goal of elective removal—pain relief—is not guaranteed:

  • One review of 100 removals found that while pain improved in a majority of symptomatic patients, a notable subset had unaltered pain, and four previously asymptomatic patients developed new anterior knee pain.
  • Another study concluded that results are "poor," with 18 of 71 patients (25%) reporting increased pain after the procedure. Evidence III
  • Complication rate for removals due to absolute indications (e.g., infection, nonunion) was 21%, nearly double the 10% rate for removals due to relative indications. Evidence III
⚠ Evidence Limitation: The source document synthesizes findings from heterogeneous study designs (case series, retrospective cohorts, narrative reviews). Only one large multicenter RCT (INSITE) was identified for the initial fixation comparison; no RCTs directly comparing removal vs. retention strategies were found.

3. Preoperative Planning & Standard Surgical Techniques

3.1 Essential Preoperative Assessment

  • Imaging: Full-length AP and lateral views of the entire femur. Scrutinize for solid fracture union, implant integrity, heterotopic ossification, or bony overgrowth at the entry point.
  • Implant Identification: It is "absolutely essential" to identify the specific manufacturer and model. Each system has a proprietary set of extraction instruments. The original operative report is invaluable. If unavailable, radiographic characteristics must be used. Evidence III
  • Patient Counseling: Discuss the full spectrum of risks: infection, neurovascular injury, iatrogenic fracture, prolonged surgery, and the possibility of failing to remove the implant entirely. For cephalomedullary nails, the specific and high risk of postoperative SFNF must be explicitly detailed.

3.2 Anesthesia & Positioning

General or spinal anesthesia is typical. Muscle paralysis is valuable for facilitating fracture reduction if needed. The lateral decubitus position provides excellent access to the greater trochanter; the supine position on a radiolucent fracture table may offer better control of limb rotation and length.

3.3 Step-by-Step Standard Extraction (AO Principles)

  1. Debridement of Bony Overgrowth: The most critical and frequently underestimated step. Bone grows over the nail top, obscuring the end cap. Meticulously remove using curette, osteotome, or high-speed burr.
  2. End Cap Removal & Extractor Engagement: Remove end cap, then insert the threaded extraction bolt. Attach the extractor BEFORE removing locking screws to maintain rotational control.
  3. Locking Screw Removal: Through small stab incisions, often reusing original insertion scars. Fluoroscopy guides placement.
  4. Controlled Nail Extraction: Using slap hammer or backslap device. Force must be applied coaxially along the femoral canal. Gentle, progressive impacts are safer than forceful ones.
  5. Wound Closure: Thorough irrigation of the medullary canal and all wounds; standard fascial and skin closure.
Critical Decision Point The success of the standard procedure hinges on achieving a secure connection between the extractor and the nail. Failure at this initial stage forces the surgeon to immediately pivot from a "standard" to a "difficult" procedure. The OR should be equipped with backup instrumentation (universal removal set, high-speed burrs, flexible reamers).

4. Advanced Strategies for Difficult Extractions

Difficult extractions are common and require a systematic, problem-solving approach. The fundamental principle underlying all advanced strategies is the need to establish a new, secure point of purchase—a "handle"—on the implant when the primary, manufacturer-designed handle fails.

4.1 Predictors of Difficulty

The likelihood of a difficult extraction increases significantly with the duration of implantation; the longer a nail has been in place, the more extensive the bony ingrowth. Older, slotted nail designs (e.g., Küntscher nails) are notorious for allowing bone to grow through the slot. Newer expandable nails present a unique challenge if the expansion mechanism fails.

4.2 Management of Inaccessible or Stripped Locking Screws

  • Primary: Meticulous cleaning of the screw recess; correct screwdriver, perfectly aligned.
  • Secondary: Conical extraction screws (reverse-thread) or hollow reamers over the screw head.
  • Salvage: High-speed metal-cutting burr to grind flat surfaces on the screw head, allowing vise-grip pliers to grasp it. For broken screws, use internally threaded extractor pins.

4.3 Techniques for Incarcerated Nails

  • Retrograde Mobilization ("Push" Technique): A distal approach through the knee with a long rigid rod passed up the canal to the distal tip of the incarcerated nail. Controlled hammer blows generate dis-impacting force. Disadvantage: intra-articular working channel in the knee.
  • Stacked Guidewires (Cannulated Nails): Multiple flexible guidewires packed tightly into the cannula. Friction allows the bundle to be gripped for extraction.
  • Schanz Pin Insertion (Cannulated Nails): A threaded Schanz pin slightly larger than the inner diameter is forcefully screwed into the cannula, creating an interference fit for extraction.
  • Vertical Osteotomy (Ultimate Salvage): A longitudinal osteotomy of the femur physically releases the implant. Highly morbid, associated with significant blood loss and fracture risk. Reserved as last resort.

4.4 Bent or Broken Nails

Bent Nails: Mild deformities (<20°) may accommodate standard extraction. Severe bends require in-situ straightening with an F-tool or complete transection and removal in two pieces (copious irrigation mandatory to prevent thermal necrosis).

Broken Nails: Two-stage process: proximal fragment first (standard extraction), then distal fragment using extraction hooks, stacked wires, Schanz pin, or retrograde pushing via a distal fenestration.

5. Complication Spectrum & Management

5.1 Intraoperative Complications

  • Iatrogenic Fracture: One of the most feared complications. Can occur at the greater trochanter or femoral shaft during forceful extraction of an incarcerated nail. Prevention: perfectly coaxial extraction, controlled force, low threshold to use advanced techniques.
  • Neurovascular Injury: Rare but possible, particularly during distal locking screw removal or extensive approaches.
  • Failure of Extraction Instrumentation: The extraction bolt itself can break off within the nail, severely escalating difficulty. Prevention: ensure extractor is fully and correctly seated before applying force.

5.2 Postoperative Complications

  • Infection & Wound Healing Deficits: Among the most frequently reported. Risk substantially higher when removal is for infection (complication rates double vs. elective removals).
  • Hematoma / Seroma: Approximately 1.5% in one large series. Most managed conservatively.
  • Persistent / Worsened / New-Onset Pain: Common and frustrating. Underscores the importance of thorough preoperative workup to ensure the hardware is the true pain source.

5.3 Special Focus: Spontaneous Femoral Neck Fracture (SFNF)

⚠ Critical Complication — SFNF Post-Cephalomedullary Nail Removal SFNF is not a rare event. Studies report an incidence of at least 15%. These fractures are typically low-energy or atraumatic and occur within the first 3 weeks after implant removal. The underlying cause is a profound iatrogenic weakening of the proximal femur.

A biomechanical cadaveric study provided stark evidence: the mean failure load of femora after cephalomedullary nail removal was only 1,835 N, compared to 4,523 N in intact controls—a reduction in strength of nearly 60%. The removal also alters the fracture pattern, predisposing to subcapital buckle-type fractures. This biomechanical deficit results from stress-shielding, decreased BMD at the femoral neck, and the significant bone void left by the large-diameter lag screw.

Prevention & Mitigation Strategies

  • Restrict Indications: Avoid removal altogether unless absolutely necessary.
  • Protected Weight-Bearing: Mandatory postoperative period of strict protected partial weight-bearing.
  • Alternatives: Exchange the prominent lag screw for a shorter one while leaving the nail in situ; replace the entire nail with a shorter version; augment the bony void with injectable bone cement.
  • Management of SFNF: Surgical emergency—typically requires revision fixation or conversion to hip arthroplasty.

6. Postoperative Rehabilitation & Return to Activity

⚠ Evidence Gap: Most detailed rehabilitation protocols in the literature are designed for the post-fracture fixation period, not the post-removal period. Applying a protocol designed for a stabilized bone to a bone that has been iatrogenically destabilized by implant removal is inappropriate and potentially dangerous.

6.1 Immediate Postoperative Protocol

  • Pain Management: Multimodal approach — narcotics for breakthrough, scheduled NSAIDs (if not contraindicated), acetaminophen. Elevation and ice for the first several days.
  • DVT Prophylaxis: Daily low-dose aspirin for 2–4 weeks, supplemented by early mobilization and ankle pump exercises.
  • Wound Care: Clean, dry dressings for the first few days. Showering permitted with waterproof dressing. No submersion until sutures removed (10–14 days).

6.2 Weight-Bearing Progression

This is the most critical and variable aspect of the rehabilitation plan:

  • Standard, Uncomplicated Removal: Some protocols permit weight-bearing as tolerated immediately, with crutches for comfort for the first few days to weeks.
  • Difficult Extraction or Poor Bone Quality: Protected partial weight-bearing for 4–6 weeks.
  • Post-Osteotomy: Strict non-weight bearing for 4–6 weeks.
  • Post-Cephalomedullary Nail Removal: Strict partial or touch-down weight-bearing for a minimum of 6 weeks is strongly recommended due to the high risk of SFNF.

6.3 Phased Rehabilitation

  • Phase 1 (Weeks 0–6): Pain/edema control, gentle ROM, isometric muscle activation (quad sets, gluteal sets), ankle pumps. Weight-bearing per restrictions.
  • Phase 2 (Weeks 6–12): Progressive strengthening — closed-chain exercises, hip abductor strengthening, balance/proprioception training.
  • Phase 3 (Month 3+): Advanced strengthening and conditioning, progression towards sport-specific or work-specific activities.

6.4 Return to High-Impact Activities & Sport

Recovery is prolonged. Return to heavy manual labor may take 4–6 months. In professional athletes, return to play after primary fracture fixation averages 9.5 months (range 7–13 months). Hardware removal explicitly delays this timeline by at least an additional 3–6 months.

Clinical milestones for return to sport include: absence of pain, full ROM, strength ≥ 90% of contralateral limb, successful functional sports testing, and radiographic evidence of bone remodeling (e.g., filling of cortical screw holes).

7. Conclusion & Evidence-Based Recommendations

7.1 Synthesized Guidelines for Clinical Practice

  1. Restrict Indications & Avoid Routine Removal: The procedure should be reserved for patients with clear absolute indications or significant, persistent symptoms rigorously attributed to the implant.
  2. Thorough Diagnostic Workup for Pain: Confirm the implant as the primary pain generator using physical examination and diagnostic local anesthetic injections.
  3. Comprehensive Preoperative Planning: Identify the specific implant system. Ensure the OR is equipped with universal and salvage instruments.
  4. Extensive, Specific Patient Counseling: Pain relief is not guaranteed. Discuss the ≥ 15% risk of SFNF for cephalomedullary nails.
  5. Sound Surgical Principles: Complete removal of bony overgrowth; extractor attached before screw removal; coaxial extraction forces; low threshold to pivot to advanced techniques.
  6. Conservative Postoperative Protocol: Protected weight-bearing for most patients. For cephalomedullary nail removal, strict partial weight-bearing for minimum 6 weeks.

7.2 Critical Gaps & Future Research

  • Prospective outcome studies comparing removal vs. retention for relative indications.
  • Standardized, evidence-based rehabilitation protocols for the post-removal period.
  • SFNF mitigation strategies: prophylactic fixation, bone void fillers, cement augmentation, less invasive techniques.
  • Improved universal extraction technology to standardize difficult removals.
Final Verdict The decision to remove an IM nail from a healed intertrochanteric fracture in a young, active adult should be considered the exception, not the rule. The biomechanical consequences—particularly the iatrogenic creation of a critically weakened proximal femur after cephalomedullary nail removal—reframe the procedure as a major reconstructive challenge. For the asymptomatic patient, the risks clearly outweigh any theoretical benefit. Primum non nocere.
