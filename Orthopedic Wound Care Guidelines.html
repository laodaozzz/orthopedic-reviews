Part I — The Landscape of SSI Prevention in Orthopedics

The Burden of Surgical Site Infections (SSIs)

Surgical site infections (SSIs) represent one of the most significant challenges in modern orthopedic surgery, acting as a primary driver of postoperative morbidity, mortality, and escalating healthcare expenditure.[1] These infections complicate an estimated 1% to 3% of all orthopedic procedures and account for approximately 20% of all hospital-acquired infections.[1]

⚠ Key Impact: The development of an SSI can increase healthcare costs by as much as 300%, largely due to prolonged hospital stays, the need for revision surgeries, and extended antimicrobial therapy.[1] In total knee arthroplasty (TKA), SSI prevalence is approximately 1.59%.[3]

A Synthesis of International & National Guidelines

Several major health organizations have published clinical practice guidelines (CPGs) for SSI prevention. While these guidelines share foundational principles, they exhibit notable differences in specific recommendations. Key organizations include:

  • World Health Organization (WHO) — Global guidelines (2016)[5]
  • U.S. Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC) — 2017 guideline update[4]
  • UK National Institute for Health and Care Excellence (NICE) — SSI prevention guideline[6]
  • American Academy of Orthopaedic Surgeons (AAOS) — 2022 CPG on Major Extremity Trauma[7]
📋 The "Guideline Gap": No single guideline provides a universally applicable protocol. Effective SSI prevention necessitates that clinicians synthesize recommendations from multiple authoritative sources and apply them with nuanced clinical judgment tailored to their specific patient populations, procedural contexts, and available resources.

The Continuum of Care

SSI prevention is a multifactorial process spanning the entire perioperative period. All major guidelines are structured around this continuum, emphasizing a "bundle" of care that includes:

  • Preoperative: Patient optimization — smoking cessation, glycemic control, nutritional support; decolonization for Staphylococcus aureus[1,11]
  • Intraoperative: Appropriate antibiotic prophylaxis, normothermia, skin antisepsis[4]
  • Postoperative: Wound dressing management, patient education, surveillance for signs of infection[11]

Part II — Evidence-Based Selection of Skin Antiseptics

CHG vs. Povidone-Iodine: Critical Appraisal

A significant body of evidence from meta-analyses has established the superiority of chlorhexidine gluconate (CHG) over povidone-iodine (PI) for preventing SSIs. LOE I

  • One meta-analysis revealed that patients prepared with CHG-based antiseptic had a 36% lower rate of SSIs compared to PI.[19]
  • Another meta-analysis of 16 RCTs found CHG associated with reductions in overall SSIs (RR 0.75), superficial SSIs (RR 0.62), and deep SSIs (RR 0.54).[20]
✅ Key Consensus: Both the CDC and WHO issue strong recommendations for alcohol-based antiseptic agents. Alcohol provides rapid, broad-spectrum bactericidal effect, while the antiseptic agent (CHG or PI) provides persistent residual activity.[4,5]

The PREPARE Trial: Paradigm Shift for Fracture Surgery

The PREPARE trial, a large-scale international RCT involving nearly 8,500 patients undergoing surgery for extremity fractures, directly compared two alcohol-based preparations: LOE I

  • 2% CHG in 70% isopropyl alcohol vs. 0.7% iodine povacrylex in 74% isopropyl alcohol[24]
🔍 Landmark Finding: For closed fractures, iodine povacrylex was associated with a significantly lower rate of SSIs. For open fractures, there was no statistically significant difference between the two agents.[24] This challenges the paradigm of a single "best" antiseptic for all orthopedic procedures.

Iodine povacrylex forms a polymer film that adheres to skin and is insoluble in water, providing more durable protection during fracture surgeries involving prolonged tissue manipulation and fluid exposure.[22]

Part III — Framework for Postoperative Dressing Selection

Evolution: Passive → Active Wound Management

Modern evidence supports moist wound healing — maintaining a balanced, moist environment enhances epithelialization, facilitates keratinocyte migration, and contains endogenous growth factors essential for tissue repair.[25] Traditional passive dressings (gauze) are prone to drying out, adhering to the wound bed, and shedding fibers that can act as a nidus for infection.[25]

Interactive & Occlusive Dressings

A systematic review and meta-analysis in TJA found that: LOE II

  • Occlusive film dressings: OR 0.35 for overall wound complications vs. standard[27]
  • Hydrofiber layer dressings: OR 0.28 for overall wound complications vs. standard[27]
  • A film dressing with high moisture vapor transmission rate was associated with a 6% blistering rate, vs. 16% and 24% for less permeable dressings.[28]

Antimicrobial Dressings

Silver-impregnated dressings are the most studied in orthopedics. Studies in TJA show associations with lower rates of acute PJI and overall SSIs compared to standard gauze.[27] However, major guidelines (CDC, WHO) have not issued strong recommendations for routine prophylactic use due to conflicting evidence, cost concerns, and potential for antimicrobial resistance.[4]

🔬 Emerging Technology — DACC Coatings: Dressings coated with Dialkylcarbamoyl chloride (DACC) are hydrophobic and irreversibly bind bacteria and fungi from wound exudate without releasing a chemically active antimicrobial agent, reducing bioburden without contributing to resistance. This aligns with antimicrobial stewardship principles.[29]

Incisional Negative Pressure Wound Therapy (iNPWT)

iNPWT applies controlled subatmospheric pressure to a closed surgical incision. Its mechanisms include stabilizing the incision, reducing lateral tension, removing infectious material/exudate, and improving local perfusion.[17]

⚠ Indications: Reserved for high-risk patients (obesity, diabetes, immunosuppression) and high-risk procedures (revision arthroplasty, high-energy fractures). Both WHO and international consensus panels recommend consideration in these populations.[17]

Dressing Management Principles

  • Aseptic non-touch technique for all dressing changes[6]
  • Leave initial OR dressing undisturbed for ≥48 hours unless clinical indication (hematoma, excessive exudate)[14]
  • Showering: Safe at 48 hours post-op if wound covered with waterproof dressing[6]
  • Cleansing: Sterile saline within first 48 hours; tap water after 48 hours for dehisced/opened wounds[6]

Part IV — Application in Specific Orthopedic Procedures

Total Joint Arthroplasty (Hip & Knee)

In TJA, the imperative is preventing periprosthetic joint infection (PJI). Strong consensus from AAHKS and EFORT supports: LOE II

  • Active/interactive dressings (silver-impregnated hydrofiber, mesh-adhesive) significantly decrease minor wound complications and major infections vs. gauze.[27]
  • For high-risk patients (high BMI, diabetes, revision surgery): iNPWT is recommended as a cost-effective intervention.[34]
  • Skin closure trend: Away from metal staples toward multilayered closure with subcuticular sutures + tissue adhesives (2-octyl cyanoacrylate).[33] Staples associated with more postoperative pain and potentially higher superficial SSI risk.[1]

Major Extremity Trauma & Fracture Fixation

The 2022 AAOS CPG is the cornerstone of modern practice: LOE I

  • Strong recommendations for irrigation with saline and definitive fixation of open fractures within 24 hours.[15]
  • Skin antisepsis: The PREPARE trial provides Level I evidence favoring alcohol-based iodine povacrylex for closed fractures.[24]
  • Dressings: AAOS recommends silver-coated dressings are not preferred — a direct contrast to TJA literature.[15]
  • iNPWT: Valid option for high-risk fractures.[7]

Spine Surgery

SSI prevention in spine surgery emphasizes preoperative optimization and meticulous layered technique. LOE III

  • Intraoperative vancomycin powder applied directly into the wound prior to closure has gained significant traction, delivering high local concentration with minimal systemic absorption and significantly reducing SSI rates.[38]
  • Dressing protocol: Common approach is leaving the initial sterile dressing in place for 72 hours, then removing and leaving the incision open to air unless irritated by clothing.[39]

Part V — High-Risk Patient Populations

The Diabetic Patient

Diabetes is a significant independent risk factor for SSI with an odds ratio of 2.25.[2] Pathophysiology includes: impaired leukocyte function, poor circulation reducing oxygen delivery, and peripheral neuropathy masking early signs of infection.[41] LOE II

⚠ Management Essentials: Tight glycemic control, vascular assessment, mechanical off-loading (foot wounds), moist wound balance, and daily foot inspection for early detection.[43]

The Obese Patient (BMI >30)

Obesity is an independent risk factor with an OR of 2.15 for BMI >24 kg/m².[2] Adipose tissue is hypovascular, creating local hypoxia. Increased subcutaneous tissue prolongs operative time and creates wound tension. LOE II

  • AAOS recommends weight loss counseling; BMI <40 is a threshold for safe elective surgery.[48]
  • These patients are prime candidates for iNPWT to stabilize incisions and manage fluid.[31]

The Smoker

Smoking is one of the most potent modifiable risk factors with an OR of 2.87 for SSI.[2] Nicotine is a powerful vasoconstrictor reducing skin and bone blood flow. Smoking impairs fibroblast and osteoblast function. LOE II

✅ Primary Intervention: Strongly recommend smoking cessation at least 6 weeks prior to elective surgery, with abstinence maintained for several months postoperatively.[49] No dressing can fully counteract this systemic insult.

The Immunocompromised Patient

High risk from common and opportunistic pathogens.[52] Classic infection signs may be blunted or absent due to attenuated inflammatory response.[53] Management requires high index of suspicion, low threshold for aggressive diagnostics (blood cultures, biopsy), and broad-spectrum empirical therapy. Dressing selection should favor fully occlusive options. LOE III

Part VI — Controversies & Future Directions

Skin Closure: Sutures vs. Staples vs. Adhesives

The optimal method remains controversial. Staples offer speed but may be associated with higher superficial SSI risk and increased postoperative pain.[1] Subcuticular sutures have a potentially lower complication profile but are more time-consuming.[33] The trend in elective surgery is toward combined approaches: antimicrobial-coated sutures for deep layers, subcuticular closure reinforced with tissue adhesives.[34]

Antimicrobial Stewardship in Dressing Selection

The principle of antimicrobial stewardship (AMS) is increasingly influencing postoperative care. The paradigm is shifting from simple microbe "killing" to managing bioburden to support host immune defenses without exerting selective resistance pressure.[29]

🔬 Future Protocol Vision:
• Low-risk wounds → Non-antimicrobial interactive dressings
• Moderate-risk wounds → AMS-friendly technologies (e.g., DACC)
• Clinically infected wounds → Traditional leaching antimicrobials (therapeutic use)

Emerging Technologies

  • Bioactive & Regenerative Dressings: Incorporating growth factors, collagen, or hyaluronic acid to actively stimulate healing.[54]
  • Smart Monitoring Systems: Integrated sensors detecting early infection via pH, temperature, or bacterial biomarker changes.[55]
  • Drug-Eluting Technologies: "Smart sutures" and adhesives delivering antimicrobials or anti-inflammatory agents directly to the incision site over sustained periods.[54]

Part VII — Synthesis & Clinical Recommendations

Algorithm for Antiseptic & Dressing Selection

1

Patient & Procedure Risk Stratification

  • Patient risk factors: BMI >30–40, uncontrolled diabetes (HbA1c >7.5%), active smoker, immunocompromised, malnutrition (albumin <3.5 g/L)
  • Procedure risk factors: Revision arthroplasty, open fracture, prolonged operative time, extensive hardware
2

Antiseptic Selection

  • Default: Use alcohol-based preparation unless contraindicated
  • Elective TJA / Spine: Alcohol-based CHG (strong meta-analytic evidence)[19,20]
  • Closed Fracture Fixation: Alcohol-based iodine povacrylex (PREPARE trial)[24]
  • Open Fractures / Near Mucosa/Eyes/Ears: Aqueous povidone-iodine or non-CHG alternative
3

Dressing Selection

  • Low-risk: Standard occlusive/interactive dressing (film + absorbent pad, hydrofiber, hydrocolloid) — 5–7 days or until saturated
  • High-risk (TJA/Spine): Antimicrobial dressing (silver-impregnated hydrofiber or DACC-coated)
  • Highest-risk (Revision TJA, High-Energy Trauma, BMI >40): iNPWT strongly indicated

🎯 Key Takeaways for Clinical Practice

  • 1. Adopt an alcohol-based antiseptic as the default for skin preparation.
  • 2. Tailor the antiseptic agent to the surgical context — CHG for elective, iodine povacrylex for closed fractures, aqueous PI for contaminated/sensitive areas.
  • 3. Prioritize advanced dressings over passive gauze for nearly all orthopedic procedures.
  • 4. Reserve antimicrobial dressings and iNPWT for clearly defined high-risk scenarios.
  • 5. Emphasize patient optimization — no dressing compensates for poor physiology (glycemic control, smoking cessation, nutrition).
  • 6. Incorporate antimicrobial stewardship principles — favor bioburden management over broad-spectrum leaching agents for prophylaxis.
