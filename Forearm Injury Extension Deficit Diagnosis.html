1. Introduction

The forearm and hand represent an extraordinarily complex biomechanical system in which osseous stability, musculotendinous gliding, and peripheral neurovascular integrity must function in perfect synergy to achieve prehensile dexterity. Traumatic crush injuries to the forearm disrupt this delicate equilibrium through a combination of high-energy mechanical tissue destruction and secondary ischemic cascades.

While the acute management of these injuries rightly prioritizes skeletal stabilization, vascular repair, and mitigation of acute compartment syndrome, clinicians frequently encounter complex sequelae in the subacute phase of recovery. A particularly challenging presentation is the delayed onset or persistence of a thumb and finger extension deficit manifesting approximately one month post-injury.

⏰ The One-Month Inflection Point At one month post-injury, edema has typically resolved, yet the processes of neural degeneration, tissue fibrosis, and scar contracture have reached maturity. An inability to actively extend the digits at this stage necessitates a rigorous, anatomically grounded diagnostic approach.

The differential diagnosis at this milestone encompasses three primary, distinct etiologies:

  1. Posterior interosseous nerve (PIN) palsy — neurological motor deficit Evidence Level I–II
  2. Extensor tendon rupture or restrictive adhesion — mechanical disruption Evidence Level II–III
  3. Subacute Volkmann's ischemic contracture — irreversible myonecrosis and fibrosis Evidence Level III

Each entity possesses a highly specific origin, dictates a divergent reconstructive trajectory, and carries vastly different prognostic implications for long-term upper extremity function. Ref 1

2. Anatomical & Biomechanical Correlates of Digital Extension

A precise understanding of forearm anatomy is an absolute prerequisite for deciphering the underlying cause of an extension deficit. The forearm is enveloped by a rigid deep fascia and divided into three primary compartments: the volar (anterior), dorsal (posterior), and mobile wad (lateral) compartments. Ref 4

2.1 Radial Nerve and Posterior Interosseous Nerve (PIN) Pathway

The radial nerve descends through the arm and crosses the elbow anterior to the lateral epicondyle. Before bifurcating, it provides motor branches to the brachioradialis and the extensor carpi radialis longus (ECRL). Ref 6

At the level of the radiocapitellar joint, the radial nerve divides into:

  • Superficial sensory branch — sensation to the dorsoradial hand
  • Deep motor branch (PIN) — motor innervation to all remaining extensors

The PIN enters the dorsal forearm through the supinator tunnel, a critical anatomical bottleneck formed by the two heads of the supinator muscle. The proximal fibrous edge of the superficial head — the arcade of Frohse — is the most rigid structure overlying the nerve and represents the most common site of PIN compression, particularly when crush-induced edema elevates interstitial pressures. Ref 1

⚠️ Sequential Innervation Pattern The PIN's proximal-to-distal innervation cascade is the cornerstone of clinical differentiation. A PIN lesion abolishes finger and thumb extension but spares wrist extension via the intact ECRL, albeit with characteristic radial deviation due to loss of the ulnar-stabilizing ECU. Ref 1

2.2 Extensor Compartment Architecture

Extensor tendons pass through six distinct fibro-osseous dorsal compartments at the wrist (Zone VII). Their superficial location with minimal protective tissue renders them exceptionally vulnerable to crush forces and adhesion formation. Ref 11

3. Pathophysiological Dynamics of Forearm Crush Trauma

3.1 Compartment Syndrome & Ischemic Thresholds

Crush trauma initiates bleeding and interstitial edema within rigid fascial compartments. Compartment syndrome is defined as tissue pressure exceeding capillary perfusion, typically cited as: Ref 15

  • Absolute pressure > 30–40 mmHg
  • Delta pressure (diastolic BP − compartment pressure) < 30 mmHg

3.2 Tissue-Specific Ischemic Timelines

🔬 Wallerian Degeneration Timeline Following axonal death, Wallerian degeneration takes approximately 21–28 days to fully complete. This explains why nerve injury severity is often ambiguous acutely but solidifies into a distinct, measurable deficit by the one-month mark. Ref 18

3.3 Myonecrosis and the Fibrotic Cascade

Necrotic skeletal muscle undergoes phagocytic clearance and is replaced by dense, inelastic fibrotic scar tissue. By four weeks, the acute inflammatory phase has subsided, and maturing collagen cross-links shrink and shorten, physically tethering tendons and progressively drawing the hand into a rigidly contracted posture — the mechanism underlying Volkmann's ischemic contracture. Ref 3, 15, 16

3.4 Tendinous Response to Crush

The crush-induced hematoma and fibrinous exudate stimulate aggressive fibroblastic response, fusing extensor tendons to dermis, periosteum, or adjacent fascia. These non-gliding adhesions abolish tendon excursion. Additionally, partially avulsed or devascularized tendons may undergo delayed attritional rupture when patients attempt mobilization against early scar tissue. Ref 2, 12

4. Differential Diagnosis: The Triad of Delayed Extension Deficits

4.1 Posterior Interosseous Nerve (PIN) Palsy

A PIN palsy results from direct contusion, stretching over a displaced fracture, or ischemic compression within the supinator tunnel. Ref 6

Clinical Phenotype:

  • Purely motor deficit — inability to extend MCP joints of fingers and IP/MCP joints of thumb
  • Normal sensation — dorsum of hand and first web space preserved (superficial radial nerve intact)
  • Wrist extension preserved but with radial deviation (ECRL intact, ECU paralyzed)
  • Resting posture: wrist drop with supple, relaxed fingers
  • Full passive range of motion Ref 1, 6, 22

4.2 Extensor Tendon Rupture and Adhesion

By one month, organizing hematoma and fibrotic exudate can encapsulate tendons in a restrictive scar bed, or weakened tendon segments may undergo silent attritional rupture. Ref 2, 12

Clinical Phenotype:

  • Deficit may be specific to certain digits depending on zone of injury
  • Restricted passive flexion if adhesions are present (tethered tendon acts as checkrein)
  • Full passive motion if completely ruptured
  • Absent tenodesis effect (rupture) or distorted/restricted tenodesis (adhesion) Ref 10, 26

4.3 Subacute Volkmann's Ischemic Contracture

The end-stage sequela of unrecognized or inadequately decompressed compartment syndrome, characterized by irreversible necrosis and fibrotic replacement of forearm musculature. Ref 16

Clinical Phenotype:

  • Rigid, unyielding deformity — often progressively worsening
  • Classic "intrinsic minus" claw hand: MCP hyperextension + PIP/DIP flexion Ref 14
  • Profound non-dermatomal sensory deficits (median/ulnar nerve ischemia)
  • Forearm tissues "wood-like", indurated, atrophic on palpation Ref 17
  • Restricted passive motion in multiple planes

5. Clinical Diagnostic Algorithm & Physical Examination

5.1 The Tenodesis Test

The single most critical maneuver for differentiating an intact but paralyzed neuromuscular unit from a mechanically disrupted one. Ref 22

  • Technique: Passively flex the patient's wrist firmly, placing dorsal extensor tendons under tension
  • Positive (intact tendons): Robust, synchronized involuntary finger and thumb extension → pathology is neurological (PIN palsy)
  • Negative (disrupted tendons): No digital extension → confirms tendon rupture or severe attenuation
  • Restricted/erratic: Dense peritendinous adhesions highly likely Ref 6, 12, 28

5.2 Passive Range of Motion (PROM) & Stretch Testing

  • Full, supple PROM + absent active ROM: PIN palsy (tenodesis intact) or tendon rupture (tenodesis absent)
  • Restricted passive flexion: Extensor tendon adhesions or dorsal capsular contracture Ref 26
  • Harsh mechanical block + ischemic pain on stretch: Volkmann's contracture — earliest reliable indicator of compartment ischemia Ref 16

5.3 Bouvier's Test

Assesses intrinsic muscle paralysis and capsular contracture in the ischemic claw hand: Ref 14

  • Block hyperextended MCP joints into slight flexion
  • IP extension present: "Simple" claw hand — central slips and intrinsics retain some capacity
  • IP extension absent: "Complex" claw — intrinsic musculature profoundly compromised, fixed capsular contractures (severe ischemic injury)

6. Advanced Diagnostic Modalities

6.1 Electromyography & Nerve Conduction Studies (EMG/NCS)

Electrodiagnostic testing is the gold standard for evaluating peripheral nerve integrity. Critical timing: perform at ≥ 3–4 weeks to allow Wallerian degeneration to complete and avoid false negatives. Ref 18, 19

  • NCS: Normal superficial radial SNAPs localize the lesion to the deep motor branch; motor studies may reveal conduction block or profound slowing Ref 8
  • Needle EMG:
    • Fibrillation potentials & positive sharp waves → structural axonal disruption (axonotmesis/neurotmesis)
    • Normal MUAPs in ECRL/brachioradialis/triceps → confirms isolated PIN injury Ref 7
    • Electrical silence → muscle necrosis (Volkmann's)
    • Widespread abnormalities across multiple nerve territories → Volkmann's contracture Ref 16

6.2 High-Resolution Dynamic Ultrasonography

US possesses significantly higher sensitivity (~84% vs ~44%) and accuracy for detecting extensor tendon pathology compared to MRI, largely due to real-time dynamic assessment. Ref 33

  • Adhesions: Complete lack of tendon excursion relative to surrounding tissue
  • Rupture: Anechoic or hematoma-filled gap between retracted tendon ends
  • Nerve compression: Enlarged, hypoechoic PIN with loss of fascicular architecture; discontinuity = neurotmesis Ref 36

6.3 Magnetic Resonance Imaging (MRI)

  • PIN palsy (T2/STIR): Diffuse homogeneous hyperintensity in denervated muscles (denervation edema) — appears 2–4 weeks post-injury Ref 22
  • Volkmann's (T2/STIR): Heterogeneous architectural distortion; liquefactive necrosis hyperintense, fibrosis as dark low-signal bands Ref 3

7. Therapeutic Strategies & Surgical Algorithms

The therapeutic approach at one month post-injury represents a critical inflection point. The transition from acute inflammation to organized fibrosis dictates that all interventions must balance aggressive anatomical restoration with the risk of exacerbating scar tissue formation. Ref 2, 12

7.1 Management of PIN Palsy

Conservative Management

If closed injury, incomplete axonal loss on EMG, or signs of nascent reinnervation (early polyphasic motor units): Ref 8

  • Peripheral nerves regenerate at ~1 mm/day (~1 inch/month)
  • Complete recovery to distal extensors: 3–6 months
  • Dynamic extension splinting to prevent joint contractures
  • Serial Tinel's sign assessment to track regeneration Ref 36

Surgical Exploration & Decompression

Indications: Complete denervation on EMG at 4–6 weeks, static non-advancing Tinel's sign, or severe neuroma-in-continuity on US. Ref 39

  • Release superficial head of supinator (arcade of Frohse)
  • Ligate leash of Henry if compressing
  • Release fibrous bands across radiocapitellar joint Ref 1

Nerve Reconstruction & Fascicular Transfers

For transected or irreversibly crushed PIN: Ref 38, 41

  • Autologous nerve grafting (sural nerve or medial antebrachial cutaneous nerve donor)
  • Distal nerve transfers (increasingly preferred): Transfer redundant median nerve fascicles (FCR, palmaris longus, or AIN branch to pronator quadratus) to distal PIN or ECRB motor branches — bypasses proximal crush zone, places axons closer to motor endplates Ref 41

Tendon Transfers for Salvage

For delayed presentations, failed nerve transfer, or non-viable neuromuscular junction: Ref 25, 43

  • PT → ECRB (wrist extension)
  • FCU or FCR → EDC (finger extension)
  • PL or FDS slip → EPL (thumb extension)

7.2 Management of Extensor Tendon Adhesions & Ruptures

The Principle of Tissue Equilibrium

⚠️ Critical Surgical Timing Principle Operating on an inflamed, indurated scar bed at one month post-injury will provoke aggressive recurrent scarring. Secondary procedures should only be undertaken when soft tissues are completely soft, mobile, and free of induration, and fractures have achieved solid union. This equilibrium is typically not reached until 3–6 months of intense hand therapy. Ref 2, 13

Tenolysis Procedure

Indicated when intensive hand therapy plateaus, passive motion is maximized, but active motion remains absent. Ref 13

  • Meticulous sharp dissection of tendons from fibrotic encasement
  • Ideally performed under WALANT (wide-awake local anesthesia, no tourniquet) — allows intraoperative active motion testing to confirm complete adhesion release Ref 44
  • High risk of devascularization or attritional rupture if overly aggressive

Tendon Reconstruction

  • Silent rupture: Primary repair usually impossible → intercalated grafts (palmaris longus or plantaris autograft) Ref 2
  • Destroyed muscle belly: By-pass tendon transfers (FCU/FCR as motor units) Ref 46

8. Rehabilitation & Orthotic Management Protocols

Regardless of pathology, rehabilitation initiated at the one-month milestone is paramount. Lack of active digital extension rapidly leads to contracture and shortening of volar structures, creating fixed secondary flexion contractures that render future surgical interventions mechanically futile. Ref 48, 49

8.1 Orthotic Splinting for Extensor Deficits

Dynamic Extension Orthosis

  • Custom-molded, low-profile orthosis with dynamic elastic traction cords attached to digital slings
  • Maintains MCP joints at 0° extension at rest
  • Tension calibrated: strong enough to overcome flexor resting tone, compliant enough to allow active fist formation
  • Enables dynamic resistance training, maintains flexor excursion, prevents extensor overstretch Ref 50, 51, 52

Static Night Resting Splint

  • Volar forearm-based; wrist in 20–30° extension, digits in neutral resting posture
  • Prolonged low-load stretching to prevent volar contracture during sleep Ref 50

9. Conclusion

The diagnostic evaluation of a delayed digital extension deficit one month following a forearm crush injury demands a highly nuanced, multidisciplinary integration of anatomical mechanics, pathophysiological timelines, and advanced diagnostic technologies. The clinician must systematically differentiate between posterior interosseous nerve palsy, extensor tendon disruption, and the subacute onset of Volkmann's ischemic contracture, as the treatment for each is fundamentally distinct.

The tenodesis test serves as the foundational clinical maneuver to differentiate neurological deficits from mechanical tendon continuity. At the one-month milestone, this clinical diagnosis must be definitively confirmed using EMG/NCS to map Wallerian degeneration, alongside dynamic ultrasound and MRI to visualize architectural damage, tendon retraction, and denervation edema.

Therapeutic intervention hinges on rigorous differentiation:

  • Resolving PIN palsies: Dynamic splinting and clinical observation
  • Irreversible nerve injuries: Neural fascicular transfers or tendon rerouting
  • Extensor adhesions: Meticulous tenolysis under tissue equilibrium principles
  • Volkmann's contracture: Aggressive debridement, muscle slides, or free functional muscle transplantation

The rigorous, timely execution of these diagnostic and therapeutic algorithms determines the boundary between permanent, debilitating loss of hand function and successful upper extremity functional restoration.
