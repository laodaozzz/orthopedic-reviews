1. Introduction & the Orthoplastic Paradigm

An open fracture is defined as any fracture in which a disruption of the integument and underlying soft-tissue envelope creates a direct anatomical communication between the external environment and the osseous fracture hematoma. This breach exposes sterile bone, marrow, and neurovascular structures to environmental pathogens while obliterating local vascular supply necessary for cellular defense, antibiotic delivery, and osteogenesis. Not specified

Epidemiology: Open fractures present with an incidence of approximately 30.7 per 100,000 persons per year, exhibiting a classic bimodal distribution: peak in young males (averaging 45 years) sustaining high-energy polytrauma (MVC, industrial accidents), and a secondary peak in the elderly from low-energy osteoporotic falls. The tibia and phalanges are the most frequently involved sites due to subcutaneous positioning and minimal muscular protection. Not specified

AO Core Principles

The AO Foundation (Arbeitsgemeinschaft für Osteosynthesefragen) established four foundational tenets for skeletal trauma management: Not specified

  1. Anatomical reduction of fracture fragments
  2. Stable internal fixation to meet local biomechanical demands
  3. Preservation of blood supply to bone and surrounding soft tissues
  4. Early, active, pain-free mobilization
The Zone of Injury

In open fractures, the kinetic energy imparted during high-velocity impact generates a three-dimensional volume of traumatized, ischemic soft tissue extending far beyond the visible cutaneous wound. This concept of the "zone of injury" is critical to understanding why visible skin wounds represent merely the epicenter of a much larger pathophysiological phenomenon. Not specified

The Orthoplastic Approach

Contemporary management has evolved into a highly integrated, multidisciplinary "Orthoplastic" approach, championed by the BOAST (British Orthopaedic Association Standards for Trauma) and BAPRAS (British Association of Plastic, Reconstructive and Aesthetic Surgeons). This model mandates concurrent involvement of both orthopedic trauma surgeons and reconstructive plastic surgeons. Not specified

2. Initial Evaluation & Classification Systems

ATLS and Initial Resuscitation

Up to 30% of open fractures occur during high-energy mechanisms with concomitant life-threatening polytrauma. The initial assessment must follow the Advanced Trauma Life Support (ATLS) algorithm, prioritizing airway, breathing, and circulation (ABCDEs). Exsanguinating hemorrhage from open extremity wounds requires immediate arrest via direct pressure or compressive dressing; in the presence of uncontrollable arterial bleeding or traumatic amputation, judicious tourniquet application is life-saving. Not specified

Hard Signs of Vascular Injury

Pulsatile bleeding, expanding/pulsatile hematoma, palpable thrill, or absent distal pulses mandate immediate surgical exploration without delay for advanced imaging. If hard signs are absent, an Ankle-Brachial Index (ABI) < 0.9 is highly predictive of significant arterial disruption, warranting urgent vascular consultation and CT angiogram. Not specified

Acute Compartment Syndrome

The presence of an open wound does NOT preclude compartment syndrome. The fascial envelope frequently remains intact despite cutaneous disruption. Continuous monitoring for disproportionate pain, escalating analgesic requirements, pain on passive stretch, and tense compartments is mandatory. Not specified

Wound Handling: Contraindication of the "Mini-Washout"

Per BOAST-4 guidelines, wounds should only be manipulated to remove gross contamination and obtain standardized medical photographs. Following photography, the injury must be covered with sterile, saline-soaked gauze and sealed with an occlusive film dressing. All formal irrigation and debridement must be reserved for the operating room — unpressurized irrigation in the ED risks driving contaminants deeper into sterile tissue planes. Not specified

Biopsychosocial Assessment

Preexisting psychiatric disorders — particularly clinical depression and substance abuse — are independent predictors of major postoperative complications. These patients demonstrate less improvement in physical and emotional function, impaired rehabilitation adherence, altered pain perception, and compromised systemic immunity. Not specified

Advanced Grading: AO and Tscherne Systems

The AO soft-tissue grading evaluates three domains independently: Integument (I), Muscle/Tendon (MT), and Neurovascular (NV). The Tscherne classification concurrently assesses closed soft-tissue trauma. Using both Gustilo-Anderson (open component) and Tscherne (closed envelope) provides the most comprehensive evaluation of the zone of injury. Not specified

3. Evidence-Based Antimicrobial & Immunological Prophylaxis

The "Golden 60 Minutes"

Systemic prophylactic antibiotics are arguably the most critical pharmacological intervention. Per EAST, the Surgical Infection Society, and ACS TQIP guidelines, antibiotics must be administered within the "golden 60 minutes" of hospital arrival. Level I

2024 ESO Trauma Index Data

Analysis of 975,433 patient records across 576 US hospitals revealed: of 44,115 patients with open long bone fractures, 88% received systemic antibiotics, with 70% receiving them within the 60-minute window. However, pediatric patients (<18 years) received timely antibiotics at only 56% — a significant disparity attributed to triage delays and weight-based dosing hesitations. Level III

The 72-Hour Paradigm Shift

Key Evidence-Based Change

Prolonged prophylactic antibiotics provide no additional benefit in reducing fracture-related infections while significantly increasing risks of multi-drug resistant organisms (MRSA, VRE), C. difficile colitis, and systemic toxicity. Level I

  • Type I & II: Discontinue within 24 hours of initial debridement Level I
  • Type III: Continue for maximum 72 hours post-injury, or 24 hours after definitive soft-tissue coverage (whichever occurs first) Level I

A meta-analysis of over 6,600 open fractures showed infection rates in Type III injuries were statistically equivalent for short-course (<72h) vs. prolonged (>72h) regimens: 17.7% vs. 21.3% (p=0.39). Systemic antibiotics physically cannot penetrate avascular, necrotic tissue — prolonged therapy is a biologically flawed substitute for meticulous debridement. Level I

Tetanus Prophylaxis

Open fractures are prototypical tetanus-prone wounds. Per CDC guidelines, all open compound fractures and deep crush injuries are classified as major, tetanus-prone injuries. Not specified

4. Surgical Debridement & Wound Irrigation

The Zone of Injury and Muscle Viability

The fundamental objective of debridement is the complete excision of all devitalized tissue, foreign debris, and avascular bone margins, converting a necrotic wound into a clean, vascularized surgical bed. Not specified

The traditional "4 Cs" for assessing muscle viability — Color, Consistency, Contractility, and Capacity to bleed — have been fundamentally challenged by modern evidence. Not specified

Critical Evidence: 4 Cs Are Unreliable

In a prospective blinded analysis of 36 muscle biopsies, all four parameters failed to achieve statistical significance for predicting histologic viability (Color p=0.07; Consistency p=0.12; Contractility p=0.51; Capacity to bleed p=0.07). Overall, surgeon assessment differed from histopathologic reality in 72% of specimens. Strikingly, 60% of specimens excised as "dead" showed entirely normal cellular architecture on pathology — representing ischemic stunning rather than true necrosis. Level III

Modern guidelines recommend a measured initial debridement focusing only on unequivocally necrotic tissue and gross contamination, followed by a mandatory "second look" 24–48 hours later to allow borderline tissue to demarcate organically. Not specified

Debridement Timing: Debunking the "Six-Hour Rule"

The historical mandate to debride within 6 hours has been thoroughly debunked. Extensive cohort analyses and secondary FLOW trial analyses demonstrate that delaying debridement up to 12 hours (high-energy solitary injuries) or 24 hours (lower-energy injuries) does not significantly increase infection or nonunion risk — provided systemic antibiotics have been administered promptly. Level I

Exceptions Requiring Immediate Surgery

Severe gross contamination (agricultural/sewage), established vascular ischemia, or impending compartment syndrome remain true surgical emergencies requiring immediate operative exploration regardless of timing. Not specified

5. Fracture Stabilization: DCO vs. Early Total Care

The "Second Hit" Phenomenon

High-energy impact acts as the "first hit," triggering Systemic Inflammatory Response Syndrome (SIRS) with massive cytokine release (notably IL-6). Prolonged definitive surgery during this vulnerable window constitutes a "second hit" that exponentially exacerbates inflammation, precipitating ARDS, coagulopathy, Multiple Organ Dysfunction Syndrome (MODS), and frequently mortality. Not specified

Early Appropriate Care (EAC) — The Physiological Compass

The EAC protocol (Vallier et al.) establishes strict physiological criteria based on resuscitation adequacy. Definitive fixation of major fractures is safe within 36 hours only if the patient meets all EAC parameters: Level III

Clinical State SBP (mmHg) Lactate (mmol/L) Base Deficit (mmol/L) Strategy
Stable (Grade I) ≥ 100 ≤ 2.2 0 to −2 Early Total Care: Immediate definitive fixation

Unstable / In Extremis < 80 > 4.0 < −5.5 Damage Control Orthopedics (DCO)

In prospective studies, patients achieving Full Protocol Resuscitation prior to surgery had a complication rate of 22.6% vs. 34.3% (p=0.041) for those with incomplete resuscitation, primarily driven by ARDS and organ failure. Level III

Damage Control Orthopedics (DCO)

DCO is indicated for patients failing EAC criteria, presenting in extremis, with severe TBI (GCS ≤ 8), profound hypothermia (<35°C), or refractory coagulopathy. DCO involves rapid temporary stabilization with spanning external fixation (applied in <30 min with minimal blood loss). Once physiology normalizes in ICU (typically 4–7 days), conversion to definitive internal fixation occurs. Not specified

Definitive Fixation: Intramedullary Nailing

Network meta-analyses from the SPRINT trial and systematic reviews show that unreamed intramedullary nailing reduces unplanned reoperation by approximately 33% vs. definitive external fixation, with even greater benefits in Type III injuries. External fixators for definitive care carry pin-tract infection rates up to 37% and present severe logistical hurdles for subsequent flap coverage. Level I

6. Soft-Tissue Management & the Orthoplastic Approach

"Fix and Flap" Revolution

The "Fix and Flap" approach (Gopal et al.) advocates immediate, single-stage orthopedic internal fixation coupled with simultaneous definitive plastic surgical coverage within 24–72 hours. In severe Gustilo Type IIIB/IIIC open tibia fractures, this approach achieves a 95% limb salvage rate and flap failure rate of only 3.5%, fundamentally minimizing deep infection rates. Level III

BOAST-4 Timeline

Definitive soft-tissue closure or flap coverage should ideally be achieved within 72 hours of injury. ACS TQIP provides a wider window of 7 days but acknowledges earlier is better. Not specified

Negative Pressure Wound Therapy (NPWT / VAC)

WOLLF Trial Results (460 patients)

NPWT showed NO statistically significant difference in Disability Rating Index at 12 months vs. standard dressings (45.5 vs. 42.4 pts, p=0.132). No reduction in deep SSI, no improvement in QoL, no reduction in secondary flap procedures. NPWT significantly increased treatment cost. Current consensus: NPWT is a useful temporary logistical tool but should NOT delay definitive coverage beyond 72h–7 days. Level I

Fasciocutaneous vs. Free Muscle Flaps

  • Fasciocutaneous (ALT flap): Based on descending branch of lateral circumflex femoral artery; durable, pliable skin paddle; facilitates postoperative monitoring; permits normal shoe wear
  • Free muscle flaps (Latissimus Dorsi, Gracilis): Conform into complex osseous crevices; obliterate dead space; heavy vascularization; undergo denervation atrophy yielding non-bulky contour. However, subsequent orthopedic interventions through atrophied muscle flaps are technically more demanding
