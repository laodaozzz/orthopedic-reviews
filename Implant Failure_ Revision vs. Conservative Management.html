1. Introduction & Core Concept

The breakage of an orthopedic plate used in fracture fixation rarely represents an isolated implant defect; instead, it is the terminal mechanical failure in a cascade of underlying biological or biomechanical inadequacies. Source Review The management of a plated fracture has been described as a "race between fracture healing and fixation failure". Source Review An implant provides temporary stability, acting as an internal splint while the bone heals. When fracture union is delayed or fails (nonunion), the implant endures cyclic loading beyond its endurance limit, leading inevitably to fatigue failure. Source Review

🔑 Central Thesis Understanding the interplay of mechanical forces, biological responses, surgical technique, and patient factors is paramount to formulating an effective treatment strategy—whether surgical revision or conservative management.

2. Biomechanical Determinants of Failure

2.1 Fatigue Failure as the Primary Mechanism

Orthopedic implants are not intended to function as permanent prostheses for an unhealed fracture. They are manufactured from stainless steel and titanium alloys that, despite their strength, are susceptible to metal fatigue when subjected to repetitive cyclical loading below their ultimate tensile strength. Source Review

Each step a patient takes applies a load cycle to the implant. Cyclical loading initiates microscopic cracks—often at points of stress concentration such as screw holes—which propagate until catastrophic failure occurs. Source Review

⚠️ Critical Finding Experimental studies have demonstrated that a locking compression plate (LCP) fixed across an 8-mm femoral gap can fracture completely after just ~42,000 load cycles—a number reached in approximately 8 days of normal walking. Level II

This starkly illustrates that the implant is merely a temporary scaffold; its survival is entirely dependent on the timely progression of biological healing. Source Review

2.2 Construct Stability: A Double-Edged Sword

The stability of the fixation construct represents a delicate balance. Both inadequate and excessive stability can lead to implant failure, albeit through different pathways. Source Review

Inadequate Stability

A construct that is too flexible permits excessive motion at the fracture site. This micromotion, beyond a threshold, disrupts callus formation, producing a hypertrophic nonunion—where the bone forms abundant callus that fails to bridge the gap. Level IV Common causes include a plate that is too short, insufficient screw purchase in poor-quality bone, and failure to address significant medial comminution (particularly in the femur).

Excessive Stability & Stress Shielding

Paradoxically, an overly rigid construct can be equally detrimental. According to Wolff's law, bone remodels in response to stress. If a construct is excessively stiff, it shields fracture fragments from the physiological micromotion necessary to stimulate callus formation (secondary bone healing), resulting in an atrophic nonunion. Source Review In this state, the implant bears 100% of the physiological load indefinitely—leading to inevitable fatigue failure.

💡 Fundamental Principle The goal is not always absolute rigidity but rather appropriate stability for the given fracture pattern. The surgeon must engineer a mechanobiological environment conducive to the specific type of bone healing required.

2.3 Influence of Implant Design & Application

Plate Length & Working Length

A shorter overall plate length has been identified as an independent risk factor for implant failure. Source Review The "working length" (distance between the innermost screws on either side of the fracture) is crucial—particularly in bridge plating. A longer working length increases construct flexibility, allowing controlled micromotion that stimulates callus while distributing stress over a larger plate area, increasing fatigue life. Source Review

A study of plate breakages in long bone fractures found a notably high ratio of fracture zone length to working plate length (0.91) in femoral fractures, indicating that a relatively short working length was a significant contributing factor. Level IV

Screw Density & Placement

The screws closest to the fracture site bear the highest axial and bending forces. Source Review While filling every hole may seem advantageous, very high screw density can create an excessively rigid construct, contributing to stress shielding. Modern plating principles often advocate for leaving holes empty over the fracture site to increase working length and construct flexibility.

Locking vs. Non-Locking Technology

Locking plates provide fixed-angle, angular-stable constructs that function as "internal-external fixators," preserving periosteal blood supply. Source Review However, their inherent rigidity makes them less forgiving of malreduction or failure to respect bridge plating principles. A rigid locked construct may remain radiographically stable until the moment of catastrophic fatigue failure—absent early warning signs such as screw toggling. Source Review

Additionally, polyaxial locking systems may reduce construct stiffness and performance under torsional loads. Source Review

3. Biological Impediments to Healing

3.1 Vascular Insufficiency & Nonunion

An adequate blood supply is the single most important prerequisite for fracture healing. Source Review The initial trauma—particularly in high-energy injuries or open fractures—can severely damage periosteal and endosteal vascular networks, potentially leading to avascular necrosis of fracture fragments. Surgical technique also plays a critical role: excessive soft-tissue stripping or aggressive anatomical reduction can further compromise a tenuous blood supply, creating a biologically barren environment.

When vascularity is insufficient, the result is often an atrophic nonunion: radiographically characterized by a lack of callus formation, with bone ends appearing tapered, sclerotic, and avascular. Level IV The implant bridges a biologically inert gap, bearing all physiological loads until fatigue failure.

3.2 Infection: The Overt and Occult Enemy

Infection is a primary cause of nonunion and subsequent implant failure. It is estimated to be present in as many as 40% of all fracture nonunions. Level IV Bacteria adhere to the implant surface and form a biofilm—a protective layer rendering them highly resistant to immune defenses and systemic antibiotics.

🦠 Clinical Pearl Infection may not always present with classic signs of fever and purulence. Low-grade or "occult" infections can manifest only as persistent pain and failure to heal. A high index of suspicion and thorough investigation (including laboratory markers and intraoperative cultures) is essential in every nonunion case.

3.3 Patient-Specific Systemic Factors

Smoking & Nicotine Use

Smoking is one of the most significant and modifiable risk factors for nonunion. Nicotine is a potent vasoconstrictor that acutely reduces blood flow to the fracture site. Smoking also inhibits osteoblast proliferation and differentiation. Source Review

Comorbidities

Diabetes mellitus is associated with microvascular disease, neuropathy, and impaired immune function. Other conditions—severe anemia, hypothyroidism, renal disease—also negatively impact healing potential. Source Review

Nutritional Status & Bone Quality

Deficiencies in protein, calories, calcium, and Vitamin D can significantly hinder callus formation. Osteoporosis presents a major challenge: poor screw purchase increases the risk of pull-out, toggle, and loss of fixation. Source Review

4. Influence of Surgical Technique & Postoperative Management

4.1 Technical Errors in Primary Fixation

Malreduction

Failure to restore acceptable anatomical alignment subjects the plate to eccentric loading, creating bending moments that concentrate stress and dramatically accelerate fatigue failure. Source Review However, there is a crucial distinction: an overzealous pursuit of anatomical reduction in diaphyseal and metaphyseal fractures can lead to extensive soft-tissue stripping, paradoxically creating an atrophic nonunion. This highlights the critical tension between restoring mechanics and preserving biology.

Improper Implant Selection

The implant must be tailored to the specific "personality" of the fracture. Applying a compression plate to a highly comminuted fracture where no bony contact can be achieved is a biomechanical error guaranteeing the plate will bear the full bending load. Source Review

4.2 Postoperative Non-Adherence

Non-compliance with weight-bearing restrictions is a frequent and preventable cause of implant failure. Source Review Premature or excessive weight-bearing before sufficient biological healing can overload the construct—causing screws to loosen, the plate to bend, or accelerating fatigue failure.

⚠️ Important Caveat While some studies (particularly regarding ankle fractures) suggest early weight-bearing may be safe for certain stable patterns, these findings are highly specific and should not be generalized to all fractures. For most load-bearing long bone fractures, especially with comminution or poor bone quality, strict adherence to weight-bearing protocols is critical until clear radiographic evidence of progressive healing. Level II

5. Comprehensive Assessment of the Failed Fixation

When a patient presents with a broken fixation plate, the diagnostic process must extend far beyond confirming mechanical failure. The primary objective is to uncover the root cause—fundamentally dictating subsequent management. Source Review

🔍 Pivotal Diagnostic Question "Is this a failure of mechanics, or a failure of biology?" The answer is the primary fork in the treatment algorithm.

5.1 Clinical Evaluation

A thorough history begins with the chief complaint: a classic presentation of acute plate breakage is sudden onset of intense pain, often with an audible "pop" and a new sensation of instability. This must be distinguished from the chronic, deep, aching pain characteristic of an established nonunion. Source Review

Key history elements include: was the initial injury an open fracture? Were there early wound complications? What were the weight-bearing instructions? A meticulous review of social and medical history is essential to identify risk factors including smoking, diabetes, nutritional status, and other comorbidities.

The physical examination aims to characterize the current limb state. Critical components include:

  • Gross instability with gentle manipulation confirming loss of mechanical integrity
  • New or progressive deformity (angulation, rotation, limb shortening)
  • Soft-tissue envelope evaluation: erythema, warmth, swelling, wound drainage, sinus tracts
  • Overall health and frailty assessment to determine surgical candidacy

5.2 Radiographic Analysis

Imaging confirms the diagnosis and, more importantly, characterizes the state of bone healing—providing a window into the underlying biological process.

CT Scan for Revision Planning

A CT scan provides a detailed, three-dimensional understanding of bone anatomy, precisely delineating the extent of nonunion, quantifying bone gap/defect size, and identifying subtle deformities. For the surgeon, the CT scan is the roadmap for the revision procedure. Level IV

5.3 Laboratory & Microbiological Investigations

Given that occult infection is a frequent cause of nonunion, a low threshold for investigation is essential.

  • Inflammatory markers (CRP, ESR): Elevated levels significantly increase suspicion for infection. Normal values have high negative predictive value but do not exclude it entirely. Level IV
  • Intraoperative cultures: Multiple deep tissue samples (at least 3–5) must be collected before perioperative antibiotics. Cultures should be held ≥14 days for slow-growing organisms. Hardware sonication may improve diagnostic yield. Level IV

6. Indications & Strategies for Revision Surgery

For the majority of patients with a symptomatic broken plate, revision surgery is the definitive treatment. The goals are to address the root cause of failure, achieve solid bone union, restore anatomical alignment and function, and alleviate pain. Source Review

📋 Key Concept Revision surgery is not a repetition of the primary procedure. It is a complex reconstructive undertaking that is technically more demanding, carries higher complication risks, and requires meticulous preoperative planning.

6.1 Absolute Indications for Surgery

  • Symptomatic Nonunion: Persistent pain, instability, and significant functional limitation long after expected healing period Level IV
  • Infected Nonunion: Confirmed or highly suspected infection; colonized implant must be removed with aggressive debridement Level IV
  • Gross Malalignment: Functionally unacceptable deformity (significant angulation, rotation, shortening) Level IV
  • Hardware Failure with Gross Instability: Loss of reduction with palpable motion Source Review

6.2 Relative Indications

  • Failure to meet functional goals: In high-demand patients, even mild-symptom nonunion may be functionally unacceptable
  • Painful or prominent hardware after union: Elective removal for soft-tissue irritation
  • Asymptomatic atrophic nonunion: Carries risk of future pain/fracture; prophylactic intervention often recommended in healthy, active patients

6.3 Principles of Revision Osteosynthesis

Preoperative Planning

Root-cause analysis is not an academic exercise—it is a vital prognostic tool. The cause of the first failure is a strong predictor of revision outcome. A failure due to a clear technical error carries a better prognosis than one rooted in profound biological compromise. Source Review

The plan must anticipate challenges: correct extraction instruments, specialized sets for stripped/broken screws, high-speed burrs, metal-cutting tools, and CT-based planning for corrective osteotomies and bone graft estimation.

Surgical Steps

  1. Exposure & Hardware Removal: Complete removal of all failed implant components; technically arduous if screws are stripped or encased in bone
  2. Debridement & Cultures: Aggressive debridement of fibrous tissue, scar, and sclerotic avascular bone back to healthy, bleeding bone ("paprika sign"); multiple deep tissue cultures before antibiotics
  3. Reduction & Deformity Correction: Restore length, alignment, rotation; formal osteotomy if malunion present
  4. Bone Grafting: Autologous iliac crest graft is essential in nearly all nonunion revision cases—providing osteogenic cells, osteoinductive factors, and osteoconductive scaffold
  5. Definitive Fixation: New, more robust construct correcting mechanical flaws of original fixation. May involve longer/stronger plate, more screws placed far from fracture, or conversion to intramedullary nail ("exchange nailing")

6.4 Expected Outcomes & Complications

When performed by an experienced surgeon with adherence to sound principles, revision surgery for aseptic nonunion has high success rates (~90% or higher). Level IV A review of revision surgery for nonunions of the distal humerus reported union in 15 of 16 patients (94%), with 69% achieving "excellent" functional outcomes (Mayo Elbow Performance Score).

However, complication rates are substantially higher than primary fixation:

  • Infection: Significantly higher than the 1–2% rate for primary procedures
  • Neurovascular injury: Scarred planes increase iatrogenic risk
  • Further bone loss / iatrogenic fracture: Hardware removal can be destructive
  • Persistent pain, stiffness, functional deficits: Even with solid union, complete return to normalcy is not always possible

7. The Role & Scope of Conservative Management

Conservative management is not a primary treatment strategy aimed at achieving fracture union. It is a carefully considered palliative or risk-mitigation approach reserved for a very small and specific subset of patients. Source Review

7.1 Patient Selection & Indications

Indication 1: Asymptomatic Patient with Healed Fracture

The patient presents with an incidental finding of a broken plate, is completely asymptomatic, and radiographs show solid bony union in acceptable alignment. The plate fulfilled its scaffolding mission before failing from fatigue. Management is simply observation. Source Review

Indication 2: High-Risk Patient with Low Functional Demand

Medically frail, elderly patients with severe comorbidities (advanced cardiovascular disease, end-stage renal disease, severe dementia) who are poor candidates for complex revision surgery. If symptoms are mild and functional demands very low, the risks of anesthesia and postoperative complications may be deemed prohibitive. Goals are palliative: pain control and functional support. Source Review

⚠️ Long-Term Trajectory Consideration For a borderline candidate, choosing conservative management may simply postpone an inevitable surgery to a time when the patient is even older, more deconditioned, and has worse bone stock—making future operation more hazardous. The decision must include a "look-ahead" analysis.

7.2 Protocols for Conservative Treatment

  • Immobilization & Activity Modification: Custom-molded brace, splint, or cast; strict weight-bearing limitations (non-weight-bearing → touchdown → partial)
  • Biological Stimulation: Bone stimulators (PEMF or LIPUS) may be considered, though efficacy in established nonunion with hardware failure is not well-established Source Review
  • Pain Management: Multimodal regimen; high-dose NSAIDs should generally be avoided as they may inhibit bone healing
  • Ongoing Surveillance: Regular clinical and radiographic follow-up to monitor for progressive displacement or deformity

7.3 Outcomes & Limitations

Conservative management is a strategy of compromise. When applied to the average healthy patient with symptomatic nonunion, it is destined to fail and can lead to:

  • Persistent pain and disability
  • Progressive deformity (making future surgery more complex)
  • Muscle atrophy and joint stiffness (potentially permanent)
  • Inferior union rates and functional outcomes vs. surgical fixation

Infection Workup
Question: Are there clinical signs or elevated inflammatory markers (CRP/ESR) suggestive of infection?
→ High Suspicion: REVISION SURGERY (primary goal: infection eradication with hardware removal, aggressive debridement, cultures; likely staged reconstruction)
→ Low Suspicion: Proceed to Step 3 (maintain high index of suspicion; obtain intraoperative cultures)
3
Radiographic Analysis: Biological Personality of Nonunion
Question: Does the nonunion appear hypertrophic (biologically active) or atrophic (biologically inert)?
→ Hypertrophic: REVISION SURGERY (focus on enhancing mechanical stability)
→ Atrophic: REVISION SURGERY (focus on biological revitalization: debridement + liberal bone grafting + stable fixation)
4
Patient Factor Assessment: Surgical Candidacy
Question: Can the patient safely tolerate major reconstructive surgery, and are their goals consistent with it?
→ Good Candidate: Proceed with planned REVISION SURGERY
→ Poor Candidate (prohibitive risk, very low demand, manageable symptoms): CONSERVATIVE MANAGEMENT (palliative: pain control, bracing, activity modification; union is not the objective)

10. Key Recommendations for Clinical Practice

1
Prioritize Biology in All Fracture Fixation. The most effective way to manage plate failure is to prevent it. Surgical techniques must prioritize preservation and enhancement of the biological environment: meticulous soft-tissue handling, biological plating principles (e.g., bridge plating), and judicious bone graft use in high-risk and all revision cases. Source Recommendation
2
Diagnose the "Why" Before Intervening. A broken plate is a symptom, not a diagnosis. A thorough workup to differentiate mechanical insufficiency, biological failure, and underlying infection is mandatory before the operating room. Source Recommendation
3
Surgery Is the Standard of Care for Symptomatic Failure. For the vast majority of reasonable surgical candidates with symptomatic nonunion after plate breakage, revision surgery offers the most reliable path to union, function restoration, and pain relief. Source Recommendation
4
Reserve Conservative Management for a Highly Select Population. Non-operative care is a strategy of compromise, reserved for: (1) truly asymptomatic patients with incidental broken plates and solidly healed fractures, or (2) medically frail, low-demand patients for whom revision risks are prohibitive. Source Recommendation
5
Counsel Patients Extensively & Set Realistic Expectations. Revision surgery is a major reconstructive procedure with a significantly higher complication profile than primary surgery. Patients must be informed about prolonged recovery, potential for residual deficits, and significant risks. Source Recommendation
🔮 Future Direction The integration of artificial intelligence and predictive analytics may refine this decision-making process. Future AI-driven algorithms could analyze vast datasets of patient characteristics, fracture patterns, and implant details to generate personalized, probabilistic outcome models—transforming the current heuristic framework into a more quantitative, patient-specific risk assessment.
