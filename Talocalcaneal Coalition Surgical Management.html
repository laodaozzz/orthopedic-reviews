1. Pathomechanics & Natural History

Tarsal coalition represents a congenital architectural anomaly of the hindfoot characterized by an aberrant bony, cartilaginous, or fibrous bridge uniting two or more tarsal bones. Embryologically, the pathology manifests due to a failure in mesenchymal differentiation and segmentation of primitive joint clefts during the 9th and 10th weeks of fetal gestation. The inheritance pattern is autosomal dominant with high penetrance, with bilateral involvement in approximately 50% of cases.Level II

Prevalence & Distribution

Population prevalence estimates range from 1% to 13%. The calcaneonavicular (CN) and talocalcaneal (TC) subtypes account for over 90% of all presentations. Among TC coalitions, the combined middle-posterior facet (MP) subtype represents the most frequently encountered variety, appearing in 50.9%–68.5% of mapped cases.Level II

Progressive Biological Timeline

The natural history follows a predictable progression:

  1. Fibrous union (syndesmosis) — presents in early childhood
  2. Cartilaginous bridge (synchondrosis) — develops during growth
  3. Complete osseous fusion (synostosis) — terminal phase coinciding with adolescent growth spurt (ages 8–16)

Biomechanical Consequences

The subtalar joint normally functions as a multidirectional torque converter, translating transverse plane rotation of the lower leg into sagittal and coronal plane motions during gait. A coalition mechanically blocks this arc, forcibly transferring loads to the Chopart joint complex and ankle joint. This aberrant distribution precipitates:

  • Articular instability and ligamentous strain
  • Rigid pes planovalgus (flatfoot) deformity
  • Chronic hindfoot/lateral ankle pain
  • Recurrent ankle sprains and progressive stiffness
  • Accelerated secondary degenerative joint disease
Conservative Management Initial treatment includes NSAIDs, physical therapy, custom orthotics, and immobilization. However, these strategies fundamentally fail to alter the structural nature of the coalition or correct secondary malalignment. Pain and deformity typically worsen as the coalition matures.Level IV

Key MRI Finding: Bone Marrow Edema Marrow edema on fluid-sensitive sequences (T2-weighted fat-suppressed imaging) is a highly sensitive biomarker of micro-instability, focal mechanical stress, and active inflammation — often the primary clue correlating the structural abnormality with the active pain generator.Level II

Os Sustentaculum: Extraarticular Variant

Recent literature indicates that extraarticular coalitions associated with an os sustentaculum represent a highly prevalent subset, accounting for up to 16.0% of all foot coalitions and 24.1% of all TC coalitions. The accessory ossicle tethers the talus to the calcaneus outside the true articular space and frequently generates profound bone marrow edema. Crucially, the hypertrophic osseous mass can induce mechanical compression of the medial plantar nerve within the tarsal tunnel, causing neuropathy and denervation edema in the abductor hallucis muscle.Level II

3. Rozansky Anatomical Classification

Developed by Rozansky, Mubarak, and colleagues using 3D CT reconstructions, this system categorizes coalitions based on structural composition, spatial location, and facet joint orientation. It dictates the specific surgical approach and osteotomy trajectory required.Level II

Table 2: Rozansky Morphological Classification of Talocalcaneal Coalitions
Type Characteristics Surgical Implications Facet Subtypes
Type I (Linear) Fibrocartilaginous; runs parallel to joint space. May feature inferiorly overgrown talus or superiorly overgrown calcaneus. Horizontal resection following the fibrous interface A, M, P
Type II (Hook) Linear anteriorly, curves into posterior hook overlapping sustentaculum tali Posterior hook must be fully excised to restore mobility A, M, P, MP (most common)
Type III (Shingled) Sloping, overlapping orientation; talar portion shingled over calcaneal. Hypoplastic sustentaculum common. Steep caudal-to-cephalic osteotomy required A, M, P
Type IV (Complete Osseous) Mature synostosis; normal borderlines entirely obliterated Extremely difficult mapping; highest risk of arthrodesis conversion Variable
Type V (Posterior Peripheral) Small bony bridge in extreme posterior recess of subtalar joint High neurovascular hazard — intimate proximity to medial bundle P

Facet Subtypes: Subtype A = anterior facets; Subtype M = middle facets; Subtype P = posterior facets. The combined Type II-MP (middle + posterior facets) is the most frequently encountered variety (50.9%–68.5% of cases). Understanding exact facet involvement is critical for determining surgical incision length.Level II

4. Indications for Resection: The 50% Rule & Biomechanical Thresholds

The 50% Rule (Cornerstone Decision Criterion) A talocalcaneal coalition is deemed resectable only if the total cross-sectional surface area of the coalition occupies <50% of the surface area of the calcaneal posterior facet, as measured on preoperative coronal CT images.Level II

The physiological rationale is rooted in load distribution: the posterior facet is the primary weight-bearing fulcrum. When a massive coalition is excised, if the remaining cartilage constitutes <50%, it becomes biomechanically insufficient, inducing catastrophic micro-instability and early-onset severe osteoarthritis.Level III

Complete Criteria for Successful Joint-Preserving Resection

All of the following must be concurrently satisfied:

  1. Coalition area <50% of posterior facet surface area on coronal CTLevel II
  2. Posterior facet cartilage health: Wide, thick layer of viable articular cartilage; no advanced degenerative changes (Kellgren and Lawrence)Level II
  3. Hindfoot valgus ≤16° on coronal imagingLevel III
  4. No lateral impingement: No pathological narrowing or abutment of the lateral talar process against the calcaneusLevel III
When to Proceed Directly to Arthrodesis If any criterion is violated — >50% involvement, rigid valgus >16°, overt posterior facet osteoarthritis, or severe lateral impingement — contemporary evidence strongly directs bypassing resection and proceeding directly to primary subtalar or triple arthrodesis.Level II

5. Operative Techniques & Advanced Surgical Execution

5.1 Open Medial Approach (Historic Standard)

Remains the primary workhorse for middle facet and combined MP coalitions. The optimal surgical window lies within the interval between the posterior tibialis and flexor digitorum longus (FDL) tendons.Level II

  • Key hazard: Medial neurovascular bundle (posterior tibial artery + tibial nerve) must be meticulously freed and retracted plantarly/posteriorly
  • Osteotomy guidance: Must strictly conform to preoperative 3D CT roadmap; divergence risks iatrogenic fractures
  • Rozansky Type III adaptation: Dynamic alteration of osteotomy inclination to steep caudal-to-cephalic trajectory
  • Intraoperative confirmation: Placement of smooth lamina spreader; visual distraction of posterior facet; restoration of sweeping subtalar inversion/eversion

5.2 Deltoid-Preserving Lateral Mini-Open Approach

A novel technique particularly suited for Type III shingled coalitions. Uses a lateral portal through the sinus tarsi and a secondary mini medial incision for neurovascular protection. The osteotomy is driven lateral-to-medial, preserving the superficial deltoid ligament.Level IV

Advantage Over Medial Approach Avoids partial release or sacrifice of the superficial deltoid ligament, which can destabilize the medial column and exacerbate postoperative pes planovalgus.

5.3 Endoscopic & Arthroscopic Resection

For Types I and II non-osseous coalitions, arthroscopic debridement follows the preoperative 3D CT mapping with minimal collateral damage. However, endoscopy is severely limited for Type IV synostoses due to obliterated anatomical borderlines. The technique carries a steep learning curve requiring exceptional spatial awareness.Level IV

6. Recurrence Prevention & Interposition Materials

Following coalition removal, interposition of a biological or synthetic barrier material is an absolute surgical standard of care to prevent hematoma ossification and re-fusion.Level II

Key Evidence: Spaans et al. Systematic Review Analyzed 581 feet (436 patients), including 153 TC coalitions. Overall recurrence: 9% (45/485). TC-specific recurrence: approximately 4%, significantly lower than CN coalitions (~11%). Median MINORS score: 7 (low methodological quality overall).Level II

7. Concurrent Flatfoot Reconstruction

A rigid TC coalition fundamentally alters hindfoot architecture, producing severe pes planovalgus. Contemporary evidence firmly establishes that isolated resection in patients with severe structural deformity leaves the foot misaligned, perpetuating pain and accelerating arthritis.Level III

Radiographic Thresholds Indicating Reconstruction

Table 4: Radiographic Thresholds for Operative Flatfoot Reconstruction
Parameter Normal Range Pathological Threshold
Calcaneal Pitch Angle 18°–32° Significantly depressed (e.g., 8°) → arch collapse
Meary's Angle (Lateral Talar-1st MT) −4° to +4° Highly negative (e.g., −11°) → severe flatfoot
Talonavicular Coverage Angle <7° 19°–22° → massive forefoot abduction
Hindfoot Valgus <10° >16° → strict threshold for reconstruction

Surgical Reconstruction Strategies

1. Evans Lateral Column Lengthening Osteotomy — Indicated when fluid subtalar motion is restored post-resection. Creates osteotomy 1.5 cm proximal to calcaneocuboid joint with structural graft insertion. Restores talonavicular coverage, reconstitutes arch height via windlass mechanism.Level II

2. Medializing Calcaneal Displacement Osteotomy (MDO) — Indicated when subtalar motion remains significantly reduced post-resection. Shifts posterior heel segment medially ~1 cm, correcting the valgus mechanical axis without relying on gliding joint kinematics.Level III

3. Subtalar Arthroereisis — Less invasive, joint-sparing option. Implant insertion into sinus tarsi mechanically blocks excessive eversion. AOFAS scores improve from mid-50s to >92; Meary's angle corrects from −11.28° to 1.89°.Level I

Arthroereisis Limitations Contraindicated with rigid deformities or secondary subtalar arthritis. Implant intolerance/sinus tarsi pain requiring removal occurs in up to 25% of cases.Level I
Single-Stage vs. Staged Contemporary consensus overwhelmingly supports single-stage coalition resection + flatfoot reconstruction. It immediately corrects malalignment, bypasses multiple anesthetic events, and significantly accelerates rehabilitation.Level II

8. Clinical Outcomes: Adolescents vs. Young Adults

Adolescent Population (<19 years)

Due to high biological plasticity and minimal secondary OA, resection with fat graft interposition yields 84%–90% good to excellent outcomes. Patients return to pain-free ambulation, restore arch architecture, and resume high-impact sports. Subtalar motion rarely returns to 100% anatomical normal but is highly functional.Level II

Adult Population

Recent systematic evaluations of 72 adult patients (the largest adult TC cohort to date) have challenged the historic dogma favoring primary arthrodesis:

  • VAS pain scores: Reduction of ~3–4 points (6.86 → 2.21)Level II
  • AOFAS scores: Improvement of ~22.7 points (45.0 → 67.7)Level II
  • Key finding: Chronological age alone is NOT a contraindication — the true limiting factor is the "biological age" of the joint (presence/absence of advanced OA)
Adult Reoperation Risk Overall reoperation rate in adults: 33.8%. Indications include hardware removal, excision of reactive exostoses, and conversion to arthrodesis. The primary predictor of failure is incomplete surgical resection.Level II
