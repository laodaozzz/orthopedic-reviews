Advanced Clinical Review: Tibial Interference Screw Migration & Osteolysis Following Synthetic ACL Reconstruction

Interactive Evidence-Based Educational Summary • Derived from Provided Clinical Review Document • 2022 ESSKA Consensus–Aligned

Introduction to the Clinical Scenario

Anterior cruciate ligament (ACL) rupture is the most common ligamentous injury of the knee in athletes and highly active populations, frequently necessitating surgical reconstruction to restore joint stability and prevent progressive degenerative joint disease. While autografts—specifically bone-patellar tendon-bone (BPTB) and quadrupled hamstring tendon (HT) grafts—remain the established gold standard for ACL reconstruction (ACLR), the desire for accelerated rehabilitation, elimination of donor-site morbidity, and provision of immediate postoperative mechanical strength has driven ongoing interest in synthetic ligaments.

The Ligament Advanced Reinforcement System (LARS; Arc-sur-Tille, France) represents a third-generation synthetic graft composed of polyethylene terephthalate (PET) fibers. Unlike its predecessors from the 1970s–1980s (Dacron, Gore-Tex)—largely abandoned due to catastrophically high rates of mechanical failure and reactive synovitis—the LARS device features an intra-articular portion of parallel longitudinal fibers twisted at 90° to mimic native ACL anatomy and reduce shearing forces, alongside a transverse knitted extra-articular structure designed to facilitate tissue ingrowth.1

⚠ Critical Clinical Scenario

Titanium interference screw backout and migration at the tibial tunnel, accompanied by severe tunnel widening and osteolysis within six months post-operatively, represents a catastrophic biomechanical and biological failure of the synthetic construct. This underscores a fundamental limitation of synthetic grafts: the inability to achieve true biological osseointegration.

This comprehensive report examines the epidemiology, dual biomechanical and biological etiology, advanced clinical evaluation protocols, and evidence-based decision algorithms for management of revision ACLR following the failure of a synthetic graft complicated by tibial hardware migration and osteolysis. The analysis strictly integrates high-level evidence from systematic reviews, meta-analyses, and international consensus guidelines, notably the 2022 European Society of Sports Traumatology, Knee Surgery and Arthroscopy (ESSKA) consensus on ACL revision.6,8

1. Epidemiology & Evidence-Based Incidence

Utilization of synthetic ligaments such as LARS is often reserved for carefully selected cases: older patients desiring rapid functional recovery, patients with multi-ligamentous knee injuries, or specific scenarios requiring early return to sport (RTS) where autograft harvest is deemed suboptimal.1

1.1 Tibial Tunnel Widening: Synthetic vs. Autograft

Bone tunnel widening (TW) is a ubiquitous radiological phenomenon following ACLR. It typically initiates within the first six weeks and stabilizes by 1–2 years post-operatively.

Autograft tunnel widening: In hamstring and BPTB autografts, Level I and Level II comparative studies report an incidence of significant tibial tunnel widening (defined as >10%–30% enlargement) ranging from 12.1% to 43.17% at 6–12 months, depending on the fixation method.II9,10 For example, Kong et al. (2023) in Medicina demonstrated a 43.17% tunnel enlargement rate for anteriorly placed screws in hamstring grafts at one year.II10 However, autograft tunnel widening is generally considered a benign, non-progressive radiological finding that does not strictly correlate with clinical instability, provided the graft undergoes biological ligamentization.

Synthetic graft tunnel widening: In LARS and other synthetic grafts, tunnel widening is frequently pathological and progressive. While short-term stability may initially appear statistically equivalent to autografts due to the immediate tensile strength of PET fibers, the medium-term failure rate driven by osteolysis is alarmingly high. Antzoulas and Giannatos (2026) reported that while LARS-augmented ACLR showed significantly better early functional outcomes (Lysholm, IKDC, Tegner scores) at 6 months compared to hamstring autografts, long-term outcomes were merely similar.I1 Tulloch et al. (2019) reported an overall mechanical failure rate of 33.3% for primary LARS ACL surgery at a median of 3.9 years, driven by progressive tunnel osteolysis.III5

1.2 Tibial Screw Migration and Backout

Tibial interference screw backout at six months is a rare but well-documented complication that predominantly occurs in the absence of intra-tunnel graft integration.21 The tibial fixation site is the weakest link in ACL reconstruction due to lower bone mineral density of the proximal tibial metaphysis and mechanical pullout force oriented parallel to the tibial tunnel axis, maximizing shear and tensile stress.26

In autograft ACLR, interference screw migration is generally reported at <1%–2% overall.II23 Bioabsorbable screws can exhibit late aseptic extrusion; DeFroda et al. (2019) reported symptomatic bioabsorbable screw issues in ~9.6% of pediatric patients, with 4.9% requiring surgical removal at a median of 24 months.II28 Titanium screw backout in autograft is virtually unheard of unless catastrophic technical error exists.32

In synthetic grafts, early titanium hardware backout within six months has an elevated incidence. Because the PET graft fails to biologically bond with cancellous bone, the titanium screw perpetually bears all cyclical loading forces. Continuous micro-motion of synthetic fibers against the screw threads acts as an abrasive saw, loosening the interference fit. Coupled with aggressive focal osteolysis, the screw loses cortical purchase and migrates retrograde.III20

2. Biomechanical & Biological Etiology of Failure

The catastrophic failure cascade characterized by early titanium screw backout and extreme tunnel widening in LARS ACLR cannot be attributed to a single factor. Current EBM literature dictates a dual-mechanism model: the catastrophic synergy of unmitigated mechanical micromotion and a hostile biological foreign-body immune response.III11

2.1 Absence of Sharpey's Fibers & Failure of Osseointegration

Following autograft ACLR, incorporation of soft tissue within bony tunnels involves a complex, multiphasic progression from an indirect to a direct tendon insertion:46

  • Weeks 1–6: A hypocellular, hypovascular interface forms between the tendon graft and the bone tunnel wall, consisting of disorganized granulation tissue.1
  • Weeks 6–12: Chondrogenic progenitors replace granulation tissue with type II collagen, initiating endochondral ossification. Crucially, Sharpey-like fibers—initially type III collagen, later remodeling into organized type I collagen fibrils—penetrate the adjacent cancellous bone.46
  • By 24 weeks: Hunt et al. (2008) demonstrated in sheep models published in AJSM that this fibrocartilaginous transition zone restores graft stiffness to 86% of the native knee.V48

Synthetic PET fibers lack the bioactivity to stimulate endochondral ossification. PET is inherently hydrophobic, limiting cellular adhesion, fibroblast proliferation, and vascular ingrowth.51 Histological analyses of revised LARS grafts consistently demonstrate a complete absence of penetrating Sharpey's fibers. Legnani et al. (2021) in KSSTA found the synthetic graft enveloped in dense, disorganized fibrous scar tissue with no direct fibrocartilaginous integration.III5 The construct therefore remains perpetually dependent on primary mechanical fixation—the titanium interference screw.

2.2 Mechanical Etiology: Windshield-Wiper & Bungee-Cord Effects

Without biological stabilization from Sharpey's fibers, the synthetic graft is subjected to continuous micromotion during normal knee kinematics, manifesting through two synergistic phenomena:9

🔄 Windshield-Wiper Effect

Transverse, coronal, and sagittal toggling of the graft within the intra-articular tunnel aperture. The unintegrated PET fibers pivot at the interference screw, creating immense shear stress against cancellous bone walls. The ultra-stiff PET fibers physically erode metaphyseal bone, expanding the tunnel into a conical or "trumpet-shaped" deformity.II38,43

↕ Bungee-Cord Effect

Longitudinal, piston-like elasticity of the graft moving along the tunnel axis. Physiological loads of 100–300 N during early rehabilitation can induce up to 0.3 mm of graft motion relative to the tunnel wall. Over thousands of cyclical loading steps within six months, this mechanically abrades the tunnel.III54

Improper tunnel placement significantly exacerbates these forces. Pearle et al. (2015) in Arthroscopy posited the IDEAL concept (isometric, direct insertion, eccentric, anatomic, low tension-flexion pattern); non-isometric placement increases maximal graft-tunnel motion, amplifying the windshield-wiper effect.II55

2.3 Biological Etiology: Wear Debris & Reactive Osteolysis

Mechanical friction generated by the windshield-wiper effect aggressively degrades PET fibers. Microscopic wear particles are sheared off and shed into the intra-articular space and bone tunnel interface.5

These synthetic wear debris particles incite a florid, destructive foreign-body immune response:III

  • Massive influx of CD163+ M2 macrophages and formation of multinucleated giant cells.
  • Under polarized light microscopy, brightly bi-refractive PET wear particles are observed phagocytosed within giant cells.5
  • Continuous macrophage activation triggers release of pro-inflammatory cytokines: TNF-α, IL-1β, and IL-6.11
  • These cytokines potently upregulate osteoclastic activity at the graft-tunnel interface, resulting in aggressive, localized osteolysis.
⚠ Dual Mechanism Summary

The profound tunnel widening observed at six months is a genuine dual mechanism: bone is simultaneously hollowed out by physical abrasion (mechanical) and actively resorbed by osteoclasts responding to wear debris (biological). The culmination is total loss of interference fixation integrity, gross knee instability, and palpable hardware migration.11,20

3. Clinical Evaluation Protocol & Imaging Guidelines

A patient presenting within six months of a LARS ACLR with recurrent instability, a palpable mass over the anteromedial tibia, joint effusion, and pain must undergo a rigorous, protocol-driven evaluation.21 The 2022 ESSKA consensus mandates a systematic diagnostic approach.IV6,8

3.1 Initial Clinical & Serological Assessment

  • Lachman test, anterior drawer, pivot-shift test for objective laxity quantification.
  • Instrumented arthrometry (KT-1000/2000): side-to-side difference >3 mm is indicative of structural failure.15
  • A prominent, tender subcutaneous mass over the anteromedial tibial metaphysis is highly pathognomonic for screw backout.21
  • Mandatory infectious workup: ESR and CRP screening. If elevated, arthrocentesis with synovial fluid analysis and prolonged cultures to rule out septic arthritis.IV42

3.2 Standard Radiography

Weight-bearing AP, lateral, and posteroanterior flexed (Schuss/Rosenberg) views, ensuring ≥15 cm of proximal tibia visible. Key assessments:42

  • Exact migration distance of the radiopaque titanium screw.
  • Posterior tibial slope (PTS): Dejour et al. and Akoto et al. (2020) in AJSM demonstrated that PTS >12° is an independent biomechanical risk factor for graft failure, exponentially increasing anterior tibial translation under axial load.II42
  • Tunnel positioning estimated using Bernard-Hertel grid (femur, aiming 4th quadrant) and Amis-Jakob line (tibia, aiming 43% of AP distance).69
  • Standard radiography is inadequate for quantifying 3D volume of osteolytic tunnel widening due to overlap, rotation, and magnification errors.72

3.3 Advanced Imaging: CT Guidelines

Computed Tomography (CT) is the undisputed gold standard for assessing bone tunnel widening, osteolysis, and spatial tunnel positioning in ACL revision planning.I11 MRI is limited in this scenario because the migrated titanium screw produces significant metallic susceptibility artifact.34

Specific CT Protocol Parameters:

  • Multiplanar Reconstruction (MPR): Scans must be reconstructed in true orthogonal planes—parallel and perpendicular to each tunnel axis. Parameters: 120 kVp tube voltage, sub-millimeter slice thickness (e.g., 0.625 mm).15
  • Measurement Locations: Cross-sectional diameter and area measured perpendicularly at three levels: intra-articular aperture, mid-substance, and extra-articular exit.72
  • 3D CT Volumetric Analysis: Crespo et al. (2014) advocate 3D-rendered models using specialized software. Three reliable methods: "best-fit transverse section" (ICC: 0.899), "best-fit cylinder" (ICC: 0.745), and "wall thickness" method.II72,73

📐 Critical Threshold

A maximal tunnel diameter exceeding 12–15 mm, or severe cavitary osteolysis compromising structural integrity of the proximal tibia, is the primary inflection point for moving from single-stage to two-stage revision protocol.IV8

4. Decision Algorithm & Management of Revision ACLR

Management of a failed LARS ACLR with titanium screw backout and osteolysis is exceptionally challenging. The surgeon faces massive metaphyseal bone loss, dense foreign-body reactive tissue, lack of viable native graft remnants, and the biomechanical imperative to restore anatomic kinematics. The algorithm pivots entirely on CT-derived tunnel geometry and concomitant alignment issues.IV6,8

4.1 Hardware Removal: Observation vs. Revision

In extremely rare instances where an extruded screw is found in an entirely asymptomatic, stable knee, isolated hardware removal with debridement could theoretically be considered.21 However, in synthetic graft osteolysis with titanium screw backout, loss of primary fixation results in total functional graft failure and clinical instability.20 Hardware removal must be integrated into a comprehensive revision strategy.67

4.2 ESSKA Algorithm: Single-Stage vs. Two-Stage Revision

Single-Stage Revision

Indicated only if preoperative 3D CT reveals salvageable bony architecture:8

  1. Existing tunnels are anatomically placed, and osteolytic widening is strictly <12–14 mm.56
  2. Alternatively, grossly non-anatomic tunnels exist, but new anatomic tunnels can be drilled entirely independently without intersecting osteolytic voids.8

Techniques include the "sandwich technique" (BPTB autograft + cancellous bone allograft chips compressing the graft against native cortical wall) or suspensory cortical button fixation for cortical blowout at the aperture.8,80

Two-Stage Revision

Given the aggressive nature of foreign-body osteolysis, tunnel diameters frequently exceed 14–15 mm by the time the titanium screw backs out.56

Indication Rationale / Clinical Context
Severe Tunnel Widening (>12–15 mm) Prevents adequate primary fixation; graft lacks cortical contact for Sharpey's fiber ingrowth.8

Active Infection Mandates hardware removal, debridement, and antibiotic spacer prior to new graft insertion.8
Severe Arthrofibrosis Requires initial lysis of adhesions and ROM restoration before new ligament placement.8

Stage 1: Hardware Removal, Debridement & Bone Grafting

  1. Meticulous Debridement: Arthroscopic excision of all PET LARS remnants and wear debris using aggressive shaving and radiofrequency ablation. Any retained synthetic fibers will perpetuate macrophage-driven osteolysis.III5,78 Sclerotic tunnel walls are reamed or rasped to healthy, bleeding cancellous bone.
  2. Structural Bone Grafting: Massive cavitary defects are densely packed with cylindrical structural allograft bone dowels (e.g., fresh-frozen femoral head allograft), sized to the reamed defect and press-fit into tunnels. Remaining voids are packed with cancellous bone chips and demineralized bone matrix (DBM).75,78,82
  3. Concomitant Osteotomies: If PTS >12°, an anterior closing wedge high tibial osteotomy (ACWO) should be performed concurrently to neutralize abnormal sagittal biomechanics.IV8

Stage 2: Definitive Revision ACLR (4–6 Months Post Stage 1)

Interval CT must confirm trabecular incorporation and consolidation of bone dowels before proceeding.56

  1. Graft Selection: Restoring pure biology is paramount. BPTB or quadriceps tendon (QT) autografts are strongly favored by ESSKA over hamstring or soft-tissue allografts. Bone blocks facilitate rapid, robust bone-to-bone integration.IV59
  2. Tunnel Placement: New tunnels drilled in exact anatomic position through newly consolidated, vascularized bone graft.75
  3. Peripheral Augmentation: High-level EBM guidelines recommend adding a lateral extra-articular tenodesis (LET) or anterolateral ligament (ALL) reconstruction. Laboudie et al. (2022) in KSSTA demonstrated that combining ACLR with ALL reconstruction significantly reduces graft failure and secondary meniscal injury rates.II22

Conclusion

Tibial titanium interference screw backout combined with profound tunnel osteolysis within six months of LARS ACL reconstruction represents a highly complex, multifactorial failure of both biomechanics and biology. The core etiology is rooted in the synthetic PET graft's inability to foster native biological osseointegration via Sharpey's fibers. This biological deficit exposes the construct to immense, unmitigated mechanical shear forces—specifically the transverse windshield-wiper and longitudinal bungee-cord effects. The resultant mechanical abrasion, combined with an aggressive macrophage-mediated foreign-body immune reaction to shed PET wear debris, drives rapid, massive tunnel widening and catastrophic loss of interference screw fixation.

Managing this severe complication requires strict adherence to evidence-based protocols:

  • Advanced 3D CT imaging with precise multiplanar reconstruction is essential to quantify osteolytic voids.
  • Defects >12–15 mm or confluent tunnels mandate a meticulous two-stage revision.
  • Definitive treatment: aggressive arthroscopic debridement of all synthetic debris, structural bone dowel grafting, a mandatory biological healing window, and delayed reconstruction with robust autograft tissue (BPTB/QT).
  • Lateral extra-articular tenodesis (LET) is highly recommended for rotational protection of the healing graft.

By systematically neutralizing both the hostile biological environment and the abnormal mechanical forces, the surgeon can successfully salvage the knee, restore stable anatomic kinematics, and safely facilitate return to functional activity.
