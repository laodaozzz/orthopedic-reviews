Evidence-Based Radiographic Evaluation of the Ankle

Interactive Educational Summary

1. Introduction & Biomechanical Context

The ankle joint is one of the most mechanically complex and frequently injured articular structures in the human body, imposing a substantial diagnostic and therapeutic burden on emergency departments and orthopedic practices globally.[1] Epidemiological data indicate an incidence of approximately 187 per 100,000 individuals annually, a figure continuing to rise due to an increasingly active aging population and higher rates of athletic participation.[2]

Within this broad diagnostic category, malleolar fractures represent the vast majority of osseous injuries:

  • 60%–70% manifest as unimalleolar fractures (predominantly lateral malleolus)
  • 15%–20% present as bimalleolar fractures
  • 7%–12% present as complex trimalleolar fractures (involving posterior tibial margin)[2]

Clinical management relies fundamentally on the rapid, accurate acquisition and interpretation of plain radiography, which universally remains the primary, first-line imaging modality before any advanced cross-sectional imaging protocols are considered.[1]

1.1 Biomechanical Architecture

The ankle joint possesses an inherent biomechanical asymmetry critical to understanding injury patterns:[1]

  • Medial aspect: Inherently rigid—defined by firm osseous attachment of the medial malleolus to the distal tibia, reinforced by the robust deltoid ligament complex.
  • Lateral aspect: Functions as a flexible support system—composed of the distal fibula, the syndesmotic ligamentous network (AITFL, PITFL, interosseous membrane), and the lateral collateral ligaments (ATFL, PTFL, CFL).

This asymmetry permits the talus to translate laterally and dorsally during external rotation in the gait cycle, before the elastic properties of the lateral complex restore its resting position. The fibula itself bears minimal axial weight, serving almost entirely as a dynamic lateral buttress.[1]

⚠ Injury Mechanism: When the foot is forced into extreme supination or pronation, the flexible ankle transforms into a rigid lever. Immense forces are transmitted directly to the enclosing mortise, resulting in predictable pull-off (avulsion) or push-off (impaction) fractures and syndesmotic diastasis.[1]

1.2 Ottawa Ankle Rules

Clinical decision-making is guided by the Ottawa Ankle Rules. Ankle radiography is indicated when a patient presents with pain in the malleolar zone and any of the following:[1,6]

  1. Bone tenderness to the posterior edge or distal tip of the lateral malleolus (distal 6 cm)
  2. Corresponding tenderness over the posterior edge or tip of the medial malleolus
  3. Complete inability to bear weight for four consecutive steps (immediately and upon evaluation)

The standard radiographic series universally incorporates three distinct projections: the anteroposterior (AP) view, the internal oblique (mortise) view, and the direct lateral view.[2]

2. Differentiating the AP and Mortise Views

Because the anatomical coronal plane of the ankle mortise—defined by the transmalleolar axis—is inherently externally rotated by approximately 15 to 20 degrees relative to the true sagittal plane, a standard AP radiograph does not provide a clear, unobstructed profile of the entire tibiotalar joint.[8] Both views serve entirely distinct diagnostic purposes.

2.1 The Standard Anteroposterior (AP) View

Positioning: Patient supine, knee fully extended, posterior knee and ankle against the table, tibia parallel to the table. Foot in neutral position (slight dorsiflexion). Central beam aimed at the mid-tibiotalar joint.[12,13]

Defining Landmark: Significant superimposition of the distal fibula over the lateral aspect of the distal tibia and talus—this obscures the lateral tibiotalar joint space.[2]

Clinical Utility:[2,9,14]

  • Optimal for evaluating the lateral process of the talus
  • Assessment of soft tissue edema adjacent to malleoli
  • Calculation of syndesmotic parameters (TFO, TFCS)—normal TFO on AP > 6 mm
  • Preferred for medial clear space measurement due to anatomical obliquity of the medial mortise

2.2 The Mortise (Internal Oblique) View

Positioning: Patient supine, knee extended, entire leg and ankle internally rotated by precisely 15–20 degrees to counteract the natural external version of the ankle. Foot in slight dorsiflexion. Central beam at mid-tibiotalar joint.[8,9]

Defining Criteria: Uninterrupted visualization of the entire mortise—uniform radiolucent space between the talar dome and tibial plafond superiorly, between medial malleolus and medial talar facet medially, and between lateral malleolus and lateral talar facet laterally.[8]

Critical Quality Check: If the mortise view shows absolutely no superimposition of the talus and fibula with clear visualization of the sinus tarsi, the leg has been excessively internally rotated—the view is suboptimal and non-diagnostic. Insufficient internal rotation will fail to open the lateral mortise, mimicking a standard AP view.[6,13]

A perfectly executed mortise view demonstrates a highly uniform joint space width of approximately 4 mm across its entire contoured surface.[9]

2.3 Quantitative Parameters: AP vs. Mortise

Table 1. Key Radiographic Measurements — AP and Mortise Projections
Parameter Projection Technique & Threshold
Tibiofibular Clear Space (TFCS) AP & Mortise Measured horizontally 1 cm proximal to tibial plafond. Distance between lateral border of posterior tibial malleolus and medial border of fibula. Abnormal if > 6 mm — indicates syndesmotic injury.[9]
Tibiofibular Overlap (TFO) AP & Mortise Measured at point of maximum osseous overlap between distal tibia and fibula. On AP: normal > 6 mm. On Mortise: only slight superimposition should exist. Less than minimal overlap suggests syndesmotic diastasis.[9]
Medial Clear Space (MCS) AP & Mortise Distance between lateral border of medial malleolus and medial articular border of talus. Should be uniform with superior joint space (typically < 4 mm). MCS > 4–5 mm indicates lateral talar shift and deltoid ligament disruption.[12]
Talocrural Angle Mortise Angle between a line parallel to the distal tibial articular surface and a line connecting the tips of the medial and lateral malleoli. Assesses fibular length and symmetrical mortise alignment.[16]
⚠ Interpretive Caution: Concurrent external rotation of the talus and ankle plantarflexion can profile the narrower posterior part of the talus within the mortise, producing a false radiological impression of widened medial clear space on an AP radiograph. Corroborating with the mortise view is mandated.[14]

3. Definitive Criteria for a True Lateral Ankle Radiograph

The lateral radiograph serves as the unquestioned cornerstone for assessing sagittal alignment, subtalar articulation congruity, posterior tibial margin integrity, and soft tissue structures of the hindfoot.[4]

3.1 Patient Positioning

  • Patient supine or lateral decubitus; lateral aspect of ankle flat against the detector[9]
  • Knee fully extended; leg and ankle externally rotated precisely 90 degrees[13]
  • Foot in neutral dorsiflexion (plantar surface ~90° to long axis of tibia) — brings widest anterior portion of talar dome into mortise and places lateral collateral ligaments under physiological tension[13]
  • Central beam aimed at the medial malleolus[13]
  • Collimation must capture: distal third of tibia/fibula, entire talus and calcaneus, and base of fifth metatarsal[2,6]

3.2 Radiographic Landmarks — Non-Negotiable Criteria

According to BOAST clinical audit standards and standardized radiological checklists:[10]

  1. Perfect superimposition of talar domes — medial and lateral arcs of the talar dome must perfectly overlap as a single, smooth, uninterrupted convex sclerotic line.[10]
  2. Fibula over posterior tibia — distal fibula must be superimposed strictly over the posterior half of the distal tibia, not projecting posterior to the posterior malleolus.[10,13]
  3. Superimposition of malleoli — lateral malleolus superimposed directly over medial malleolus; its inferior tip will naturally extend below the projected contour of the medial malleolus.[9,13]
  4. Open, uniform tibiotalar joint space — the radiolucent gap between tibial plafond and superior talar dome must be completely open, well-defined, and uniform.[10]
  5. Anatomical inclusion — distal third of tibia/fibula, talus, calcaneus, and proximal base of fifth metatarsal must all be captured.[2]

3.3 Identifying Malrotation

Table 2. Malrotation Patterns on Lateral Ankle Radiographs
Error Type Direction Radiographic Appearance Risk
Excessive External Rotation AP plane Lateral talar dome shifts posteriorly relative to medial dome[13] False step-off mimicking OCD fracture
Excessive Internal Rotation AP plane Fibular shaft projects too anteriorly, away from posterior half of tibia[13] Obscures posterior malleolus
Proximal Tibial Elevation Coronal (superoinferior) tilt Lateral talar dome migrates proximally — creates false "step-off" or double-contour[13] Misinterpreted as osteochondral fracture

4. Clinical Value of Suboptimal Lateral Radiographs

In the acute trauma setting, patients present with intense pain, extreme swelling, severe deformity, and restricted mobility. Obtaining a flawless lateral radiograph is challenging, yet accepting substandard images introduces substantial risks for misdiagnosis, inappropriate surgical planning, and long-term joint morbidity.[2,4]

4.1 Missed Diagnoses

Ankle fractures consistently rank among the most frequently misinterpreted acute injuries in global emergency departments.[4] Key missed pathology includes:

  • Anterior process of calcaneus fractures: Perhaps the most commonly missed ankle fracture, primarily visible only on the lateral view. Off-axis films obscure this structure.[2]
  • Proximal fifth metatarsal fractures: Frequently present as lateral ankle pain mimicking sprains; missed when collimation fails to include the midfoot zone.[2]
  • Pediatric physeal injuries: Missed fractures can lead to permanent growth plate disturbances and progressive angular deformities requiring secondary reconstructive osteotomies.[2]

Consequences of diagnostic delays include persistent pain, chronic instability, fracture nonunion, and rapidly accelerated post-traumatic osteoarthrosis.[2]

4.2 Impact on Joint Effusion Detection

The lateral radiograph is the single most sensitive projection for detecting ankle joint effusions—highly predictive secondary signs of occult osseous fractures.[2]

Key Evidence (Ref [22]): Mean anterior ankle effusion in patients without fracture: 11.2 mm. In patients with occult fractures (later identified on CT): 17.1 mm. An anterior effusion ≥ 15 mm yields a positive predictive value of 83% for an underlying occult fracture. Level II

However, malrotation can artificially obscure the fat pad or create false-positive shadows, drastically lowering diagnostic accuracy.[24]

4.3 Degradation of Morphometric Reliability

Two critical angles on the lateral projection—Böhler's angle and the crucial angle of Gissane—are universally used to assess intra-articular calcaneal fractures.[4]

Key Evidence (Ref [26]): In a cadaveric study, when the radiographic beam deviated by merely 5–25 degrees from a true perpendicular lateral position, Böhler's angle deviated significantly from the true anatomically verified angle, leading to unacceptably poor inter-observer reliability. This can result in catastrophic under-treatment or unnecessary over-treatment. Level III

In pediatric populations, normal mean Böhler's angle is 35.2° (range 14.3°–58.1°) and Gissane angle is 111.3°, making baseline variances difficult enough without projectional malrotation.[28]

4.4 BOAST 12 Guidelines & Quality Improvement

The British Orthopaedic Association Standards for Trauma (BOAST 12) mandate that initial and post-reduction radiographs must include a true lateral and a mortise view. Adequate reduction must be confirmed before discharge.[29]

Table 3. BOAST Audit Compliance — Pre- and Post-Intervention[4,10]
Audit Criterion Target Pre-Intervention Post-Intervention p-value

Following targeted educational interventions and visual reference posters, compliance improved drastically across all difficult criteria, proving that suboptimal imaging frequency can be heavily mitigated through systemic adherence to positioning landmarks.[10] Level II

5. Key Pathologies Assessed on Lateral Radiographs

5.1 The Posterior Malleolus & Syndesmotic Stability

The posterior malleolus (Volkmann fragment when fractured) is critically and primarily evaluated on the lateral radiograph. These fractures correlate with severe disruptions of the PITFL, which comprises an estimated 42% of the mechanical stabilizing force of the entire syndesmotic complex.[31] Level III

Measurement Controversy

Historically, operative fixation was indicated if the fragment involved ≥25–33% of the tibial plafond articular surface, measured using 2D linear calculation on the lateral radiograph.[32,34] However, this methodology is increasingly contested:

Table 4. Posterior Malleolus Measurement — Radiograph vs. 3D CT[34]
Method Mean Involvement SD 95% CI
Plain Radiograph Linear (Lateral) 24.6% ± 7.9% 23.1%–26.1%
3D CT Axial Linear 26.0% ± 8.9% 24.3%–27.7%
3D CT Sagittal Linear 25.8% ± 7.8% 24.3%–27.3%
3D CT Surface Area (True Volumetric) 18.8% ± 7.9% 17.3%–20.3%

Plain radiography systematically overestimates fragment size by a mean discrepancy of approximately 8.58% compared to true 3D surface area mapping. The Haraguchi classification (Type I: posterolateral; Type II: posteromedial extension; Type III: shell avulsions) further highlights the morphological complexity that 2D imaging cannot capture.[32] Level III

Current AO Foundation Guidance: Surgical indications should not be based on fragment size alone, but must consider joint dislocation, articular surface collapse, and Haraguchi morphology. The lateral radiograph remains the vital initial screening tool to determine if CT is warranted.[33]

5.2 Osteochondral Lesions of the Talus (OCDs)

Approximately 70% of the total talar surface is covered in thick hyaline cartilage. OCDs are frequently misdiagnosed as routine ankle sprains due to delayed-onset deep pain, mechanical clicking, and subjective instability.[2,38]

Table 5. Medial vs. Lateral Talar Dome Lesions[11]
Feature Medial Lesions Lateral Lesions
Location Posterior and central on dome Anterior and central on dome
Mechanism Inversion + external rotation + forced plantarflexion Inversion + forced dorsiflexion
Morphology Larger, deeper, "cup-shaped" (substantial bone) Smaller, superficial, "wafer-shaped" (cartilage shearing)
Etiology Atraumatic (ischemic microtrauma) or traumatic Almost exclusively acute mechanical trauma
Healing Potential Higher spontaneous conservative healing if non-displaced Lower healing; frequently displaced and chronically symptomatic

Talar Dome Morphology

Recent 3D morphological studies reveal the talar dome functions as a skewed, truncated conic saddle shape with its apex oriented laterally—not a simple truncated cone as historically modeled.[44] The radius of curvature of the medial border (mean posterior radius: 22.75 mm) is demonstrably larger and flatter than the tighter, more convex lateral border (mean: 18.76 mm).[44] Because these arcs have fundamentally different curvatures, achieving perfect superimposition on a 2D lateral radiograph requires extraordinarily precise beam alignment.[46] Level IV

5.3 Pediatric Considerations

In children, the distal tibial and fibular physes are the weakest biomechanical links, frequently failing before collateral ligaments tear.[5] The distal tibial physis accounts for 35–40% of overall tibial growth (~3–4 mm/year) and 15–20% of total lower extremity growth.

Ankle fractures account for up to 40% of all pediatric physeal injuries. The lateral radiograph is vital for detecting:[5]

  • Salter-Harris Type II fractures (Thurston-Holland fragment visible only on lateral view)
  • Tillaux fractures (AITFL avulsion of anterolateral tibial margin)
  • Triplane fractures (posterior metaphyseal spike visible on lateral view)

The complex asymmetrical closure pattern of the distal tibial physis—central → anteromedial → posteromedial → lateral over 18 months—creates unique transitional fracture vulnerabilities.[5]

5.4 The Calcaneus, Fifth Metatarsal, and Talar Processes

  • Anterior process of calcaneus: Avulsion from bifurcate ligament during extreme inversion/plantarflexion; primarily and optimally evaluated on the lateral view.[2]
  • Base of fifth metatarsal: Avulsion fractures (pseudo-Jones) or transverse fractures (true Jones) at the metaphyseal-diaphyseal junction—mandatory inclusion on lateral collimation.[2]
  • Posterior process of talus (Cedell fracture): Fracture of the lateral tubercle from forced dorsiflexion (avulsion) or compressive plantarflexion; completely obscured on AP and mortise views.[2]

5.5 Soft Tissue Structures

Achilles Tendon

Outlined by Kager's fat pad anteriorly. Normal anterior-to-posterior thickness: ~6 mm. A measurement > 8 mm is a highly reliable indicator of pathology (tendinosis, partial tearing, inflammatory arthropathy, or fibrotic scarring).[2]

Periarticular Fat Pads

Severe edema or capsular hemorrhage from acute ligamentous tearing manifests as soft tissue swelling anterior to the lateral malleolus, obliterating expected fat lucencies. This often serves as the clinical catalyst prompting MRI or ultrasound to detect occult ligamentous injuries.[2,22]

7. Conclusion

The evidence-based radiographic evaluation of the ankle remains a highly complex, technically demanding, yet absolutely fundamental pillar of modern emergency traumatology and orthopedic medicine. Critical differentiation between the standard AP and mortise views is defined by the intentional 15–20 degrees of internal rotation required in the mortise view to clear the lateral tibiotalar joint space.

A true lateral radiograph is bound by exacting criteria: neutral dorsiflexion, 90-degree external rotation, and strict geometric superimposition of talar domes and distal fibula over the posterior tibia. Suboptimal images introduce profound diagnostic hazards—obscuring anterior calcaneal process and fifth metatarsal fractures, degrading effusion sign validity, and distorting morphometric calculations for posterior malleolus and calcaneal fracture management.

Through the lens of evidence-based orthopedics, a pristine lateral radiograph is indispensable for identifying osteochondral lesions, evaluating posterior malleolus morphology (including Haraguchi classification), assessing pediatric physeal injuries, and evaluating Achilles tendon and periarticular soft tissue pathology. Mastery of these precise radiographic landmarks, combined with rigorous adherence to BOAST positioning criteria, is essential for preventing diagnostic delays, optimizing surgical interventions, and preserving long-term ankle joint function.
