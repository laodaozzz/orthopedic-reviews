1. Introduction: The Evolving Landscape of Orthopaedic Infectious Disease

Musculoskeletal infections (MSKI)—encompassing periprosthetic joint infection (PJI), fracture-related infection (FRI), and chronic osteomyelitis—represent some of the most devastating, biologically complex, and economically burdensome complications in modern orthopaedic trauma and reconstructive surgery. The presence of a foreign body (joint arthroplasty, internal fixation plate, or intramedullary nail) fundamentally alters the local immunological microenvironment. Bacteria adhering to these implants undergo a phenotypic shift, encasing themselves in an extracellular polymeric substance (EPS) to form a mature biofilm. This biofilm acts as a formidable biological shield, rendering embedded microorganisms highly tolerant to the host's innate immune responses and exponentially increasing resistance to systemic antimicrobial therapies.[1-5]

Historical Limitation
A landmark systematic review revealed that merely 2% of randomized controlled trials in fracture fixation utilized any standardized definition of infection when reporting surgical complications—a testament to the methodological chaos that plagued the field for decades.[1]

The period from 2020 to 2025 has catalyzed a renaissance in orthopaedic infectious disease. Driven by the global antimicrobial resistance (AMR) crisis and unprecedented interdisciplinary collaboration, the international community has fundamentally redefined paradigms of prevention, diagnosis, and therapeutic intervention. This report synthesizes evidence heavily drawn from guidelines by the European Bone and Joint Infection Society (EBJIS), the International Consensus Meeting (ICM) on MSKI, and high-impact publications including the Journal of Bone and Joint Surgery (JBJS) and the Journal of Bone and Joint Infection (JBJI).

2. Consensus Definitions: Standardization of Fracture-Related Infection (FRI)

2.1 The EBJIS/AO Consensus Diagnostic Framework

For decades, heterogenous terminology ("post-traumatic osteomyelitis," "surgical site infection," "deep incisional infection," "infected non-union") was utilized interchangeably, precluding accurate epidemiological tracking and standardized treatment protocols. An international collaborative effort—spearheaded by the EBJIS, the AO Foundation's AIGEC, the OTA, and the PRO-IMPLANT Foundation—formally adopted the unified term "Fracture-Related Infection" (FRI).[1] Level II

The pathophysiology of FRI is uniquely multifactorial: an interdependent relationship between bacterial contamination, extensive soft-tissue trauma, compromised neovascularity, and mechanical fracture instability supports canalicular invasion, intracellular infection of osteoblasts, and formation of staphylococcal abscess communities. A vicious cycle ensues wherein mechanical instability promotes ongoing soft-tissue trauma and localized osteolysis, creating a supportive microenvironment for bacterial persistence.[5]

Of these, 480 were definitively diagnosed with FRI. However, a 2024 bibliometric analysis of OTA annual meetings (2019–2024) revealed adoption of the FRI Consensus definition remained surprisingly low: only 8.5% of accepted poster abstracts strictly utilized the formal criteria, with no significant upward trend.[7] Level III

A 2024 national survey of Brazilian orthopaedic trauma centers highlighted massive variability in diagnostic methodologies. Key Implementation Insight
The success of the FRI definition relies not on further clinical validation, but on aggressive global educational outreach and the structural reorganization of trauma centers to support multidisciplinary care.

3. Diagnostic Innovations: Beyond Conventional Microbiology

Accurate, rapid pathogen identification is the cornerstone of effective MSKI management. Conventional cultures possess profound limitations when dealing with biofilm-forming bacteria, slow-growing fastidious organisms, or patients with prior antibiotic therapy—frequently resulting in Culture-Negative PJI (CN-PJI).[9]

3.1 Metagenomic Next-Generation Sequencing (mNGS)

mNGS extracts and high-throughput sequences all nucleic acids (DNA and RNA) within a clinical sample, completely bypassing the requirement for in vitro cultivation. It shifts the paradigm from phenotype-dependent, cultivation-based methodology to a rapid, genotype-dependent, nucleic-acid-based methodology.[10]

Risk Factors for Diagnostic Discordance

Rigorous analysis identified three critical drivers of discordance between culture and mNGS:[10]

  1. Prior Antibiotic Exposure: 2.137× higher odds of discordance (95% CI 1.069–4.272). Antibiotics suppress in vitro bacterial replication but fragmented nucleic acids remain detectable by mNGS.
  2. Polymicrobial Infections: 3.245× higher odds of discordance (95% CI 1.278–8.243). Dominant pathogens suppress co-infectants in culture; mNGS provides unbiased representation of the complete biofilm flora.
  3. Rare/Atypical Pathogens: 2.735× higher odds for infections caused by Mycoplasma, Candida parapsilosis, Brucella, Coxiella burnetii, and filamentous fungi. Culture media are not optimized for these organisms.
2025 ICM & EBJIS Position
mNGS is recognized as a highly powerful adjunct but strictly cautioned against as a standalone replacement for conventional culture. It should be prioritized for: culture-negative PJI, known preoperative antibiotic exposure, and suspected atypical or polymicrobial etiology. Maintaining parallel conventional cultures remains vital for phenotypic MIC data.[13]

Implementation Challenges

  • mNGS cannot differentiate nucleic acids from live pathogens vs. dead microbial debris
  • Host DNA saturation overshadows microbial reads, complicating bioinformatic analysis
  • Genotypic AMR predictions match true phenotypic resistance profiles in only 37.5% of culture-positive PJIs[12]
  • High cost, complex bioinformatic requirements, and need for ultra-sterile environments limit deployment to specialized tertiary centers

Biomarkers validated for primary PJI diagnosis lose diagnostic power entirely in the two-stage revision interval setting. 4. Therapeutic Shifts & Antimicrobial Stewardship

The overarching theme of the 2020–2025 literature has been a highly targeted move toward preservation of host biology, salvage of functional anatomy, and sharp reduction in unnecessary systemic medical toxicity.

4.1 One-Stage vs. Two-Stage Exchange Arthroplasty

For decades, two-stage exchange was the unchallenged "gold standard" for chronic PJI, favored by over 75% of North American surgeons. European centers increasingly championed single-stage exchange to reduce morbidity and cost.[21]

2025 ICM Istanbul Consensus: Single-Stage Eligibility Criteria

There is no absolute contraindication to single-stage revision, provided:[22]

  1. Microbiological Certainty: Causative organism definitively identified preoperatively with favorable susceptibility profile
  2. Host Competence: Immunocompetent, free of systemic sepsis
  3. Tissue Viability: No severe soft-tissue compromise, massive bone loss, or actively draining sinuses

4.2 Debridement, Antimicrobial Therapy, and Implant Retention (DAIR)

For acute PJIs (early postoperative or acute hematogenous), DAIR preserves the original prosthesis. The 2025 EBJIS position paper crystallized protocols:[23] Level II

Key Principles

  • Biofilm maturation is the defining factor: Chronic infections (symptoms >3–4 weeks or >90 days) are absolute biological contraindications for DAIR
  • Indication: Symptoms lasting <3 weeks, mechanically stable implant, good soft tissue viability
  • Antimicrobial duration: 12 weeks total, initiated with 1-week intensive IV induction
  • Biofilm-active agents mandated: Fluoroquinolone + rifampicin for staphylococci; targeted high-dose fluoroquinolones for Gram-negatives
  • Modular component exchange during debridement increases success from 50% to 68%—eliminates sequestered biofilm on modular interfaces

The 13% failure rate perfectly matched the original OVIVA trial, proving early oral therapy is entirely non-inferior. 4.4 Bacteriophage Therapy: A Biological Renaissance

As conventional antibiotic efficacy wanes under global MDR pressure, bacteriophage therapy has experienced a dramatic renaissance. Phages are naturally occurring, host-specific viruses that self-amplify at infection sites and enzymatically degrade mature biofilm EPS—a feat largely impossible for traditional antibiotics.[26]

Clinical Progress (2024–2025)

  • Major European initiatives: PHAGEFORCE (standardizing protocols for FRI) and PHAGEinLYON clinical program[26]
  • Customized lytic phages for refractory MDR S. aureus PJIs/FRIs have resulted in rapid wound healing, CRP normalization, and functional joint salvage in patients facing limb amputation[31]
  • Synergistic strategy: Phage-induced biofilm disruption restores susceptibility of dormant bacteria to standard antibiotics
Remaining Barriers
  • Immune neutralization limits systemic (IV) phage administration
  • Research pivoting to local delivery: bioresorbable hydrogels, lipid microparticles, PMMA cement coatings
  • Regulatory frameworks designed for static small-molecule pharmaceuticals conflict with patient-specific, rapidly evolving biological cocktails

5. Innovations in Prevention & Local Prophylaxis

Preventing initial bacterial adherence to an implant is exponentially more successful than eradicating a mature biofilm. The last five years have seen immense innovation in local antibiotic delivery systems and intraoperative irrigation protocols.

5.2 Citrate-Based Irrigation Solutions

A major breakthrough has been the introduction and validation of citrate-based irrigation solutions (e.g., XPERIENCE™). Formulated with citric acid, sodium citrate, and sodium lauryl sulfate, they operate via potent ionic chelation—sequestering essential metal ions (Ca²⁺, Mg²⁺) to physically destabilize the biofilm EPS matrix.[34]

6. Conclusion

The landscape of MSKI management from 2020 to 2025 has undergone a profound, evidence-driven transformation, fundamentally moving from empirical historical dogmas toward precision microbiology, standardized diagnostic criteria, and highly selective, individualized therapeutic interventions.

Key paradigm shifts include:

  1. Diagnostic standardization through EBJIS/AO FRI consensus criteria—validated but requiring aggressive educational outreach for global adoption
  2. Multi-omic diagnostics: mNGS rescues culture-negative scenarios; ML models resolve "inconclusive" cases within 24 hours; synovial biomarkers provide rapid rule-in capacity
  3. Surgical philosophy shift: Single-stage exchange arthroplasty validated in selected patients; DAIR optimized with modular exchange and rifampicin-based protocols
  4. Medical paradigm change: OVIVA validated oral transition after ~7 days IV, eliminating catheter-related morbidity and supporting antimicrobial stewardship
  5. Biological frontier: Bacteriophage therapy with local hydrogel delivery; citrate-based irrigation disrupting biofilm with zero cytotoxicity; advanced antibiotic carriers replacing legacy PMMA

The rapid synthesis and clinical adoption of these advancements ensure the global orthopaedic community is fundamentally better equipped to prevent, accurately diagnose, and definitively eradicate complex musculoskeletal infections in the decades to come.
