1. Introduction: The Paradigm Shift to Arthroscopic Preservation

Modern orthopaedic surgery has undergone a fundamental transformation as arthroscopic techniques have evolved from diagnostic adjuncts to sophisticated reconstructive disciplines capable of addressing complex intra-articular pathology with minimal soft tissue disruption. This report synthesises evidence across three conceptually linked domains: hip preservation surgery, advanced elbow arthroscopy, and management of popliteal cysts.

In the hip, recognition of Femoroacetabular Impingement (FAI) syndrome has shifted the approach from salvage to preservation. The labrum is no longer viewed as a disposable gasket but as a critical seal essential for fluid pressurisation and chondral protection. Similarly, the elbow — often described as the "unforgiving joint" — demands rigorous adherence to anatomical safety zones. Finally, management of popliteal (Baker's) cysts has evolved from high-recurrence open excisions to arthroscopic internal drainage that addresses the root valvular mechanism.

Core Principle

The transition from open to arthroscopic management represents a maturation where preservation of biology matches precision of mechanics.

Hip 2. Hip Arthroscopy: Precision in FAI Correction & Labral Restoration

2.1 Anatomical Landmarks & Portal Placement

The establishment of portals in the hip is unique due to the depth of the joint and the requirement for distraction. The "safe zone" is defined by the tensor fascia lata (TFL) laterally and neurovascular structures medially and posteriorly.

2.1.1 Neurovascular Anatomy & Safety Zones

Lateral Femoral Cutaneous Nerve (LFCN): The most frequently injured nerve in hip arthroscopy, with injury resulting in meralgia paresthetica. Research indicates the LFCN crosses the inguinal ligament at a mean distance of 1.87 ± 0.48 cm medial to the ASIS, and in approximately 4% of the population it may pass over or lateral to the ASIS. Level V — Anatomical Study

Surgical Pearl

All anterior portals must be placed strictly lateral to a longitudinal line dropped from the ASIS, utilising the interval between the TFL and the sartorius.

Sciatic Nerve: At risk during posterolateral portal placement, lying approximately 2.9 cm posterior to the posterior margin of the greater trochanter. External rotation narrows this distance; therefore, the posterolateral portal must be established with the leg in neutral rotation. Level V — Cadaveric Study

Superior Gluteal Nerve: Courses approximately 4.4 cm superior to the standard anterolateral portal. Aggressive proximal capsulotomy can endanger this nerve, potentially affecting the abductor musculature.

2.2 Management of Femoroacetabular Impingement (FAI)

FAI is a mechanical mismatch between the femoral head and acetabulum. Surgical management aims to reshape the joint via osteoplasty and repair soft tissue damage.

2.2.1 Capsular Management — Interportal Capsulotomy

The interportal capsulotomy connects the AL and mMAP portals, running parallel to the acetabular rim (typically 1.5–2 cm). Traction sutures (high-strength suture tapes) through superior and inferior capsule leaflets are clamped under tension to "suspend" the capsule, significantly improving the working volume. Level IV — Case Series / Technical Note

2.2.2 Acetabuloplasty (Pincer Correction)

A 5.5 mm burr resects acetabular overhang until the "crossover sign" resolves or the pincer angle is reduced below 30 degrees. The labrum is detached and reflected (or reconstructed if ossified).

⚠ Safety Pitfall

Over-resection leads to iatrogenic dysplasia and instability. The lateral centre-edge angle (LCEA) must be monitored — an LCEA <20° is a contraindication for rim trimming. Level IV

2.2.3 Femoroplasty (Cam Correction)

Traction is released and the hip flexed to 45°. The cam lesion is identified (chondral delamination / "wave sign") and contoured with a burr. Resection extends medially to the medial synovial fold and laterally to the retinacular vessels. Dynamic examination confirms no residual impingement in flexion and internal rotation.

⚠ Vascular Safety

The retinacular vessels (supplying the femoral head) penetrate the posterolateral neck. Resection must not extend posterior to the 12 o'clock position on the neck to avoid avascular necrosis. Level IV

2.3 Labral Repair Techniques

The labrum functions as a suction seal, maintaining negative intra-articular pressure for load distribution and cartilage nutrition. Loss of this seal is a precursor to osteoarthritis.

Key Evidence

Base refixation is the gold standard. Systematic reviews confirm labral repair yields significantly better Patient-Reported Outcomes and lower conversion to THA (Hazard Ratio 0.24 favouring repair over debridement). Level II — Systematic Review

2.3.2 Step-by-Step Base Refixation Technique

  1. Preparation: Prepare the acetabular rim to a bleeding bone surface using a burr to stimulate a healing response.
  2. Anchor Placement: Use 1.4–2.3 mm soft/suture anchors placed 1–2 mm from the articular edge. Drill must be directed away from articular cartilage. The DALA portal helps achieve the correct angle for anterior anchors.
  3. Suture Passing: A suture passer pierces the labrum at the chondrolabral junction (capsular → articular side), creating a vertical loop that passes through the labrum rather than wrapping around it.
  4. Knot Tying / Tensioning: Tension is applied to invert the labrum slightly, restoring contact with the femoral head. Use "post-less" traction or intermittent traction to verify the seal without over-tensioning.

Key technical challenges: A survey of high-volume experts identified labral treatment and femoroplasty as the most demanding steps determining surgical success. Level V — Expert Survey

Outcomes: The Modified Harris Hip Score (mHHS) typically improves by 20–30 points post-operatively. Overall major complication rate is 0.45%–1.4%. The most common complication is transient neuropraxia (pudendal or LFCN) in up to 7.9% of cases.

Traction Safety

Traction time should be kept under 2 hours and traction force should not exceed 50 lbs (22.7 kg). Intermittent traction and well-padded perineal posts are essential. Level IV

Elbow 3. Elbow Arthroscopy: Navigating the "Unforgiving Joint"

The elbow joint capsule holds only 15–25 mL, and major neurovascular structures lie millimetres from the capsule. Success depends entirely on understanding spatial relationships and how joint position alters them.

3.1.2 Standardised Portal Sequence (Proximal-to-Distal)

  1. Proximal Anteromedial (PAM): 2 cm proximal to medial epicondyle, just anterior to the intermuscular septum. Starting portal; provides view of the lateral compartment.
  2. Proximal Anterolateral (PAL): 1–2 cm proximal to lateral epicondyle, directly anterior to the humerus. Established after distension and direct visualisation from PAM. Elbow must be in 90° flexion and pronation.
  3. Posterior Portals: Posterolateral (2–3 cm proximal to olecranon, lateral to triceps) and Direct Posterior (transtricipital, midline, 3 cm proximal to olecranon).

3.2 Management of the Stiff Elbow — Arthroscopic Arthroplasty

Indicated for post-traumatic or degenerative stiffness failing conservative care. The procedure addresses both anterior and posterior columns systematically.

Step 1: Anterior Debridement

  • View from PAM, work from PAL.
  • Radiofrequency wand or shaver strips the anterior capsule from the humerus (medial to lateral).
  • Critical safety step: A retractor (switching stick) lifts the anterior muscle mass and neurovascular bundle away. Level IV
  • Burr deepens the coronoid fossa and resects coronoid osteophytes to restore flexion.

Step 2: Posterior Debridement

  • View from posterolateral, work from direct posterior.
  • Excise scar tissue in the olecranon fossa.
  • Olecranon tip resection to prevent impingement in extension. Level IV
Outcome Data

Arthroscopic release achieves ROM improvements comparable to open release (~92% satisfactory results) but with lower pain scores and fewer wound complications. Level II — Systematic Review

3.3 Arthroscopic Treatment for Lateral Epicondylitis

Indicated for recalcitrant lateral epicondylitis failing 6–12 months of non-operative therapy. Concomitant intra-articular pathology occurs in up to 69% of cases, making the arthroscopic approach advantageous. Level IV

ECRB Release — Step-by-Step

  1. Diagnostic arthroscopy: Inspect radiocapitellar joint for chondral defects or plica.
  2. Lateral capsulotomy: RF probe/shaver via PAL portal.
  3. ECRB resection: Degenerative tissue (grey, friable) is resected until healthy muscular fibres of the EDC are visualised behind the ECRB. Resection extends proximally to the supracondylar ridge and distally to the mid-point of the radiocapitellar joint.
  4. Decortication (optional): Burr decortication of the lateral epicondyle to stimulate healing.
Evidence

Meta-analyses show arthroscopic release offers better functional outcomes (DASH scores) and lower complication rates (91–96% success) compared to open release (82% success). Level I — Meta-Analysis

3.4 Learning Curve

Elbow arthroscopy has a flatter learning curve than the hip but higher absolute risks. Proficiency generally requires 30–50 cases. The overall complication rate is approximately 11%, with nerve injury (mostly ulnar) accounting for 3.4%. Level II — Systematic Review

Knee 4. Arthroscopic Popliteal (Baker's) Cyst Management

A popliteal cyst is a distension of the gastrocnemius-semimembranosus bursa driven by a valvular mechanism — a transverse capsular fold that allows fluid to enter the cyst from the knee but prevents return. Traditional open excision has a recurrence rate of up to 63% because it fails to address this valve and the underlying intra-articular pathology. Level IV

4.1 Anatomical Landmarks & Safety

The valve is located between the medial head of the gastrocnemius and the semimembranosus tendon. The popliteal neurovascular bundle lies lateral to the septum between the gastrocnemius heads. The safe zone for posteromedial portals is strictly medial to the medial gastrocnemius head.

4.2 Standardised Surgical Steps

  1. Address intra-articular causes: Treat any meniscal tears or chondral lesions causing effusion. Medial meniscal tears are present in ~48% of cases.
  2. Identify the valve: The arthroscope advances to the posteromedial compartment. Pearl: Injecting methylene blue percutaneously into the cyst pre-operatively highlights the leak point. The valve appears as a transverse synovial fold superior to the medial gastrocnemius head.
  3. Valve resection (internal drainage): Shaver or basket punch resects the valvular fold, enlarging the communication to at least 8–10 mm. This converts one-way flow to a bidirectional channel.
  4. Cystectomy ("Inside-Out" technique): The shaver is advanced through the enlarged valve opening into the cyst cavity. Transillumination guides safe posterior portal creation. The inner lining is debrided.
Outcomes

Arthroscopic internal drainage combined with cystectomy achieves a recurrence rate of approximately 0%–2.1%, vastly superior to open excision. Patients report significant improvements in Rauschning & Lindgren scores. Level II — Systematic Review
