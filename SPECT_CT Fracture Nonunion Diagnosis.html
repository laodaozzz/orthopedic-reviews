SPECT/CT Diagnostic Criteria for Fracture Nonunion

1. Introduction: The Clinical Imperative

Fracture nonunion remains one of the most persistent challenges in orthopedic surgery. Nonunion rates for long bone fractures are estimated between 5% and 10% of the approximately 5.6 million fractures occurring annually in the United States. Level II The U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA) standardizes nonunion as a fractured bone that has failed to completely heal within nine months of injury and has not shown progression towards healing for three consecutive months. Level V

This temporal definition, however, fails to capture the underlying biological state of the fracture environment—the primary determinant of therapeutic success. The fundamental clinical dilemma lies in distinguishing between differing biological subtypes of nonunion:

  • Hypertrophic Nonunions: Abundant callus and vascularity; result from inadequate mechanical stability. Heal with improved fixation.
  • Atrophic Nonunions: Biological quiescence and poor vascularity; require biological augmentation (bone grafting).
  • Oligotrophic Nonunions: Absent radiographic callus but variable biological potential—an ambiguous category that SPECT/CT helps resolve.
  • Septic Pseudarthrosis: Superimposed infection fundamentally alters the treatment algorithm, necessitating staged debridement and antibiotic therapy.
Level II Level III
The Imaging Gap
Standard imaging (radiography, CT) excels at morphological assessment—cortical bridging, implant position, fracture gaps—but cannot quantify metabolic activity. MRI is frequently compromised by susceptibility artifacts from metallic hardware. SPECT/CT bridges this gap by fusing high-sensitivity metabolic data with high-resolution anatomical CT data.

2. Pathophysiological Basis of SPECT/CT Imaging

Bone scintigraphy using Technetium-99m labeled diphosphonates (Tc-99m MDP or Tc-99m DPD) relies on chemisorption—the radiotracer adsorbs onto hydroxyapatite crystals in proportion to two key factors: local blood flow and osteoblastic activity. Level II

2.1 The Metabolic Signature of Healing

In normal fracture healing, the reparative phase is characterized by intense osteoblastic activity and soft callus formation, corresponding to increased radiotracer uptake. Normalization typically occurs within 6 to 12 months. In nonunion, this trajectory is disrupted:

  • Hypertrophic Nonunion: Biological drive persists. Fracture ends are hypervascular with active osteoblasts attempting to bridge the gap. SPECT/CT shows intense, expansive uptake.
  • Atrophic Nonunion: Biological drive has failed. SPECT/CT shows a "cold" defect or significantly reduced uptake.
  • Necrotic Fragments: Bone segments that have lost blood supply appear as photopenic (cold) voids, often surrounded by a rim of hyperactive living bone (involucrum).
Level III

2.2 Advantage of Hybrid Imaging (SPECT/CT vs. Planar)

Traditional planar scintigraphy produces 2D summation, causing the "superposition effect" where overlying soft tissue activity obscures the fracture gap. SPECT/CT overcomes this through:

  1. Attenuation Correction: Correcting for photon loss through deep tissue, enabling true quantification.
  2. Anatomical Localization: Assigning "hot" or "cold" voxels to specific morphological structures (fracture gap, screw tip, cortical fragment).
Level II

3. Quantitative Diagnostic Criteria: Aseptic Nonunion

The advent of quantitative SPECT (Q-SPECT) enables calculation of absolute metrics such as SUVmax (Maximum Standardized Uptake Value), similar to PET quantification. This shift from subjective to objective assessment is critical for reproducible diagnostic criteria. Level II

3.1 Differentiating Hypertrophic vs. Non-Hypertrophic Nonunion

Quantitative analysis of Tc-99m MDP uptake demonstrates statistically significant differences in metabolic activity between these subtypes. The "Control Ratio" is defined as the uptake value in the high-uptake area of the nonunion divided by the uptake in normal control bone.

Table 1. Quantitative SPECT Parameters for Nonunion Subtype Differentiation (Source: Ref. 9)
Quantitative Parameter Hypertrophic Nonunion (Mean ± SD) Non-Hypertrophic Nonunion (Mean ± SD) p-value
SUVmax Control Ratio 12.13 ± 4.95 7.82 ± 4.76 0.065
SUVpeak Control Ratio 11.65 ± 4.58 7.56 ± 4.51 0.065
SUVmean Control Ratio 11.94 ± 5.03 7.59 ± 5.18 0.076
Clinical Interpretation
Hypertrophic nonunions exhibit uptake approximately 12× higher than normal bone. A Target-to-Background Ratio (TBR) exceeding 10–11 strongly suggests a hypertrophic process—biological healing machinery is active but lacks mechanical stability. Ratios below this threshold, particularly approaching 4–5, suggest biological failure requiring osteogenic augmentation.
Level II

3.2 Reclassification of Radiographic "Oligotrophic" Nonunions

Quantitative SPECT often reclassifies patients. A fracture appearing "oligotrophic" (minimal callus) on X-ray may demonstrate high quantitative uptake (high SUVmax), reclassifying it as biologically active. This prevents unnecessary harvesting of bone graft in patients who simply need stiffer fixation. Level III

3.3 Viability Assessment: "Vital" vs. "Avital"

  • Vital Nonunions: Measurable uptake (increased SUV) at fracture ends, confirming perfusion and cellular activity.
  • Avital (Atrophic) Nonunions: "Cold defect" or photopenic gap with no tracer accumulation, indicating cessation of osteoblastic activity.
Diagnostic Performance for Viability (Ref. 10)
SPECT/CT demonstrated specificity of 100% for diagnosing avital nonunions. If the scan shows uptake, the bone is viable. Sensitivity was lower (50%), as some radiologically atrophic nonunions retain subtle biological activity that SPECT detects as "vital."
Level III

4. Morphological Uptake Patterns: The Niikura Classification

While quantitative numbers provide objective data, the spatial distribution of tracer uptake is equally diagnostic. The Niikura et al. classification resolves the ambiguity of "oligotrophic" nonunions by categorizing uptake into distinct types. Level III

INTENSE UNIFORM UPTAKE

Type 1: Intense Uniform

No photon-deficient (cold) area within callus or fracture line. Robust biological activity and vascularity.

Prevalence: 56% of radiographic "oligotrophic" nonunions

Management: Secure fixation alone; bone grafting unnecessary

HOT ── COLD ── HOT

Type 2A: Cold Cleft

"Hot-cold-hot" sandwich pattern: photon-deficient cleft between intense uptake at bone ends. Biological gap present.

Management: Biological augmentation (bone graft) + fixation

PATCHY COLD AREAS

Type 2B: Cold Area (No Cleft)

Photon-deficient area without distinct cleft (e.g., necrotic comminuted fragment). Barrier to healing present.

Management: Debridement + bone grafting + fixation

ASYMMETRIC PATCHY

Type 3: Uneven Uptake

Asymmetric or patchy uptake without a distinct cold area. Intermediate state in comminuted fractures.

Management: Tailored; often stability-focused unless necrotic fragment identified

Clinical Impact
By identifying that over half of "oligotrophic" patients have Type 1 patterns, SPECT/CT spares a significant proportion of patients from the morbidity of iliac crest bone harvesting. Level III

5. Detection of Necrotic Fragments (Sequestra)

One of SPECT/CT's specific strengths is identifying necrotic bone fragments. On standard CT, a necrotic fragment looks identical to a viable cortical fragment—both are dense and calcified. SPECT/CT differentiates them by metabolic activity. Level III

The jump in sensitivity from 67% to 100% is attributable to the CT component's ability to spatially isolate sequestered bone fragments. The high NPV (100%) means that if SPECT/CT does not show a cold spot, surgeons can be extremely confident no significant necrotic sequestrum is present.

6. Diagnostic Criteria: Infected Nonunion (Septic Pseudarthrosis)

Distinguishing aseptic inflammation from septic nonunion is among the most difficult tasks in orthopedics. Both present with pain, elevated inflammatory markers, and increased uptake on bone scans. Level II

6.1 Gold Standard: WBC Scintigraphy + SPECT/CT

The most accurate method uses radiolabeled leukocytes combined with Technetium-99m Sulfur Colloid marrow imaging. Diagnostic criteria rely on comparing WBC distribution with Sulfur Colloid (marrow) distribution:

  • Concordant Pattern (No Infection): WBC uptake matches marrow uptake—leukocytes accumulate in physiological red marrow. Negative for infection.
  • Discordant Pattern (Infection): Focal WBC uptake without corresponding marrow uptake—"mismatch" indicates active infection via chemotaxis. SPECT/CT confirms location (bone/implant interface vs. soft tissue).

Quantitative Cut-off for Infection
An LBR of approximately ≥ 9.5 on quantitative bone scan is highly specific (88.9%) for infection. Aseptic nonunions generally show elevated LBR (mean ~6.8) due to remodeling, but rarely reach the metabolic intensity of frank osteomyelitis (mean ~14.5).

7. Comparative Efficacy: SPECT/CT vs. Standard Modalities

7.1 SPECT/CT vs. Planar Scintigraphy

In complex anatomical regions (spine, pelvis, hindfoot) or comminuted fractures, planar images are often equivocal. SPECT/CT significantly outperforms planar imaging (AUC 0.977 vs. 0.811 for necrotic fragments). For solitary spinal lesions, SPECT/CT increased sensitivity from 6.1% (planar) to 78.8%, eliminating false-negative results. Level II

7.2 SPECT/CT vs. CT Alone

CT provides excellent cortical definition but cannot determine the age or activity of a fracture line. A fracture line may persist on CT for years even with stable fibrous union. SPECT/CT adds the metabolic dimension: if the gap is "cold" → atrophic; if "hot" (Type 1) → active. Level III

7.3 SPECT/CT vs. MRI

MRI is standard for soft tissue but severely hampered by metal artifacts from fixation devices. In a comparative study of non-specific wrist pain (often scaphoid nonunion):

  • MRI: Sensitivity 86%, Specificity 20%
  • SPECT/CT: Sensitivity 71%, Specificity 100%

MRI produces high false-positive rates due to postoperative edema and metallic artifacts. SPECT/CT is highly specific—a positive finding is almost certainly pathological. Level II

8. Clinical Management Algorithm

SPECT/CT criteria directly populate the "Diamond Concept" framework, which posits that healing requires: (1) Osteogenic cells, (2) Osteoconductive scaffolds, (3) Growth factors, (4) Mechanical stability, and (5) Vascularity. SPECT/CT primarily assesses Vascularity and Metabolic Activity. Level III

Step 1: Clinical Presentation

Persistent pain, radiographic gap > 9 months, no healing progression for 3 consecutive months

Step 2: Perform Tc-99m MDP SPECT/CT

Quantitative assessment of uptake; apply Niikura Classification

✅ Type 1: Intense Uniform Uptake

Diagnosis: Viable, biologically active (Hypertrophic / Active Oligotrophic)
Implication: Biology adequate; mechanics failing
→ Action: Revision Fixation (Stability)
Bone graft not required

⚠️ Type 2: Cold Cleft / Atrophic

Diagnosis: Avital, biologically inactive or necrotic interposition
Implication: Biology failing; stability alone insufficient
→ Action: Debridement + Bone Graft (Biology) + Fixation

Step 3: Assess for Sequestrum

Focal photopenia (cold spot) corresponding to a bone fragment → Sequestrectomy (removal of dead bone)

Step 4: Assess for Infection

LBR > 9.44? Discordant WBC uptake? → If positive, treat as Septic Nonunion: Two-stage revision (Antibiotic Spacer → Reconstruction)

9. Limitations and Technical Considerations

  • Radiation Dose: Both radiopharmaceutical and CT components contribute radiation burden—must be weighed against diagnostic benefit, especially in young patients. Level III
  • Resolution Limits: SPECT resolution (~4–5 mm) remains inferior to PET. Small necrotic fragments (< 5 mm) may be missed due to partial volume effects. Level II
  • The "Remodeling" Confounder: In the first 6–12 months post-fracture, physiological remodeling is naturally high. Differentiating "normal high" from "pathological high" uptake requires quantitative baselines and clinical correlation. Level II
  • Metal Artifacts: While SPECT is immune to metal artifacts, the CT component is not. Streak artifacts from heavy implants can obscure the morphological assessment, although metabolic data usually remains robust. Level III
