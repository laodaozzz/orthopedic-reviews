ASCR Surgical Guide

Arthroscopic Superior Capsular Reconstruction — Evidence-Based Review

I. Introduction & Biomechanical Rationale

The management of massive, irreparable rotator cuff tears (IRCTs) represents one of the most significant challenges in modern shoulder surgery. These injuries—characterized by substantial tendon retraction, advanced muscle atrophy, and fatty infiltration—are often not amenable to a direct, tension-free anatomic repair to the humeral footprint.Level IV

Historically, treatment options ranged from nonoperative management to debridement, partial repair, tendon transfers, and reverse shoulder arthroplasty (RSA), many yielding mixed results. High failure rates of traditional repair attempts can exceed 60% for large and massive tears, underscoring the biomechanical complexity of this pathology.Level IV

Superior Humeral Escape

A massive tear of the posterosuperior rotator cuff disrupts the "force couple" between the rotator cuff and deltoid. The supraspinatus tendon and superior capsule normally act as a "suspension bridge" resisting superior shear forces. When lost, the unopposed deltoid pull causes progressive superior humeral escape, leading to:

  • Painful subacromial impingement
  • Altered glenohumeral kinematics
  • Pseudoparalysis (inability to actively elevate >90°)
  • Eventual cuff tear arthropathy

The Superior Capsule as a Critical Static Stabilizer

Cadaveric studies demonstrated that creating a defect in the superior capsule alone—even with an intact rotator cuff—significantly increases glenohumeral translation in all directions and markedly elevates subacromial contact pressure.Level III A patch graft sutured only to tendon edges fails to restore the critical medial attachment to the glenoid neck, leading to persistent instability and mechanical failure.

Key Conceptual Shift

The transition from viewing an IRCT as a "tendon problem" to understanding it as a profound "stability problem" is the foundational premise of ASCR.

Biomechanical Goal of ASCR

Pioneered by Dr. Teruhisa Mihata, ASCR is designed to restore the passive superior static restraint by securing a durable tissue graft from the superior glenoid neck to the greater tuberosity.Level IV The reconstructed capsule acts as a passive "checkrein," re-centering the humeral head and allowing the deltoid and remaining intact muscles to function on a mechanically sound joint.

This principle dictates stringent patient selection: a robust, fully functional deltoid and an intact, repairable subscapularis are absolute prerequisites.

II. Patient Selection: Indications & Contraindications

The ideal candidate is a relatively young (<65–70 years), active individual with a symptomatic, massive, irreparable posterosuperior tear seeking a joint-preserving option to delay RSA.Level IV

Key Anatomical & Functional Criteria

  • Minimal Glenohumeral Arthritis: Hamada grade 1–2 (some surgeons consider select grade 3).Level IV
  • Intact Deltoid Function: Fully functional deltoid with intact axillary nerve—non-negotiable.Level IV
  • Intact or Repairable Subscapularis: Critical anterior force couple. Irreparable subscapularis = absolute contraindication.Level IV
  • Failed Conservative Management: Adequate physical therapy and injections have been attempted.

III. Preoperative Assessment & Surgical Planning

Clinical Examination

Comprehensive history and physical examination focusing on active/passive ROM, pseudoparalysis signs, rotator cuff strength, and specific subscapularis tests (lift-off, belly-press).Level IV

Imaging Workup

  • Radiographs: True AP (Grashey), scapular-Y, axillary views—evaluate Hamada grade, acromiohumeral distance (AHD), and bone defects.Level IV
  • MRI: Cornerstone for soft tissue assessment—tear pattern (Patte classification), muscle atrophy, and fatty infiltration (Goutallier classification).Level IV

Advanced 3D-CT Planning

Two-dimensional imaging can be unreliable for measuring glenoid version and inclination due to out-of-plane errors.Level III 3D virtual planning represents the new gold standard, enabling precise calculation of glenoid morphology and preoperative mapping of optimal glenoid anchor trajectories. This transforms the medial fixation step from "freehand" to meticulously planned, improving accuracy and reproducibility.

Clinical Pearl

3D-CT planning software used for RSA baseplate positioning can be repurposed to plan superior glenoid anchor placement in ASCR.

IV. Operative Technique: Step-by-Step

Positioning & Setup

General anesthesia + interscalene block. Beach chair or lateral decubitus per surgeon preference. Arm in neutral rotation, 20–30° abduction.Level IV

Portal Strategy

  • Posterior portal: Primary viewing for glenohumeral arthroscopy
  • Anterior portal: Rotator interval, instrumentation
  • Lateral portal: 3–4 cm distal to acromion; large-bore (10 mm) flexible cannula (e.g., PassPort) for suture management and graft passage—critical technical pearlLevel IV
  • Neviaser portal: Posterosuperior glenoid anchor trajectory
  • Accessory portals: Anterosuperior / superolateral as needed

Diagnostic Arthroscopy & Preparation

Confirm irreparability after mobilization. Assess cartilage, biceps, labrum. Address subscapularis tears and biceps pathology. Complete subacromial bursectomy.Level IV

Bone Bed Preparation

  • Glenoid: Decorticate superior glenoid neck from coracoid base anteriorly to posterosuperior glenoid—just medial to the superior labrum (native capsule attachment).
  • Humeral: Prepare entire RCT footprint on greater tuberosity to bleeding cancellous surface.

Anchor Placement

  • Glenoid: 2–3 suture anchors (3.0 mm knotless or knotted) at 10 o'clock and 2 o'clock (right shoulder); optional 12 o'clock anchor.Level IV
  • Humeral medial row: Two anchors (e.g., 4.75 mm) with high-strength suture tape (FiberTape) adjacent to articular margin.

Graft Sizing & Preparation

Measure inter-anchor distances arthroscopically. Transfer to graft on back table. Oversize by 5 mm medially/anteriorly/posteriorly and 10 mm laterally—typically resulting in a trapezoidal shape. Create suture passage holes with 1.5 mm biopsy punch.Level IV

Graft Passage & Medial Fixation

Retrieve all suture limbs through lateral cannula; organize by quadrant. Fold graft like a cigar, introduce through lateral cannula. Use glenoid eyelets as pulleys to shuttle medial edge to glenoid neck. Secure medial sutures (lock knotless or tie arthroscopic knots).Level IV

Lateral Fixation: Transosseous-Equivalent SpeedBridge

Tension suture tapes from medial row humeral anchors; fix laterally with two additional knotless anchors. Creates a wide, compressive interface maximizing healing surface area.Level IV

Side-to-Side Integration

Secure posterior graft margin to anterior infraspinatus edge; anterior margin to remnant supraspinatus or rotator interval. This margin convergence closes defects, restores the rotator cable, and seals the construct.Level IV

Technical Pitfall

Failure to achieve secure medial fixation to the glenoid neck is the most common technical error leading to graft failure—3D preoperative planning can mitigate this risk.

A systematic review found neither graft demonstrated clear superiority overall, with high variability in complication and failure rates across studies. The choice should be individualized based on patient anatomy, defect size, and surgeon experience.Level II

VI. Clinical Outcomes, Complications & Failure Management

Patient-Reported Outcomes

In appropriately selected patients, ASCR consistently produces clinically meaningful improvements:Level II

  • Pain (VAS): Pre-op ~4–6/10 → Post-op ~1–2/10
  • Function (ASES): 30–40-point improvement (significant disability → high function)
  • Active Forward Flexion: +30–40° average gain
  • Acromiohumeral Distance (AHD): +2–3 mm increase on radiographs

Graft Healing & Failure

Reported graft failure rates vary widely (4.5%–75%),Level II influenced by graft type, technique, and follow-up modality. One study reported a 67.8% graft healing rate on MRI at 1 year.Level IV

Important Clinical Finding

Radiographic graft integrity does not always correlate with clinical outcome. Patients with MRI-confirmed re-tears often maintain excellent pain relief and function—suggesting the graft may function as a "biologic spacer" inducing scarring that provides passive restraint even without true ligamentization.Level III

Complications

  • Postoperative stiffness — managed with aggressive PT; may require MUA or capsular release
  • Suture anchor pullout — particularly in osteoporotic bone; highlights need for preoperative bone quality assessment
  • Persistent pain — may indicate progression of arthritis, AC joint pathology, or other undiagnosed issues
  • Infection — low rate (<1%), but devastating if it occurs

Salvage: Reverse Shoulder Arthroplasty

ASCR does not "burn bridges"—failed ASCR remains a candidate for RSA without compromising bone stock or soft tissues. This makes it an attractive first-line option in younger patients.Level IV

IX. Conclusion

Arthroscopic Superior Capsular Reconstruction has emerged as a transformative, joint-preserving procedure for massive irreparable rotator cuff tears. Rooted in a sophisticated understanding of shoulder biomechanics, ASCR restores the superior static restraint, recenters the humeral head, and enables the deltoid and remaining muscles to function efficiently.

Success depends on: (1) meticulous patient selection (young, active, minimal arthritis, intact deltoid and subscapularis); (2) precise surgical execution with robust medial and lateral fixation; (3) advanced preoperative planning (3D-CT); and (4) a prolonged, disciplined rehabilitation protocol.

While graft choice debates continue and long-term data mature, the existing evidence supports ASCR as a valuable intervention that provides outcomes comparable or superior to alternatives, with the profound advantage of preserving the native joint. ASCR has rightfully earned its place as a cornerstone in the modern treatment algorithm for irreparable rotator cuff tears.
