1. The Diagnostic Imperative in the Aging Spine

The radiological presentation of vertebral compression fractures (VCFs) in the elderly presents a formidable diagnostic challenge: distinguishing benign osteoporotic fractures (OVCF), malignant infiltration (multiple myeloma, MM), and spinal infection (spondylodiscitis). Misdiagnosis carries catastrophic therapeutic consequences.

Evidence Level: Not specified in source

The diagnostic stakes are exceptionally high because the therapeutic algorithms for these three conditions are divergent and often contradictory.1

⚠ Critical Risk of Misdiagnosis
  • Injecting bone cement (PVP) into an infected vertebra can seed systemic sepsis or necessitate catastrophic revision surgery.3
  • Failing to identify multiple myeloma in a presumed osteoporotic patient delays cytoreductive therapy, allowing unchecked skeletal-related events (SREs).1

This report provides an exhaustive analysis of the radiological biomarkers differentiating these entities using advanced CT and MRI techniques (including DWI), delineates evidence-based indications for vertebral augmentation in myeloma, and establishes management protocols for inadvertent PVP in the setting of active spinal infection.

2. Pathophysiologic Basis of Imaging Findings

2.1 Mechanisms of Vertebral Collapse

Osteoporosis

Vertebral collapse in osteoporosis is primarily a mechanical failure of trabecular bone microarchitecture. Loss of horizontal trabeculae reduces load-bearing capacity, leading to microfractures that coalesce into clinical collapse. This process is typically acute or subacute, followed by a reparative inflammatory response with edema and, in cases of non-union, ischemic necrosis.5

Level III–IV Evidence

Multiple Myeloma

MM involves neoplastic proliferation of plasma cells within bone marrow. These cells secrete osteoclast-activating factors (e.g., RANKL), causing uncoupled bone remodeling where osteolysis outpaces osteoblastogenesis. This results in "punched-out" lytic lesions or diffuse marrow replacement, often without reactive sclerosis.7

Level III–IV Evidence

Spinal Infection

Pyogenic organisms (most commonly Staphylococcus aureus) or granulomatous pathogens (e.g., Mycobacterium tuberculosis) incite a vigorous inflammatory response. Infection typically begins in subchondral arterioles—end-artery loops supplying the vertebral endplate—causing septic thrombosis, bone infarction, and subsequent spread into the intervertebral disc.9

Level III–IV Evidence

2.2 The Bio-Barrier of the Intervertebral Disc

🔬 Central Diagnostic Tenet The intervertebral disc is avascular in adults. In spondylodiscitis, proteolytic enzymes from bacteria and leukocytes rapidly degrade the cartilaginous endplate, breaching the disc space. Conversely, the annulus fibrosus and nucleus pulposus serve as a robust barrier to neoplastic spread—malignant tumors respect disc boundaries until very advanced disease. The disc is the primary pivot point of differential diagnosis.9

3. Radiological Differential Diagnosis: Structural & Morphological Analysis

3.1 Intervertebral Disc Integrity and Signal

The status of the intervertebral disc is the most reliable discriminator between infection and other etiologies.

Level II–III Evidence
  • Spondylodiscitis: T2 hyperintensity within the disc ("hot disc" sign). T1 indistinct disc–vertebra margin. Intense, heterogeneous enhancement post-gadolinium.9
  • Multiple Myeloma & OVCF: Disc space generally preserved. No pathological disc enhancement. The "preservation of the disc" sign is a strong negative predictor for infection.9

3.2 Endplate Architecture and Bone Destruction Patterns

High-resolution Multidetector CT (MDCT) is superior to MRI for assessing trabecular patterns and cortical integrity.14

Level II Evidence
  • Osteoporotic Fractures: Fracture line (band of compaction), central depression (codfish vertebrae) or anterior wedging. Generalized osteopenia without focal lysis. MRI shows a linear band of T1/T2 hypointensity parallel to the endplate (impaction of trabeculae).15
  • Multiple Myeloma: Discrete "punched-out" defects with no sclerotic rim, or "moth-eaten" permeative pattern. Endplates remain sharp and intact until extensively undermined. In diffuse infiltration, CT may mimic osteoporosis—MRI is essential.4
  • Spinal Infection: "Swiss-cheese-like" fragmentation in pyogenic osteomyelitis. Early endplate erosion with loss of sharp cortical definition. In TB: anterior destruction → kyphosis (Gibbus deformity) with subligamentous spread.9

3.3 Posterior Element Involvement

Level III Evidence
  • Malignancy: The "pedicle sign" / "winking owl sign" is highly suggestive. Pedicles contain red marrow—fertile soil for hematogenous seeding. MM frequently involves pedicles with expansile lesions.2
  • Osteoporosis: Posterior elements typically spared. Involvement should raise immediate suspicion for a non-osteoporotic etiology.16
  • Infection: Pyogenic infection centers on the vertebral body and disc. TB can involve posterior elements in atypical presentations but this is less common than in malignancy.9

3.4 Paravertebral Soft Tissue Mass

Level II–III Evidence
  • Osteoporotic Fractures: Mild, circumferential swelling (~5.62 mm mean thickness), resolving within weeks.17
  • Spinal Infection: Abscess formation. Pyogenic: psoas tracking, T2 hyperintense fluid, thick peripherally enhancing rims. TB: large "cold abscesses" with possible calcification.9
  • Multiple Myeloma: Solid, enhancing paraspinal masses (plasmacytoma). No fluid-like necrosis unless very large. Lack of inflammatory stranding.12

4. Advanced MRI Characteristics: Marrow Signal & Functional Imaging

4.1 Marrow Signal Intensity and Patterns

Level II–III Evidence

T1-Weighted Imaging

T1 is the most sensitive sequence for marrow pathology. Normal adult marrow is fatty and hyperintense. In MM, plasma cell infiltration replaces fat → hypointensity (focal, diffuse, or "salt-and-pepper").

⚠ Diagnostic Pitfall Approximately 38% of myeloma fractures appear "benign" on MRI because the infiltration is interstitial, preserving enough adipocytes to maintain T1 signal. DWI and chemical shift imaging are essential adjuncts.5

STIR / Fat-Sat T2

Edema (water) and tumor (cellularity/water) both appear hyperintense. Sensitive but nonspecific. Absence of STIR high signal in a chronic collapsed vertebra suggests healed OVCF; persistent high signal may indicate active tumor or non-union.15

4.2 Chemical Shift Imaging (In-Phase / Out-of-Phase)

Level II–III Evidence

Exploits different resonance frequencies of fat and water protons.

  • Benign fractures: Edematous marrow with preserved fatty architecture → signal drop on out-of-phase images (fat and water protons cancel each other).
  • Malignant infiltration: Fat completely replaced by tumor → no signal drop on out-of-phase images.

This sequence is particularly valuable when T1 findings are equivocal.22

4.3 Intravertebral Vacuum Cleft (IVC) and Fluid Sign

Level II Evidence

Both signs are strongly associated with benign osteoporotic collapse (ischemic vertebral osteonecrosis / Kümmell's disease).

  • Intravertebral Vacuum Cleft: Gas collection (nitrogen) within a non-healing fracture cleft. Specificity up to 99% for benign osteonecrosis. Rare in malignancy (6.4% in myeloma).23
  • Fluid Sign: T2/STIR hyperintense fluid within the necrotic cavity. Present in 40% of osteoporotic fractures vs. only 6% of neoplastic fractures (cohort of 87 patients).25

4.4 Diffusion-Weighted Imaging (DWI) and ADC Mapping

Level II Evidence (meta-analyses, prospective studies)

DWI provides quantitative data on cellularity and water mobility—cornerstone of functional spinal imaging.

Biophysical Basis

  • Benign Fractures (Edema): Increased extracellular space → free diffusion → intermediate–high DWI signal, high ADC values.27
  • Malignancy (Hypercellularity): Densely packed cells → restricted diffusion → high DWI signal, low ADC values.27
  • Infection (Pus): Highly viscous and cellular → marked diffusion restriction → low ADC (similar to brain abscesses). Chronic granulomatous tissue may show variable ADC.30

5. Complementary Role of CT and Nuclear Medicine

5.1 Multidetector CT (MDCT)

Level II Evidence

MDCT is indispensable for evaluating fracture risk and cortical stability, detecting subtle cortical erosions MRI may miss. In myeloma, MDCT detected 15 additional fractures and 6 vertebrae at risk compared to plain radiography.14

5.2 FDG-PET/CT

Level II–III Evidence
  • Infection: Intensely hypermetabolic (high glucose utilization by neutrophils/macrophages). Highly sensitive for skip lesions and distant septic foci.19
  • Myeloma: Active lesions are FDG-avid. Superior to bone scintigraphy (Tc-99m) for distinguishing active vs. inactive disease and identifying extramedullary disease. Bone scans yield frequent false negatives ("cold" lesions) because myeloma is primarily osteolytic with suppressed osteoblast function.7

6. Multiple Myeloma: Interventional Management (PVP/BKP)

Vertebral compression fractures affect 70–80% of patients with multiple myeloma, representing a major cause of intractable pain and disability.4 Percutaneous Vertebroplasty (PVP) and Balloon Kyphoplasty (BKP) are established palliative interventions.

Level I–II Evidence (IMWG Consensus)

6.1 Indications for Vertebral Augmentation (IMWG Consensus)

Level I — Consensus Statement

Absolute Indications

  1. Refractory Pain: Significant, persistent pain localized to a fractured vertebra, unresponsive to conservative management for ≥ 4 weeks.32
  2. Impact on Function: Pain significantly restricting ADLs or mobility.32
  3. Acute Disease Complications: In patients with acute myeloma complications (renal failure, hypercalcemia) where prolonged bed rest would be deleterious—early VAP may be indicated even without a 4-week trial.32

Relative Indications

  1. Thoracolumbar Junction Fractures (T10–L2): Mechanically vulnerable; prone to rapid kyphosis progression.32
  2. Progressive Height Loss: Documented on serial imaging.32
  3. Posterior Wall Defects: Carefully performed kyphoplasty (lower pressure) may be considered.32

6.2 Timing of Intervention

Optimal Window: 4–8 Weeks Early treatment prevents chronic deformity, muscle deconditioning, and pulmonary complications. Fracture is still mobile and potentially reducible before consolidation. However, stabilization of life-threatening systemic complications (e.g., renal failure) takes precedence.32

6.3 Procedural Safety Guidelines

  • Level Limit: Maximum 3 vertebral levels in a single session to mitigate cement toxicity and embolic events.34
  • Pre-procedural MRI (STIR): Mandatory to confirm acute (edematous) fracture corresponding to pain site.
  • CT Scan: Assess posterior wall integrity and pedicle anatomy.32

7. Management of Inadvertent PVP in Active Spinal Infection

🚨 Catastrophic Complication Performing PVP on an infected vertebra transforms a sterile procedure into a complex septic condition involving a foreign body (PMMA cement). Bacteria form biofilm on PMMA surfaces, conferring resistance to systemic antibiotics—eradication is nearly impossible without foreign material removal.10
Level II–III Evidence (systematic reviews)

7.1 Pathophysiology of Infected Vertebroplasty

PMMA polymerization heat is insufficient to sterilize established infection. Cement acts as a bacterial adhesion locus. Biofilm creates a physical barrier and induces persister cell metabolic states, rendering systemic antibiotics ineffective.10

7.2 Diagnosis of Post-Procedural Infection

  • Suspect in new/worsening pain, fever, or elevated inflammatory markers (CRP, ESR) days to weeks post-procedure.
  • Mean time to diagnosis: ~5.2 months (range: acute to delayed).3
  • MRI: New endplate erosion, disc involvement, or abscess adjacent to cement bolus.36

7.3 Management Strategies

Strategy A: Conservative (Antibiotic Therapy)

High Failure Rate — Mortality ~29% Reserved for poor surgical candidates. Requires culture-guided therapy.3
  • IV Phase: 2–6 weeks of IV antibiotics (cover S. aureus incl. MRSA and Gram-negatives).3
  • Oral Phase: Total duration 6–12 weeks (12 weeks recommended when retained foreign material present).38
  • Suppressive Therapy: Indefinite oral suppressive antibiotics if infection cannot be eradicated in inoperable cases.

Strategy B: Surgical Management (Revision & Debridement)

Performed in >93% of reported cases in systematic reviews.3

Indications: Neurological deficit, spinal instability, progressive kyphosis, failure of conservative therapy, sepsis from spinal focus.36

  • Posterior Approach: Limited debridement, stabilization. Ultrasonic bone scalpels (piezosurgery) for cement fragmentation.41
  • Anterior/Combined Approach: Definitive "salvage." Anterior corpectomy for en-bloc removal of infected vertebra + cement. Reconstruction (cage/graft) + posterior instrumentation.40
  • Antibiotic-Impregnated Cement: PMMA loaded with vancomycin/tobramycin provides high local antimicrobial concentrations, peaking in first days and persisting for weeks.35

7.4 Prognosis

Key Prognostic Factor Preoperative neurological deficits are the strongest predictor of postoperative mobility limitations (OR = 6.0). Overall mortality: ~12.3%.3
