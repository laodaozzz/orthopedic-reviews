Surgical Management of Inferior Patellar Pole Avulsion Fractures

Evidence-Based Review of Techniques, Fixation Principles, and Clinical Outcomes — Interactive Educational Summary

I. Introduction: Pathoanatomy, Biomechanics & Epidemiology #

1.1 Anatomy & Biomechanics of the Patellofemoral Joint

The patella, the largest sesamoid bone in the human body, is an integral component of the knee extensor mechanism. Its primary function is to act as a fulcrum, displacing the quadriceps tendon anteriorly away from the center of rotation of the knee. This arrangement increases the mechanical advantage of the extensor mechanism by 30–50% Ref 1, which is essential for activities such as standing, walking, and jumping.

The posterior surface—particularly the superior three-quarters—is covered by the thickest articular cartilage in the body (up to 1 cm) Ref 2. During knee flexion, the patella is subjected to tensile forces from the patellar and quadriceps tendons and immense compressive loads that can reach up to 7× body weight during stair climbing and squatting Ref 2. This highlights the high load-bearing capacity required for any internal fixation construct.

🩸 Blood Supply Consideration The patella's blood supply is derived from an anastomotic ring of geniculate arteries, with the most critical supply concentrated at the inferior pole. Excessive soft tissue dissection or extensive stripping of the anterior patellar surface can compromise this centripetal blood supply and potentially lead to osteonecrosis Ref 4.

1.2 Etiology & Mechanism of Injury

Inferior patellar pole avulsion fractures arise from an indirect mechanism: a rapid, forceful, eccentric contraction of the quadriceps while the knee is in flexion Ref 1. This causes the patella to fail in tension, avulsing the bony fragment at the patellar tendon's attachment Ref 2. The forceful contraction often continues beyond the bone, resulting in a transverse tear of the medial and lateral retinacula—the primary stabilizers of the extensor mechanism—potentially causing a complete disruption of knee extension Ref 1.

This is in contrast to fractures from a direct blow (e.g., dashboard injury), which typically cause comminuted or stellate fracture patterns from compression, often with an intact retinaculum Ref 1.

1.3 Fracture Classification & Epidemiology

  • Patellar fractures account for ~1% of all skeletal injuries, with a male-to-female ratio of 2:1 Ref 1.
  • Incidence is bimodal: peaks in younger patients (high-energy trauma) and older patients (osteoporotic fragility) Ref 3.
  • Inferior pole avulsions are especially prevalent in adolescents and young adults Ref 3.
  • A rare variant, the patellar sleeve avulsion fracture, occurs almost exclusively in skeletally immature patients (ages 8–16) and involves avulsion of a cartilaginous "sleeve" that may be nearly invisible on plain radiographs Ref 5, 9.
⚠️ Pediatric Consideration In children, ligaments and tendons are often stronger than the developing ossification centers, making sleeve fractures a biomechanically logical injury pattern Ref 10. Comparison radiographs of the contralateral knee may be necessary Ref 12.

II. Diagnosis & Indications for Surgery #

2.1 Clinical Presentation

Patients typically present with a history of indirect trauma, acute knee pain, significant swelling (hemarthrosis), and point tenderness over the patella Ref 1. A crucial finding indicating extensor mechanism compromise is the inability to perform a straight leg raise against gravity Ref 1.

2.2 Imaging: A Hierarchical Approach

Standard AP and lateral radiographs are the first step, with a lateral view at 30° flexion providing the most accurate assessment of fragment displacement Ref 2. However, advanced imaging is not optional—it can change the surgical plan in up to 49% of patients Ref 3:

  • CT scan: Superior for delineating comminuted fragments obscured on plain films Ref 5.
  • MRI: Critical for evaluating osteochondral injuries, extensor mechanism integrity, and diagnosing cartilaginous sleeve avulsions in children Ref 8.
🚨 Clinical Pearl Relying solely on plain radiographs carries a high risk of missed or misdiagnosed injury, leading to suboptimal management and poor functional outcomes Ref 11.

III. Foundational Surgical Principles #

3.1 Core Principles of Patellar Osteosynthesis

The fundamental goals are anatomical reduction of the articular surface and stable internal fixation to restore the extensor mechanism Ref 6. The AO tension band principle is the cornerstone: it converts the strong tensile forces from the quadriceps during flexion into compressive forces at the fracture site Ref 5, promoting union and early controlled motion.

📏 Patellar Height Preservation Post-operative patellar height (Insall-Salvati ratio) is a strong predictor of long-term outcome. Loss of patellar height (patella baja) correlates with decreased ROM, weakness, and anterior knee pain Ref 5.

3.2 Surgical Approaches

A midline longitudinal incision centered over the patella is most common. The subcutaneous location of the patella makes it vulnerable to wound problems, infection, and symptomatic hardware—a major driver for newer low-profile and non-metallic fixation techniques Ref 2, 5.

3.3 Intraoperative Technique

Meticulous irrigation and debridement of hematoma and debris are performed before fixation. Reduction occurs with the knee in full extension, confirmed by intraoperative fluoroscopy Ref 16.

IV. Modern Fixation Techniques #

4.1 Tension Band Construct

The most widely adopted method. The classic technique involves two longitudinal K-wires secured with an 18-gauge stainless steel wire in a figure-of-eight configuration Ref 1. Modifications include:

  • Cannulated screws replacing K-wires: Biomechanically stronger, reduced hardware failure Ref 5.
  • Cerclage wire supplementation: Additional stability for comminuted fractures Ref 5.

4.2 Plate Fixation

  • Basket Plates: Conform to inferior patella shape, capture comminuted fragments. Enable immediate mobilization. A retrospective study demonstrated superior patellofemoral scores (94.1 vs. 81.2) and better preservation of patellar height compared to pole resection Ref 19, 25.
  • Rim Plating: Low-profile plate along the patellar rim. Preliminary studies show excellent outcomes—100% union, full ROM within weeks, no hardware-related complications Ref 30.

4.3 Suture-Based & Hardware-Sparing Techniques

Driven by the high rate of symptomatic hardware (up to 50%) and the burden of re-operation Ref 22:

  • Transosseous Tunnel Suture (TTS): Strong non-absorbable sutures through drill tunnels. Cost-effective but requires longer incision Ref 13, 28.
  • Suture Anchor (AS) Fixation: Faster procedure, smaller incision, higher cost. Comparable clinical outcomes to TTS with shorter operative time Ref 27, 28.
  • All-Suture Repair & Suture Button Constructs (e.g., Arthrex TightRope): Eliminates need for hardware removal. High union rates, no hardware complications reported Ref 27, 32.

V. Comparative Analysis & Clinical Outcomes #

5.1 Internal Fixation vs. Pole Resection

A landmark retrospective study directly compared internal fixation (basket plates) to pole resection Level III – Retrospective comparative study [Ref 25]:

  • Patellofemoral score: 94.1 (fixation) vs. 81.2 (resection)
  • Patellar height preserved: 10/11 patients (fixation) vs. 3/13 patients (resection)

This causal link between patella baja and poor outcome confirms that preserving the patellar pole through internal fixation is now standard of care Level III.

5.3 Summary of Functional Outcomes

Overall prognosis is excellent: good or excellent results in >95% of patients with proper technique Ref 5. Average flexion ranges from 120° to 135° Ref 13. Poorer outcomes are associated with significant comminution, open fractures, and total patellectomy (which can cause >50% loss of extensor strength) Ref 5.

VI. Complications & Their Management #

6.1 Hardware-Related Complications

Symptomatic hardware is the most common complication, occurring in up to 50% of cases and the primary driver of re-operation Ref 5. This is a direct consequence of the patella's subcutaneous location and motivates the shift to non-metallic fixation Ref 27.

VII. Postoperative Rehabilitation #

Rehabilitation is tailored to fixation stability:

  • Stable fixation (basket/rim plates, suture buttons): Immediate or early mobilization and partial weight-bearing Ref 19.
  • Less stable constructs: Brace locked in extension for 4–6 weeks, gradual ROM progression Ref 1.
  • All patients: Static isometric quadriceps exercises beginning postoperative day 1 to prevent atrophy. Progress to active/active-assisted ROM and weight-bearing as tolerated with crutches Ref 1.

VIII. Conclusion & Future Directions #

The evidence clearly supports surgical intervention for displaced inferior pole avulsion fractures, guided by strict criteria for displacement (>3–4 mm) and articular congruity (>2–3 mm step-off). Internal fixation is definitively superior to pole resection, with direct correlation between patellar height preservation and positive long-term outcomes.

While tension banding remains the workhorse, its burden of symptomatic hardware has driven innovation. Hardware-sparing methods (all-suture, suture anchors, suture buttons) and low-profile plate constructs represent significant advances, providing stable fixation while eliminating the need for re-operation.
