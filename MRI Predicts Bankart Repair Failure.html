Advanced MRI Assessment in Anterior Shoulder Instability

A Radiologist's Evidence-Based Guide to Predicting Bankart Repair Failure & Informing the Bankart-versus-Latarjet Decision

The Unstable Shoulder: An Imaging-Centric View of Pathoanatomy

The glenohumeral joint affords the human body its greatest range of motion—a functional advantage that comes at the cost of inherent instability. Its architecture, often likened to a golf ball on a tee, features a large, spherical humeral head articulating with a significantly smaller and shallower glenoid fossa.Ref 1 This mismatch necessitates a complex interplay of static and dynamic stabilizers to maintain joint congruity.

Static & Dynamic Stabilizers

Stability is maintained by two synergistic systems:Ref 2

  • Static stabilizers: Bony geometry, negative intra-articular pressure, and—most critically—the capsulolabral complex. The glenoid labrum deepens the socket by approximately 50%, increasing contact surface area and acting as a crucial "bumper" against humeral head translation.Ref 3
  • Dynamic stabilizers: The rotator cuff muscles provide compressive force centering the humeral head, complemented by the long head of the biceps tendon and periscapular muscles.Ref 2

Arthroscopic Bankart repair restores the integrity of the static capsulolabral structures, but its success is fundamentally predicated on the adequacy of the underlying bony architecture. The procedure cannot compensate for significant osseous deficiencies—a reality that underscores the critical importance of preoperative imaging.

The Spectrum of Injury: From Soft-Tissue to Bipolar Bone Loss

Traumatic anterior shoulder dislocation initiates a cascade of injuries:Ref 2,3,11

  • Bankart lesion: Avulsion of the anteroinferior labrum from the glenoid rim with the anterior band of the IGHL—the "essential lesion" found in up to 90% of traumatic anterior dislocations.Ref 2
  • Bony Bankart / Glenoid Bone Loss (GBL): Fracture of the anteroinferior glenoid rim, or chronic attritional erosion from recurrent dislocations.Ref 4,11
  • Hill-Sachs lesion: Compression fracture of the posterosuperior-lateral humeral head from impact against the anterior glenoid. Prevalence is 40–90% initially and approaches 100% in recurrent instability.Ref 14
  • Bipolar bone lesion: Simultaneous GBL + Hill-Sachs lesion—recognized as a single, interacting biomechanical unit.Ref 17
⚠ Critical Insight

The primary cause of failure after arthroscopic Bankart repair is not a re-tear of the soft-tissue repair but rather the inability of that repair to overcome the instability created by unaddressed bipolar bone loss.Ref 2

Why Bone Loss Matters: The Biomechanical Cascade

Glenoid bone loss directly reduces the anteroposterior diameter of the glenoid, effectively shortening the articular arc and diminishing the contact surface area.Ref 21 As GBL progresses, the normal pear shape can become an "inverted pear," significantly compromising the glenoid's buttress function.Ref 23

On the humeral side, a Hill-Sachs lesion can "engage" the anterior glenoid rim during abduction and external rotation, acting as a fulcrum to lever the humeral head out of the socket.Ref 14,16 This creates a self-propagating vicious cycle: instability causes bone loss, which causes more instability, leading to further bone loss with each episode.Ref 22

Quantifying Glenoid Bone Loss (GBL): Methodologies & Evolving Thresholds

The percentage of GBL is the primary predictor of failure for arthroscopic Bankart repair and the key determinant for bony augmentation.Ref 27 Understanding of what constitutes "critical" bone loss has evolved significantly over the past two decades.

💡 Evolving Paradigm

The decision is no longer based on a simple anatomical cutoff for preventing catastrophic failure (redislocation) but on a patient-centered assessment of risk that incorporates functional expectations. A 15% GBL may be "subcritical" for a sedentary individual but functionally "critical" for a competitive overhead athlete.

Step-by-Step MRI Measurement: The Best-Fit Circle Method

The most widely used and validated method for quantifying GBL on MRI is the best-fit circle method, leveraging the consistent circular shape of the inferior two-thirds of the glenoid.Ref 22

  1. Image Selection: Obtain a true en face view of the glenoid on sagittal oblique MRI, planned parallel to the glenoid face.Ref 22 3D MRI reconstructions can also produce an ideal view.Ref 31
  2. Drawing the Best-Fit Circle: Position a circle to perfectly match the intact posterior and inferior glenoid margins.Ref 17
  3. Measuring Intact Glenoid Diameter (D): Draw a line through the circle center perpendicular to the long glenoid axis.Ref 17
  4. Measuring Bone Loss Width (d): Measure the distance from the blunted anterior glenoid rim to the anterior edge of the best-fit circle.Ref 17
  5. Calculating GBL:
%GBL = (d ÷ D) × 100

Diagnostic Performance: MRI vs CT

Historically, 3D-CT was considered the gold standard. However, substantial recent evidence demonstrates that MRI is a highly accurate and reliable alternative:Ref 34,39,40

  • Correlation coefficient r = 0.95 between CT and MRI for glenoid width measurement
  • Correlation r = 0.84 between MRI and the reference standard of arthroscopy
  • A 2024 systematic review of 11 studies: no significant difference between MRI and CT measurements
  • Good-to-excellent inter- and intra-observer reliability with standardized protocols
✅ Clinical Implication

A well-performed MRI can serve as a "one-stop-shop" evaluation—providing all necessary information on both soft-tissue and osseous pathology, eliminating the need for additional CT scans.Ref 34

The Hill-Sachs Interval (HSI)

The most critical measurement for modern surgical planning is the Hill-Sachs Interval (HSI)—the key humeral-sided input for the glenoid track calculation.Ref 17

The HSI is defined as the width of the Hill-Sachs lesion plus the width of the intact "bone bridge" between the lateral margin of the lesion and the rotator cuff (infraspinatus tendon) insertion. It is measured on the axial MRI slice demonstrating the greatest medial extent of the lesion.Ref 36

This shifts the radiological report from being purely descriptive to being actively prescriptive in guiding the surgical algorithm.

The Glenoid Track Concept: A Unifying Framework

The glenoid track concept represents a paradigm shift from separate, static assessment of glenoid and humeral defects toward a unified, dynamic model that evaluates their interaction.Ref 17

Core Principles

The glenoid track is the zone of contact between the glenoid and humeral head as the arm moves into the high-risk position of abduction and external rotation. Biomechanical studies have determined that this contact zone is approximately 83% of the total glenoid diameter.Ref 17

📐 The Glenoid Track Decision Rule

On-Track (HSI < GT): The humeral defect remains on the glenoid articular surface throughout ROM → low risk of engagement.
Off-Track (HSI > GT): The medial margin of the defect extends beyond the glenoid track → engagement, levering, recurrent instability.

Step-by-Step Calculation on MRI

Diagnostic Performance & Reliability

MRI demonstrates 84% overall accuracy for correctly classifying lesions as on-track or off-track when compared to intraoperative arthroscopic findings.Ref 23

However, reliability is experience-dependent: inter- and intra-observer reliability is good among fellowship-trained surgeons but only moderate to poor among residents. The HSI measurement has lower reliability than glenoid-sided measurements.Ref 49

⚠ Evidence Gap

These findings highlight the critical importance of standardized measurement protocols, adequate training, and collaboration between experienced musculoskeletal radiologists and orthopedic surgeons.

Translating Imaging Findings into a Surgical Algorithm

MRI Predictors of Bankart Repair Failure

  • Primary predictor — Off-track lesion (HSI > GT): The single strongest MRI-based predictor of soft-tissue repair failure.Ref 17
  • Secondary predictor — GBL degree: GBL >17–20% markedly increases risk; "subcritical" GBL >13.5% predicts poor functional outcomes in high-demand patients.Ref 19,28
  • Other risk factors:
    • Large Hill-Sachs lesion (>25–40% of humeral head), especially medial/inferior locationRef 33
    • Larger Bankart tear sizeRef 19
    • GLAD lesion (superficial labral tear + adjacent cartilage damage)Ref 13,55

The Latarjet Procedure: Indications

The Latarjet provides a "triple effect" of stability:Ref 26

  1. Bony effect: Restores glenoid bone stock
  2. Ligament effect: Reinforces the anterior capsule
  3. Sling effect: Dynamic muscular sling from the conjoined tendon

Primary MRI indication: Off-track bipolar bone lesion.
Secondary MRI indication: GBL >20% (strong); lowered to >17% or >13.5% in high-risk patients.Ref 8,28

Advanced Imaging Horizons & Reporting Recommendations

The Role of 3D MRI

Isotropic 3D MRI sequences (e.g., FRACTURE—Fast Field Echo Resembling a CT Using Restricted Echo-spacing) generate images with CT-like bone contrast without ionizing radiation.Ref 38 These can be reformatted to create perfect en face views and 3D reconstructions, potentially improving inter-observer reliability.Ref 31,41

Essential Elements of a Clinically Actionable MRI Report

📋 Required Quantitative Data

1. Glenoid Bone Loss (GBL): Percentage via best-fit circle method
2. Glenoid Track (GT) Width: In millimeters
3. Hill-Sachs Interval (HSI): In millimeters
4. On-Track / Off-Track Status: Clear concluding statement

Required Qualitative Data: Detailed capsulolabral injury description (Bankart, ALPSA, GLAD variants), rotator cuff integrity, biceps tendon assessment, and articular cartilage evaluation.

📝 Exemplary Impression

"1. Bipolar bone lesion: estimated 18% anterior GBL with large Hill-Sachs lesion.
2. HSI (19 mm) > calculated GT (16.5 mm) → off-track lesion.
3. Anteroinferior labral tear consistent with Bankart lesion.
4. These findings are associated with a high risk of recurrent instability following isolated arthroscopic Bankart repair."

Synthesized Recommendations for the Clinical Team

  1. Adopt a Quantitative Approach: All preoperative MRI examinations should include routine, quantitative assessment of osseous defects. Descriptive reports are insufficient.Level II
  2. Embrace the Glenoid Track Concept: The on-track vs. off-track determination should be a primary goal, as an off-track finding is the strongest predictor of Bankart repair failure.Ref 17
  3. Recognize Evolving GBL Thresholds: "Critical" GBL is a spectrum. While >20% remains strong indication for augmentation, thresholds of 16–17% predict recurrence, and "subcritical" >13.5% predicts poor functional outcomes.Level I
  4. Standardize MRI Reporting: Include %GBL, GT width, HSI, and on/off-track determination in every report.
  5. Integrate with Clinical Risk Factors: Use tools like the ISIS to contextualize imaging data and ensure surgical procedures align with patient age, activity level, and overall risk profile.Ref 57
