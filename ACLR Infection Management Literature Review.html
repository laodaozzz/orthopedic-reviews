Management of Delayed Surgical Site Infection Following All-Inside Anterior Cruciate Ligament Reconstruction

A Comprehensive Evidence-Based Interactive Summary • Based on Provided Research Document

1. Introduction to the Clinical Paradigm

Postoperative surgical site infection (SSI) following anterior cruciate ligament reconstruction (ACLR) represents a rare but potentially catastrophic complication, with contemporary epidemiological data indicating incidence rates generally ranging between 0.3% and 1.7% in primary reconstructive procedures.1 While the overwhelming majority of these infections manifest during the acute or subacute postoperative phases—typically within the first two to eight weeks—delayed presentations occurring several months postoperatively introduce a profoundly distinct diagnostic and therapeutic challenge.2

The advent and widespread global adoption of the "All-Inside" ACLR technique has fundamentally altered both the biomechanics of graft incorporation and the localized microenvironment where foreign bodies are introduced.4 This technique is characterized by:

  • Creation of closed-end bone sockets (half-tunnels)
  • Preservation of native bone stock
  • Utilization of adjustable-loop suspensory cortical fixation on both femoral and tibial sides
🔬 Specific Clinical Scenario Under Investigation A patient precisely 6 months post-all-inside ACLR, presenting with a localized, purulent extra-articular infection specifically isolated to the sites of the tibial and femoral cortical suspensory fixation (e.g., EndoButton, TightRope). Diagnostic arthroscopy unequivocally confirms a pristine, clear intra-articular joint cavity containing a viable, adequately tensioned graft displaying extensive synovialization.6

This presentation distinctively separates the underlying pathology from catastrophic intra-articular septic arthritis (such as Gächter stage III or IV), definitively reclassifying it as a delayed, implant-associated extra-articular SSI.3 The fundamental clinical dilemma is whether complete excision of the metallic cortical buttons and their associated braided ultra-high-molecular-weight polyethylene (UHMWPE) adjustable loops will result in catastrophic loss of graft tension and subsequent clinical failure, or if the biological graft-to-bone integration at 6 months is sufficiently robust to allow for hardware explantation without compromising joint stability.7

2. Graft-to-Bone Healing vs. Hardware Removal at 6 Months

The primary biomechanical apprehension when contemplating the removal of cortical suspensory fixation devices is the immediate risk of graft slippage, catastrophic loss of tension, or complete graft pull-out from the bone sockets.7 In the all-inside ACLR technique, the initial "time-zero" fixation relies entirely on the mechanical interface between the metallic button resting on the outer cortical bone and the adjustable braided loop suspending the soft-tissue graft within the closed-end socket.10 However, the ultimate longitudinal success of the procedure is completely dependent on the biological transition from this temporary mechanical fixation to permanent biological integration—defined by the processes of ligamentization and osseointegration.11

2.1 The Chronology of Ligamentization and Osseointegration

The biological healing of a free tendon autograft (such as the quadrupled semitendinosus graft most frequently utilized in all-inside ACLR) within a newly drilled cancellous bone socket undergoes three distinct, overlapping biological phases:12

Phase 1: Early Healing (0–4 Weeks)

Characterized by an acute inflammatory response, formation of a localized hematoma within the bone socket, and rapid influx of macrophages, neutrophils, and fibroblasts. The graft undergoes profound central necrosis due to acute lack of vascularity. The graft is entirely dependent on the mechanical suspensory hardware, and biological pull-out strength drops precipitously.12

Phase 2: Proliferation (4–12 Weeks)

Marked by intense cellular activity, revascularization (synovialization), and deposition of an immature, highly cellular extracellular matrix. Critical initiation of fibrovascular tissue formation at the precise graft-bone interface, progressively bridging the physical gap between the outer layer of the tendon and the cancellous bone wall of the socket.1114

Phase 3: Maturation & Ligamentization (12 Weeks–24 Months)

Randomly organized type III collagen is gradually absorbed and replaced by highly organized, longitudinally oriented type I collagen. The graft tissue is biologically remodeled under physiological mechanical stress to mimic the ultrastructure of the native ACL.14

✅ Key Finding at 6 Months By the 6-month postoperative mark, the intra-articular portion of the graft has progressed deeply into the maturation phase, while the extra-articular (intra-socket) portion has established significant, permanent structural connections to the host bone.14 The biological environment has fundamentally shifted away from mechanical hardware dependence.

2.2 Formation and Mineralization of Sharpey's Fibers

The structural integrity and ultimate tensile strength of a soft-tissue graft within a bone tunnel are dictated exclusively by the formation of an indirect enthesis, characterized by the development of Sharpey-like penetrating fibers.15 These robust collagenous fibers serve as biological anchors, penetrating from the periosteum and the outer adventitia of the remodeling tendon directly into the mineralizing bone matrix of the socket wall.

Histological and biomechanical studies (Level II–III) demonstrate a highly predictable timeline:17

  • 3–6 weeks: Initial emergence of anchoring Sharpey-like fibers at the healing tendon-bone interface.17
  • 12 weeks: Substantial, organized Sharpey's fibers are formed. The biomechanical "weakest link" transitions from the tendon-bone interface to the mid-substance of the intra-articular graft (mid-substance rupture). This indicates that biological fixation strength has officially surpassed the intrinsic tensile strength of the remodeling tendon tissue.13
  • 24 weeks (6 months): Histological cross-sections exhibit a highly distinct, mature four-layered architecture resembling native indirect ligamentous insertions.20 High-level animal models (Meller et al., Arthroscopy) demonstrate that maximum load to failure is restored to approximately 70% of native knee strength.17

2.3 Biomechanical Superiority of All-Inside Sockets vs. Full Tunnels

The all-inside ACLR technique utilizing blind-ended sockets provides a highly unique microenvironment that further accelerates and fortifies graft integration, making hardware removal at 6 months significantly safer than in traditional techniques. Traditional full-tunnels with aperture fixation via resorbable interference screws (RIS) suffer from limited biological contact because the interference screw physically blocks osteoblast migration.10

A prospective Level II cohort study demonstrated that adjustable suspensory fixation (ASF) provided significantly more favorable biological conditions:10

3. Biofilm Eradication: The Absolute Mandate for Hardware Removal

3.1 Pathogenesis of Implant-Associated Biofilms

Whenever a synthetic foreign body is introduced into the human body, it immediately triggers a localized, transient immunocompromised zone. The presence of the avascular hardware locally paralyzes macrophage function and provides an inert surface devoid of host immune surveillance.24 A landmark study presented at the AAOS 2025 Annual Meeting by Cavendish et al. revealed that detectable levels of subclinical bacterial contamination occur in up to 50% of ACLR surgeries, with the highest risk occurring immediately prior to graft passage.22

Once planktonic pathogens adhere to hardware, they undergo a profound phenotypic shift, secreting a dense polymeric glycocalyx—the biofilm.24 This biofilm serves as an impenetrable physical and chemical barrier against host leukocytes, antibodies, and systemic pharmacological interventions.25

⚠️ Minimum Biofilm Eradication Concentration (MBEC) The MBEC frequently requires local antibiotic concentrations that are 100 to 1000 times higher than the Minimum Inhibitory Concentration (MIC) for the same strain in a planktonic state.8 Achieving such concentrations systemically is impossible due to host toxicity limits.

3.2 The Distinct Microbiological Threat of Braided Sutures

High-level Level II studies demonstrate that braided sutures retain, harbor, and protect significantly higher bacterial loads than monofilament sutures or solid metallic hardware.27 The architecture of a braided suture features microscopic interstices—tiny gaps between the woven polyethylene filaments—that provide an ideal, protected microenvironment for bacterial nesting. Because host macrophages are physically larger than these interstitial gaps, bacteria proliferate entirely unmolested.27

3.3 Evidence Supporting Mandatory Hardware Explantation

Contemporary guidelines endorsed by the European Bone and Joint Infection Society (EBJIS) and the European Society of Sports Traumatology, Knee Surgery and Arthroscopy (ESSKA) provide a definitive framework for managing infectious complications following ACLR.31 While early, acute intra-articular infections are managed with arthroscopic lavage and graft retention, delayed infections specifically localized to the hardware mandate a radically different approach.33

A compelling case report in the World Journal of Orthopedics described a 33-year-old patient presenting with a delayed recurrent abscess centered around the femoral EndoButton, fully 5 years post-ACLR.9 Initial isolated I&D procedures failed. Complete eradication was only achieved following radical secondary surgery involving complete excision of the sinus tract and total removal of the EndoButton and all associated braided sutures, while diagnostic arthroscopy confirmed the graft remained entirely viable.9

🔑 Critical Clinical Imperative A chronic, biofilm-driven SSI cannot be definitively cured with suppressive antibiotic therapy or superficial irrigation alone.8 The failure to comprehensively remove braided sutures and metallic hardware invariably leads to infection recurrence, chronic draining sinus tracts, and eventual osteomyelitis of the bone tunnels.7

4. Clinical Outcomes: Hardware Removal with Graft Retention

4.1 Eradication Efficacy and Late Graft Failure Rates

A comprehensive systematic review published in the American Journal of Sports Medicine by Lo Presti et al. analyzed outcomes of graft-preserving arthroscopic debridement paired with extra-articular hardware removal.36 The study reported a 100% eradication rate of infection following hardware removal. Among retained grafts, clinical failure (insufficient ligamentous tension requiring revision) occurred in only 1 out of 24 patients.36

A broader meta-analysis by Kuršumović et al. indicates that modern graft preservation protocols achieve an approximately 85% overall success rate across various infection severities.3

4.2 Long-Term Functional Outcomes: IKDC and Lysholm Scores

Level III–IV prospective cohort studies comparing graft-salvage patients against matched, uncomplicated ACLR controls offer vital clinical insights:53435

  • Lysholm Scores: A landmark study by Hantes et al. (The Knee) reported excellent mean Lysholm scores of 92 in patients whose grafts were salvaged, statistically comparable to uncomplicated primary ACLR (typically 90 ± 4).5
  • IKDC Scores: Consistently reach the mid-to-high 80s (mean IKDC score of 87) in patients with successfully retained grafts.34
  • Instrumented Laxity: KT-1000 arthrometer at 134N shows minimal side-to-side differences (<3 mm translation), confirming hardware removal does not induce late-stage laxity.35

4.3 The Paradox of Arthrofibrosis

⚠️ Critical Caveat: Arthrofibrosis Risk Lo Presti et al. (AJSM 2020) highlighted that while hardware removal and graft retention were highly effective in clearing infection (100%) and restoring stability, concomitant signs of severe arthrofibrosis were detected in 81% of patients on postoperative MRI. Severe cartilage defects (ICRS ≥3) were reported in 63% of cases over the long term, likely driven by inflammatory cytokines (IL-1β, TNF-α) released during the infectious period.37

5. Evidence-Based Treatment Algorithm

Synthesizing the microbiological, biomechanical, and clinical literature, a clear, structured algorithm emerges for the specific clinical presentation of a delayed (6-month) extra-articular SSI with a viable intra-articular graft following all-inside ACLR.

  1. Preoperative Optimization and Microbiological Diagnosis

    Sterile aspiration of extra-articular fluid for Gram staining, aerobic and anaerobic cultures, and specific sensitivities. Empirical antibiotics should NOT be initiated until cultures are obtained to prevent false-negative results.32 MRI with metal artifact reduction sequences to confirm absence of deep osteomyelitis.9

  2. Diagnostic Arthroscopy (Intra-articular Phase)

    Surgical procedure must begin with diagnostic arthroscopy, completely isolated from extra-articular purulence. The surgeon must meticulously avoid breaching the intra-articular space with any instruments that have contacted the extra-articular purulence to prevent iatrogenic seeding.7 High-volume lavage of the joint cavity to dilute inflammatory cytokines.

  3. Extra-articular Hardware Excision

    Approaching via separate, localized incisions over palpable hardware sites—radical, oncologic-style excision of all synthetic materials. The braided adjustable loops and metallic buttons are the irreversible nidus of the biofilm and must be completely extracted.27 Cortical bone bed should be lightly curetted.

  4. Biomechanical Assurance and Graft Probing

    Gently probe the graft intra-articularly post-hardware removal to confirm maintained resting tension. Redundant suture anchors or secondary fixation are virtually never required at this late stage, as biological osseointegration has permanently superseded mechanical fixation.10 Adding new hardware into an infected field is absolutely contraindicated.

  5. Targeted Antimicrobial Therapy

    Targeted IV antibiotics based on definitive culture results. For biofilm-producing pathogens (MRSA, S. epidermidis), transition to oral agents with high bone-penetrance. Rifampin + fluoroquinolone (e.g., Levofloxacin) is recommended by ESSKA/EBJIS for 4–6 weeks (up to 12 weeks in complex cases).32

  6. Aggressive Rehabilitation and Arthrofibrosis Mitigation

    Immediate aggressive range-of-motion (ROM) and patellar mobilization rehabilitation. Because the graft's structural integrity is biologically secured within the bone tunnels, there is no biomechanical requirement to restrict weight-bearing or ROM beyond standard pain-tolerance limits.3637

6. Conclusion

For a patient presenting at exactly 6 months post-operation with a localized, extra-articular infection and an intact, viable intra-articular graft, the high-level evidence unequivocally supports a standardized protocol of mandatory hardware explantation strictly coupled with intra-articular graft retention.

Extensive biomechanical and histological literature robustly demonstrates that by the 6-month postoperative mark, the intra-tunnel tendon-to-bone healing—anchored by dense, maturing, and mineralized Sharpey's fibers—is sufficiently robust to maintain graft tension and global knee stability entirely independent of the initial suspensory cortical buttons and braided adjustable loops.

Conversely, the microbiological risk of retaining the hardware is absolute and unforgiving. Conservative management is functionally incapable of eradicating deep interstitial biofilm. By flawlessly executing a meticulous, staged surgical explantation while preserving the pristine intra-articular space, surgeons can reliably achieve 100% infection eradication with excellent long-term functional outcomes—reflected in Lysholm and IKDC scores—that closely mirror those of uncomplicated primary ACL reconstructions.53436
