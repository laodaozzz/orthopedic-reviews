1. Introduction: The Landscape of Pediatric Forearm Fractures

1.1 Epidemiology and Clinical Significance

Forearm fractures are exceptionally common in children, accounting for 50–59% of all long bone fractures in the pediatric population III. They typically result from falls onto an outstretched hand during playground activities or sports. Approximately 75% occur at the distal radius near the wrist III. The primary treatment goal is restoration of anatomical alignment to ensure proper forearm rotation; malunion can cause permanent loss of pronation and supination IV.

1.2 Unique Aspects of the Pediatric Skeleton

Pediatric fractures differ fundamentally from adult injuries due to three key biological features:

  • Rapid Healing: Children possess a robust periosteal blood supply and heal significantly faster than adults, necessitating prompt treatment before malalignment becomes fixed III.
  • Remodeling Potential: The pediatric skeleton can correct angular deformity through remodeling. This capacity is greatest in children under 8–10 years of age and for deformities in the plane of adjacent joint motion (flexion/extension). It is limited and less predictable for rotational deformities and in children approaching skeletal maturity IV.
  • Growth Plates (Physes): Distal physes of the radius and ulna contribute ~80% of longitudinal growth, offering substantial remodeling potential for distal fractures. Proximal physes contribute only 20–25%, meaning proximal fractures have far less capacity to remodel IV.

1.3 Common Fracture Patterns

  • Greenstick Fracture: Incomplete fracture with one cortex intact; often stable once reduced.
  • Buckle (Torus) Fracture: Stable compression injury requiring only simple immobilization III.
  • Complete Fracture: Full break with potential displacement; more likely to be unstable and require fixation.
  • Plastic Deformation: Bone bends without a clear fracture line; challenging to correct and may require sustained reduction force.
⚠ Associated Injury Alert
Clinicians must be vigilant for Monteggia fractures (ulnar shaft fracture + radial head dislocation) and Galeazzi fractures (radial shaft fracture + DRUJ disruption), as these require specific treatment algorithms IV.

The Remodeling Paradox

While robust remodeling in young children historically justified non-operative treatment for even significantly angulated fractures IV, there has been a sevenfold increase in surgical management over the past two decades. Operative rates in the United States rose from 59.3% to 70.0% between 2000 and 2012 II. This shift is driven by the availability of minimally invasive technologies like ESIN and a growing "intolerance of remaining deformity" from both surgeons and families II. The decision to operate now balances natural biological potential against modern socio-technological pressures and patient expectations.

2. Indications & Patient Selection for ESIN

2.1 General Indications for Surgical Intervention

The principal indication for surgery is the inability to achieve or maintain acceptable alignment with closed reduction and casting II.

Additional Key Indications for Surgery

  • Unstable Fractures: Displacement within the cast after initial reduction II
  • Open Fractures: Require urgent irrigation, debridement, and stabilization III
  • Floating Elbow: Concurrent humerus + forearm fracture II
  • Neurovascular Compromise not resolving with closed reduction II
  • Polytrauma: Multiple injuries benefit from stable fixation III
  • Refractures through a previously healed site II

2.2 Specific Patient Selection for ESIN

  • Age Range: Generally 5–16 years; particularly effective for diaphyseal fractures in skeletally immature patients III
  • Ideal Fracture Pattern: Transverse or short oblique diaphyseal fractures; increasingly applied to long oblique, spiral, and metaphyseal-diaphyseal junction fractures III
  • Antegrade ESIN has proven effective for unstable metaphyseal-diaphyseal junction fractures III

Relative Contraindications

  • Skeletal Maturity: Thicker cortex and narrower canal make nailing difficult; ORIF with plates preferred IV
  • Comminuted/Length-Unstable Fractures: Risk of shortening; end caps may mitigate but plating may be more reliable IV
  • Intra-articular Fractures: Demand anatomical reduction best achieved with open reduction IV
📘 Evidence Note
ESIN has evolved from a "last resort" for complex polytrauma cases (1977–1980) to the standard of care for unstable pediatric forearm fractures. This progression demonstrates a major paradigm shift driven by proven efficacy, minimally invasive nature, and biological advantages III.

3. Surgical Technique: Step-by-Step Guide

3.1 Preoperative Planning

  • Positioning: Supine under general anesthesia; arm on radiolucent side table with C-arm fluoroscopy access IV
  • Nail Sizing: Diameter should be 60–70% of the medullary canal at its narrowest point, achieving three-point fixation. Combined diameter of two nails should fill ~80% of canal. Typical diameters: 2.0–3.0 mm IV
  • Nail Pre-contouring: Titanium nail bent into a smooth "C" curve matching the radial bow, with apex at fracture level. This pre-bend generates elastic spring forces for stabilization III
⚠ Common Technical Error
Using nails that are too thin is a frequent error leading to instability, loss of reduction, and delayed healing IV.

3.2 Operative Procedure

Order of Fixation

Fixing the radius first is often advantageous as it is the more complex bone and dictates forearm rotation. Alternatively, the easier bone may be fixed first to provide a stable post IV.

Radial Nail Insertion (Retrograde Technique)

  1. Entry Point: 1.5–2 cm incision over distal radial metaphysis
  2. Cortical Opening: Awl/perforation perpendicular to bone initially, then angled ~45° to create oblique channel. Never hammer the awl — risk of plunging through far cortex IV
  3. Nail Advancement: Tip introduced facing far cortex, then rotated 180° for smooth gliding. Advanced via gentle oscillating wrist movements IV
  4. Fracture Crossing: Nail used as joystick for reduction. If closed reduction fails after 3–4 attempts, a "mini-open" incision allows direct visualization IV

Ulnar Nail Insertion (Antegrade Technique)

Entry point just distal to olecranon apophysis. Nail passed antegrade (elbow → wrist) across fracture, seated in distal ulnar metaphysis, short of growth plate IV.

Final Positioning

  • Radius nail concave side faces ulna to restore radial bow and tension interosseous membrane IV
  • Nails cut leaving 1–2 cm protruding, bent away from bone, impacted flat beneath skin IV
🔴 Critical Decision: Radial Entry Point

Lateral approach (most common): risk of superficial branch of radial nerve (SBRN) injury → numbness over dorsal thumb/hand III.

Dorsal approach (Lister's tubercle): risk of Extensor Pollicis Longus (EPL) tendon irritation/rupture. One study found 100% of EPL ruptures occurred with dorsal entry point III.

The surgeon must weigh temporary sensory nerve injury vs. a major tendon complication requiring reconstructive surgery.

Decision Algorithm: Radial Entry Point ↓ Fracture requires retrograde radial ESIN ↓ Lateral Approach (preferred) → Risk: SBRN injury (often transient)
OR
Dorsal Approach (Lister's tubercle) → Risk: EPL tendon rupture ↓ If dorsal used → Consider end cap or leave nail long to protect EPL

4. Postoperative Management & Outcomes

4.1 Immediate Postoperative Care

  • Pain Management: Elevation; regular ibuprofen + paracetamol for 4–5 days; oral narcotics for breakthrough pain IV
  • Neurovascular Monitoring: Regular checks of finger sensation, motor function, and capillary refill. Increasing pain, pain with passive finger stretch, or sensory changes → high suspicion for compartment syndrome (surgical emergency requiring fasciotomy) IV
  • Immobilization: ESIN's intrinsic stability allows a major shift away from routine casting. AO Foundation guidelines state: "Cast immobilization is not necessary and hinders early recovery" IV. A temporary splint or sling for comfort only. Exception: highly unstable patterns (e.g., Monteggia) may require cast for 2–4 weeks IV
✅ Key Paradigm Shift
The ability to bypass prolonged immobilization accelerates the entire recovery process — earlier ROM, faster return to function, and avoidance of cast-related stiffness and atrophy III.

4.2 Rehabilitation

  • Early finger, wrist, and elbow mobilization as soon as pain subsides IV
  • Pronation/supination exercises begin ~postoperative week 3 IV
  • Full functional ROM expected; residual stiffness resolves within weeks of mobilization III
  • Sports/high-impact activities restricted for 4–6 weeks after radiographic healing III

5. Complications: Identification & Management

5.1 Overview

Reported complication rates vary widely (3.1%–67%), largely due to inconsistent definitions III. Large single-center studies report more reliable rates: 8.9% overall (6.9% requiring reoperation) in a study of 202 fractures III, and 6.25% in 240 fractures III. Complications are twofold more frequent in patients over age 10 III.

5.2 Detailed Management of Key Complications

EPL Tendon Rupture

Results in inability to extend thumb at interphalangeal joint. Requires tendon transfer using Extensor Indicis Proprius (EIP). Prevention is paramount: favor lateral entry or use end caps with dorsal approach III.

Refracture with Nails in situ

Uncommon; caused by new trauma after healing. Can often be managed by closed reduction under anesthesia, "rebending" the nails in place — avoiding revision surgery IV. Note: nail biomechanical properties may be compromised after re-bending.

Refracture After Nail Removal

Almost exclusively linked to premature removal. Stress-shielding leaves bone temporarily weaker; stress riser at entry point. Treatment: often requires ORIF with plates. Prevention: delay removal until mature, quadricortical healing III.

Prominent Hardware

Most frequently reported minor complication. Prevented by proper cutting/bending technique. Definitive treatment: implant removal III.

📘 Key Comparative Finding
A systematic review and meta-analysis concluded that functional outcomes and union rates are largely equivalent between ESIN and plating I. Similarly, a prospective RCT found no significant difference between ESIN and K-wires in radiographic or functional outcomes I. The choice of implant should be guided by fracture characteristics, patient age, and surgeon expertise and proficiency with each technique I.

This nuanced reality suggests the debate is less about which technology is universally superior and more about the interplay between technique and technology. A well-performed plating procedure will yield a better result than a poorly performed nailing procedure, and vice versa.

7. Emerging Trends & Future Directions

7.1 Bioresorbable Elastic Stable Intramedullary Nailing (BESIN)

Bioresorbable nails made from Poly(L-lactide-co-glycolide) (PLGA) provide stable fixation during healing and are gradually resorbed, eliminating the need for removal surgery III.

  • A systematic review and meta-analysis concluded bioresorbable nails are a "safe and effective alternative" to titanium, with equivalent outcomes I
  • A clinical study of 38 PLGA patients showed effective healing, excellent joint motion, minimal scarring, and high satisfaction III
  • A prospective RCT (BESIN vs. ESIN) is currently underway (NCT03474900) I
  • Concerns: Material failure reported in 22% of patients; local inflammatory reactions noted. Further optimization needed I

7.2 Hybrid Fixation Techniques

Combining different implant types (e.g., plate on radius + ESIN on ulna) within the same patient to leverage unique advantages of each method IV. Studies report satisfactory results, potentially reducing incision length and operative time compared to double plating IV.

8. Clinical Practice Guidelines & Landmark Literature

8.1 Professional Society Guidelines

AAOS, POSNA, and OTA agree on core principles: operative treatment for fractures that are open, irreducible, or unstable after reduction II. Key principles include thorough neurovascular examination, meticulous recreation of anatomy (radial bow, rotational alignment), and age-based treatment decisions. For many specific questions (e.g., optimal implant removal timing), evidence is graded as "Limited" or "Inconclusive" II.
