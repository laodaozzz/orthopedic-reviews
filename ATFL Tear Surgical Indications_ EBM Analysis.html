1 Introduction & Epidemiology

Lateral ankle sprains represent one of the most common musculoskeletal injuries in sports medicine, with far-reaching consequences when inadequately treated.

Lateral ankle sprains are among the most ubiquitous injuries in orthopedic sports medicine, with over two million cases annually in the United States, accounting for approximately 50% of all lower extremity injuries in competitive athletes and up to 76% of all sports-related injuries involving running or jumping.1 Despite the historical tendency to dismiss these injuries as benign and self-limiting, contemporary long-term outcome studies reveal a more concerning reality: between 20–40% of patients fail to achieve physiological tissue healing and progress to develop Chronic Lateral Ankle Instability (CLAI).4

CLAI is a debilitating pathological state characterized by persistent joint pain, recurrent episodes of ankle buckling or "giving way," and measurable decrements in proprioceptive and neuromuscular control.5 When left untreated, isolated ligament tears evolve into complex syndromes involving osteochondral lesions of the talus (OLT), peroneal tendinopathy, and progressive post-traumatic ankle osteoarthritis.9

Anatomy of the Lateral Ankle Complex

The lateral ankle complex consists of a three-part ligamentous network:3

  • Anterior Talofibular Ligament (ATFL): Originates from the anterior colliculus of the lateral malleolus and inserts onto the lateral talar articular facet. Serves as the primary restraint to inversion in plantarflexion, making it the most vulnerable and frequently disrupted structure during the classic supination-plantarflexion mechanism.8
  • Calcaneofibular Ligament (CFL): Crosses both the talocrural and subtalar joints, restricting inversion in neutral and dorsiflexion while providing critical rotational stability to the subtalar joint.8
  • Posterior Talofibular Ligament (PTFL): The strongest of the triad; rarely fails unless massive dislocation occurs.8

Over the past decade, surgical management has undergone a paradigm shift driven by advancements in all-inside arthroscopic techniques, high-resolution musculoskeletal imaging, and the introduction of ultra-high-molecular-weight polyethylene (UHMWPE) biomaterials. The historic gold standard—the open Modified Broström-Gould (MBG) procedure—is now frequently challenged, augmented, or replaced by minimally invasive arthroscopic repairs and synthetic suture-tape augmentations.14

2 Pathophysiology: Mechanical vs. Functional Instability

A fundamental differentiation between Mechanical Ankle Instability (MAI) and Functional Ankle Instability (FAI) is critical for guiding therapeutic strategy.

Key Clinical Recommendation

While FAI and MAI can exist independently, they frequently coexist in the chronic state, creating a positive feedback loop where mechanical laxity perpetuates further mechanoreceptor damage, and neuromuscular deficits fail to protect compromised static restraints from repetitive microtrauma.11

The Universal Threshold for Surgical Transition

The natural history of acute lateral ankle sprains generally favors biological healing with appropriate early intervention. The universal therapeutic algorithm dictates that non-operative management is the absolute first line of treatment for both FAI and MAI.11

Comprehensive conservative management must include a phased approach: initial protection and edema control → rigorous functional rehabilitation (Star Excursion Balance Test, wobble board exercises) → peroneal muscle strengthening → external orthoses or lace-up bracing for dynamic stabilization.5

Surgical Indication Threshold Level II · Grade 2C

Operative intervention for CLAI is formally indicated only after documented failure of comprehensive non-operative management spanning 3 to 6 months. Failure is defined as the persistence of mechanical laxity, recurrent instability events, and limiting pain that precludes return to pre-injury activity level. Delaying surgery indefinitely in documented MAI exposes cartilage to chronic shear forces, accelerating post-traumatic osteoarthritis.5

3 Diagnostic Thresholds & Imaging Modalities

Definitive diagnosis of ATFL insufficiency requires multimodal synthesis of manual stress testing, high-resolution structural imaging, and dynamic functional assessment.

Imaging Hierarchy

  • Standard Weight-Bearing Radiographs (AP, lateral, mortise): Universally mandated to assess global foot alignment, identify cavovarus deformities, rule out fractures, and evaluate joint space narrowing.5
  • MRI: Essential for preoperative planning. Not only visualizes ATFL/CFL discontinuity, but critically identifies concomitant intra-articular pathologies (OLT, peroneal tendinopathy, syndesmotic injuries) which occur in the majority of CLAI cases.5 Limitation: evaluates ankle in a static, unloaded state.
  • Stress Radiography: Quantifies magnitude of mechanical instability objectively. The ESSKA-AFAS consensus notes low sensitivity (~57%) but high specificity (~100%).11
  • Dynamic Stress Ultrasonography: Cost-effective, radiation-free alternative allowing real-time visualization of ligamentous behavior under load.21

Diagnostic Algorithm Summary

The diagnostic transition from conservative to operative management relies on a rigorously defined multifactorial algorithm: (1) absolute failure of 3–6 months of targeted functional physiotherapy; (2) subjective reports of functionally limiting instability (FAI); and (3) objective corroboration of mechanical incompetence via MRI discontinuity or quantifiable stress radiographic thresholds (ATT > 5 mm, TTT > 10°).5, 11, 20

4 Concomitant Lesions: OLT & CFL Tears

The concept of "isolated" ATFL rupture is increasingly recognized as a clinical rarity. Concomitant OLTs and CFL tears profoundly alter surgical decision-making algorithms.

Osteochondral Lesions of the Talus (OLT)

OLTs are alarmingly common, identified in up to 70% of acute lateral ankle sprains and highly prevalent in the CLAI population.9 The ankle cartilage is intrinsically thin (~1.2 mm), rendering it exceptionally susceptible to shear-induced injury when the mortise loses ligamentous congruency.24

OLT Treatment Thresholds Level I/II

Microfracture (Bone Marrow Stimulation): For focal defects < 15 mm diameter (< 1.0–1.5 cm² area) without massive subchondral cystic changes — arthroscopic debridement and microfracture performed concurrently with ATFL repair is the standard of care.27

Scaffolding / Transplantation: Lesions > 1.0–1.5 cm², or chronic lesions with significant subchondral bone loss and deep cystic formation require OATS, ACI, or AMIC scaffolding.27

Key Evidence Level II · Grade 2C

Simultaneous surgical intervention for both CLAI and concomitant OLTs is strongly recommended. Staging or failing to address articular pathology concurrently results in persistently inferior functional outcomes, continued pain, and unacceptably high revision rates.13 Incorporating microfracture for OLTs < 15 mm does not negatively impact the functional recovery or stability of the primary ATFL repair.28

Calcaneofibular Ligament (CFL) Complete Tears

The CFL bridges the talocrural and subtalar joints. Complete disruption invariably introduces subtalar instability — estimated in up to 80% of severe chronic instability cases.14

CFL Deficiency: Critical Decision Point Level II · Expert Consensus

In the presence of complete chronic CFL tear with significant tissue attenuation, persistent subtalar instability, high BMI, or generalized ligamentous laxity, isolated primary Broström repair is contraindicated due to unacceptably high failure rates. An anatomic reconstruction using autograft (semitendinosus, gracilis, peroneus brevis) or allograft routed through osseous tunnels in fibula, talus, and calcaneus is required.14

In the acute setting, the presence of a complete combined ATFL + CFL rupture in elite/professional athletes frequently warrants acute surgical repair, as the risk of developing CLAI and career-altering downtime is unacceptable in this high-demand demographic.5 Level II/III

Demographic Stratification

  • General Population: Both techniques offer excellent, durable outcomes. The decision favors arthroscopy if the surgeon has advanced arthroscopic proficiency and the patient values early cosmesis, reduced scarring, and faster weight-bearing.16
  • High-Demand / Elite Athletes: Many elite sports medicine surgeons favor open or mini-open procedures to allow meticulous, direct visualization of tissue tensioning and reliable incorporation of the extensor retinaculum. The most critical advancement for this demographic is the mandatory integration of synthetic ligament augmentation.5
⚠ Technical Caveat

Arthroscopic repair carries a steep learning curve. Improper portal placement or careless instrumentation can result in severe iatrogenic entrapment of the superficial peroneal nerve or sural nerve, leading to chronic regional pain syndromes.14

6 Internal Brace (Suture Tape) Augmentation

The integration of UHMWPE suture-tape augmentation has revolutionized CLAI surgery by protecting the biological repair and dramatically accelerating return to play.

Biomechanical Rationale

Suture-tape augmentation involves anatomic placement of a braided, non-absorbable UHMWPE tape (often collagen-coated) spanning the ATFL footprint using knotless biotenodesis anchors.17 The tape functions as an "internal seatbelt" — it does not replace the native ligament but mechanically offloads the primary biological repair, restricting pathological inversion and anterior translation while native tissue heals.17

Biomechanical cadaveric analyses demonstrate that a Broström repair augmented with suture tape possesses structural stiffness, ultimate load-to-failure, and resistance to elongation equivalent or superior to the native, uninjured lateral ligament complex.41

Strict EBM Indications for Augmentation

Level I/II — Supported by multiple prospective RCTs and systematic meta-analyses.15

  1. High-Demand & Elite Athletes: Rapid, predictable return to play and maximal construct stiffness are career-critical.14
  2. Generalized Ligamentous Laxity: Patients with systemic hypermobility (high Beighton score, Ehlers-Danlos) with inherently poor collagen cross-linking; synthetic tape provides permanent non-biological constraint.14
  3. Revision Surgery: Failed primary Broström procedure lacking sufficient healthy tissue for standard imbrication.44
  4. Poor Native Tissue Quality: Intraoperative observation of heavily attenuated, friable, or insufficient ATFL/CFL remnants.47

7 Synthesized Clinical Algorithm

Consolidated, actionable algorithmic guidelines for clinical practice based on Level I–III evidence.

  1. Conservative Trial First: All patients with lateral ankle sprains undergo comprehensive non-operative management for 3–6 months (phased rehab, proprioceptive training, peroneal strengthening, bracing). Level II · Grade 2C
  2. Objective Assessment of Failure: If symptoms persist, obtain stress radiography. Surgical thresholds: ATT > 5.0 mm (or > 2.0 mm side-to-side) and/or Talar Tilt > 10–15° (or > 5° side-to-side). Concurrent MRI for concomitant pathology assessment. Level III
  3. Evaluate Concomitant Pathology:
    • OLT present: Concurrent treatment mandatory. Microfracture for lesions < 1.5 cm²; OATS/ACI/AMIC for larger lesions. Level II
    • Complete CFL tear + tissue attenuation: Abandon Broström repair → anatomic reconstruction with tendon autograft/allograft. Level II
  4. Procedure Selection:
    • General population: Open MBG or arthroscopic repair — both yield equivalent long-term outcomes. Level I
    • Arthroscopic preferred if surgeon proficiency is high — earlier functional recovery, superior early scores at 6 months. Level I
    • Elite athletes: Open/mini-open favored; mandatory consideration of suture tape augmentation. Level II
  5. Suture Tape Augmentation: Indicated for elite athletes, hyperlax patients, revision cases, and poor tissue quality. Reduces RTP from 17.5 to 13.3 weeks. Cost-effective long-term. Level II
  6. Postoperative Rehabilitation: Early ROM encouraged. Augmented repairs: protected weight-bearing in boot by weeks 3–6, dynamic functional testing by week 12, sport-specific drills thereafter. Level II
