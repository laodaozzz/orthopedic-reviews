Surgical Exposure & Wound Closure in Orthopedics

A Comprehensive Interactive Treatise for Resident Education

Introduction

The transition from a junior resident to a competent orthopedic surgeon is marked not merely by the ability to fix a fracture or replace a joint, but by the mastery of fundamental interactions between instrument and tissue. While the glamour of the profession often resides in the implant and the reconstruction, the success of any orthopedic procedure is inextricably bound to the quality of the surgical exposure and the integrity of the soft tissue closure.

Exposure is the gateway to pathology; without adequate visualization, diagnosis is compromised and technical execution is fraught with peril. Conversely, closure is the surgeon's signature — the final act that determines the biological fate of the reconstruction and the patient's perception of the outcome.

This report serves as an exhaustive guide to the techniques, ergonomics, instrument mechanics, and biological principles of surgical retraction and wound closure, synthesized from current literature, expert opinion, and biomechanical data.

Part I: Physiology & Biomechanics of Surgical Exposure

Surgical exposure is the art of making the internal external while maintaining the viability of the intervening structures. It is a contest between the surgeon's will to see and the body's elastic resistance. Understanding the physics of retraction and the physiology of tissue response is the first step toward safe surgery.

1.1 The Mechanics of Soft Tissue Retraction

Retraction operates on the principles of tension, compression, and shear. When a retractor is placed, it applies a compressive load to the tissue immediately beneath the blade and a tensile load to the tissue spanning the gap of the incision.

1.1.1 Stress Relaxation and Creep Level V — Expert Opinion [1]

Soft tissues are viscoelastic materials. When a constant deformation (retraction) is applied, the stress within the tissue decreases over time — this is known as stress relaxation. Conversely, creep is the tendency of tissue to slowly deform under a constant load.

Clinical Implication A common mistake is to apply maximum force immediately. This risks tearing the tissue or causing acute ischemia. The optimal technique is to apply firm, steady pressure and allow the tissue to relax (creep) into the desired position. This "gentle persuasion" is safer and more effective than sudden, violent pulling [1].

1.1.2 Ischemia and Reperfusion Level V — Expert Opinion [1, 2]

The pressure exerted by a retractor blade can exceed capillary perfusion pressure, leading to local ischemia. If maintained for prolonged periods, this causes cellular necrosis.

The "Tourniquet Effect" Just as a pneumatic tourniquet causes ischemia of the limb, a retractor acts as a focal tourniquet on the wound edge. In high-risk areas (e.g., skin flaps in trauma), this can lead to edge necrosis and dehiscence [2].

Mitigation Strategy: The concept of intermittent release is vital. During long cases, or when the surgeon is not actively working in a specific area, the assistant should relax retractor tension to allow a "washout" of metabolic byproducts and reperfusion of the tissue.

1.2 The Physics of Levers in Orthopedics Level V — Expert Opinion [1, 3]

Orthopedic retraction differs from general surgery because it utilizes bone as a fulcrum. Instruments like the Hohmann retractor function as Class 1 or Class 2 levers.

  • Class 1 Lever: The tip of the retractor hooks behind the bone (fulcrum), the hand applies force at the handle (effort), and the blade moves the soft tissue (load). The mechanical advantage is determined by the ratio of handle length to blade length.
  • The Fulcrum Effect: A critical error is failing to maintain the fulcrum. If the tip slips off the bone, the lever arm is lost and the retractor becomes a simple pulling instrument — far less efficient and far more fatiguing [1].

Part II: The Surgical Athlete — Ergonomics & Fatigue Management

Surgical fatigue is a pervasive but often ignored reality in orthopedic training. Junior residents frequently suffer from musculoskeletal pain (MSP) due to poor posture, static loading, and improper body mechanics [4, 5]. Fatigue is not just a comfort issue — it is a safety issue. A fatigued resident is more likely to let a retractor slip, lose visualization, or cause iatrogenic injury.

2.1 The Biomechanics of the Hold

The most efficient way to hold a retractor maximizes skeletal stability and minimizes muscular effort. The goal is to transfer the load from the small, fatigable muscles of the forearm and shoulder to the large, fatigue-resistant muscles of the core and legs.

2.1.1 Proximal Stabilization Level V — Expert Opinion [1]

  • Elbow Adduction: The resident must keep the elbow of the retracting arm tucked firmly against the lateral chest wall. This engages the latissimus dorsi and stabilizes the shoulder girdle. When the elbow "wings" out (abducts), the deltoid must support the entire weight of the arm plus the retraction load, leading to rapid fatigue.
  • "Power Grip" vs. "Pencil Grip":
    • Power Grip: Used for heavy retraction (e.g., Richardson, large Hohmann). The handle is grasped with the full fist, wrist neutral. Force is generated by leaning back with the body, not by flexing the biceps.
    • Pencil Grip: Used for delicate instruments (e.g., Ferreira, skin hooks). The instrument is balanced between the thumb and first two fingers. Allows fine manipulation but generates less force.

2.1.2 Stance and Weight Transfer Level V — Expert Opinion [1]

The feet are the foundation of retraction. A narrow stance is unstable and forces the upper body to compensate.

  • Athletic Stance: Feet shoulder-width apart, knees slightly bent.
  • Using Body Weight: Instead of pulling the retractor toward oneself using arm muscles, the resident should lock the arm in position and lean backward or away from the field. This utilizes the resident's body mass (gravity) to generate retractive force — essentially making the retraction "free" in terms of muscular energy expenditure [1].

2.2 Active Recovery: Microbreaks and Stretching Level III — Multicenter Survey [5]; Expert Recommendations [6–9]

Static postures restrict blood flow to muscles, leading to lactic acid accumulation and fatigue. Recent research advocates for "microbreaks" — short pauses (1.5 to 2 minutes) every 30–120 minutes during surgery to perform targeted stretching [6].

2.3 Operating Room Setup and Environment Level III — Survey [5]; Level V — Recommendations [4, 9]

  • Table Height: The operating table should be at a height where the surgeon's and assistant's elbows are at approximately 90° or slightly lower. A table too high forces shoulder abduction; too low forces lumbar flexion [4].
  • Monitor Placement: In arthroscopy or fluoroscopy-guided cases, monitors must be placed directly in the line of sight. Looking sideways for hours causes cervical rotation strain. The resident should proactively adjust monitor position before the case starts [4].

Orthopedic surgery utilizes a vast array of retractors, each designed for a specific anatomical niche. The resident must not only know their names but understand their mechanics, optimal handling, and potential dangers.

3.2 Self-Retaining Retractors

These instruments lock into place, freeing the assistant's hands. However, their static nature makes them dangerous if placed incorrectly or ignored.

  • Gelpi Retractor: Two sharp, single prongs that distract when handles are closed. Used for deep wounds, spine, fracture fixation. Risk: Never place blindly near a nerve (e.g., posterior hip near sciatic nerve) [22].
  • Weitlaner Retractor: Multiple prongs (sharp or blunt) on a ratcheted frame. Place prongs closed, then open gently. Ensure skin edges are not crushed against metal arms. Angling slightly downward keeps a low profile [1].
  • Charnley Retractor: Frame-based system specific to hip arthroplasty using a square frame to hold two or more blades. Provides circumferential hip wound retraction. Complication: Femoral nerve palsy if anterior blade compresses psoas too long; sciatic neurapraxia from posterior blade [24].
  • Table-Mounted Systems (Thompson, Omni-Tract): Used for major trauma (pelvic fractures) or anterior spine. Require complex assembly. The resident must anticipate the need and ensure all sterile joints are tight — a loose joint causes frame collapse mid-surgery [14].

Part IV: Anatomic Specifics — Regional Retraction Strategies

Different anatomical regions present unique challenges and "danger zones" for retraction.

4.1 The Hip: Posterior Approach Level V — Expert/Anatomy [24–26]

The most common approach for arthroplasty, fraught with sciatic nerve risk.

  • Danger Zone: The sciatic nerve runs vertically, posterior to the short external rotators (piriformis, obturator internus).
  • Retraction Strategy: A Charnley or self-retainer is often used. The posterior blade must be placed carefully. The best way to protect the nerve is to stay "on bone" — when placing a posterior retractor (e.g., Hohmann behind the posterior acetabular rim), the tip must maintain contact with bone to ensure it is deep to the nerve [25].
  • Dynamic Safety: The nerve moves. In extension, it falls away; in flexion, it tightens. Avoid extreme retraction when the hip is flexed [26].
Common Error Placing a sharp Hohmann blindly into the posterior soft tissues can skewer the sciatic nerve. Always maintain bony contact [25].

4.2 The Hip: Direct Anterior Approach (DAA) Level V — Surgical Technique [13, 14]

Increasingly popular, utilizing an internervous plane (femoral vs. superior gluteal nerves).

  • Femoral Neck Exposure: Requires curved Hohmanns or Cobras placed superiorly and inferiorly around the neck [13].
  • Lateral Femoral Cutaneous Nerve (LFCN): The superficial incision puts this sensory nerve at risk. Retractors on the superficial fascia should be blunt and gentle [13].
  • Table-Less vs. Table: In table-less DAA, the resident plays a massive role in manipulating the leg (figure-of-four position) to aid exposure [13].

4.3 The Knee: Total Knee Arthroplasty Level V — Expert Opinion [28, 56]

  • Relies on eversion of the patella (or subluxation) and mobilization of the tibia.
  • Collateral Ligament Safety: When retracting the medial or lateral gutters, care must be taken not to place excessive torque on MCL/LCL. A bent Hohmann is often placed inside the capsule to protect the MCL during sawing [28].
  • Extensor Mechanism: Vigorous retraction of the patellar tendon can cause avulsion from the tibial tubercle — a devastating complication.

4.4 The Shoulder: Deltopectoral Approach Level V — Expert/Anatomy [17]

  • Cephalic Vein: The internervous plane between deltoid and pectoralis major, marked by the cephalic vein.
  • Retraction: Richardson or specialized Fukuda retractor holds the deltoid [17].
  • Musculocutaneous Nerve: The conjoined tendon must be retracted medially, but the musculocutaneous nerve enters these muscles 5–8 cm distal to the coracoid. Excessive medial retraction causes traction palsy [17].

Part V: The Biology of Tissue Handling

The instrument is only as good as the hands that hold it. "Rough handling" is not a subjective complaint — it is a biological event characterized by crush injury, endothelial damage, and inflammatory cascade activation.

5.1 Forceps Technique: The Paradox of Teeth Level V — Expert Opinion [30, 31]

  • Toothed Forceps (Adson, Bonney): Fine teeth (1×2 or 2×3) penetrate at focal points, providing a secure hold with minimal closing pressure. Tissue between the teeth is spared [30].
  • Smooth/Dressing Forceps: Rely on friction. To hold slippery fascia, the user must squeeze tightly — crushing tissue between the jaws, causing widespread cellular necrosis [31].
Rule: Use toothed forceps for skin and fascia. Use smooth forceps only for delicate viscera or nerves [30].

5.2 The "No-Touch" Technique Level V — Expert Opinion [30]

The skin edge is the source of epidermal regeneration. Every time forceps pinch the skin edge, they kill a zone of cells. When suturing or retracting, grasp the subcutaneous fat or deep dermis rather than the epidermal edge. Alternatively, use a skin hook to lift the edge from the inside — preserving blood supply to the healing margin [30].

5.3 Dead Space Management Level V — Expert/Consensus [33, 34]

Orthopedic wounds often involve large voids left by removed bone or retracted muscle.

Why it matters: Dead space fills with hematoma. Hematoma is an ideal culture medium for bacteria, leading to surgical site infection (SSI) [34].
  • Layered Closure: Suturing separate anatomical planes (capsule, deep fascia, superficial fascia, fat) collapses the void [33].
  • Drains: Used selectively in high-output wounds (e.g., revision THA), though evidence is mixed.
  • Quilting Sutures: Placing sutures that grab underlying muscle and tack the skin flap down — used in large degloving injuries [33].

6.2 Staples vs. Sutures: The Debate Level I — Meta-analysis [36]; Level III — Prospective Audit [37]

  • Staples:
    • Pros: Fast, allow blood drainage between staples (preventing hematoma), low tissue reactivity.
    • Cons: "Railroad track" scarring, higher risk of superficial infection in hip surgery (meta-analysis: 3× risk vs. sutures) [36].
    • Indication: Often used in knees where ROM puts high tension, or in trauma where speed is critical.
  • Sutures (Subcuticular):
    • Pros: Superior cosmesis, watertight seal (keeps bacteria out), no removal needed.
    • Cons: Slower, can seal in hematoma, potential for "spitting" stitches if knots not buried deep [29, 37].

Part VII: Suturing Techniques & Knot Mastery

The mechanical integrity of the closure relies on the geometry of the stitch and the security of the knot.

7.1 The Vertical Mattress Suture Level V — Expert Technique [38–40]

This is the "orthopedic workhorse" for difficult, high-tension wounds (e.g., over the olecranon or patella). It provides both deep tension relief and superficial edge eversion [38].

Technique (Far-Far, Near-Near):
  1. Far: Enter skin 1 cm from edge, pass deep. Exit 1 cm from opposite edge.
  2. Near: Reverse needle (backhand). Enter 2 mm from edge, stay superficial (dermal). Exit 2 mm from original edge.
  3. Result: The "Far" loop creates a pulley system to close the gap. The "Near" loop flips edges up (eversion), maximizing dermal layer contact.

7.2 The Running Subcuticular Stitch Level V — Expert Technique [41, 42]

The hallmark of elective arthroplasty closure. A continuous line of Monocryl running horizontally through the dermis, just below the epidermal junction.

  • Pitfall: Taking unequal bites (distance from the apex) on opposing sides leads to "dog ears" or puckering at the end of the wound.
  • Burying the Knot: Use the "Deep-to-Superficial / Superficial-to-Deep" technique. The knot is tied deep in the wound, and the tail is cut short. The loop is then advanced [41, 42].

7.3 The Aberdeen Knot Level II — Cadaveric/Biomechanical Study [45]; Level V — Technique [43, 44]

Ending a running suture line is notoriously difficult. A simple square knot creates bulk that can cause wound necrosis or palpable irritation. The Aberdeen knot (hand-tied) is the superior solution [43–45].

Mechanism: Utilizes the loop of the last stitch. The surgeon reaches through the loop with fingers, grabs the suture tail, and pulls a new loop through. Repeated 3–4 times (like a crochet chain). On the final throw, the free end is pulled entirely through the loop to lock it. The needle is then driven deep into tissue and out through skin 2 cm away. Elastic recoil pulls the tail underground [44].

7.4 Knot Tying Fundamentals Level V — Expert Instruction [46, 47]

  • Square vs. Granny: A square knot (threads exit on same side of loop) locks under tension. A granny knot (threads exit on opposite sides) slips. Residents must consciously ensure throws are square (flat) [46].
  • Surgeon's Knot: A double throw on the first loop increases friction to hold tension while the second throw is placed. Essential for closing fascia under tension [47].

Part VIII: Managing Complications & Troubleshooting

Even with perfect technique, complications arise. The resident must be equipped to handle the "difficult wound."

8.1 The High-Tension Wound Level V — Expert Technique [48–51]

  • Undermining: Use Metzenbaum scissors to separate subcutaneous fat from deep fascia for 2–3 cm on either side. This recruits elasticity from the lateral skin [48].
  • Pie-Crusting: In extreme swelling (e.g., compartment syndrome closure), make multiple small (2 mm) stab incisions parallel to the wound edge. This meshes the skin, allowing it to stretch and preventing edge necrosis. Leaves small scars but saves the primary wound [50, 51].

8.2 The "Dog Ear" Level V — Expert Technique [52]

A pucker of excess skin at the end of a closure. Do not just sew over it. Extend the incision slightly to lay it flat, or use a "halving" technique (suture the middle of the remaining defect, then the middle of the new defects) to distribute excess length [52].

8.3 Stitch Abscess / Spitting Suture Level V — Expert Technique [38]

Caused by a knot placed too superficially or a reaction to Vicryl.

Prevention: Use the "inverted pyramid" technique for buried knots (wide at base, narrow at top) to force the knot downward. Ensure all Vicryl knots are covered by a distinct fascial or subcutaneous layer [38].

Conclusion

The journey to surgical expertise is long, but it is built on the foundation of exposure and closure. A resident who can provide excellent visualization through ergonomic, intelligent retraction is an asset to any team and learns more by seeing more. A resident who can close a wound with precision, respecting the biology of the tissue, saves the patient from the devastating complications of infection and dehiscence.

Mastery of these "basic" skills requires deliberate practice. It demands that the resident treats the placement of a Hohmann retractor with the same seriousness as the placement of a pedicle screw. It requires the discipline to perform an Aberdeen knot correctly every time, not just when the attending is watching. It requires the physical conditioning to maintain stability when fatigued.

By integrating the techniques, biomechanics, and philosophies outlined in this report — from the pencil grip of the skin hook to the far-far/near-near mechanics of the vertical mattress — the junior resident will not only survive their training but will thrive, ultimately providing the highest standard of care to their patients.
