1. Introduction & Burden of SSIs and PJIs

Total joint arthroplasty (TJA) and complex spinal reconstruction stand among the most transformative interventions in modern medicine. However, the success of these procedures is continually threatened by surgical site infections (SSIs) and periprosthetic joint infections (PJIs). As the annual volume of primary and revision arthroplasty procedures is projected to rise exponentially, the absolute incidence of SSIs and PJIs is expected to experience a commensurate surge. STRONG CONSENSUS

The occurrence of a PJI precipitates catastrophic physical and psychological morbidity for the patient, often necessitating multiple revision surgeries, explantation of hardware, prolonged intravenous antimicrobial therapy, and extensive rehabilitation. The financial burden imposed on healthcare infrastructure by a single infected arthroplasty adds tens of thousands of dollars in treatment costs compared to aseptic outcomes.

🔑 Core Imperative

The prevention of SSIs and PJIs has been universally recognized as the single most critical imperative in orthopedic surgical practice. STRONG CONSENSUS

While systemic antimicrobial prophylaxis and preoperative host optimization serve as foundational pillars, the intraoperative management of the sterile field remains the primary defense against exogenous bacterial inoculation.

The contamination of the surgical site is fundamentally a probabilistic event driven by three key factors:

  • Density of airborne particulate matter — microbial sedimentation on exposed instruments
  • Duration of tissue and implant exposure — time-dependent contamination curves
  • Mechanical integrity of sterile barriers — drape integrity, glove perforations, strike-through

2. Preoperative Host Optimization

The patient represents the primary reservoir for endogenous contamination. The ICM and AORN place immense emphasis on preoperative risk stratification and bioburden reduction. STRONG CONSENSUS

2.1 Dermal Bioburden Reduction

The human dermis and mucosal surfaces harbor trillions of microorganisms, including Staphylococcus aureus and coagulase-negative staphylococci, the most common etiologic agents in implant-related infections. LEVEL I

Current guidelines mandate:

  • Preoperative patient bathing with antiseptic soap (e.g., chlorhexidine gluconate) in the days leading up to surgery
  • Intraoperative skin preparation utilizing an alcohol-based antiseptic agent STRONG CONSENSUS
  • Mechanical application must begin at the proposed incision site and move concentrically toward the periphery to prevent dragging peripheral flora into the wound STRONG CONSENSUS

2.2 Metabolic Optimization

Preoperative hyperglycemia is increasingly prevalent, with evidence indicating that over 30% of seemingly healthy patients undergoing TJA possess undiagnosed glycemic dysfunction.

  • Elective arthroplasty should be delayed in patients with HbA1c > 7% or unmanaged preoperative anemia STRONG CONSENSUS
  • Fructosamine may serve as a superior biomarker to HbA1c in predicting adverse infectious outcomes, given its ability to reflect short-term glycemic control LEVEL II
Critical Threshold: HbA1c > 7% is the consensus-mandated cutoff for delaying elective arthroplasty. Ensure comprehensive preoperative glycemic screening for all elective cases.

4. Tray Exposure & Airborne Contamination Dynamics

4.1 Operative Duration and Infection Risk

Protracted operative time is a universally acknowledged, independent risk factor for postoperative SSIs and PJIs. The pathophysiology linking prolonged duration to infection risk is multifactorial:

  • Progressive desiccation of exposed soft tissues
  • Depletion of prophylactic antimicrobial tissue concentrations below minimum inhibitory thresholds
  • Deleterious effects of prolonged pneumatic tourniquet use leading to local tissue hypoxia
  • Continuous accumulation of airborne pathogens on exposed instruments
Landmark Meta-Analysis (17 studies, 427,361 patients) LEVEL I/II:
  • Surgeries > 90 minutes: Pooled OR = 1.50 (p < 0.001) for PJI
  • Surgeries > 120 minutes: Pooled OR = 1.56 (p < 0.001) for PJI
  • TKAs specifically: SSI risk increases substantially past the 2-hour mark (OR 1.50, p = 0.001)
  • THAs: Did not demonstrate as sharp a statistical divergence at 120 minutes (OR 1.06, p = 0.44)

The ICM on PJI quantifies the escalating risk:

  • 13% increased SSI risk per additional 15 minutes of surgery
  • 17% increased SSI risk per additional 30 minutes
  • 37% increased SSI risk per additional 60 minutes

STRONG CONSENSUS

4.2 Time-Dependent Contamination of Sterile Back Tables

The "open-time" of sterile surgical instrument trays prior to incision represents a critical, yet frequently mismanaged, variable. The back table serves as a primary repository for airborne microbial sedimentation. The assumption that an instrument remains sterile simply because it rests within the sterile field is a dangerous clinical fallacy.

Controlled Prospective Trial (Level II Evidence):
Airborne bacterial sedimentation acts as a direct mathematical function of time. Contamination rates for uncovered trays:
30 min → 4%  |  60 min → 15%  |  120 min → 22%  |  180 min → 26%  |  240 min → 30%

Critical Finding: Standard OR traffic (door openings every 10 min) did not yield a statistically significant difference vs. zero-traffic environments (p > 0.05). Baseline ambient air exposure duration is the predominant determinant.

🛡️ Protective Intervention

Covering prepared sterile back tables with sterile surgical drapes completely arrests the time-dependent contamination curve (0% at all time points vs. 30% for uncovered at 4 hours). LEVEL II

4.3 Economics of Tray Contamination

Contamination of a surgical tray mid-procedure introduces severe logistical and economic burden:

  • Average OR delay per contamination event: 15.35 minutes
  • OR time value: ~$34.00/minute
  • Total cost per contaminated tray: >$500 in wasted operative time + ~$50 reprocessing cost LEVEL III
Financial Imperative: Optimizing tray open-times and strictly enforcing sterile covering protocols is not merely a clinical best practice—it is an economic necessity for healthcare institutions.

6. Orthopedic Implant Handling & Double Sterile Barrier

6.1 The Double Sterile Barrier (DSB) System

The introduction of definitive total joint components, spinal pedicle screws, and biologic allografts to the sterile field requires flawless coordination. Regulatory frameworks mandate that medical device packaging must facilitate "aseptic presentation" — the transfer of contents using conditions and procedures that absolutely minimize the risk of microbial contamination. STRONG CONSENSUS

The DSB system (nested paper-plastic peel pouches or rigid thermoformed blister trays) allows the circulating nurse to manipulate the unsterile exterior while exposing the sterile interior for extraction by the scrubbed team.

6.2 Opening & Handling Protocols

  • Pre-opening inspection: Verify intact seals, absence of moisture or punctures, correct color change of chemical indicators STRONG CONSENSUS
  • Peel mechanics: Pull flaps back symmetrically; prevent item from sliding over unsterile edges; fingers must not touch internal sterile boundaries STRONG CONSENSUS
  • Unscrubbed persons must never extend arms or hands over the sterile field STRONG CONSENSUS

6.3 Transfer Techniques

Contamination Rule: If any sterile item or its inner packaging inadvertently touches the sealed edge of the outer pouch, or if any portion falls below the horizontal plane of the sterile field (table edge), the item must instantly be declared contaminated and discarded. STRONG CONSENSUS

6.4 Spinal Implant Contamination

In complex spinal surgery, large volumes of pedicle screws, rods, interbody cages, and K-wires are frequently arrayed on the back table well in advance. Postoperative spinal infections lead to devastating morbidity, with permanent hardware providing an ideal substrate for bacterial biofilm formation.

  • Bacterial growth detected on uncovered hardware within 30 minutes of opening LEVEL II
  • Overall implant contamination rate: 9.5%
  • Covered implants: 2.0% contamination rate
  • Uncovered implants: 16.7% contamination rate (p < 0.05) LEVEL II

7. Sharp Instrument Management & Hands-Free Technique

7.1 Movement Minimization

AORN guidelines mandate:

  • Minimize the number of personnel in the OR
  • Keep doors closed to prevent positive pressure loss
  • Strictly limit nonessential movement around the sterile field STRONG CONSENSUS

Rapid movements generate micro-currents that can draw aerosolized bacteria under the protective LAF curtain onto the sterile back table.

7.2 The Hands-Free Technique (HFT)

The HFT (neutral zone passing) dictates that no two surgical team members touch the same sharp item simultaneously. Instruments are placed in a designated receptacle or neutral zone on the surgical field.

When HFT is utilized for ≥ 75% of intraoperative passes:
59% reduction in percutaneous injuries, glove tears, and subsequent cross-contaminations, particularly in procedures with substantial blood loss. LEVEL II

7.3 Glove Perforation Prevention

Glove perforations are intimately tied to infection control, as the warm, moist environment inside a surgical glove fosters exponential proliferation of resident skin flora.

  • Glove perforations are highly time-dependent, occurring frequently after 90–134 minutes of operative time
  • Perforations overwhelmingly occur in the nondominant hand, targeting the index finger and thumb
  • Double-gloving with colored perforation indicators is universally recommended for immediate detection of barrier breaches LEVEL II
  • Surgeons should preemptively change gloves after high-risk activities: use of reamers, oscillating saws, manipulation of sharp bony fragments LEVEL II

8. Fluid Dispensing & Strike-Through Prevention

8.1 Pathophysiology of Strike-Through

Strike-through contamination occurs when fluid penetrates a sterile surgical drape, creating a continuous liquid bridge between an unsterile surface below and the sterile field above. Through capillary action, resident skin flora or environmental pathogens are rapidly drawn upwards through the saturated drape matrix.

If strike-through occurs or a drape lifts, the risk of transferring microbes to the surgical site rises up to six-fold. LEVEL II

8.2 Strike-Through Management Protocol

  • Use low-linting, highly impervious surgical drapes that resist wet bacterial penetration under sustained pressure (BS EN 13795-1) STRONG CONSENSUS
  • Drapes should channel fluids into designated fluid-catch pouches
  • If strike-through is identified: place a new, sterile, impervious drape over the compromised area immediately
  • Do NOT remove the contaminated drape — removal risks aerosolizing trapped pathogens, spreading contaminated lint, and dragging soiled undersurface across the sterile field STRONG CONSENSUS

8.3 Sterile Transfer Device Protocol

The traditional practice of pouring saline from decapped bottles is heavily condemned. Pouring generates unpredictable fluid dynamics, splashing, droplet generation, and fluid dripping down the unsterile exterior of the bottle onto the sterile field.

🔧 AORN Mandated Protocol

  • Use a sterile transfer device (decanter, sterile pouring spout, or spiked dispensing pin) for all fluid transfers STRONG CONSENSUS
  • Wipe access port/stopper with surgical-grade alcohol antiseptic and allow complete drying before puncturing
  • Never remove rubber stoppers from medication containers to facilitate pouring STRONG CONSENSUS
  • Transfer one medication/fluid at a time with concurrent verbal verification of name, strength, dosage, and expiration

  • Dilute povidone-iodine (1% solution) or chlorhexidine combined with precise 4–15 psi mechanical lavage provides an optimized biochemical and biomechanical approach LEVEL II

10. Splash Basin Hazards

Open fluid reservoirs kept on the back table exhibit exceptionally high rates of microbial colonization during prolonged surgeries.

Bacterial contamination rates in splash basins: 24.1% to 74.4% in randomly selected orthopedic procedures, often featuring polymicrobial signals with counts exceeding significant thresholds. LEVEL II

AORN guidelines strictly prohibit the use of open basin water for intraoperative soaking of instruments that will be subsequently reintroduced to the surgical wound. Any fluid for wiping or storing contaminated instruments must be:

  • Completely segregated from the primary sterile field
  • Placed in a separate ring stand
  • Never used for direct patient irrigation or flushing of lumens STRONG CONSENSUS
