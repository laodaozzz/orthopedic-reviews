1. Paradigm Shift: From Fixation to Replacement

The treatment of complex periarticular and intra-articular fractures has undergone a significant evolution, particularly in the context of an aging population with increasing prevalence of osteoporosis. While open reduction and internal fixation (ORIF) remains the mainstay for many fractures, its limitations in certain patient populations and fracture patterns have driven a shift toward primary arthroplasty.[1]

Historically, the primary goal of fracture surgery was anatomic reduction and stable internal fixation to promote biological healing. While highly effective in young patients with good bone quality, this approach has demonstrated unacceptably high failure rates in elderly patients.[1] The inherent challenge of achieving durable fixation in osteoporotic bone, coupled with impaired healing potential, frequently leads to:

  • Fixation failure and screw cutout
  • Varus collapse
  • Non-union
  • Post-traumatic avascular necrosis (AVN)[2]

Primary arthroplasty emerged as a definitive solution for these fractures. The fundamental goal is not merely to treat the fracture but to restore limb function as quickly and reliably as possible. By replacing the fractured (often devascularised) joint segment with a prosthesis, surgeons can circumvent the uncertainty of biological healing. This immediate mechanical stability enables patients to begin rehabilitation almost immediately, mitigating serious systemic complications of prolonged bedrest in the elderly, including venous thromboembolism (VTE), pneumonia, pressure ulcers, and cognitive decline.[1]

Core Principle: The shift from "joint preservation" to "function preservation" acknowledges that for a select group of patients, the most effective way to maintain quality of life and independence is to replace an unsalvageable joint.

2. Patient-Centered Decision Making

The decision between ORIF and arthroplasty is based not merely on fracture pattern but on a comprehensive assessment of the patient's unique physiological and functional status.[2]

2.1 Patient Age and Physiological Status

Chronological age is important, but physiological age and functional status are more critical determinants. Arthroplasty is generally more appropriate for older, lower-demand patients for whom predictable, rapid recovery is paramount.[2] Conversely, for young, active patients, every effort should be made to preserve the native joint via ORIF, as prosthesis longevity is a major concern and revision arthroplasty is more complex than the index procedure.[5]

Clinical Pearl: A physiologically young and active 75-year-old may be a better candidate for ORIF of a proximal humerus fracture than a frail, comorbid 60-year-old.

2.2 Bone Quality

Osteoporosis is the primary factor driving the adoption of arthroplasty. Poor bone quality profoundly compromises screw purchase and the stability of ORIF constructs. The cancellous bone of the humeral head, femoral head, and tibial plateau becomes particularly fragile, increasing the risk of fixation failure, implant migration, and joint subsidence under physiological loading.[1] Arthroplasty addresses this by anchoring the prosthesis in the denser cortical bone of the diaphysis (via press-fit or cemented fixation), providing a more durable foundation for immediate mobilisation.[10]

2.3 Pre-existing Joint Disease

Symptomatic pre-existing joint disease is one of the strongest indications for primary arthroplasty after fracture. Performing complex ORIF on a joint already affected by end-stage osteoarthritis or inflammatory arthritis (e.g., rheumatoid arthritis) is a fundamentally flawed strategy.[1] Even if the fracture heals, the patient is left with a painful, stiff joint due to underlying arthropathy. A single definitive surgery that addresses both the fracture and arthritis simultaneously is more efficient, cost-effective, and provides superior pain relief and functional restoration.[1][13]

2.4 Functional Demand and Comorbidities

The patient's pre-injury functional level and comorbidities significantly impact treatment decisions. Functionally independent patients may accept the prolonged rehabilitation and potential risks of ORIF to preserve their native joint.[15] For frail patients with multiple comorbidities or low functional demands (e.g., non-ambulatory or household ambulator), the reliability of arthroplasty, shorter operative time, and rapid recovery offer substantial advantages.[6]

3. Fracture-Specific Indications for Arthroplasty

3.1 Articular Surface Integrity

When the degree of articular comminution exceeds the possibility of anatomic reconstruction, arthroplasty becomes the only viable option to restore a smooth, congruent articular surface. This includes severe articular split fractures, head-split components (humeral or femoral), and severe osteochondral impaction with bone loss.[1] Attempting to piece together multiple small, devascularised cartilage fragments is fraught with risk of post-traumatic arthritis, stiffness, and pain.

3.2 Metaphyseal Comminution and Bone Loss

Extensive metaphyseal comminution, particularly when it results in loss of key structural buttress support, severely compromises ORIF construct stability. For proximal humerus and proximal femur fractures, integrity of the medial calcar is critical for resisting varus forces. Loss of medial support in osteoporotic patients almost invariably leads to varus collapse of the fixation construct.[14]

3.3 Vascular Supply and AVN Risk

Certain fracture patterns disrupt the tenuous blood supply to the joint segment, creating a high risk of post-traumatic AVN and subsequent articular surface collapse:

  • Femoral neck: Displaced intracapsular fractures (Garden III & IV) almost invariably damage the retinacular arteries, making arthroplasty the standard of care in the elderly.[4]
  • Proximal humerus: Complex 3- and 4-part fractures and fracture-dislocations can devascularise the humeral head. The Hertel criteria (calcar length <8 mm, medial hinge disruption, complex fracture type) help predict humeral head ischemia.[16]

4. Upper Extremity Arthroplasty

4.1 Proximal Humerus: Hemiarthroplasty vs. Reverse Shoulder Arthroplasty

Hemiarthroplasty (HHR)

The traditional arthroplasty option, involving replacement of only the humeral head. Best suited for younger, active patients (typically 65–70 years) with a healthy, functional rotator cuff and tuberosity fragments of sufficient quality for repair, but with irreconstructable head-split fractures or fracture-dislocations.[5] HHR fundamentally requires a functional rotator cuff to power the shoulder and stabilise the prosthetic head on the native glenoid. Tuberosity healing failure is the most common cause of poor outcomes after HHR for fracture.[2]

Reverse Shoulder Arthroplasty (RSA)

RSA has become the primary treatment for complex proximal humerus fractures in the elderly population (generally >65–70 years).[15] Primary indications include irreparable or dysfunctional rotator cuff, or tuberosities that are severely comminuted or osteoporotic and cannot heal reliably.[2]

The RSA reverses the joint polarity — placing a sphere (glenosphere) on the glenoid and a cup (humeral socket) on the humerus. This biomechanical shift medialises and inferiorises the centre of rotation, increasing the deltoid lever arm and making the deltoid the primary elevator of the arm, thereby bypassing the need for a functional rotator cuff.[29]

Evidence Summary: Meta-analyses consistently show that for displaced proximal humerus fractures, ORIF provides superior clinical outcomes and range of motion compared to HHR.[30] However, in the elderly, RSA demonstrates superior outcomes over HHR and comparable or superior outcomes to ORIF, with better patient-reported outcome scores and greater active forward elevation.[15] Level I

Key Surgical Techniques

  • Approach: Deltopectoral approach with patient in beach-chair position.[27]
  • Tuberosity management: Heavy non-absorbable sutures placed at bone-tendon junction of each tuberosity fragment. Head fragment used as autograft. Tuberosities repaired ~8 mm below prosthetic head apex to avoid subacromial impingement.[16][27]
  • Humeral height: Prosthetic head top should be ~5.6 cm proximal to the upper border of the pectoralis major tendon insertion.[16]
  • Version: 20–30° of retroversion relative to forearm axis.[27]

4.2 Distal Humerus: Total Elbow Arthroplasty (TEA)

The primary indication for TEA in acute trauma is severely comminuted intra-articular distal humerus fractures (AO/OTA 13-C3) in low-demand elderly patients with poor bone quality.[6] TEA is also strongly indicated for patients with pre-existing inflammatory disease (e.g., rheumatoid arthritis).[12]

Contraindication: TEA is contraindicated in young, active patients or those unable to comply with lifelong activity restrictions (permanent weight-lifting limit of 5–10 lbs) due to concerns about long-term implant survivorship and aseptic loosening from repetitive loading.[6]

Meta-analysis comparing TEA vs. ORIF in patients >65 years shows TEA significantly reduces reoperation rates and improves arc of motion, with comparable patient-reported functional scores.[39] Level II

5. Lower Extremity Arthroplasty

5.1 Femoral Neck: Hemiarthroplasty vs. Total Hip Arthroplasty (THA)

Displaced intracapsular femoral neck fractures (Garden III & IV) in the elderly are the quintessential indication for fracture arthroplasty. The high likelihood of retinacular blood supply disruption leads to unacceptable rates of AVN and non-union with internal fixation.[4]

Key Technical Principles

  • Acetabular component: ~45° abduction, 15–20° anteversion.[23]
  • Femoral component: ~15° anteversion relative to femoral condyles.[23]
  • Fixation: Cemented femoral fixation preferred in elderly osteoporotic bone — provides immediate rigid fixation and reduces periprosthetic fracture risk vs. press-fit.[10]
  • Stability assessment: Intraoperative trial of full ROM to check for impingement or subluxation before final closure.[23]

5.2 Knee: Primary TKA for Distal Femur and Tibial Plateau Fractures

Primary TKA is an increasingly recognised treatment for specific complex fractures. The ideal candidate is an elderly, osteoporotic patient with severely comminuted intra-articular distal femoral (AO/OTA 33-C) or proximal tibial (AO/OTA 41-C) fractures who also has symptomatic end-stage knee osteoarthritis.[1]

Technical Considerations

  • Implant constraint: Fracture patterns disrupting collateral ligaments require constrained implants (CCK or rotating hinge). Standard PS implants may suffice if ligaments are intact.[1]
  • Stems and augments: Femoral and tibial stems almost always required to bypass comminuted metaphyseal bone. Modular metal augments fill bone defects and restore joint line.[9]
  • Auxiliary fixation: Cerclage wires or plate-and-screw constructs may be needed to secure fracture fragments before final component seating.[44]

6. Periprosthetic Fractures: The Intersection of Trauma and Revision

Periprosthetic fractures (PPF) are among the most complex challenges in orthopaedic surgery. Successful treatment requires the surgeon to possess both trauma and revision arthroplasty skill sets. Management revolves around two critical questions:[22]

  1. Is the implant stable or loose? — If stable, ORIF around the existing implant. If loose, revision arthroplasty is required.
  2. What is the quality and quantity of remaining bone? — Determines fixation feasibility and reconstruction complexity.

Key Concept: A B1 fracture is essentially a trauma problem — the implant is stable, so focus on fixing bone around it. However, B2/B3 fractures are combined trauma-revision problems. ORIF alone for a B2 fracture will fail because the loose stem's micromotion prevents fracture healing. The solution must be integrated: revise the loose stem to a long, stable stem and fix fracture fragments to provide a stable environment for healing.

6.3 Shoulder Periprosthetic Fractures: Wright & Cofield Classification

  • Type A: Centred at stem tip, extends proximally.
  • Type B: Centred at stem tip, extends distally.
  • Type C: Entirely distal to stem tip.

Treatment follows the same core principles. Type C fractures can be treated with ORIF as standard humeral shaft fractures. For Types A and B with a stable stem, non-operative treatment (functional bracing) may be considered for long, oblique/spiral patterns, though reported union rates are as low as 50%.[22] Transverse or short oblique fractures typically require ORIF. If the stem is loose, revision arthroplasty with a long-stem component is indicated regardless of fracture type.[22]

7. Complications: Mitigation & Management

7.1 Periprosthetic Joint Infection (PJI)

PJI is one of the most devastating complications, with rates of ~1% after primary THA and 0.4–2.0% after TKA, but even higher in the trauma setting.[65]

Prevention (Multimodal Strategy)

  • Preoperative: Screening and decolonisation of S. aureus carriers; aggressive glucose control in diabetics (target <11.1 mmol/L); smoking cessation; nutritional optimisation.[67]
  • Perioperative antibiotics: Within 1 hour of incision, at least 5 minutes before tourniquet inflation. Limit to 24 hours postoperatively.[67]
  • Intraoperative: Laminar flow theatres; minimised traffic; chlorhexidine-alcohol skin prep; dual gloves; normothermia; normovolaemia.[67]

Management

Confirmed PJI typically requires two-stage exchange arthroplasty: (1) removal of all components and cement, thorough debridement, placement of antibiotic-loaded cement spacer, followed by targeted IV antibiotics (~6 weeks); (2) reimplantation of new revision components once infection is eradicated.[65][68]

7.2 Instability and Dislocation

Dislocation rates after fracture THA can be as high as 10%, significantly exceeding elective rates.[10]

  • Prevention in THA: Precise component positioning; robust posterior soft-tissue repair; larger femoral heads (36 mm or dual-mobility).[23]
  • Prevention in RSA: Correct glenosphere placement (slight inferior tilt); restoration of humeral length and rotation; adequate deltoid tension.[69]

7.3 Aseptic Loosening, Osteolysis, and Implant Wear

Aseptic loosening is the most common cause of late implant failure. Modern highly cross-linked polyethylene (HXLPE) bearing surfaces dramatically reduce polyethylene wear particle generation and the subsequent osteolytic cascade.[68] For osteoporotic bone, meticulous cement technique is critical for lasting fixation.[10]

7.4 Non-union and Malunion

Although arthroplasty generally bypasses the need for fracture union, healing of certain key fragments remains critical. Tuberosity non-union after shoulder arthroplasty is the most prominent example, leading to rotator cuff dysfunction, severe weakness (especially external rotation), and catastrophic functional failure.[16]

7.5 Systemic Complications

Standardised VTE prophylaxis (mechanical and pharmacological) is essential.[4] Collaborative multidisciplinary management with geriatricians or hospitalists helps optimise comorbidities, manage perioperative medical issues, and reduce overall morbidity and mortality.[10]

8. Future Directions

8.1 Precision: Robotics, Navigation, and AI

Robotic-assisted systems (Mako, ROSA, VELYS) and computer navigation use preoperative CT or intraoperative mapping to create 3D models of patient anatomy, enabling precise, pre-planned bone cuts and implant positioning.[73][78] In fracture cases with deformed landmarks and bone loss, these systems may be particularly beneficial. AI algorithms can analyse vast datasets to predict optimal implant selection, sizing, and positioning, as well as develop patient-specific risk models for complications like PJI or dislocation.[73]

8.2 Implant Innovation

  • Advanced biomaterials: HXLPE, advanced metal alloys, ceramics, bioactive coatings (hydroxyapatite, titanium plasma spray), and antimicrobial coatings (silver, iodine, antibiotic-eluting).[73]
  • 3D-printed, patient-specific implants: Additive manufacturing enables custom implants and guides, particularly advantageous in revision or post-traumatic cases with significant bone loss. Porous, trabecular-like structures promote tissue ingrowth.[83]
  • "Smart" implants: Embedded sensors could provide real-time data on implant loading, micromotion, and temperature for remote monitoring.[82]

8.3 Biologics in Augmenting Healing

The "diamond concept" of bone healing emphasises that successful union requires not only mechanical stability but also an osteoconductive scaffold, osteogenic cells, and osteoinductive growth factors.[85]

  • Bone grafts & substitutes: Autograft remains gold standard. Structural and morselised allografts are essential for defect reconstruction.[62][85]
  • Growth factors: rhBMP-2 and rhBMP-7 for recalcitrant nonunions or large segmental defects.[85]
  • Cell-based therapies: Bone marrow aspirate concentrate (BMAC) and platelet-rich plasma (PRP) to enhance the biological environment at fracture sites.[85]

9. Key Landmark Studies & Evidence Summary

Limitation Note: The source document provides references to numerous studies and reviews but does not consistently specify individual study designs or sample sizes for all cited works. Evidence levels below are inferred from study descriptions in the source text. Only studies clearly identifiable as high-level evidence are highlighted.

1. Meta-analyses: RSA vs. ORIF vs. HHR for Proximal Humerus Fractures Level I

Systematic reviews and meta-analyses consistently demonstrate that RSA produces superior patient-reported outcomes and greater active forward elevation compared to HHR, and comparable or superior outcomes to ORIF in elderly patients with displaced proximal humerus fractures.[15][30]

2. Meta-analysis: TEA vs. ORIF for Distal Humerus Fractures in Elderly Level II

In patients ≥65 years, TEA significantly reduces reoperation rates and improves arc of motion, with comparable patient-reported functional scores to ORIF.[39][41]

3. Systematic Review: Arthroplasty vs. Internal Fixation for Femoral Neck Fractures Level I

Demonstrates THA provides superior long-term pain relief and function compared to internal fixation and hemiarthroplasty for displaced intracapsular femoral neck fractures in the elderly.[42]

4. Vancouver Classification Validation Studies Level III

The Vancouver classification system is the most widely used and validated tool for periprosthetic femoral fractures, combining fracture location, implant stability, and bone stock into a clear treatment algorithm.[49][53]

5. Network Meta-analysis: ORIF vs. IMN vs. DFR for Periprosthetic Distal Femur Fractures Level I

Network meta-analysis comparing open reduction internal fixation, intramedullary nail fixation, and distal femoral replacement for periprosthetic distal femur fractures.[48]

Evidence Gap: The source document references primarily systematic reviews, meta-analyses, and retrospective studies. Prospective randomised controlled trials comparing arthroplasty vs. ORIF for specific fracture types remain limited. Most evidence is Level I–III for proximal humerus and femoral neck fractures, but lower levels (IV–V) for less common scenarios such as primary TKA for acute fractures and shoulder periprosthetic fracture management.
