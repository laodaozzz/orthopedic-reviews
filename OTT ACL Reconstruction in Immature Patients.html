Introduction and Evolving Paradigm

The epidemiological landscape of pediatric orthopaedic sports medicine has undergone a profound shift over the past three decades. The incidence of anterior cruciate ligament (ACL) injuries among prepubescent and early adolescent athletes has increased by an estimated 924% since the mid-1990s. Evidence Level I[1] This demographic—defined by widely open physes and classified under Tanner stages 1 and 2—presents an extraordinarily complex biomechanical and biological challenge.

Historically, the standard of care for a skeletally immature patient presenting with ACL rupture was stringent non-operative management relying on physical therapy, functional bracing, and activity modification until skeletal maturity.[1] However, contemporary longitudinal data have unequivocally demonstrated that delaying surgical intervention is biologically deleterious.

⚠ Critical Finding: Consequences of Delayed Surgery In the absence of the primary anterior and rotational restraint provided by the native ACL, the pediatric knee experiences chronic micro-instability and recurrent macroscopic giving-way episodes. Delayed treatment is associated with a dramatic escalation in irreparable meniscal tears (particularly bucket-handle tears of the medial meniscus), irreversible full-thickness chondral damage, and early-onset osteoarthritis.[2] Level I

Consequently, early surgical reconstruction is now the definitive, evidence-based standard of care for the active paediatric patient.[5]

The fundamental surgical dilemma lies in restoring translational and rotational stability without causing iatrogenic injury to the distal femoral and proximal tibial physes. These growth plates collectively account for approximately 65% of total lower extremity longitudinal growth:

  • Distal femoral physis: contributes 70% of femoral growth (~9 mm/year)[7]
  • Proximal tibial physis: contributes 60% of tibial growth (~6 mm/year)[7]

Standard transphyseal techniques utilized in adult populations—which necessitate drilling 8–10 mm osseous tunnels directly through the metaphysis and epiphysis—pose an unacceptable risk of premature physeal closure (PPC), leg length discrepancy (LLD), and severe angular deformities.[8]

According to consensus data from the Pediatric Research in Sports Medicine (PRiSM) society and the International Olympic Committee (IOC), surgical indications and technique selection must be highly tailored, age-specific, and maturity-dependent. For patients aged 8–10 years (Tanner stages 1 and 2) with significant remaining growth potential (>4 years), the "over-the-top" (OTT) extraphyseal reconstruction is the most frequently utilized and strongly recommended technique.[5] Consensus

Anatomical Architecture and Physeal Dynamics

The distal femoral and proximal tibial physes are not planar discs of cartilage; they are complex, undulating, three-dimensional structures with distinct histological zones that dictate both latitudinal and longitudinal bone growth.

The Vulnerability of the Physis

The physis is divided into the resting, proliferative, hypertrophic, and calcification zones. Surgical trauma—whether mechanical (drilling, rasping), thermal (frictional heat from reamers), or vascular (disruption of epiphyseal or metaphyseal blood supply)—can trigger an osteogenic response that forms a bony bridge or "bar" across the physis, halting endochondral ossification.[8]

The periphery of the physis is encircled by two critical structures:

  • Perichondrial ring of LaCroix: a dense fibrous tether providing mechanical stability, resisting shear forces[14]
  • Zone of Ranvier: responsible for latitudinal growth (widening of the bone)[14]
⚠ Surgical Risk Zone The Over-the-Top technique operates in perilous proximity to the perichondrial ring on the posterolateral aspect of the distal femur. Iatrogenic disruption during aggressive exposure or over-zealous periosteal rasping can result in a peripheral physeal arrest, leading to relentless angular deformities as uninjured portions continue to grow.[8]

Iatrogenic Growth Disturbance Pathomechanisms

Iatrogenic growth disturbances manifest in three distinct clinical pathologies:

  1. Premature Physeal Closure (PPC) and Shortening: Leg length shortening is directly attributed to central physeal bar formation. An 8–10 mm transphyseal drill hole with bone plug or rigid hardware creates a permanent mechanical tether, resulting in global physeal arrest.[8]
  2. Limb Overgrowth (Lengthening): Epiphyseal sockets (as in All-Epiphyseal techniques) induce a local inflammatory and hypervascular response, excessively stimulating the resting zone and accelerating chondrocyte proliferation.[8]
  3. Angular Deformities (Coronal and Sagittal): Via eccentric or peripheral physeal arrest. According to the Hueter-Volkmann principle, asymmetrical mechanical tethering alters growth. Lateral distal femoral arrest → valgus; medial proximal tibial arrest → varus. Sagittal deformities occur following anterior tibial physeal arrest.[8]

Surgical Technique Nuances

The architectural premise of the OTT technique is the complete avoidance of osseous tunnels that traverse the critical physeal anatomy. The graft is uniquely routed to approximate the native ACL vector using extra-articular anatomical pathways, converting an intra-articular reconstruction into a hybrid extra- and intra-articular procedure.[16]

The Over-the-Top Femoral Pathway

The femoral routing completely avoids drilling into the lateral femoral condyle. Instead, the posterior aspect of the lateral femoral condyle serves as a biological pulley:

  • A posterolateral arthrotomy is performed under direct arthroscopic visualization[19]
  • A curved hemostat is passed through the intercondylar notch to penetrate the posterior capsule
  • The graft is shuttled "over the top" of the lateral femoral condyle[4]
  • The graft rests directly on the posterior cortex, entirely external to the joint capsule[17]
📍 Non-Anatomic Position This positioning is fundamentally non-anatomic relative to the native ACL femoral footprint; it is situated significantly superior and posterior to the anatomical origin.[15] This profoundly alters graft isometry, requiring meticulous tensioning to prevent over-constraint or graft stretch-out.
Femoral Fixation Principle: The graft is secured to the lateral femoral metaphysis/periosteum strictly proximal to the distal femoral physis, with the knee at precisely 70 degrees of flexion and in slight external tibial rotation.[2] This prevents over-constraining during terminal extension while imparting rotational control.

Iliotibial Band Autograft: The Micheli-Kocher Technique

The Micheli-Kocher technique, an evolution of the historical MacIntosh lateral substitution procedure from the 1970s, is the premier ITB-based OTT reconstruction for Tanner 1–2 patients.[17] It is a tunnel-free operation functioning as a combined intra-articular and extra-articular reconstruction, intrinsically providing a lateral extra-articular tenodesis (LET) effect.[17]

Graft Harvest and Preparation

  • Longitudinal lateral incision over the distal thigh to access the iliotibial band
  • Central strip harvest of minimum 15 cm length[23]
  • Distal insertion at Gerdy's tubercle is preserved, maintaining natural vascularity and proprioceptive innervation[15]
  • Proximal end detached from vastus lateralis fascia and tubularized with high-tensile non-absorbable sutures[15]

Deep Routing and the "LET Effect"

The proximal ITB graft end is routed deep to the lateral collateral ligament (LCL).[18] This mechanically recreates the constraint of the native anterolateral ligament (ALL) and anterolateral capsule, providing an immediate check-rein against internal tibial rotation.

Tibial Routing: "Over the Front" Pathway

The Micheli-Kocher technique employs a highly specific "over the front" tibial routing to avoid transphyseal drilling:[25]

  • Subperiosteal epiphyseal groove on the anterior tibia, strictly proximal to the tibial tubercle apophysis and proximal tibial physis
  • Graft passed deep to the anterior intermeniscal ligament—a vital technical pearl preventing anterior migration, impingement, and cyclops lesion formation[24]
  • Free end sutured to robust anteromedial tibial periosteum and sartorial fascia[27]

Hamstring Tendon Autograft Variations

Hamstring tendon autografts (semitendinosus and gracilis) are increasingly adapted for OTT extraphyseal routing, offering superior intra-articular tensile strength, higher cross-sectional graft volume (6–8 mm diameter even in small children), and less donor-site morbidity.[4]

Purely Extraphyseal Hamstring Routing

The natural tibial insertion at the pes anserinus can be preserved as a living distal anchor. The proximal ends are routed "over the front," deep to the intermeniscal ligament, through the intercondylar notch, and pulled OTT around the lateral femoral condyle.[25]

Hybrid Fixation: All-Epiphyseal Tibial Sockets

If hamstring tendons are completely harvested as free grafts, the tibial side may use a small-diameter (4.5 mm) all-epiphyseal half-socket remaining entirely within the tibial epiphysis. This provides anatomic distal fixation but introduces risk of hyperemic overgrowth.[32]

Fixation Modalities

⚠ Hardware Warning for Tanner 1–2 Rigid hardware (interference screws, metal staples, transcortical endobuttons) across or near the physis introduces severe tethering effects. In strictly extraphyseal techniques, high-tensile non-absorbable sutures are the gold standard.[2] Literature indicates a 12% incidence of secondary surgery required for staple removal due to irritation or impending physeal impingement.[35]

Actionable Surgical Pearls

Graft Length: Minimum 15 cm for ITB harvest. A shorter graft will fail to reach the medial tibial periosteum after complex routing, resulting in inadequate tensioning and construct failure.[23]
Intermeniscal Ligament Navigation: Passing the graft deep to the anterior intermeniscal ligament is non-negotiable. Failure leads to anterior bowstringing, impingement, and arthrofibrosis.[24]
Femoral Isometry: Fix at precisely 70° flexion with slight external tibial rotation. Securing in full extension results in immediate joint capture and failure to flex.[2]
Absolute Hardware Avoidance in Tanner 1: Rely exclusively on robust periosteal suturing with multiple passes of high-tensile non-absorbable sutures.[2]
Perichondrial Preservation: Avoid aggressive rasping or curettage on the posterior lateral femoral condyle to prevent damage to the perichondrial ring of LaCroix, which causes valgus or flexion deformity.[14]

Biomechanics vs. Safety: The Clinical Paradox

The primary biomechanical criticism of the OTT technique is its non-anatomic positioning—the functional origin is superior and posterior to the native ACL footprint.[15]

Robotic Kinematics and Pivot-Shift Deficit

In-vitro robotic studies using the Universal Musculoskeletal Simulator (UMS)—a 6-degree-of-freedom system—have quantified the kinematic deficits of the OTT approach during a simulated clinical pivot-shift test (10 Nm valgus torque, 5 Nm internal rotation, and continuous compressive loads).[36] Evidence Level II

The Clinical Paradox: In-Vivo Triumph

Despite pronounced kinematic deficits in cadaveric robotic models, in-vivo clinical outcomes present a striking paradox:

  • In 237 prepubescent knees (Micheli-Kocher ITB), at 6.2-year follow-up: 96.8% grade A Lachman and 98.8% grade A pivot-shift[38]
  • Systematic review of 1,177 pediatric patients: no clinically significant differences between OTT and AE in negative pivot-shift (95.2% vs. 93.9%), normal Lachman (90.8% vs. 93.9%), or IKDC scores (93.6 vs. 94.0)[21]

Complications & Growth Disturbances

The overriding objective of the OTT technique is to avert iatrogenic injury to the epiphyseal plates. A highly robust systematic review evaluating 2,693 pediatric ACL reconstructions identified an overall growth disturbance rate of 2.6% across all modalities.[8] Level I

Long-Term Graft Survivorship & Rehabilitation

Graft Failure and Contralateral Risk

The clinical graft failure (re-rupture) rate for OTT reconstructions ranges between 6.6% and 10.6%.[15] A long-term follow-up study (mean 11.0 ± 2.7 years) of 43 patients reported a 9% ipsilateral ACL revision rate at an average of 5.3 years postoperatively.[35] Level III

⚠ High Contralateral Injury Rate In the 11-year follow-up cohort, an alarming 19% of patients required contralateral ACL reconstruction, culminating in a 28% overall secondary ACL injury rate across the athletic lifespan.[35] This underscores that OTT surgery stabilizes the index knee but does not correct underlying neuromuscular deficits or high-risk movement patterns.

Patient-Reported Outcomes

Overall PROMs remain excellent: KOOS subscales universally exceed patient-acceptable symptom states, and subjective IKDC scores consistently map in the excellent range (mean 93.6).[39]

A critical demographic nuance exists: patients under age 13 at surgery demonstrate statistically worse Lysholm scores (82.0 vs. 94.6, p=0.025) and worse KOOS-ADL scores (96.7 vs. 99.9, p=0.025) compared to older adolescent cohorts.[35]

PRiSM Consensus on Rehabilitation

Given the non-anatomic routing and reliance on extra-articular soft-tissue healing, PRiSM guidelines recommend specific rehabilitation pacing:[42] Consensus

  • ITB-based OTT: Touch Toe Weight-Bearing (TTWB) for up to 6 weeks[43]
  • AE techniques: Weight-Bearing As Tolerated (WBAT) by weeks 2–4[43]
  • Early patellar mobilization is universally mandated to prevent infrapatellar contracture[43]
  • Return-to-play criteria emphasize specialized motion analysis laboratories assessing dynamic single-leg jumping, deceleration tasks, and landing asymmetry before clearance (typically >9–12 months)[42]

Conclusion

The Over-the-Top physeal-sparing ACL reconstruction represents a highly specialized, biomechanically complex, yet clinically robust solution for addressing ACL ruptures in Tanner stage 1 and 2 patients. By completely eschewing transphyseal drilling in favour of sophisticated extra-articular routing—utilizing the posterior lateral femoral condyle as a biological fulcrum and passing the graft "over the front" of the tibia deep to the intermeniscal ligament—the technique successfully safeguards the profoundly vulnerable chondroepiphyseal architecture.

While in-vitro robotic models highlight quantifiable deficits in true kinematic fidelity, the in-vivo clinical reality demonstrates overwhelming rotational stability. This success is heavily predicated on the biological integration of the ITB autograft (Micheli-Kocher technique) or properly augmented hamstring autografts. The deep routing inherently functions as a lateral extra-articular tenodesis, neutralizing rotational forces.

Crucially, the OTT technique boasts the absolute lowest overall risk of iatrogenic growth disturbances in the paediatric orthopaedic literature, with incidence rates approaching a true 0% in long-term cohorts. Meticulous adherence to surgical technique—ensuring graft length >15 cm, femoral fixation at 70° flexion, rigorously avoiding hardware across the perichondrial ring, and mitigating aggressive posterolateral rasping—is imperative to maintain this safety profile.
