1. The Clinical Imperative for Hip Preservation in Young Adults

Management of significant hip pathology in young, active adults—defined broadly as individuals under 50–60 years of age—is governed by a foundational clinical principle: preservation of the native hip joint whenever feasible.V This doctrine arises from the unique characteristics of this demographic: good bone quality, high functional demands, and a long life expectancy. These factors collectively argue against premature use of prosthetic joint replacement.V

Key Principle — The Preservation Paradox Hip-preserving surgeries carry high complication rates (nonunion, AVN, malunion, implant failure), frequently necessitating revision or conversion to THA. Yet the initial treatment strategy remains firmly rooted in attempting to salvage the biological joint.III

1.1 Rationale and Standard of Care

Total hip arthroplasty (THA) involves implanting devices with a finite lifespan. In a young patient, this raises the probability of one or more complex revision surgeries over their lifetime, each carrying progressively higher complication risks and diminished functional returns.III The standard of care positions hip preservation as the primary goal, with arthroplasty relegated to a salvage procedure employed only after biological reconstruction has failed or is deemed impossible.V

The benefits of a successfully preserved hip are profound: the potential for unrestricted, high-impact activity and delaying the "countdown" on prosthetic wear for decades. The treatment paradigm for young adults is fundamentally different from that in the geriatric population, where early mobility and a single, definitive procedure take precedence. In young adults, the focus is on restoring the natural anatomy and mechanics of the hip joint.V

2. Management of Femoral Neck Fractures via Internal Fixation

Femoral neck fractures in young adults are uncommon injuries, typically resulting from high-energy trauma (motor vehicle collisions, falls from height), in contrast to the low-energy fragility fractures seen in the elderly.V These injuries pose a formidable surgical challenge due to the tenuous blood supply to the femoral head and the high mechanical shear forces across the fracture site.

2.1 The Paramount Goal: Anatomic Reduction and Stable Fixation

Successful treatment hinges on achieving two interdependent goals:

  1. Absolute anatomic reduction of the fracture fragments
  2. Stable internal fixation construct
Critical Surgical Principle The quality of reduction is the single most critical, surgeon-controlled variable dictating outcome. Failure to restore normal anatomy—allowing residual varus angulation or posterior tilt—dramatically increases fixation stress and is associated with nonunion rates as high as 80%.V There must be a very low threshold to convert from closed to open reduction.

The Watson-Jones anterolateral exposure is a commonly recommended approach for open reduction. The potential harm of a single, well-executed open reduction is far outweighed by the predictably poor outcomes of internal fixation of a malreduced fracture.V

2.2 Surgical Techniques and Implant Choice

Stable Fractures (Garden I/II)

For stable, non-displaced or impacted valgus fractures, fixation with multiple cannulated cancellous screws in an inverted triangle configuration is often sufficient. The inferior screw should be placed along the calcar femorale for a strong anchor in dense bone.V

Unstable Fractures (Garden III/IV, Pauwels Type III)

A sliding hip screw (SHS/dynamic hip screw) is often preferred for these high-shear patterns. These fixed-angle devices are biomechanically superior in resisting shear forces and allow controlled impaction and compression across the fracture site. An additional superior cannulated screw may enhance rotational control.III

Newer Fixation Strategies

  • Femoral Neck System (FNS): A newer fixed-angle blade device designed for minimally invasive insertion. Studies have reported shorter operative times, less blood loss, significantly less postoperative femoral neck shortening, and superior Harris Hip Scores.II
  • Medial Buttress Plate (MBP): A network meta-analysis concluded that MBP may result in the best postoperative hip function and lowest overall complication rates, and should be strongly considered.I

2.3 Key Surgical Controversies

Timing of Surgery

Classic teaching holds that femoral neck fractures are surgical urgencies, with some guidelines recommending surgery within 24–48 hours.V However, evidence for emergent fixation (within 6–12 hours) is equivocal. Several retrospective studies failed to demonstrate a statistically significant correlation between surgical delay and AVN incidence.III The current consensus has shifted toward "urgent" fixation: performed in a timely manner by a skilled surgeon once the patient is fully resuscitated and medically optimized, rather than a mandate for middle-of-the-night emergency surgery by a less experienced team.V

Routine Capsulotomy

The theoretical rationale is compelling: incising the hip capsule evacuates the tense fracture hematoma, potentially decompressing the retinacular arteries. However, this theoretical benefit has not been conclusively proven in prospective, controlled trials. The practice varies widely, and it is not a universally accepted standard of care.Not specified in source

3. Management of Early-Stage Osteonecrosis (ONFH)

Osteonecrosis of the femoral head is a pathologic process resulting from interruption of blood supply to the subchondral bone, leading to bone death and, eventually, mechanical collapse and degenerative arthritis.V In young adults, the diagnosis is devastating—untreated ONFH often leads to progressive collapse and the need for arthroplasty at a young age.III

3.1 The Diagnostic Pathway and the "Golden Window"

Success of joint-preserving procedures is almost entirely dependent on early diagnosis before structural compromise.III Initial workup includes AP pelvis and frog-leg lateral radiographs. However, in earliest stages (Ficat Stage I, ARCO Stage 1), radiographs are by definition normal.V

MRI: The Gold Standard MRI has sensitivity and specificity both exceeding 99% for early ONFH diagnosis. It can detect bone marrow edema and the characteristic "double-line sign" long before plain film changes, allowing diagnosis within the crucial pre-collapse window.V

Outcomes data starkly illustrate the golden window: One long-term study reported a 10-year hip survival rate of 96% for Ficat Stage I disease, which plummeted to just 35% for Stage III disease.III

3.2 Patient Selection and Staging

The most critical distinction is between pre-collapse (Ficat I/II) and post-collapse (Ficat III/IV) stages.V The presence of a subchondral fracture ("crescent sign") marks transition to Stage III and is a harbinger of imminent collapse.

Additionally, lesion size and location are powerful prognostic indicators.III Smaller lesions (involving <15–30% of femoral head volume) and those located more medially have much better prognosis than large, superolaterally located lesions.

3.3 Surgical Preservation Techniques

Core Decompression (CD)

The most frequently performed joint-preserving procedure for ONFH.V Involves drilling channels from the lateral femoral cortex into the necrotic lesion. Dual rationale: mechanical decompression (reducing intraosseous pressure) and creating channels for revascularization and new bone formation.III Success is highly dependent on proper patient selection, with excellent outcomes for Ficat Stage I but declining sharply after the crescent sign.III

Bone Grafting

A Bayesian network meta-analysis found that all forms of bone grafting combined with CD were superior to CD alone in preventing radiographic progression of ONFH.I

  • Non-vascularized bone grafting: Autogenous cancellous/corticocancellous bone, often from iliac crest. Advanced "trapdoor" or "lightbulb" procedures allow thorough necrotic bone removal and structural grafting.IV
  • Vascularized fibular graft (VFG): For younger patients with larger pre-collapse lesions. The fibular strut is harvested with its nutrient artery and vein, inserted into the femoral head, and microsurgically anastomosed to vessels around the hip. This demanding procedure has shown high rates of success in properly selected patients.III

Rotational Osteotomy

A proximal femoral osteotomy rotates the femoral head to move the necrotic segment out of the weight-bearing zone. Technically demanding but can be effective for well-contained lesions in younger patients.V

4. Primary Total Hip Arthroplasty as a Definitive Solution

A critical re-evaluation of primary THA's role is underway, driven by persistently high failure rates of internal fixation for high-risk fractures and rapid technological advancements in arthroplasty implants.III

4.1 Evolving Indications

For femoral neck fractures, primary THA is emerging as viable first-line treatment when high-risk indicators converge:

  • Severe displacement (Garden III/IV)
  • Biomechanically unstable vertical pattern (Pauwels Type III)
  • Significant posterior neck comminution
  • Poor bone stock, pre-existing symptomatic hip arthritis
  • Significant comorbidities making a second salvage surgery risky
Economic Consideration Decision analyses suggest that for patients over age 44–54 (depending on comorbidity burden), the higher initial cost of THA may be more cost-effective than the cumulative cost of treating a failed internal fixation.III

For osteonecrosis, THA is the definitive treatment for advanced-stage disease with femoral head collapse (Ficat Stage III with significant flattening, or Stage IV with secondary acetabular arthritis).V

4.2 Modern Arthroplasty Advances

Fixation and Bone Preservation

In younger patients with good bone quality, uncemented (press-fit) femoral and acetabular components are the standard of care. Porous/textured surfaces encourage biological fixation, avoiding bone cement degradation and making future revision easier.V

Modern implants use larger-diameter femoral heads (32 mm, 36 mm, or larger). The "jump distance" is proportional to head radius, dramatically enhancing stability. Larger heads have been shown to reduce dislocation risk by as much as a factor of ten.III

5.2 Conversion to THA: The Salvage Trajectory

Multiple studies have quantified the conversion rate:

  • Population-based study (796 patients, age 18–50): 1 in 7 patients (14%) required conversion to THA.III
  • Other series report rates of 18.8% to 32%.III
  • Median time from fixation to conversion: approximately 2 years (IQR 1–4 years).III
  • Most common indication: symptomatic osteonecrosis (~50%), followed by post-traumatic arthritis, malunion, and nonunion.III

The "Compounded Failure" Effect The failure of the initial preservation surgery leads to a salvage surgery that is itself more prone to failure than the primary alternative would have been. Conversion THA requires dealing with retained hardware, distorted anatomy, poor bone quality, and extensive soft tissue scarring—it is more analogous in risk to revision THA than to primary THA.III

5.4 Functional Outcomes and Return to Activity

When internal fixation is successful, outcomes can be excellent—Harris Hip Scores in the low-to-mid 90s.II However, patients who experience fixation failure have significantly inferior long-term function, creating a bimodal distribution of outcomes.III

Primary THA provides a more predictable path: HHS consistently improves from the 40s to the mid-90s.III Randomized controlled trials comparing THA to IF have demonstrated superior functional scores and less pain in the THA group at both short- and long-term follow-up.I

Regarding return to sport, most surgeons now support return to low-impact sports after THA (~86% of patients). High-impact activities are still generally discouraged, but evidence suggests low risk of implant complications during sporting activity.III

6. Evidence-Based Framework for Clinical Decision-Making

Navigating the "preservation paradox" requires a departure from dogmatic, age-based protocols toward a nuanced, evidence-based framework centered on individualized risk stratification and shared decision-making.V

6.1 Critical Factors in Patient Selection

Patient Factors

  • Physiological age & activity level: Younger, more active patients have the most to gain from a durable biological solution.V
  • Comorbidities & bone quality: Chronic alcohol misuse, smoking, metabolic bone disease weigh against internal fixation.V

Fracture Factors (for FNF)

  • Displacement (Garden): Undisplaced (I/II) → IF is standard of care. Displaced (III/IV) → Risk stratification needed.V
  • Geometry (Pauwels): Type III (>50°) creates unstable shear environment with predictably high fixation failure; strong indication for robust fixation or primary THA.III
  • Posterior comminution: Compromises fixation stability; independent predictor of failure.III

Osteonecrosis Factors

  • Disease stage (Ficat/ARCO): Pre-collapse (I/II) → preservation candidates. Post-collapse (III/IV) → THA standard of care.V
  • Lesion size & location: Large (>30–50% volume), superolateral lesions are less likely to respond to simple CD.V

7. Future Horizons in Management

7.1 Biologics in Joint Preservation

For early-stage ONFH, augmenting standard procedures with biological agents is a major research frontier:III

  • Bone Marrow Aspirate Concentrate (BMAC) & Mesenchymal Stem Cells (MSCs): Autologous bone marrow is aspirated, concentrated via centrifugation, and injected into the decompressed lesion. Clinical trials are evaluating efficacy vs. CD alone, with early results suggesting improved outcomes and reduced conversion to THA.II
  • Platelet-Rich Plasma (PRP) / Fibrin (PRF): High concentrations of growth factors promoting angiogenesis and tissue healing; being studied as injectates to augment CD.II
  • Bisphosphonates: May slow bone resorption and prevent mechanical collapse in early ONFH.II
  • Synthetic bone graft substitutes: Porous β-TCP bioceramics provide osteoconductive scaffold that can be resorbed and replaced by new bone.IV

7.2 Technological Advances in Arthroplasty

  • Robotic-Assisted THA: Uses preoperative CT to create patient-specific 3D surgical plans. Robotic arm assists with high-accuracy implant positioning. Early studies show excellent functional results and low complication rates.III
  • Advanced Bearing Materials: Research continues into enhanced ceramics, new polyethylene formulations, and novel surface coatings (titanium nitride, diamond-like carbon) for harder, smoother, more scratch-resistant surfaces.V

8. Conclusions & Expert Recommendations

The management of femoral neck fractures and early-stage osteonecrosis in young, active adults remains among the most challenging areas in orthopaedic surgery. A comprehensive review reveals a nuanced landscape where traditional dogma is being challenged by sobering outcome data and rapid technological progress.

Five Key Recommendations
  1. Prioritize Anatomic Reduction Above All Else: Every effort must achieve perfect alignment. Extremely low threshold to convert from closed to open reduction.V
  2. Embrace Individualized Risk Stratification: Move beyond rigid age-based protocols. Incorporate Garden/Pauwels classifications, posterior comminution, patient comorbidities, and ONFH staging.III
  3. Counsel Patients Realistically: Transparently discuss the high, evidence-based rates of failure (~25–45%) and the compounded risk of conversion THA.III
  4. Strongly Consider Primary THA in High-Risk Subgroups: For patients >45 with Pauwels III/posterior comminution/comorbidities — modern implants make this a rational, evidence-supported choice.III
  5. Maintain High Index of Suspicion for Early ONFH: Low threshold for MRI in young adults with persistent unexplained hip pain. Early detection before collapse is the only opportunity for joint preservation.V
