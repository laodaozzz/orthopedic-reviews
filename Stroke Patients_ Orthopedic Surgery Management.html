1 Preoperative Risk Stratification & Surgical Planning

The initial phase of managing a post-stroke patient scheduled for major orthopedic surgery is the most critical determinant of a safe outcome. This period requires meticulous, multidisciplinary evaluation to quantify competing risks, determine optimal surgical timing, and implement targeted preoperative optimization strategies. Failure to conduct a thorough assessment can precipitate preventable adverse events including recurrent stroke, major hemorrhage, and other cardiovascular complications.¹

1.1 The Dual Thrombosis Challenge

The central clinical challenge is the reconciliation of two opposing high-risk states: the patient's intrinsic high risk for arterial thromboembolism (particularly recurrent stroke) and the procedure's inherent high risk for both venous thromboembolism (VTE) and major hemorrhage.²

⚠ Key Mortality Data Perioperative stroke mortality rates are reported to be as high as 26% to 60%. The single most important predictor of perioperative stroke is a history of a previous stroke or TIA.¹ ¹⁴

Major orthopedic procedures—total hip arthroplasty (THA), total knee arthroplasty (TKA), and major spinal surgery—carry a very high baseline risk of VTE, with deep vein thrombosis rates historically ranging from 40–80% without prophylaxis.⁶ Simultaneously, these extensive procedures can result in substantial blood loss—hip arthroplasty alone is associated with approximately 1,500 mL of total blood loss (including hidden loss).⁸

🔗 The Triangular Risk Relationship This is not a simple "clotting vs. bleeding" balance. It is a complex triangular relationship between arterial thrombosis, venous thrombosis, and hemorrhage. Aggressive anticoagulation to prevent recurrent stroke can cause surgical site hematoma → delayed mobilization → increased VTE risk. Conversely, under-anticoagulation leaves the patient vulnerable to both stroke and VTE.¹²

1.2 Patient-Specific Thromboembolic Risk Assessment

A comprehensive assessment must document specific details of the prior cerebrovascular event:

  • Type: Ischemic versus hemorrhagic
  • Date of occurrence (critical for timing decisions)
  • Underlying etiology: Cardioembolic, large-artery atherosclerosis, small-vessel disease
  • Residual neurological deficits¹

The majority of perioperative strokes are ischemic, often arising from thrombosis, embolism, or low-flow states exacerbated by the surgical environment.¹²

Standardized Risk Scoring Tools

  • CHA₂DS₂-VASc Score: Essential for patients with atrial fibrillation to estimate annual stroke risk and guide perioperative anticoagulation intensity. A score of 5–6 categorizes the patient as high-risk for thromboembolism.¹ ¹⁸
  • ACS-NSQIP Surgical Risk Calculator: Recommended by the AHA/ASA for individualized, quantitative risk estimates for stroke and other major adverse events. Integrates patient comorbidities and procedural factors.¹² ¹⁵
  • Machine-learning models: Emerging evidence shows superior predictive performance by incorporating both preoperative and intraoperative variables. Key predictors include: advanced age, cerebrovascular disease history, diabetes mellitus, and intraoperative hypotension.¹³ ³⁰

High-Risk Cardiac Conditions

  • Mechanical heart valves—particularly older-generation models (caged-ball or tilting-disc) or any mitral position prosthesis—confer very high risk of valve thrombosis and systemic embolism.¹
  • Recency of thromboembolic event: A stroke, TIA, or VTE within the preceding 3 months dramatically elevates short-term recurrence risk.¹

1.3 Procedure-Specific Hemorrhagic Risk

Major orthopedic surgeries are uniformly classified as high-bleed-risk procedures, directly dictating the need for and duration of antithrombotic interruption.²

  • Spine surgery: Performed in an enclosed, non-compressible space. Even small bleeding can cause catastrophic epidural hematoma → spinal cord compression → permanent paralysis.²³ Exceptionally high-risk from a bleeding perspective.
  • Hip arthroplasty: Associated with substantial and predictable blood loss (~1,500 mL). Frequently requires proactive blood management strategies including tranexamic acid (TXA) and transfusion protocols.⁸ ⁹

2 Optimal Timing of Elective Surgery After Stroke

⏱ The 90-Day Rule Elective major orthopedic surgery should be deferred for a minimum of 3 months (≥90 days) following an ischemic stroke. Surgery within this window is associated with markedly increased risk of MACE and mortality.³⁴ ¹²

2.1 Evolution of Evidence

Initial recommendations (based on Danish national registry data) suggested risk plateaued at 9–12 months. The 2021 AHA/ASA scientific statement advised delaying 6–9 months.¹²

Landmark 2022 study (5.8+ million Medicare patients): Found that while recurrent stroke risk was 8-fold elevated and death risk 2.5-fold elevated for surgery within 30 days of prior stroke, this heightened risk leveled off after 90 days. Waiting longer than 90 days did not confer additional significant risk reduction.³⁴

Current 2024 guideline (AHA/ACC joint guideline): Recommends delaying elective noncardiac surgery for ≥3 months (≥90 days) after a cerebrovascular event.¹² ³⁷

2.2 The Neurobiological Rationale

The 3-month delay is not arbitrary—it serves as a "neurobiological cooling-off period" allowing:

  • Stabilization of the blood-brain barrier
  • Restoration of cerebral autoregulation
  • Resolution of the acute post-stroke inflammatory state

Surgery induces systemic inflammation and a hypercoagulable state. If performed during the acute/subacute phase with impaired cerebral autoregulation, the brain is exquisitely vulnerable to hemodynamic swings—increasing risk of ischemic extension from hypotension and hemorrhagic conversion from hypertension.¹ ¹²

📌 Special Case: Time-Sensitive Surgery For fractures or cancer-related orthopedic conditions, rigid adherence to the 3-month rule may not be appropriate. A formal, case-by-case multidisciplinary risk-benefit analysis is imperative involving surgeon, anesthesiologist, neurologist, and the patient.¹

3 Preoperative Neurological & Cardiovascular Optimization

3.1 Carotid Imaging & Revascularization

Patients with TIA or ischemic stroke of potential large-vessel etiology should undergo preoperative carotid imaging. Patients with symptomatic, high-grade carotid stenosis (>50–70%) should be referred for revascularization (CEA or stenting) prior to elective non-cardiac surgery.¹

Time-sensitivity: Therapeutic benefit of revascularization is greatest within the first 2 weeks of the ischemic event and diminishes significantly after 12 weeks.¹

3.2 Comorbidity Optimization

  • Atrial Fibrillation: Ensure adequate rate/rhythm control; develop a clear, documented perioperative anticoagulation plan. New-onset perioperative AF requires close follow-up.¹³ ¹⁶ ²⁰
  • Renal Dysfunction: Consistent and significant risk factor for perioperative stroke. Profoundly impacts DOAC management—necessitates dose adjustments and longer interruption intervals to prevent drug accumulation.¹ ¹² ²¹
  • Heart Failure: Comprehensive cardiovascular evaluation; ensure compensated, stable state before proceeding.²⁰

4 Perioperative Management of Antithrombotic Therapy

⚠ Core Principle Minimize sub-therapeutic anticoagulation duration to prevent thromboembolic events while allowing adequate hemostasis—particularly delicate in high-bleed-risk orthopedic surgery.²

4.1 Vitamin K Antagonists (Warfarin)

  • Preoperative hold: Stop 5 days before surgery; target INR <1.5. Longer hold may be needed in elderly or liver dysfunction patients.¹ ³²
  • Bridging: NOT routine. Reserved for highest thromboembolic risk patients only (see Table 1). When indicated: therapeutic-dose LMWH starting 3 days before surgery; last dose 24 hours before incision.¹ ¹⁹ ³²
  • Postoperative: Resume within 12–24 hours, typically evening of surgery, if hemostasis is adequate. Postoperative bridging should be used with extreme caution—delay therapeutic-dose LMWH for at least 48–72 hours.² ¹⁹
📊 BRIDGE Trial Evidence Forgoing bridging in most AF patients resulted in a significant reduction in major bleeding complications (~3% risk) without a corresponding increase in arterial thromboembolism. Routine bridging has been largely abandoned.¹⁹

4.2 Direct Oral Anticoagulants (DOACs)

DOACs generally simplify perioperative management due to predictable pharmacokinetics, rapid onset, and shorter half-lives—no routine bridging needed.⁴

Preoperative Interruption Protocols

  • High-bleed-risk surgery (normal renal function): Stop 2–3 days before (skip 4 doses total).²
  • Dabigatran (CrCl <50 mL/min): Extend hold to 4 days before high-risk surgery.¹
  • Postoperative resumption: Delay 48–72 hours after high-bleed-risk procedure. Further delay if excessive bleeding.²

4.3 Antiplatelet Agents

Aspirin (ASA)

  • Primary prevention: Hold 7–10 days before surgery.
  • Secondary prevention: Decision is nuanced—continuing may be reasonable when cardiac event risk outweighs bleeding risk. Especially relevant for patients with coronary stents or recent stroke.¹² ³⁷

P2Y₁₂ Inhibitors

  • Clopidogrel: Stop 5–7 days before surgery
  • Ticagrelor: Stop 3–5 days before surgery (reversible agent)
  • Prasugrel: Stop 7–10 days before surgery (potent agent)
  • Resume: Within 24 hours post-op if thrombotic risk is high and bleeding risk acceptable.⁴⁰ ⁴¹

Dual Antiplatelet Therapy (DAPT) with Coronary Stents

This is a particularly high-risk scenario. Aspirin is continued throughout. The P2Y₁₂ inhibitor follows standard interruption intervals and should be resumed ASAP postoperatively (typically within 24 hours).¹² ³⁷ ⁴¹

📋 Guideline Hierarchy Conflict Note: No single unified guideline addresses all aspects of care for this patient population. Orthopedic surgeons (AAOS), anesthesiologists (ASRA), and cardiologists (ACC/AHA) may follow conflicting recommendations, especially regarding aspirin and neuraxial anesthesia timing. A formal documented multidisciplinary consensus plan is the only way to resolve competing priorities.¹²

5 Hemodynamic Management Across the Perioperative Continuum

5.1 Preoperative Blood Pressure Optimization

🚫 Postpone Threshold Elective surgery should be postponed if SBP ≥180 mmHg or DBP ≥110 mmHg. Proceeding with severe hypertension increases risk of myocardial ischemia, arrhythmias, and dangerous BP lability during induction.³⁹

Day-of-Surgery Medications

  • Beta-blockers: CONTINUE—abrupt withdrawal causes rebound tachycardia/hypertension. Do NOT initiate new beta-blockers immediately before surgery (associated with increased stroke/death risk).¹² ³⁹
  • ACEi/ARBs: HOLD 24 hours before surgery—can blunt compensatory renin-angiotensin response, causing refractory intraoperative hypotension. Some guidelines suggest continuation may be reasonable for HF patients.³⁷ ³⁹
  • Diuretics: WITHHOLD morning of surgery to avoid hypovolemia.³⁹
  • Calcium Channel Blockers: CONTINUE.³⁹

5.2 Intraoperative Hemodynamic Goals

The central goal is to avoid hypotension and ensure adequate cerebral perfusion, particularly in a brain with potentially compromised autoregulation.¹² ¹³

🎯 Personalized MAP Target

Guidelines recommend MAP >70 mmHg (especially in moderate-to-high stroke risk patients).¹² However, the more critical principle is maintaining intraoperative BP within 20% of the patient's preoperative baseline.¹³ European guidelines similarly suggest 70–100% of baseline.³⁹

For a patient with baseline MAP 110 mmHg, an intraoperative MAP of 70 mmHg represents severe relative hypotension—potentially causing watershed ischemia.

5.3 Postoperative Blood Pressure Control

  • Permissive hypertension may be tolerated to ensure adequate ischemic penumbra perfusion
  • General target: BP <220/120 mmHg (without thrombolytic therapy)¹⁴
  • If thrombolysis administered: Strict target <185/110 mmHg¹⁴
  • Resume held oral antihypertensives ASAP when clinically feasible to prevent rebound hypertension³⁷

6 Anesthetic Considerations & Intraoperative Management

6.1 General vs. Neuraxial Anesthesia

Available evidence shows a trend favoring neuraxial anesthesia for major lower extremity joint arthroplasty, with associated benefits including:⁴⁷⁻⁵²

  • Lower rates of postoperative cardiovascular and pulmonary complications
  • Reduced intraoperative blood loss and transfusion requirements
  • Shorter hospital length of stay
  • Lower combined 30-day stroke/MI/death incidence in hip fracture surgery
⚖ The Neuraxial Anesthesia Paradox Evidence suggests neuraxial may offer superior systemic outcomes—highly desirable in a frail, vasculopathic post-stroke patient. However, ASRA guidelines mandate interrupting vital antithrombotic medications for a safe neuraxial procedure. For a patient with recent stroke, mechanical valve, or high CHA₂DS₂-VASc score, this interruption creates a high-risk window for thromboembolic events. The anesthetic technique that may be systemically "better" carries a procedural requirement that may be neurologically "worse."⁴¹

6.3 Intraoperative Neuroprotection Strategies

  • Glycemic control: Maintain serum glucose 140–180 mg/dL in the postoperative period. Hyperglycemia worsens ischemic neuronal injury.¹² ¹³
  • Ventilation: Maintain normocarbia. Both hypocapnia (cerebral vasoconstriction) and hypercapnia (vasodilation/"steal" phenomena) are detrimental.¹²
  • Transfusion thresholds: AHA/ASA recommends maintaining hemoglobin >8 g/dL in patients with recent stroke/cerebrovascular disease. Consider >9 g/dL if taking beta-blockers.¹²

7 Postoperative Surveillance & Management

7.1 Early Detection of Perioperative Stroke

🔍 Critical Detection Window The majority of perioperative strokes occur on postoperative days 2 through 9—not immediately in PACU. Presentation is often obscured by residual sedation, opioid analgesia, and postoperative delirium.¹² ²⁸ Routine, frequent, structured neurological assessments are vital.

Emergent Stroke Evaluation Protocol

  1. Establish "Time Last Known Well"
  2. Standardized neurologic exam (e.g., NIHSS)
  3. Emergent non-contrast CT head—differentiate ischemic vs. hemorrhagic stroke (treatments are diametrically opposed)¹⁴
  4. Multidisciplinary consultation—neurology, neuro-intervention, surgical service—to determine eligibility for acute therapies (rtPA, thrombectomy)¹²

7.2 The Postoperative Therapeutic Gap

⚠ The 24–72 Hour Vulnerability Window This critical period represents the time of highest net risk for the patient:
  • Surgery-induced hypercoagulable state is peaking
  • Underlying high thromboembolic risk is returning as preop anticoagulants wear off
  • Clinical guidelines mandate delaying therapeutic anticoagulation to prevent catastrophic surgical bleeding

This creates an unavoidable period of minimal anticoagulation when thrombotic risk is at its highest.² ¹⁴

7.3 VTE Prophylaxis & Therapy Resumption

  • VTE prophylaxis: Achieved primarily by timely resumption of therapeutic anticoagulation. If delayed, prophylactic-dose LMWH may serve as interim measure.²
  • Non-pharmacologic measures: Intermittent pneumatic compression devices, early mobilization.⁴³
  • Aspirin: Often restarted within 24–48 hours if held.¹²
  • Therapeutic anticoagulation (DOACs/LMWH): Delay 48–72 hours after high-risk orthopedic surgery; only when hemostasis is secure.²
