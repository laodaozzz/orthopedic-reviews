The Architect of Stability: Hans Willenegger & Septic Fracture Surgery

1. Introduction: The Clinical Dilemma

Twentieth-century orthopaedic surgery was defined by a fundamental tension between two imperatives: biological preservation of the host's defence mechanisms and mechanical reconstruction of the skeletal framework. For the first half of the century, these goals were viewed as mutually exclusive—especially in the setting of infection, where the introduction of metal implants was considered an absolute contraindication. Source Document

The dominant conservative approach, championed by the "Böhler School" (Lorenz Böhler), relied on traction and plaster casts. While it minimized iatrogenic infection, it exacted a devastating functional toll. Source Document [1]

"Fracture Disease" — the constellation of complications from prolonged immobilization — included permanent joint stiffness, severe muscle atrophy, complex regional pain syndrome, and post-thrombotic edema. Patients frequently emerged with a healed bone but a useless limb. Source Document [2]

The Arbeitsgemeinschaft für Osteosynthesefragen (AO), founded in Switzerland in 1958, was built on three pillars: the mechanical genius of Maurice Müller, the scientific rigour of Martin Allgöwer, and the clinical conscience of Hans Willenegger. It was Willenegger who recognized that the brilliance of compression plating would fail if it could not address the inevitability of infection. Source Document [2]

Through decades of clinical practice in Liestal and experimental research in Davos, Willenegger proved that mechanical instability—not the foreign body itself—was the primary potentiator of osteomyelitis, and that rigid fixation could allow the host immune system to eradicate bacteria even in the presence of metal hardware. Source Document [4]

2. The Historical Burden: Osteomyelitis Before the AO

2.1 The Pre-Antibiotic Era

The struggle against bone infection is as old as surgery itself. Ancient Egyptian papyri (c. 2500–3000 BCE) contain the first written descriptions of osteomyelitis. The Hippocratic corpus declared: "Diseases which are not cured by medicines are cured by ironing; those not cured by ironing are cured by fire; those not cured by fire are incurable." Source Document [6, 7]

Through the Middle Ages and into the 19th century, mortality for compound fractures frequently exceeded 50% in military settings. Lister's antisepsis (1860s) and Koch's sterilization techniques began a scientific approach to infection prevention, but once infection was established in bone, antisepsis alone could not penetrate the rigid architecture and avascular sequestra. Source Document [1]

2.2 The Foreign Body Dogma

Early pioneers such as Albin Lambotte (Belgium) and W. A. Lane (England) experimented with internal fixation, but their results—ravaged by corrosion and fulminant infection—solidified a surgical dogma: internal fixation was banned in potentially contaminated fields. Source Document [8]

Elek & Conen quantified the foreign body effect: the presence of a suture reduced the infective dose of Staphylococcus aureus by a factor of 10,000. This scientific fact paradoxically reinforced the dogma against implant use in infected fields. Source Document [9]

The resulting vicious cycle: infected fracture → hardware removal → destabilisation → motion at the fracture site → sequestra formation → persistent infection. Surgeons were trapped, failing to recognise that instability—rather than the metal—was the true perpetuator of disease. Source Document

2.3 The Cost of Conservatism

Swiss Accident Insurance (SUVA) statistics from 1945 revealed that 40% of tibial fracture and 70% of femoral fracture patients received lifetime invalidity pensions due to poor outcomes under conservative management. Source Document [2]

3.1 The Liestal Era & the Founding of the AO

Maurice Müller, inspired by Robert Danis's 1949 book Théorie et Pratique de l'Ostéosynthèse, gathered a circle of like-minded Swiss surgeons. Willenegger's role was distinct: while Müller innovated and Allgöwer researched, Willenegger focused on safety and complications, dedicating the Liestal clinic to the rigorous study of postoperative infection. Source Document [2, 15]

3.2 The "Foreign Minister" and the Developing World

Willenegger's linguistic skills and global perspective (shaped by the Ethiopian experience) made him the ideal ambassador. He personally recruited Siegfried Weller to spread AO principles in India, Thailand, Indonesia, and Japan. His work laid the foundation for the AO Alliance, which continues to improve fracture care in low-income countries today. Source Document [16, 17]

4. Pathophysiology: "Stability Fights Infection"

4.1 The Mechanism of Instability-Induced Sepsis

Willenegger identified a cascade of deleterious effects caused by interfragmentary motion Source Document [4, 20, 21]:

  1. Interfragmentary Resorption: Unstable motion creates shear forces at the fracture interface. According to Perren's strain theory, excessive strain prevents bone formation; instead, osteoclastic resorption widens the gap. In a septic environment, these cavities fill with pus, bacteria, and necrotic debris. [20]
  2. Vascular Disruption: Constant macro- and micro-motion mechanically shears the delicate capillary buds attempting angiogenesis, maintaining the fracture zone in ischemia. [4]
  3. Immune Privilege & Sequestration: The combination of necrosis and ischemia creates a zone of "immune privilege." Macrophages and granulocytes cannot reach the bacteria. Bacteria thrive in dead bone (sequestra), forming biofilms impervious to systemic antibiotics. Source Document
Willenegger's Inversion: By applying rigid internal fixation (compression plating), the surgeon could arrest interfragmentary motion, stop the resorption cycle, protect neovascularisation, and allow the host's immune defences to reach the bacteria. The metal implant was not a liability—it was a therapeutic tool.

4.2 The Landmark Sheep Studies (Rittmann & Perren, 1974)

Conducted at the Laboratory for Experimental Surgery in Davos (est. 1959 with AO funding [3]), this definitive study used a sheep tibial osteotomy model inoculated with Staphylococcus aureus. Source Document [22]

Table 1. Experimental Groups and Outcomes — Rittmann & Perren (1974)
Group Fixation Type Stability Infection Outcome Healing Outcome
Group 1 Two compression plates + strain gauges Absolute stability Infection localised / cleared Direct bone healing (primary intention)
Group 2 Single compression plate Absolute stability Infection localised / cleared Fracture consolidated
Group 3 Single plate with defined gap (~100 μm) or flexible plate Relative instability Fulminant, chronic osteomyelitis Septic non-union; screw loosening; sequestra formation

Seminal Conclusion: "The advantage of the stabilizing effect of implants outweighs the disadvantage of a foreign body effect." — Rittmann & Perren (1974), under the intellectual guidance of Willenegger. Source Document [24]

5. Clinical Translation: The Willenegger Protocols

5.1 The Spüldrainage (Closed Irrigation-Suction) Technique

Standardised in the 1960s at Liestal, this protocol integrated continuous irrigation with stable internal fixation for managing acute and chronic osteomyelitis. Source Document [1]

Protocol Steps:

  1. Radical Debridement: Thorough excision of all necrotic soft tissue and dead bone (sequestrectomy). Antibiotics cannot sterilise dead tissue. [4]
  2. Assessment of Stability: If the implant was stable, it was left in situ—a radical departure from automatic removal. If loose, it was removed and replaced to re-establish rigid stability. [28]
  3. Installation of Drainage: Two tubes inserted into the wound bed, with exit points 3–4 cm from the main incision through healthy skin to prevent retrograde contamination. [26]
  4. Continuous Irrigation: Inflow tube connected to antibiotic solution (penicillin, streptomycin, chloramphenicol, or neomycin/bacitracin in Ringer's solution); outflow connected to suction pump. [1, 31]
  5. Duration: 1–3 weeks of continuous flow to mechanically wash out bacteria while delivering high local antimicrobial concentrations. [31]
Clinical Efficacy: In a landmark study of 40 cases of postoperative wound infection after internal fixation (followed up to 29 years), Willenegger & Roth reported 77.5% (31/40) achieved spontaneous bone healing without bone grafting. Full working capacity was restored in 35 patients. Source Document [28]

5.2 Antibiotic Stewardship & Local Delivery

Willenegger's early thesis on sulfonamides gave him a sophisticated appreciation for the pharmacokinetics of antimicrobials. He understood that systemic antibiotics often failed to reach therapeutic concentrations in ischemic bone. The Spüldrainage was an early form of local delivery, achieving high tissue concentrations without systemic toxicity—a concept that evolved into PMMA gentamicin beads, which remain standard of care today. Source Document [1, 31]

6. The 1986 Willenegger–Roth Classification

Published with B. Roth in Unfallchirurgie, this classification provided a temporal framework for decision-making that remains cited in modern consensus guidelines. Source Document [24]

Table 2. Willenegger–Roth Classification of Fracture-Related Infection (1986)
Type Time of Onset Clinical Presentation Biofilm Status Recommended Treatment
Early Infection < 2 weeks Classic inflammation (rubor, calor, dolor, tumor), wound breakdown, high fever Immature biofilm; planktonic bacteria; vital bone DAIR: Debridement, Antibiotics, Implant Retention
Delayed Infection 3 – 10 weeks Indolent course, reduced pain, recurring sinus; often after symptom-free interval Maturing biofilm; stronger adhesion; early bone invasion Grey Zone: Depends on organism virulence & implant stability
Late Infection > 10 weeks Chronic draining sinus, pain, radiologic loosening & osteolysis Mature biofilm; bacteria embedded in glycocalyx; sequestra present Implant Removal + debridement + re-stabilisation (often external fixation)

Level III — Expert consensus based on extensive clinical experience

Biological Basis: The classification links timing to biological capacity. In the first two weeks, bacteria have not formed an impregnable biofilm; thorough debridement can "reset" the biological clock and allow implant retention. After 10 weeks, mature biofilm and necrotic bone make hardware salvage impossible—the implant itself must go. Source Document [24]

9. Second-Order Insights

The Symbiosis of Mechanics and Biology: Willenegger's work reveals that mechanical engineering (compression) and immunology (macrophage function) are not separate disciplines in trauma surgery—they are interdependent. Mechanical input determines biological output. Source Document
Contextualising the "Foreign Body" Theory: Willenegger did not disprove the foreign body effect; he showed that the negative impact of the foreign body (biofilm surface) is mathematically less than the positive impact of stability (vascular preservation). Source Document
Standardisation as Safety: By creating rigid protocols (e.g., the irrigation-suction technique), Willenegger transformed infection management from an "art" dependent on individual intuition into a reproducible "science," democratizing successful outcomes globally. Source Document

10. Global Legacy & Modern Consensus

Hans Willenegger passed away on 22 December 1998, but his paradigm endures. The recent consensus papers published by the AO Foundation and the European Bone and Joint Infection Society (EBJIS) explicitly acknowledge the debt owed to Willenegger. His 1986 classification remains the scaffold upon which modern diagnostic criteria for FRI are built. Source Document [24]

The principles he articulated—gentle soft tissue handling, preservation of blood supply, and the necessity of stability—are universal, applicable in every healthcare setting from Zurich to rural Ethiopia. The AO Alliance, building on his vision, continues capacity building in low-income countries, particularly in Sub-Saharan Africa and Asia. Source Document [17]

As the source document concludes: "Without stability, there is no cure."
