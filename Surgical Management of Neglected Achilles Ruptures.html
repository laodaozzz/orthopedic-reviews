1Introduction: The Clinical Challenge

The management of chronic, neglected Achilles tendon ruptures (CATR) constitutes one of the most formidable challenges in contemporary foot and ankle surgery. While acute ruptures are typically managed with established protocols within days of the inciting event, chronic ruptures present a distinct and far more complex pathological entity. Level I

A rupture is classified as chronic or neglected if diagnosis and treatment are delayed by more than four to six weeks following the injury. This delay transforms a simple soft-tissue injury into a complex reconstructive problem characterized by tendon retraction, extensive scar formation, and degradation of the musculotendinous unit.

⚠️ Critical Epidemiological Insight Approximately 20% to 25% of all Achilles tendon ruptures are initially misdiagnosed by the first examining physician. This high rate of missed diagnosis is frequently attributed to the subtle presentation in elderly or sedentary patients and the preservation of active plantarflexion through accessory muscle recruitment (tibialis posterior, peroneus longus/brevis, flexor hallucis longus). Level III

Pathophysiology of Chronic Rupture

Over the weeks following the injury, the proximal musculotendinous unit retracts significantly due to unopposed tonic contraction of the triceps surae. The resultant gap is filled with a disorganized, fibrous scar-tissue bridge (pseudotendon) that lacks the mechanical stiffness required for effective heel-rise and push-off. Simultaneously, the gastrocnemius and soleus muscles undergo disuse atrophy and irreversible fatty infiltration (fatty degeneration), compromising excursion and contractile force generation even if tendon continuity is surgically restored. Level IV

Key Concept: The term "neglected" does not necessarily indicate patient apathy. Many cases result from initial misdiagnosis rather than failure to seek treatment.

2Anatomy & Pathomechanics

2.1 The Triceps Surae Complex

The Achilles tendon is the strongest and thickest tendon in the human body, serving as the conjoined insertion of the gastrocnemius and soleus muscles. It inserts into the middle third of the posterior calcaneal tuberosity. The gastrocnemius, crossing both the knee and ankle, is the primary generator of explosive power, while the soleus, crossing only the ankle, acts as the primary postural stabilizer and endurance muscle. Level V

In chronic rupture, the retraction of the muscle belly places the sarcomeres in a shortened position where they are biomechanically disadvantaged. Even if the tendon is repaired with lengthening, the muscle operates on the descending limb of the Blix curve, leading to permanent weakness in end-range plantarflexion.

2.2 Vascularity & the Watershed Zone

The blood supply to the Achilles tendon derives from three sources: the musculotendinous junction proximally, the osseous insertion distally, and the paratenon (mesotenon) supplying the majority of the mid-substance via discrete vascular arcades.

🔬 Watershed Zone Vulnerability A region of relative hypovascularity exists approximately 2 to 6 cm proximal to the calcaneal insertion — the most common site for both acute and chronic ruptures. In chronic cases, vascularity is further compromised by avascular scar tissue formation. Extensive surgical dissection (such as turndown flaps) jeopardizes the delicate paratenon blood supply, increasing wound necrosis risk. Level III

2.3 Role of Accessory Muscles

In the absence of a functional Achilles tendon, the deep posterior compartment muscles (FHL, FDL, tibialis posterior) and the lateral compartment muscles (peroneals) compensate. While they can produce plantarflexion, their lever arms are significantly shorter and their cross-sectional area is a fraction of the triceps surae. This explains why patients can walk with a limp but cannot perform a single-leg heel rise. Level IV

3Diagnostic Evaluation

3.1 Clinical Assessment

Diagnosis of CATR is primarily clinical, requiring a high index of suspicion. Patients typically present with functional complaints: persistent limp, inability to run, difficulty climbing stairs, or a feeling of instability. Pain is variable; often the acute pain has subsided, replaced by a dull ache or fatigue. Level V

Test Technique Finding in Rupture Notes
Inspection Visual comparison of bilateral calves Calf atrophy; possible visible defect Defect may be obscured by scar tissue
Palpation Palpate along tendon course Palpable gap or thin pseudotendon Pseudotendon may feel continuous
Thompson Test Squeeze calf prone No passive plantarflexion Highly specific; false negatives with tight scar
Matles Test Prone, knees flexed 90°, observe foot Foot falls to neutral/dorsiflexion Particularly sensitive for length-tension disruption
Copeland's Test BP cuff at 100 mmHg, dorsiflex foot Pressure remains constant Less commonly used in modern practice
Single-Leg Heel Rise Stand on affected leg, rise on toes Unable to perform Ultimate functional test for complete rupture

3.2 Imaging Modalities

  • Plain Radiography: Non-diagnostic for the rupture itself but crucial for ruling out insertional calcific tendonitis, avulsion fractures, or Haglund's deformity. Level V
  • Ultrasonography: Cost-effective, dynamic tool that can identify tendon end separation and differentiate fluid-filled from scar-bridged gaps. Operator-dependent. Level III
  • MRI (Gold Standard): Provides three-dimensional assessment. Key parameters include:
    • Diastasis (Gap Size): Distance between viable proximal and distal tendon stumps — dictates surgical algorithm.
    • Tendon Quality: Degree of mucinous degeneration or tendinosis.
    • Muscle Atrophy & Fatty Infiltration: Graded using Goutallier classification (Stages 0–4). Severe fatty degeneration (Stage 3–4) predicts poor power generation, potentially steering toward tendon transfer. Level IV

4Classification Systems

Effective communication and treatment planning rely on standardized classification. The Kuwada and Myerson classifications are the most widely cited, both stratifying injuries by defect size. Level III

Table 1: Kuwada Classification of Achilles Tendon Ruptures
Type Description Treatment Recommendation
Type I Partial rupture (<50%) Conservative management (casting/immobilization)
Type II Complete rupture, defect ≤3 cm End-to-end anastomosis
Type III Complete rupture, defect 3–6 cm Tendon graft or synthetic graft
Type IV Complete rupture, defect >6 cm Gastrocnemius recession, turndown flap, or free tendon graft
Table 2: Myerson Classification of Achilles Tendon Defects
Type Defect Size Recommended Surgical Strategy
Type I < 2 cm End-to-end repair ± posterior compartment fasciotomy
Type II 2 – 5 cm V-Y lengthening; tendon transfer if needed
Type III > 5 cm Tendon transfer (FHL/FDL), V-Y advancement, or turndown flap
📌 Critical Surgical Threshold As the gap exceeds 2–3 cm, simple repair becomes impossible and reconstructive techniques are required. As the gap exceeds 5–6 cm, local tissue (V-Y) may become insufficient, necessitating regional tendon transfers (FHL). Level III

5Treatment Algorithm Based on Defect Gap Size

The following algorithm integrates the best available evidence. The "gap size" refers to the diastasis present after debridement of fibrous scar tissue to healthy, viable tendon. This is often larger than the gap appreciated on preoperative MRI. Level III

🗺️ Surgical Decision Algorithm

< 2 cm
Primary: Direct End-to-End Repair
Scar tissue excised; proximal gastrocnemius mobilized. Ankle placed in plantarflexion to approximate ends. If tension is borderline, a gastrocnemius recession (Strayer/Baumann) can provide 2–3 cm of extra length. High-tensile non-absorbable suture (#2 FiberWire) in locking configuration (Krackow/Kessler). Level IV
2 – 5 cm
Primary: V-Y Advancement (V-Y Plasty) or Turndown Flaps
V-Y Advancement is the preferred autologous option — it lengthens the tendon while maintaining muscle fiber continuity, avoiding devascularization. Turndown flaps (Bosworth, Lindholm) are useful at the upper range but carry higher wound complication rates. An FHL transfer may be added for poor tissue quality (chronic steroid use, elderly). Level III
> 5 cm
Primary: Flexor Hallucis Longus (FHL) Transfer
The gold standard for large gaps. Bridges the defect, provides a vascular bed, and adds a strong, in-phase motor unit. A composite reconstruction may combine FHL with V-Y advancement or turndown flap for maximum coverage. Allografts/synthetics reserved for salvage. Level III

6Detailed Surgical Techniques

6.1 V-Y Advancement (V-Y Plasty)

Historical Context: First described by Abraham and Pankovich in 1975, this technique utilizes the broad aponeurosis of the gastrocnemius to gain length. Level IV

  1. Incision: Long posterior midline incision from calcaneus to proximal calf. The sural nerve (crossing medial to lateral in proximal third) must be identified and protected.
  2. Debridement: Fibrous pseudotendon excised back to healthy fascicles.
  3. V-Incision: Inverted "V" marked on gastrocnemius aponeurosis. Geometry is critical — V limb length should be 1.5–2× the defect length.
  4. Lengthening: Incision through fascia only, preserving underlying muscle fibers and vascular supply.
  5. Slide: Traction on distal stump causes V to slide open into Y, advancing proximal tendon distally.
  6. Repair: Defect closed end-to-end with locking sutures; Y limbs sutured in lengthened position to prevent retraction.
📊 Biomechanical Outcome Isokinetic testing shows persistent plantarflexion strength deficits of 15% to 30% compared to the contralateral limb. However, AOFAS patient-reported outcomes are generally good, with most returning to daily activities without a limp. Level IV

6.2 Turndown Flaps

Table 3: Comparison of Turndown Flap Techniques
Technique Description Key Feature
Bosworth Central strip of gastrocnemius fascia (1.5–2 cm wide) turned down through distal stump Single strip; passed through calcaneal tunnel if stump is short
Lindholm Two lateral strips of fascia, twisted 180°, turned down Smooth surface placed against cutaneous tissues
Christensen Similar to Bosworth with tubularization of flap Reduces bulk for better wound closure
⚠️ Higher Complication Rate Meta-analysis demonstrates a wound complication rate of 12.5% for turndown flaps, compared to 5.7% for FHL transfers. Added bulk compresses thin posterior skin, leading to necrosis. This technique is increasingly reserved for cases where V-Y or FHL are not feasible. Level II

6.3 Flexor Hallucis Longus (FHL) Transfer

Rationale for FHL as Ideal Donor:

  • Strength: Second strongest plantarflexor after triceps surae (stronger than FDL and Peroneus Brevis). Level IV
  • Phase: Contracts in phase with the gastrocnemius during the stance phase of gait.
  • Vascularity: FHL muscle belly extends low into the ankle, allowing vascularized muscle tissue to be placed over the avascular zone of rupture. Level IV
Table 4: FHL Harvest Techniques Compared
Approach Incisions Harvest Length Fixation Trade-off
Single Incision (Short Harvest) Main Achilles incision only Shorter (harvested at tarsal tunnel entrance) Interference screw fixation Minimizes risk to medial plantar nerve
Double Incision (Long Harvest) Achilles incision + medial midfoot incision at Knot of Henry Maximum length Trans-osseous tunnel; tenodesis of distal stump to FDL Preserves toe flexion via FDL tenodesis

FHL transfer consistently produces the highest satisfaction scores for large defects. Postoperative MRI shows hypertrophy of the FHL muscle belly by up to 52%, indicating significant functional adaptation. While hallux flexion strength is reduced, few patients report functional limitations. Level III

7Emerging & Minimally Invasive Options

7.1 Minimally Invasive Repair Systems (PARS / SpeedBridge)

Systems like the Percutaneous Achilles Repair System (PARS) and SpeedBridge (Arthrex) use jigs to pass sutures percutaneously and anchor them into the calcaneus with knotless SwiveLock anchors. Level IV

  • Adaptation for Chronic: In small-to-moderate gaps (<3–4 cm), used after limited open debridement. The jig captures the proximal healthy tendon; anchors allow tensioning down to the calcaneus.
  • Benefits: Drastically reduced incision size and dissection, lowering infection risk.
  • Limitations: Cannot address massive gaps or provide biological augmentation in poor tissue beds.

7.2 Endoscopic FHL Transfer

Advanced arthroscopic surgeons can perform FHL harvest and transfer entirely endoscopically via posteromedial and posterolateral portals. This offers the lowest theoretical risk of wound complications but has a steep learning curve. Level IV

7.3 Synthetic Scaffolds

Synthetic augmentation (e.g., Artelon, LARS, InternalBrace) provides immediate mechanical stability, allowing the biological repair to heal without elongation. Particularly useful as an "internal splint" when native tissue quality is poor. Level IV

🚨 Catastrophic Complication: Deep Infection Deep infection in the presence of a large reconstruction often requires aggressive debridement, removal of synthetic materials, and coverage with vascularized free flaps (e.g., Anterolateral Thigh Flap or Radial Forearm Flap) to salvage the limb. Level IV

Sural Nerve Injury: At risk during proximal dissection for V-Y plasty and turndown flaps. Injury results in lateral foot numbness or painful neuroma. Careful identification and retraction are mandatory. Level IV

9Rehabilitation Protocols

Rehabilitation for chronic ruptures is generally more conservative than acute repairs due to extensive soft tissue dissection and reliance on tissue healing at tension. Level IV

Table 7: Postoperative Rehabilitation Protocol for CATR Reconstruction
Phase Timeline Weight Bearing Key Activities
Phase 1 Weeks 0–2 Strict non-weight bearing (NWB) Splint/cast in 20–30° plantarflexion; wound healing priority
Phase 2 Weeks 2–6 Progressive WBAT in boot Walking boot with heel wedges; ROM limited to plantarflexion/neutral; NO dorsiflexion past neutral
Phase 3 Weeks 6–12 Gradual weaning from boot Remove heel wedges; transition to shoe with heel lift; concentric strengthening begins
Phase 4 Months 3–6 Full Eccentric strengthening; proprioceptive training; return to low-impact activities
Phase 5 Month 6+ Full Return to sport (delayed vs. acute repairs — tissue needs more maturation time)

10Conclusion

The surgical management of chronic neglected Achilles tendon ruptures requires a bespoke approach that respects the altered biology and biomechanics of the injury. The "one size fits all" mentality of acute repair does not apply.

🎯 Summary Treatment Paradigm

< 2 cm
Debridement & Primary Repair — occasionally requiring gastrocnemius recession
2 – 5 cm
V-Y Advancement — provides length with autologous tissue. Turndown flaps viable but carry higher wound complication risk.
> 5 cm
FHL Transfer — gold standard providing vascularized, in-phase motor unit. Combined with V-Y for most challenging cases.

Surgeons must weigh the need for anatomical restoration against the risks of wound complications and donor morbidity. With the advent of interference screw fixation, minimally invasive jigs, and synthetic scaffolds, the morbidity of these salvage procedures continues to decline. Future research should refine indications for combined techniques and explore long-term durability of MIS approaches in the chronic setting. Level V
