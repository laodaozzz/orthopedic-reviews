1. Introduction and Epidemiology

The distal radius fracture — eponymously known as the Colles fracture when associated with dorsal displacement — represents one of the most ubiquitous and clinically significant injuries in orthopaedic trauma. Accounting for approximately 17.5% of all adult fractures presenting to emergency departments[1], this injury serves as a bellwether for skeletal health, often marking the first clinical manifestation of osteopenia or osteoporosis in the elderly population.

The incidence follows a bimodal distribution: younger patients via high-energy trauma and older patients through low-energy falls. Despite the proliferation of volar locking plate technology, closed reduction and cast immobilization remains the gold standard for a vast majority of stable, extra-articular fractures[2]. However, efficacy is entirely dependent on the quality of the initial reduction and the precision of subsequent immobilization[4].

1.1 Functional Anatomy and the "Dinner Fork" Deformity

Osseous Architecture

The distal radius normally exhibits[5]:

  • Radial inclination: ~23°
  • Radial height: 11–12 mm relative to the ulna
  • Volar (palmar) tilt: ~11°

The Deformity

In a Colles fracture, the distal fragment is sheared and displaced dorsally. The extensive comminution of the dorsal cortex creates a void, while the cancellous bone of the metaphysis is impacted. This results in the classic "dinner fork" deformity: dorsal translation, dorsal angulation (loss of volar tilt), and radial shortening[1].

DRUJ Component

The distal radioulnar joint (DRUJ) is an often-overlooked victim. As the distal radius shortens and tilts dorsally, the congruence of the sigmoid notch and the ulnar head is lost, leading to potential instability or acute dislocation[7].

Deforming Muscular Forces

Once cortical continuity is disrupted, the forearm musculature dictates fragment position:

  • Brachioradialis: Primary antagonist to reduction — its proximal and radial vector causes the hallmark radial shortening and radial deviation of the distal fragment[8].
  • Extensor tendons: Dorsal to the wrist, they contribute to dorsal angulation, acting as a tension band maintaining the deformity[1].

2. Pre-Reduction Management: Optimizing the Biological Environment

A successful reduction begins long before the surgeon's hands touch the patient's wrist. The environment must be optimized to neutralize muscular forces that resist realignment[10].

2.1 Anesthesia and Muscle Relaxation

Pain triggers a reflex arc that induces tetanic spasm in the forearm musculature, "locking" the fragments in their displaced position. Effective analgesia is a biomechanical prerequisite[10].

💡 Clinical Pearl — Large-Volume Hematoma Block Some practitioners inject a large volume (20 mL) into the fracture hematoma to "hydraulic" the tissues, creating pressure that may assist in disimpaction, though this is secondary to the analgesic effect[10].

2.2 The Physics of Traction: Finger Traps and the "90-90" Position

Manual traction is exhausting and inconsistent; gravity is relentless and consistent. The use of "Chinese finger traps" is widely considered the standard of care[11].

Finger Trap Setup

  • Digit selection: Thumb, index, and long fingers (digits 1–3) — this distribution pulls primarily along the radial column, directly opposing the brachioradialis deforming force[11].
  • Weight application: 5–10 lbs may be suspended from the upper arm in muscular patients[11].

The "90-90" Position

  1. Shoulder abduction to 90°
  2. Elbow flexion to 90°

This configuration shortens the biceps brachii (reducing tension on the radius) and allows the vertical gravitational force to act perpendicular to the humerus, creating a pure longitudinal distraction vector at the wrist[13].

⏳ The Element of Time — Viscoelastic Creep "Be patient! Allow 5–10 minutes for traction to result in disimpaction"[13]. Rapid traction triggers a myotatic stretch reflex (muscle spindle activation). Slow, sustained traction fatigues the muscle fibers and allows the collagenous elements of the soft tissue to lengthen gradually[14]. During this 10-minute window, the surgeon should not touch the fracture — this passive phase allows the fragments to "float" apart, often achieving 80% of the reduction before a single hand is laid on the patient[8].

3. The Physiology of Disimpaction: The "Unlocking" Move

A critical error is attempting to flex the distal fragment without first disimpacting it. The dorsal cortical wall of the distal fragment is impacted into the cancellous marrow of the proximal shaft — the jagged cortical spikes interlock like the teeth of two gears jammed together[4].

3.1 Why Traction Alone Fails

If the surgeon applies volar flexion force while fragments are still impacted, the dorsal cortex acts as a fulcrum, resulting in:

  1. Crushing of the dorsal cortex — further comminution, rendering the fracture more unstable.
  2. Failure of volar cortical contact — the pivot point is dorsal, preventing the volar cortices from meeting[4].

3.2 The "Make it Worse" Principle

To disengage the fragments, one must paradoxically recreate the mechanism of injury[4]:

  1. The maneuver: With the arm in traction, the surgeon grasps the distal fragment and gently hyperextends (dorsiflexes) the wrist, momentarily increasing dorsal angulation.
  2. Biomechanical rationale: This levers the dorsal cortical lip of the distal fragment away from the proximal shaft — lifting the "teeth" out of the "gears"[4, 15].
  3. Sensory feedback: The surgeon will often feel (and sometimes hear) a distinct "crunch" or sudden release of resistance — the disimpaction event[4].

3.3 The Reduction Algorithm

Following disimpaction, the clinician must apply exaggerated traction — pull the distal fragment out to and slightly beyond its anatomical length to clear the fracture spikes before aligning[8].

⚠️ Fundamental Sequence: Unlock → Distract → Reduce
  1. Unlock: Hyperextend to disengage impacted cortices.
  2. Distract: Pull longitudinally to clear bone ends (restore length).
  3. Flex: Only after length is restored should the volar flexion maneuver begin.
Omitting the first step is the most common cause of "irreducible" deformities in the emergency department[8].

4.3 The Critical Role of Pronation

Modern biomechanical evidence has largely settled the debate in favor of pronation for the standard Colles fracture[9].

Pronator Quadratus (PQ) Effect

  • In supination: The PQ is stretched — its tension creates a deforming vector that pulls the distal fragment into ulnar variance and dorsal tilt[9].
  • In pronation: The PQ is relaxed — a significant deforming force is neutralized from the fracture site[9].
📊 Biomechanical Evidence — Pronation vs. Supination A landmark prospective study found[9]:
  • Supination: Mean dorsal angulation increase of 10.7° (recurrence of deformity).
  • Pronation: Mean increase of only 4.5°.
  • Redisplacement was significantly more common in the supination group.
Source: Acta Orthopaedica — Prospective comparative study[9]. Evidence Level: LOE II

DRUJ stability: In pronation, the dorsal capsule and ligaments are tightened, compressing the ulnar head into the sigmoid notch. Pronation of the carpus also utilizes ligamentotaxis via the radioscaphocapitate ligament to correct the supination deformity of the distal fragment[7, 21].

Conclusion: Unless there is a specific contraindication (e.g., Smith's fracture), the reduction should conclude with the forearm in pronation, and the cast should maintain this position[9].

5. Immobilization Technique: The Art of Three-Point Molding

A reduction is ephemeral; immobilization is what makes it permanent. The most common cause of late displacement is not "bad bone" but a "bad cast"[22].

5.1 The Physics of Three-Point Molding

For a Colles fracture (dorsal displacement), the cast must apply pressure at three specific points to create a bending moment opposing the deforming force[22]:

  1. Point 1 (Fulcrum): Pressure on the dorsal aspect of the distal radius (over the fracture fragment), pushing volarly.
  2. Point 2 (Proximal counter-force): Pressure on the volar aspect of the proximal forearm, pushing dorsally.
  3. Point 3 (Distal counter-force): Pressure on the volar aspect of the hand/carpus (or dorsal elbow as fulcrum)[22].
💡 The Charnley Maxim "Bent plaster, straight bone." The cast often looks curved (volarly) to keep the bone straight. A straight cast will allow the bone to curve (dorsally)[22].

5.2 Step-by-Step Casting Technique

  1. The Foundation — Sugar Tong Splint
    Superior to a simple dorsal slab because it controls rotation by wrapping around the elbow. Padding: 2–3 layers of Webril — do not over-pad (thick padding compresses, creating a loose cast). Use cool/lukewarm water (hot water sets plaster too fast, denying molding time; risks thermal burns)[11].
  2. The Interosseous Mold
    The forearm cross-section is oval, not circular. Use both palms to compress the cast centrally, squeezing between radius and ulna. This preserves the interosseous space and prevents 20–30° of rotational toggle inside the cast[11].
  3. Applying the Three-Point Mold
    Stand on the ulnar side. Hand 1 (palm on dorsal wrist) pushes down (volar); Hand 2 (palm on volar forearm) pushes up (dorsal). Many surgeons use their knee against the dorsal elbow as a fulcrum for the third point. Mold the ulnar border flat and straight — a curved ulnar border encourages radial deviation (shortening)[11, 13].
🚫 Danger: The Cotton-Loder Position is Forbidden Extreme flexion (>20–30°) dramatically increases carpal tunnel pressure, compressing the median nerve. It also places extensor tendons under maximal tension, preventing finger motion and leading to stiffness and CRPS[1]. The modern standard: neutral flexion (10–15° palmar flexion) and slight ulnar deviation. The reduction is held by the three-point mold, not by extreme joint position[24].

6. Radiographic Parameters and Decision Making

The definition of "acceptable" alignment is a sliding scale based on the patient's physiological age and functional demand[25].

Radial Inclination 22–23° > 15° < 15° Altered load transfer to lunate; weak grip strength
Volar Tilt (Palmar Tilt) 11° Volar Neutral (0°) to < 10° Dorsal > 10° Dorsal Tilt Dorsal load shift, instability, decreased flexion[25]
Intra-articular Step-off 0 mm < 2 mm > 2 mm Radiocarpal osteoarthritis (90% risk if >2 mm)[6]
Carpal Alignment Congruent < 5 mm shift Subluxation Rapid joint degeneration; functional loss

Deep Dive on "The Big Three"

  1. Radial Shortening: The most functionally significant parameter. Shortening of just 2.5 mm increases the load on the ulna from 18% to 42% of total axial load, leading to chronic ulnar-sided wrist pain and impaction[6]. LOE II
  2. Dorsal Tilt: Beyond 20° of dorsal angulation, the contact patch shifts dorsally, leading to instability and eccentric wear[25]. LOE II
  3. Step-off: In young patients, an articular step-off >2 mm is a strong predictor of arthritis; in the elderly, this correlation is weaker[2]. LOE II

6.2 The Geriatric Perspective: "Acceptable Malunion"

In patients >65 years, the paradigm shifts from "Restoration of Anatomy" to "Restoration of Function."

The AAOS Clinical Practice Guidelines (2020) state with strong evidence (LOE I) that for geriatric patients, operative treatment does not lead to improved long-term patient-reported outcomes (pain, DASH scores) compared to non-operative treatment, even if radiographic alignment is imperfect[2].

🏥 Why the Geriatric Difference?
  • Lower demand: ADLs rarely require high-impact loading or extreme ROM.
  • Tolerance of deformity: Elderly wrists often function surprisingly well despite dorsal tilt >20° or shortening. Surgical stiffness may be more debilitating than malunion[27].
  • Complication risks: Screw cutout, infection, tendon irritation in osteoporotic bone.

Decision rule for the elderly: Stop reduction attempts and accept the position if the wrist is stable (no progressive collapse), carpus is aligned (no radiocarpal dislocation), and the patient has functional finger motion. Accept dorsal tilt up to 20–30° and shortening up to 5 mm in low-demand patients[27].

6.3 When to Abandon Closed Reduction — "Red Flags"

🚨 Red Flags — Abandon Closed Reduction
  1. "Elastic" Recoil: Fracture reduces but immediately snaps back — indicates volar comminution or soft-tissue entrapment; inherently unstable[16].
  2. Die-Punch Fragments: Depressed lunate fossa fragment cannot be reduced by ligamentotaxis alone (no soft tissue attachments)[21].
  3. Acute Carpal Tunnel Syndrome: Dense median nerve paresthesia that does not resolve or worsens with reduction — urgent surgical release indicated[6].
  4. Neurovascular Compromise: Any sign of limb ischemia — absolute indication for immediate surgical exploration[28].

7. Complications of Closed Reduction

Manual reduction is not benign — the forces applied can cause iatrogenic injury[1].

8. Conclusion: The Algorithm for Successful Non-Operative Management

The manual reduction of a Colles fracture is an art form grounded in biomechanics — requiring the surgeon to visualize the invisible: the interlocking cortical spikes, the tension of the periosteal hinge, and the vector of the deforming muscles[8].

  1. Unlock: Never pull against a locked fracture. Exaggerate the deformity to disimpact the cortex[4].
  2. Distract: Use time and gravity (finger traps) to fatigue the brachioradialis and restore length. Allow 5–10 minutes of sustained traction[13, 14].
  3. Reduce: Employ the Jones/Fernandez maneuver with pronation to neutralize the pronator quadratus and stabilize the DRUJ[9, 20].
  4. Mold: Apply a dynamic three-point mold with the wrist in neutral flexion (10–15°), preserving the interosseous space and straightening the ulnar border[11, 22].
  5. Decide: Adhere to strict radiographic parameters for the young (<3 mm shortening, <10° dorsal tilt), but embrace a functional, permissive approach for the elderly (>65 years)[2, 25, 27].
