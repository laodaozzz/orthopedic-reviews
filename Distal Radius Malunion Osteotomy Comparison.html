Corrective Osteotomy for Distal Radius Malunion

Evidence-Based Systematic Review Summary

1. Introduction & Epidemiology

Fractures of the distal radius represent one of the most prevalent orthopedic injuries, accounting for approximately 17% of all fractures in emergency departments and up to 18% in the elderly population.[1] Evidence Level II

While the majority heal satisfactorily, malunion remains the most frequent complication:

  • Surgically treated fractures: ~11% malunion rate
  • Non-operatively treated (closed reduction/cast): 23.5–24% malunion rate[2] Evidence Level II

Distal radius malunion (DRM) is defined by healing in a non-anatomical position, involving a complex, three-dimensional deformity with:

  • Radial shortening
  • Loss of radial inclination
  • Dorsal or volar angulation
  • Rotational malalignment

Symptomatic patients present with pain, decreased range of motion, reduced grip strength, and visible deformity. Corrective osteotomy aims to restore anatomic relationships and normalize load transmission and joint kinematics.

2. Pathomechanics of Deformity

2.1 Radiocarpal Kinematics & Load Transmission

The normal distal radius has a volar tilt of ~11° and radial inclination of ~22°.[5] Evidence Level II

⚠️ Dorsal Angulation > 20–25° Leads to dorsal intercalated segment instability (DISI) pattern. The lunate extends dorsally, the contact patch shifts dorsally, reducing contact area and significantly increasing contact pressures, predisposing to eccentric cartilage wear and early post-traumatic osteoarthritis.[2]

2.2 Distal Radioulnar Joint (DRUJ) Dysfunction

Radial shortening is the single most significant predictor of poor functional outcome.[5] Evidence Level II

ℹ️ Ulnocarpal Impaction Threshold Positive ulnar variance of just 2.5 mm significantly increases the load on the TFCC, leading to perforation, ulnar-sided wrist pain, and lunotriquetral ligament attenuation.[2]
Rotational Block: Dorsal angulation tightens the distal interosseous membrane (IOM) in pronation; radial shortening distorts the proximal and distal bands of the IOM, limiting global forearm rotation.[6]

2.3 Intra-Articular Incongruity

Articular step-offs > 1–2 mm are strongly correlated with radiocarpal arthritis development by altering contact stresses and causing rapid chondrolysis.[8] Evidence Level II

3. Preoperative Planning & Imaging

3.1 Conventional Radiography & 2D Planning

Standard PA and lateral radiographs measure radial height, radial inclination, volar/dorsal tilt, and ulnar variance. The contralateral wrist serves as a reference.[10]

⚠ Limitation: 2D planning assumes single-plane deformity. Malunions are frequently multiplanar. Studies suggest 2D planning fails to identify rotational errors, leading to incomplete correction in up to 60% of cases when strict criteria are applied.[8]

3.2 CT and 3D Modeling

For complex and intra-articular malunions, CT with 3D reconstruction is essential:[8, 11] Evidence Level II

  • Quantification of rotational deformity via axial cuts (sigmoid notch vs. radial styloid)
  • Articular mapping via 3D surface rendering
  • Virtual Surgical Planning (VSP): Contralateral mirror-image template superimposed over the malunion for precise osteotomy planning

4. Corrective Osteotomy Techniques

4.1 Opening Wedge Osteotomy

The most commonly performed procedure for dorsal malunion, particularly with radial shortening. A transverse osteotomy is created and the distal fragment is levered open, creating a trapezoidal bone void.[5] Evidence Level II

Surgical Approach: Volar vs. Dorsal

✅ Standard of Care: Volar Approach Uses the FCR–radial artery interval. The volar locking plate acts as a reduction tool: fixing the distal limb first, then leveraging the proximal limb to the shaft inherently corrects dorsal tilt. A retrospective cohort found the volar group achieved significantly better final wrist flexion than the dorsal approach.[5]

Management of the Bone Void

Graft Options for Opening Wedge Osteotomy
Graft Type Advantages Disadvantages / Complication Rate Evidence
Autograft (ICBG) Osteoconduction, osteoinduction, osteogenesis Donor site morbidity (~2% persistent pain at 2 years) Level II[15]
Allograft No donor morbidity Theoretical disease transmission; delayed incorporation Not specified
Synthetic (HA/CaP) No donor morbidity; structural support 45% complication rate, 26.3% revision rate (leakage, soft tissue reaction) Systematic Review[16]
No Graft Eliminates graft-related morbidity Requires rigid VLP fixation; medial cortical hinge intact; gaps <10 mm Level II[3]

Recent evidence suggests grafting may be unnecessary for gaps <10 mm with stable VLP fixation, showing 97% union rates and 12 weeks to union comparable to grafted osteotomies.[3] Evidence Level II

4.2 Closing Wedge Osteotomy

Involves resecting a bone wedge to correct angular deformity, allowing bone-on-bone contact for enhanced stability.[10]

⚠ Contraindication Closing wedge is contraindicated as an isolated procedure for malunions with significant negative ulnar variance, as it further shortens an already shortened radius.

The Wada Technique (Radial Closing Wedge + Ulnar Shortening)

Described by Wada et al. for extra-articular malunion with dorsal angulation and positive ulnar variance:[10, 18] Evidence Level II

  1. Plan wedge angle (Dorsal Tilt + Normal 11° Volar Tilt)
  2. Resect radial wedge; dorsal cortex often left as a living hinge
  3. Resect transverse ulnar diaphysis segment; compress with plate
✅ Superior Outcomes vs. Opening Wedge Statistically superior restoration of ulnar variance, extension-flexion arc, and Mayo Wrist Score (p < 0.01).[18]

4.3 3D-Printed Patient-Specific Instrumentation (PSI)

Represents the state-of-the-art for complex, multiplanar, and intra-articular malunions.[9, 21]

Engineering Workflow

  1. CT Acquisition → Segmentation → 3D Models
  2. Mirror contralateral radius for template
  3. Design polyamide guides with:
    • Drill cylinders (screw hole trajectory)
    • Saw slots (osteotomy cuts)
  4. Auto-reduction: Plate placed into pre-drilled holes forces fragments into alignment

Accuracy

  • 98.5% achieved acceptable limits for inclination, ulnar variance (<3 mm), and tilt
  • Rotational correction accuracy: median 2.6°
  • Intra-articular step-off reduced to mean 1.1 mm[9, 21] Systematic Review

5.1 Radiographic Outcomes

  • Opening Wedge: Restores radial length but ulnar variance restoration is variable (residual positive variance reported).[19]
  • Closing Wedge + USO: Most consistent ulnar variance restoration (mean 0 mm vs. 2 mm for opening wedge).[19]
  • PSI: Highest precision; median difference <2 mm and <3° from plan.[21]

5.2 Functional Outcomes (ROM & Grip Strength)

  • Closing wedge: statistically superior flexion-extension arc.[18]
  • PSI: significant improvement in pronation/supination (mean 25–30°).[22]
  • Grip strength reaches 70–90% of contralateral side regardless of technique.[23]

5.3 Patient-Reported Outcomes (PROMs)

  • DASH/QuickDASH: Preoperative 40–50 → Postoperative 10–20 (all techniques).[1]
  • Pain (VAS): Improves from ~6/10 to ~1–2/10.[25]
  • Clinical Ceiling Effect: High-precision techniques (PSI) often show no significant PROM difference vs. freehand techniques, suggesting further radiographic precision does not translate to perceptible patient benefit due to irreversible cartilage injury.[23]

6. Complications & Safety Profile

Overall complication rate approaches ~50% in some series, though many are minor hardware issues.[25, 26] Evidence Level II

6.1 Non-Union

  • Opening wedge (distraction): ~16.7% delayed healing/non-union (lack of cortical contact).[25]
  • Closing wedge (compression): Rare (broad contact, compressive forces).

6.2 Tendon Pathology (~20% of complications)

  • EPL Rupture: ~5% – attrition against dorsal cortex/screw tip.[25]
  • ECRL Laceration: During osteotomy cut from volar to dorsal without protection.
  • Flexor Tendon Irritation: Volar plates placed distal to watershed line → FPL/FCR rupture.[5]

6.3 Radiation Exposure

ℹ️ Fluoroscopy Dose Mean DAP for distal radius fixation: 9.24 cGy·cm². Intraoperative 3D imaging increases sensitivity (90% vs. 55%) but dose increases 10× (DAP 41.87 vs. 3.13 cGy·cm²).[27, 28] PSI theoretically reduces intraoperative radiation but pre-operative CT adds to cumulative dose.[29]

7. Economic & Health Systems Analysis

  • Non-surgical management is most cost-effective initially ($477), but malunion leading to osteotomy raises total cost to >$4,152.[31]
  • Primary ORIF ($1,309) is cheaper than late corrective osteotomy (~$6,000).[31]
  • PSI adds $1,000–$3,000 per case. Not definitively proven more cost-effective for simple malunions.[13]
  • In-hospital 3D printing is emerging as a cost-reduction strategy.[34]

8. Biological Considerations of Bone Grafting

ℹ️ The "Diamond Concept" For gaps stabilized by rigid locking plates, the local hematoma + mechanical stability may be sufficient for healing. Studies show 97% union rates without graft, challenging the dogma that all gaps must be filled.[11, 16]
  • Autograft: Gold standard for osteoinduction & osteoconduction; preferred for non-unions & large defects (>1 cm).
  • Allograft: Osteoconduction only; suitable for structural support in healthy vascular beds.
  • Synthetic (CaP cements): High compressive strength but brittle; 45% complication rate if resorption rate exceeds bone formation rate.[16]

10. Conclusion

  1. Volar Opening Wedge is the workhorse for standard dorsal malunions. With locking plates, iliac crest bone graft is often unnecessary.Level I/II
  2. Closing Wedge + Ulnar Shortening is superior for malunions with positive ulnar variance – better restoration of ulnar kinematics and functional scores.[18]
  3. PSI is indicated for multiplanar, rotational, and intra-articular malunions. Its cost is justified in complex cases by achieving accuracy impossible with freehand techniques.[8, 21]
  4. Complications are common. Distraction osteotomies carry non-union risk; tendon rupture is preventable with meticulous technique.
  5. Future directions: In-hospital 3D printing will reduce PSI costs; preventing malunion through appropriate primary management remains the most cost-effective strategy.[31, 34]
