Surgical Management of Thoracolumbar Inferior Endplate Fractures

Interactive Evidence-Based Educational Summary · Derived from provided source document · For senior residents & spine surgeons

1. Introduction: The Biomechanical Imperative of Endplate Integrity

The surgical management of thoracolumbar burst fractures constitutes a significant portion of spinal trauma practice, yet the nuances distinguishing superior from inferior endplate injuries remain under-discussed. While the classic Denis Type B fracture (superior endplate) is the most frequently encountered pattern due to axial loading combined with flexion, the Denis Type C fracture—isolated failure of the inferior endplate—presents a unique set of anatomical and biomechanical challenges. Level: not specified

The distinction is not merely academic; it fundamentally alters the vector mechanics required for successful indirect reduction via ligamentotaxis, dictates the sequence of instrumentation maneuvers, and influences the long-term risk profile regarding post-traumatic kyphosis and implant subsidence.3

1.1 The Thoracolumbar Junction

The thoracolumbar junction (T10–L2) is a zone of high biomechanical stress, representing the transition from the rigid, kyphotic thoracic spine to the mobile, lordotic lumbar spine.6 Fractures in this region are prone to progressive deformity if not adequately stabilized. The inferior endplate serves as the critical foundation for the caudal intervertebral disc; its disruption compromises this foundation, creating a distinct instability pattern that differs from the "roof" collapse seen in superior endplate fractures.2

2. Anatomical & Pathomorphological Considerations

2.1 The Disco-Ligamentous Complex and Ligamentotaxis

Ligamentotaxis—the indirect reduction of fracture fragments via tensioning of surrounding soft tissues—relies on anatomical continuity of the Posterior Longitudinal Ligament (PLL) and the annulus fibrosus.8

2.1.1 The Hourglass Morphology of the PLL

Anatomical studies demonstrate the PLL is denticulate or hourglass-shaped: significantly wider at the disc level (intimately blending with annulus fibrosus fibers) and narrower at the vertebral body level.12

  • Disc-level adhesion: PLL fibers are strongly adherent to the annulus fibrosus and endplates via Sharpey's fibers—this is the primary lever arm for ligamentotaxis.14
  • Vertebral body detachment: At mid-vertebral body level, the PLL is often separated from cortical bone by the basivertebral venous plexus and only loosely attached.12
  • Key implication: Because the PLL is tethered most securely at the disc margins, distraction forces are transmitted primarily through the annulus and PLL at the endplate level. In an inferior endplate fracture, the integrity of the caudal disco-ligamentous complex determines reduction success.1

2.1.2 The Annulus Fibrosus as a Reduction Tool

In burst fractures, the centrifugal explosion of the vertebral body often spares the peripheral annular rim while the center collapses. Tensioning the annulus via distraction pulls peripheral fragments back into alignment, buttressing the central cancellous bone. However, this mechanism is less effective if the annulus is completely avulsed from the endplate (more common in high-energy shear injuries).1

2.2 Classification: Denis C vs. Denis B

The Denis classification remains vital for describing fracture topography, even as the AO Spine system is used for severity scoring.1

2.3 The "Reverse Cortical Sign": A Morphological Trap

⚠ Critical Radiological Finding

Definition: The reverse cortical sign describes a posterior wall fragment that has rotated 180 degrees: the smooth cortical surface faces anteriorly (toward the centrum) while the rough cancellous surface faces posteriorly (toward the spinal cord).8

Mechanism: This rotation implies complete PLL avulsion or a "buttonholing" event where the fragment punches through the ligament. The PLL—if partially intact—is no longer attached in a way that facilitates reduction.19

"Pseudo-Reverse" sign: In this variant, a vertical fracture creates a depressed/tilted fragment that is not flipped 180°. The PLL often remains in continuity, and ligamentotaxis can still be effective. Differentiation requires meticulous sagittal CT analysis; axial cuts alone can be misleading.21

3. Biomechanics of Ligamentotaxis: Superior vs. Inferior Endplate

3.1 Vector Mechanics and Ligament Recruitment

When the vertebral column is lengthened (distraction) and extended (lordosis), the PLL tightens, exerting an anteriorly-directed force vector that pushes retropulsed fragments back into the vertebral body footprint.11

3.1.1 Superior Endplate (Denis B) Mechanics

Restorative force is provided by PLL fibers spanning the superior intervertebral disc. Optimal distraction is applied between the cephalad vertebra and the fractured vertebra. Lordosis closes the posterior elements, further tensioning the anterior column.1

3.1.2 Inferior Endplate (Denis C) Mechanics

The pathology is inverted. The retropulsed fragment is caudal, and the relevant PLL fibers span the inferior intervertebral disc. Standard symmetric distraction may be insufficient—the surgeon must ensure adequate distraction specifically across the inferior motion segment.4

  • Caudal anchor importance: The screw in the vertebra below the fracture bears a significant reduction load. Aggressive distraction or poor bone quality risks screw pullout or subsidence into the compromised inferior endplate.23

3.2 The Role of the Anterior Longitudinal Ligament (ALL)

The ALL acts as the fulcrum for lordotic restoration. When a lordotic moment is applied, the intact ALL prevents anterior separation, forcing the pivot point anteriorly. As the spine extends, the posterior aspect of the vertebral body opens up, creating space for retropulsed fragments to reduce.1

In inferior endplate fractures, using the ALL as a tension band via lordotic positioning is essential to restore the anterior height of the inferior cortex.22

4.1 Lordotic Distraction for Inferior Endplate Fractures

The four-step application sequence:4

  1. Contouring: Rod contoured to match physiologic lordosis.
  2. Insertion & caudal lock: Rod inserted and locked caudally first (in vertebra below fracture).
  3. Leverage: Rod levered into cephalad screws—this levering action induces lordosis across the fracture level.
  4. Distraction: Distraction applied specifically between the fractured vertebra and the caudal vertebra to open the collapsed inferior disc space and tension the PLL.

4.2 The "Flick-Up" Effect: Monoaxial Screw Mechanics

Unlike polyaxial screws (mobile head that pivots), monoaxial screws have a fixed relationship between shaft and head. When a lordotic rod is pushed into the saddle, the screw shaft changes angle to match rod curvature—this force translates directly to the vertebral body, tilting the anterior portion upward. This "flick-up" motion exerts powerful internal distraction on the central vertebral body, pulling depressed endplate fragments up via centrifugal tension of intact annular attachments.9

Studies comparing monoaxial and polyaxial screws in short-segment fixation found the monoaxial technique results in superior restoration of anterior vertebral height and better maintenance of the wedge angle. Polyaxial screws dissipate reduction force through head mobility.9,10

4.3 The Xue and Zhao Distraction-Compression Technique

Standardised Protocol4

  1. Placement: Monoaxial screws in fractured vertebra and adjacent levels.
  2. Distraction phase: Restore vertebral height, tension PLL (ligamentotaxis) → reduce retropulsed fragments.
  3. Locking: Intermediate (fracture level) screw locked to maintain height.
  4. Compression phase: Compression applied across posterior elements (cranial ↔ caudal screws) after height restoration—induces segmental lordosis, locks facets, increases construct stiffness, prevents kyphosis recurrence.27

Rationale: Distraction for anterior column (endplate reduction) + compression for posterior column (stability) = balanced reconstruction.

5. Pitfalls in AO Type A3/A4 Injuries

5.1 The Reverse Cortical Sign (RCS): The "Buttonhole" Trap

Attempting ligamentotaxis on a fracture with a true reverse cortical sign can lead to iatrogenic neurologic injury. Because the fragment is rotated 180°, the PLL is ruptured or stripped. Distraction tightens the dural sac against the sharp cancellous edge rather than pulling the fragment away.19

  • Radiological diagnosis: Axial CT "crescent" sign—convex smooth cortical side faces vertebral body; concave rough cancellous side faces spinal cord.8
  • Management:
    • Do not rely on indirect reduction.
    • Posterior option: Direct laminectomy + impactor to push fragment anteriorly.19
    • Anterior option: Corpectomy for safe canal decompression if fragment is large or adherent.17

5.2 Subsidence and the "Empty Shell" Phenomenon

Following reduction of a burst fracture (especially AO Type A4 with entire vertebral body comminution), height restoration creates a void within the centrum—the "empty shell" or "eggshell" phenomenon.33

  • Inferior endplate vulnerability: This endplate bears axial load transmitted to the disc below. Severe comminution provides little structural support.
  • Subsidence mechanism: Without anterior column support, the disc can herniate into the void (traumatic Schmorl's node), leading to loss of correction and kyphosis recurrence.4
  • Risk factors: Cage tilt angle >7.5° relative to endplate significantly increases subsidence risk.35
  • Mitigation:
    • Transpedicular grafting: Fill void via pedicle (transpedicular vertebroplasty).33
    • Index screw fixation: 6-screw construct significantly reduces endplate load by transferring stress through screws.36

5.3 The "Reverse Burst" Fracture in Osteoporotic/Kyphotic Spines

In elderly patients or those with pre-existing kyphosis, a frontal impact can cause a hyperextension mechanism on a macro scale that mimics a burst fracture.38

🚨
Positioning trap: Standard supine positioning or bolster-induced lordosis can be catastrophic—exacerbating hyperextension, causing further anterior column distraction and potential rupture of great vessels or retroperitoneal hemorrhage.38 These injuries often require stabilization in situ or with very gentle reduction.

7.2 Fusion vs. Non-Fusion

Randomized controlled trials (e.g., Wang et al.47) have shown no significant difference in clinical or radiographic outcomes between fusion and non-fusion groups for burst fractures. Non-fusion approaches offer shorter operative time, less blood loss, and potential implant removal to restore motion.47

Level I evidence from the Wang et al. RCT (5–7 year follow-up).47

For inferior endplate fractures, if the fracture heals (bony union), the motion segment may be preserved. However, if the disc is irreparably damaged (invagination), spontaneous fusion may occur regardless of grafting technique.

8. Clinical Decision Algorithm

1
Diagnostic PhaseObtain CT with sagittal and coronal reconstructions. Check: Is it superior (Denis B) or inferior (Denis C)? Is there a Reverse Cortical Sign? Assess PLL via MRI if neurologic deficit present.
2
Operative PlanningApproach: Posterior only sufficient for most (if RCS absent). Instrumentation: Short-segment with index-level screws (6-screw construct). Screw type: Monoaxial at fracture level for "flick-up" effect.
3
Intraoperative ReductionPositioning: Hyperextension on table (postural reduction). Sequence: Insert monoaxial screws → Insert lordotic rod → "Flick-up" maneuver → Lordotic distraction across inferior segment → Follow Xue/Zhao protocol (distract → lock → compress).
4
Post-Operative VigilanceMonitor for subsidence (height loss) in first 3 months. In elderly patients, beware of "Reverse Burst" mechanism and avoid over-distraction.
⚠️
Key Principle: The inferior endplate fracture demands a caudal-focused reduction strategy. The integration of lordotic distraction, the "flick-up" mechanics of monoaxial screws, and careful avoidance of the reverse cortical sign trap are the pillars of successful management.
