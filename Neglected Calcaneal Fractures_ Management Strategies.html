1. Introduction to Calcaneal Trauma

Fractures of the calcaneus represent some of the most complex and biomechanically devastating injuries in orthopedic traumatology. As the largest tarsal bone, the calcaneus serves as the foundational weight-bearing structure of the heel and acts as a critical lever arm for the Achilles tendon during gait.not specified in source [1] Calcaneal fractures account for approximately 1–2% of all fractures yet constitute roughly 60% of all major tarsal injuries, with an annual incidence of 11.5 per 100,000 individuals.Level IV [3] They disproportionately affect young, active males, particularly industrial and construction workers.[3]

The primary mechanism is high-energy axial loading — most commonly from falls from height or high-velocity motor vehicle collisions.not specified in source [1] Critically, approximately 75% of these injuries are classified as displaced intra-articular calcaneal fractures (DIACFs), meaning fracture lines extend into and disrupt the congruity of the subtalar joint's posterior articular facet.Level IV [5]

⚖️ The Central Debate Early ORIF aims to restore Böhler's angle, maintain physiological calcaneal width/height, and maximize return-to-work potential.[4] However, the extensile lateral approach carries formidable wound complication rates (up to 18%), particularly in smokers, diabetics, and patients with peripheral vascular disease.[6] Prospective trials have yielded conflicting results, with some demonstrating comparable functional outcomes between operative and non-operative management.[4,14,15]

Conservative management inherently fails to correct the initial morphological distortion. Clinical evidence demonstrates that displaced calcaneal fractures managed conservatively are 5–6 times more likely to develop severe, symptomatic post-traumatic subtalar arthritis requiring secondary salvage arthrodesis.Level IV [9]

2. The Continuum: Acute Fractures → Neglected Fractures → Established Malunions

Distinguishing between temporal phases is essential, as timing dictates biological potential and drastically alters surgical options.Level V [22]

Acute Phase (0–3 weeks)

During this window, fracture fragments remain relatively mobile. After allowing soft-tissue swelling to subside and the "wrinkle sign" to appear (typically 8–14 days post-injury), acute ORIF can be safely performed using calcaneal locking plates and screws to reconstruct posterior facet congruity.not specified in source [16]

Neglected Fracture (3 weeks – 3 months)

By the third week, early woven bone and robust callus bridge the cancellous calcaneal body, rigidly encasing previously mobile articular fragments.[23] Attempting acute-style ORIF requires painstakingly recreating fracture lines (analogous to a Romash osteotomy).[24] In severely comminuted patterns (Sanders Type III–IV), the decision shifts toward primary subtalar arthrodesis (PSTA) combined with internal fixation, acknowledging the joint surface is unsalvageable.[9]

Established Malunion (>3–6 months)

The fracture achieves complete bony union in a deformed, displaced position.[19] The acute inflammatory response has long ceased, and the patient presents with a mature, rigid, three-dimensional hindfoot deformity. Subtalar cartilage undergoes rapid deterioration into end-stage post-traumatic osteoarthritis.[18] Treatment requires aggressive osteotomies, extensive soft-tissue releases, and frequently massive structural bone grafting.[19]

3. Pathoanatomy & Biomechanical Sequelae

The morphological derangement in a mature calcaneal malunion is a highly complex, three-dimensional geometric collapse of the entire hindfoot architecture.[17] Primary pathological features include:

  • Profound loss of calcaneal height
  • Massive lateral wall exostosis
  • Varus or valgus hindfoot malalignment
  • Collapse of the subtalar articular surface
  • Secondary arch collapse
  • Multidirectional soft-tissue impingement

Loss of Talocalcaneal Height

The entire heel collapses vertically, fundamentally altering ankle and subtalar mechanics. The normal talar declination angle (~21°) is severely decreased or reversed, causing the talus to assume a horizontal position.[2] This directly produces anterior tibiotalar impingement — the dorsal talar neck strikes the anterior tibial plafond during dorsiflexion, severely restricting ankle motion and causing excruciating anterior ankle pain.[2,30] Simultaneously, the shortened heel decreases the Achilles tendon moment arm, profoundly weakening plantarflexion power.[2]

Lateral Wall Exostosis

During injury, the rigid posterior talar facet acts as an anvil, bursting the lateral calcaneal wall outward. This heals as a massive solid bony exostosis occupying the subfibular space, causing:

  • Calcaneofibular abutment (impingement against the lateral malleolus)[33]
  • Peroneal tendon compression, stenosing tenosynovitis, and recurrent dislocations[18]
  • Chronic sural neuralgia from nerve compression[18]

Post-Traumatic Osteoarthritis

Initial chondrocyte death and deep cartilage fissures, combined with residual joint incongruity, cause accelerated cartilage degradation.[19] A varus malunion shifts the weight-bearing axis laterally, overloading the lateral compartment and locking the transverse tarsal joints, rendering the midfoot completely rigid.[19,27]

⚠️ Key Biomechanical Consequence The combination of joint incongruity, rigid malalignment, and cartilage necrosis renders the subtalar joint stiff and excruciatingly painful during any attempt at inversion or eversion.[19] Patients cannot ambulate on uneven terrain and fail to accommodate standard footwear.[6]

4. Diagnostic Evaluation & Advanced Imaging

Clinical Examination

A meticulous, systematic assessment must isolate specific pain generators, as symptoms are multifactorial:[19]

  • Subtalar arthrosis: Deep aching hindfoot pain exacerbated by walking on uneven ground; pain with passive inversion/eversion.[19]
  • Lateral subfibular impingement: Sharp, localized pain over the distal fibula and expanded lateral wall; palpation reproduces peroneal tenosynovitis symptoms.[30]
  • Anterior tibiotalar impingement: Sharp anterior ankle pain during forced dorsiflexion, particularly if rigidly limited to <10° past neutral.[30]

The Coleman block test differentiates whether hindfoot varus is driven by rigid forefoot valgus or represents a fixed calcaneal structural deformity.[19] Gait analysis is highly recommended for surgical planning.[19]

Radiographic Evaluation

A comprehensive series includes weight-bearing lateral, axial (Harris), and AP views of the foot.[30] Critical measurements include:

  • Talocalcaneal height
  • Cuboid-to-floor and navicular-to-floor distances
  • Calcaneal pitch
  • Talar declination angle
  • Böhler's angle and Gissane's angle (severely depressed in malunion)[24]
  • Saltzman alignment view for coronal plane deformity[19]

CT Imaging — The Gold Standard

Computed tomography is universally recognized as the gold standard for evaluating calcaneal malunions.Level IV [28] Thin-slice coronal and axial CT provides unparalleled resolution for mapping subtalar arthritis, measuring lateral wall blowout width, confirming peroneal tendon relationships, and assessing subchondral cysts/sclerosis.[20] Modern 3D volume-rendered CT reconstructions are increasingly utilized for preoperative planning, critically determining whether enough viable cartilage exists for joint-preserving osteotomy or whether salvage arthrodesis is required.[25,27]

5. The Stephens-Sanders CT-Based Classification System

Stephens and Sanders (1996) introduced a landmark prognostic CT classification system based on coronal CT cross-sections.Level IV [26,28] Unlike acute Sanders or Essex-Lopresti systems, this classification elegantly categorizes malunions into three distinct, progressive types based on three specific anatomical derangements:

  1. Lateral wall exostosis
  2. Subtalar arthrosis
  3. Coronal plane malalignment (varus/valgus)
Table 1: Stephens-Sanders Classification System for Calcaneal Malunions
Type Primary CT Features Alignment Subtalar Cartilage Surgical Algorithm
Type I Massive lateral wall exostosis; significant subfibular impingement Neutral or minimal Largely intact; no or minimal far-lateral arthrosis Lateral wall exostectomy (LWE), peroneal tenolysis, joint-preserving intra-articular osteotomies
Type II Lateral wall exostosis present Neutral or minimal (<10°) Extensive, full-width subtalar arthrosis LWE, peroneal tenolysis, in situ subtalar arthrodesis
Type III Lateral wall exostosis; severe talocalcaneal height loss Substantial varus/valgus (>10°) Extensive arthrosis; total joint collapse LWE, peroneal tenolysis, calcaneal realignment osteotomy, distraction bone block subtalar arthrodesis
🎯 Classification as a Decision Tree The critical decision point differentiating Type I from Type II is viability of the subtalar cartilage (joint preservation vs. fusion). The critical decision point differentiating Type II from Type III is severe axial deformity and height loss (simple in situ fusion vs. distraction with structural bone grafting).[27]

6. Conservative Management

Initial management should theoretically involve a trial of non-operative therapy, particularly for patients with significant comorbidities constituting contraindications for complex reconstruction.[29]

Contraindications to Surgery

  • Severe peripheral vascular insufficiency
  • Poorly controlled diabetes mellitus with neuropathy
  • Active local or systemic infection
  • Profound osteoporosis
  • Severe soft-tissue compromise
  • Heavy, active tobacco use[9,13]

Non-Operative Modalities

  • Activity modification and weight-loss programs
  • Pharmacological management: NSAIDs for arthritic inflammation; neuropathic pain modulators (gabapentin/pregabalin) for sural neuritis[29]
  • Corticosteroid injections: Intra-articular for subtalar synovitis; peri-tendinous for peroneal tenosynovitis[29]
  • Orthotic management: Custom AFOs or rigid leather gauntlet braces (e.g., Arizona braces) to lock ankle and subtalar joints[29]
  • Accommodative footwear: Extra-depth, wide heel box to mitigate pressure on the lateral exostosis[42]
  • Heel lifts/wedges: Partially compensating for shortened calcaneal lever arm[42]
⚠️ Limitation of Conservative Treatment Non-operative treatment is inherently incapable of reversing fixed morphological changes (e.g., restoring talocalcaneal height or reducing a solid lateral exostosis).[45] Studies indicate that for the majority of symptomatic malunion patients, ≥6 months of customized orthotics fails to yield satisfactory improvements, inevitably necessitating surgical intervention.Level IV [29]

7. Surgical Algorithms: Lateral Wall Exostectomy as Foundation

Regardless of Stephens-Sanders type, the foundational surgical approach and initial reconstructive steps share common critical elements:[24]

  1. Extensile lateral approach: Classic L-shaped incision providing unparalleled exposure of the lateral wall, subfibular space, peroneal tendons, calcaneocuboid joint, and posterior facet.[8]
  2. Sural nerve neurolysis: Meticulous identification, mobilization, and retraction to prevent postoperative causalgia.[25]
  3. Peroneal tenolysis: Incision of thickened, stenotic sheath; tendons freed from fibrotic adhesions and retracted superiorly.[8]
  4. Lateral wall exostectomy (LWE): The massive malunited cortical bulge is resected flush with the lateral talar border, physically narrowing the pathologically widened heel and completely decompressing the subfibular corridor.[8]

The excised cortical bone can often be morselized and utilized as highly osteogenic local autograft material.[36] The exposed healthy cancellous bone serves as an excellent vascularized bed for subsequent procedures.

8. Stephens-Sanders Type I: Joint-Preserving Reconstruction

Type I malunions are defined by prominent lateral wall exostosis with largely intact subtalar articular cartilage and neutral or minimal hindfoot malalignment.Level IV [25,27,28]

Surgical Technique

Following LWE and peroneal tenolysis, if the posterior facet exhibits an articular "step-off" with healthy cartilage, a highly complex intra-articular corrective osteotomy is performed:[25]

  • Sagittal osteotomy along the original fracture line to mobilize the depressed fragment
  • Transverse horizontal osteotomy beneath the posterior facet, separating the articular block from the calcaneal body
  • The fragment is elevated and anatomically reduced under direct visualization and fluoroscopy
  • Structural void packed with cancellous autograft (iliac crest or recycled exostectomy bone)[36]

Secondary axial deformities are addressed via modifying extra-articular osteotomies: the Dwyer osteotomy (lateral closing wedge) or a modified lateral opening wedge for coronal alignment.[24] The Romash osteotomy purposefully recreates the original fracture lines for total three-dimensional anatomical reduction before rigid fixation with calcaneal locking plates.[24]

✅ Outcomes — Type I Joint Preservation By avoiding arthrodesis, patients preserve native hindfoot inversion/eversion, critical for uneven terrain and adjacent-joint health.[19] AOFAS scores improve dramatically from ~25–33 preoperatively to 81–86 postoperatively.Level IV [8] Radiographic parameters (Böhler's angle, talocalcaneal height) demonstrate significant sustained correction.[25]

9. Stephens-Sanders Type II: In Situ Subtalar Arthrodesis

Type II malunions exhibit severe lateral wall exostosis complicated by widespread, end-stage subtalar osteoarthritis, but hindfoot alignment remains relatively neutral (<10° malalignment) and height loss is not profound enough to cause rigid anterior impingement (forced dorsiflexion >10° present).[27,28]

🚫 Contraindication Joint-sparing intra-articular osteotomies are absolute contraindications for Type II malunions. Attempting to preserve a severely arthritic joint results in persistent, debilitating pain.[18]

Surgical Technique

The gold standard consists of LWE, comprehensive peroneal tenolysis, and in situ subtalar arthrodesis:[8]

  • Aggressive joint preparation: curettes, osteotomes, and high-speed burrs denude all necrotic/fibrotic cartilage past the subchondral bone, exposing healthy bleeding cancellous bone[47]
  • Fusion performed in situ — talus and calcaneus are fused in their current position without structural distraction[19]
  • Fixation with 2–3 large-fragment (6.5mm or 7.3mm) cannulated compression screws directed from the calcaneal tuberosity into the talar body[26]
  • Local bone morsels from LWE packed around the fusion site as biological augmentation[41]
✅ Outcomes — In Situ Arthrodesis Primary union rates: 84–94%.Level IV [51] VAS pain scores drop from preoperative mean 6.0 to postoperative mean 1.6.[8] The technique avoids iliac crest donor site morbidity and possesses significantly lower rates of delayed union compared to distraction techniques.[26]

10. Stephens-Sanders Type III: Distraction Bone Block Arthrodesis

Type III malunions represent the most devastating manifestation: hallmark lateral impingement, end-stage arthrosis, profound talocalcaneal height loss, and severe rigid varus/valgus (>10°).[27] A simple in situ fusion would catastrophically fail to address the disabled Achilles lever arm, anterior impingement, and severe varus thrust.[2]

Distraction Bone Block Arthrodesis (Carr et al., 1988)

Following aggressive joint preparation, a heavy-duty lamina spreader or articulated distractor is inserted into the posterior facet space:[2]

  • Controlled progressive distraction forces the calcaneal body plantarly and pushes the talar head/neck dorsally
  • This re-establishes talocalcaneal height, increases talar declination angle toward the normal 21°, and "unkinks" the ankle — lifting the dorsal talar neck away from the tibia, decompressing the anterior ankle joint, and restoring dorsiflexion[2]
  • A precisely contoured massive structural bone block is impacted into the distracted space as a permanent biological strut
  • Fixation with large cannulated screws as positional (non-compressive) fixation — lag compression would crush the graft and sacrifice height restoration[26]

If severe rigid varus persists, a concurrent corrective sliding calcaneal osteotomy or modified Dwyer osteotomy physically translates or angulates the tuberosity laterally to restore the mechanical axis.[19]

Graft Material Primary Advantages Critical Disadvantages Reported Union Rates
Iliac Crest Tricortical Autograft Highest osteogenic/osteoinductive/osteoconductive potential; massive structural stability; historical gold standard Severe donor site morbidity (chronic pelvic pain, meralgia paresthetica, hematoma); increased surgical time and blood loss 89–96% [2]
Femoral Head/Neck Structural Allograft Eliminates donor site morbidity; excellent structural dimensions; decreases operative time Disease transmission risk; relies on creeping substitution; significantly higher nonunion and late collapse risk in smokers 76–90% [57]

Clinical Outcomes of Distraction Arthrodesis

Meta-analyses confirm significant increases in talocalcaneal height (e.g., from 68.7 mm to 74.5 mm), restoration of calcaneal pitch, and normalization of talar declination angle (from 6.5° to 24.8°).[2] AOFAS scores improve by a weighted average of 33–44 points, elevating patients from severe preoperative dysfunction (~33 points) to highly satisfactory functional status (~79 points).Level II [54]

📊 Notable Finding While distraction arthrodesis is radiographically superior to in situ fusion in restoring geometric parameters, comparative studies often show no statistically significant difference in final subjective functional outcomes — provided procedures are accurately indicated according to strict Stephens-Sanders criteria.[47]

12. Complications & Critical Risk Stratification

Reconstructive surgery for calcaneal malunion is inherently fraught with high complication rates despite evidence-based algorithmic adherence.[6]

Wound Healing Disasters

The extensile lateral approach carries a notorious risk of delayed healing, wound edge necrosis, and infection — up to 18% in major cohorts requiring secondary intervention.[8] The risk is logarithmic in the presence of a triad: active smoking, uncontrolled diabetes, and peripheral vascular insufficiency. In patients with all three factors, catastrophic soft-tissue failure risk approaches 90%.Level IV [13]

Nonunion

Overall nonunion rates: 4–16%.[30] Smoking profoundly depresses osteogenesis; union rates in nonsmokers exceed 90% but plummet to ~75% in active smokers, especially with allografts.[30] Revision requires dead graft excision, aggressive decortication, autologous grafting, and massively augmented fixation.[2]

Neurological Complications

  • Sural neuralgia from intraoperative transection, retraction neuropraxia, or scar tissue encasement[26]
  • Complex regional pain syndrome (CRPS): Catastrophic complication with intractable burning pain, vasomotor instability, and trophic skin changes[8]

Hardware Irritation & Adjacent-Segment Degeneration

Symptomatic plates or prominent screws require elective removal in 10–15% of patients after solid union.[54] Subtalar fusion places exponential stress on adjacent transverse tarsal joints, predisposing to adjacent-segment osteoarthritis requiring potential triple arthrodesis.[17]

13. Evolving Paradigms in Calcaneal Trauma

Trend 1: Minimally Invasive Acute Interventions

The sinus tarsi approach (STA) and percutaneous reduction with crossbar external fixation demonstrate the ability to achieve anatomical reduction while statistically significantly reducing wound complications compared to the extensile lateral approach.[6] By minimizing acute soft-tissue disasters, the future incidence of devastating Type III malunions is anticipated to decline.Level IV [67]

Trend 2: Advanced Imaging & 3D Printing

3D weight-bearing CT scans enable precise dynamic topographical analysis under physiological load.[43] 3D-printed patient-specific osteotomy guides and resection templates (based on mirrored contralateral anatomy) transform subjective "free-hand" procedures into mathematically precise, reproducible corrections, eliminating historical rates of varus under/over-correction.Level IV [11,39]

Trend 3: Socioeconomic Imperative

These injuries disproportionately affect young, male industrial workers in their prime earning years. Aggressive surgical restoration is not merely a structural orthopedic endeavor — it is a fundamental socioeconomic necessity. Operatively treated patients demonstrate a significantly higher likelihood of returning to pre-injury employment compared to those managed conservatively.Level IV [4]

14. Conclusion

The management of neglected calcaneal fractures and established malunions remains among the most formidable challenges in orthopedic foot and ankle surgery. The Stephens-Sanders classification system stands as an indispensable prognostic tool, seamlessly linking the three-dimensional pathoanatomy visible on CT imaging to a logical, escalating surgical algorithm:

  • Type I (viable cartilage): Aggressive joint-preserving strategies — LWE, multi-planar corrective osteotomies — successfully decompress the subfibular space and restore normal kinematics, avoiding arthrodesis entirely.
  • Type II (arthritic, preserved height): In situ subtalar fusion reliably eliminates pain with excellent, reproducible union rates (84–94%).
  • Type III (collapse + malalignment): Distraction bone block arthrodesis is mandatory to restore the talocalcaneal relationship, clear anterior impingement, and optimize the Achilles lever arm.

While these interventions carry significant risk — particularly regarding wound healing failures and nonunion in compromised hosts — they offer the only biologically viable pathway to functional recovery. The ongoing evolution from minimally invasive acute techniques, through 3D-printed patient-specific guides, to advanced allograft biologics, continues to refine these algorithms with the ultimate goal of minimizing morbidity while permanently restoring a stable, plantigrade, pain-free foot.
