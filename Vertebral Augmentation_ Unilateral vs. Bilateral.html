Executive Summary

Percutaneous vertebral augmentation (PVA), encompassing both vertebroplasty (PVP) and kyphoplasty (PKP), is a cornerstone in the management of painful osteoporotic vertebral compression fractures (OVCFs) in the lumbar spine. A central and long-standing debate concerns the optimal surgical approach: unilateral (unipedicular) versus bilateral (bipedicular) access.

The highest level of clinical evidence, derived from numerous meta-analyses of randomized controlled trials LOE I, demonstrates a clear equivalence between unilateral and bilateral approaches in achieving primary clinical endpoints — short- and long-term pain relief (VAS) and functional improvement (ODI). Radiographic outcomes including vertebral height restoration and kyphotic angle correction are largely comparable.

⚠️ The Clinical-Biomechanical Paradox
While patient-reported outcomes are similar, finite element and biomechanical studies LOE II–III consistently show that a bilateral approach creates a biomechanically superior construct with more balanced stress distribution, reduced peak stresses on adjacent vertebrae, and lower theoretical risk of subsequent fractures.

The unilateral approach offers undeniable procedural advantages: significantly shorter operative times, reduced radiation exposure, and lower PMMA cement volume, which is associated with reduced risk of cement extravasation LOE I.

This report posits that the decision is evolving beyond a simple binary choice. The advent of advanced unipedicular techniques — curved/steerable instrumentation and high-viscosity bone cement — aims to achieve a bilateral-quality, symmetrical cement fill through a single puncture. This "Advanced Unilateral" approach combines the procedural efficiency of single access with the biomechanical stability of bilateral distribution.

Introduction: The Evolving Paradigm in Vertebral Augmentation

The Clinical Burden of OVCFs

Osteoporotic vertebral compression fractures represent a significant global health burden. These fractures most commonly occur at the thoracolumbar junction and lumbar spine, with L1 being a frequent site LOE III. The clinical sequelae extend far beyond back pain — patients suffer from debilitating persistent pain, severely limited mobility, impaired quality of life, and a cascade of complications from immobility including pneumonia, UTIs, DVT, and muscle deconditioning LOE III. Structural collapse leads to progressive kyphotic deformity, further compromising function.

Emergence of Percutaneous Vertebral Augmentation

Conservative management often provides insufficient pain relief, prompting development of minimally invasive interventions LOE III. PVA encompasses two primary techniques:

  • Vertebroplasty (PVP): Direct, high-pressure percutaneous injection of PMMA cement into the collapsed vertebral body LOE III.
  • Kyphoplasty (PKP): Insertion of inflatable bone tamps to create a cavity and restore vertebral height, followed by low-pressure cement injection into the void LOE III. Theoretically offers better height restoration, improved kyphosis correction, and lower cement extravasation risk.

The Unilateral vs. Bilateral Debate

Initially, the bilateral (bipedicular) approach was standard, based on the biomechanical principle that symmetrical cement distribution was necessary for balanced reinforcement LOE III. The unilateral approach subsequently gained acceptance due to its procedural advantages: simpler, faster, reduced radiation exposure, and lower anesthesia time LOE III.

🔑 Key Paradigm Shift
The focus is shifting from comparing one versus two puncture sites to analyzing the ultimate objective: achieving an optimal, biomechanically sound cement distribution regardless of the number of access points LOE III.

Comparative Analysis of Clinical & Radiographic Efficacy

Pain Relief and Functional Recovery

Multiple large-scale meta-analyses pooling data from hundreds of patients across numerous RCTs have found no statistically significant difference between unilateral and bilateral approaches for either PVP or PKP in VAS or ODI scores LOE I. Both techniques result in dramatic, clinically meaningful pain reductions compared to preoperative levels.

A few studies have reported minor deviations: one meta-analysis noted that long-term VAS was marginally higher in the unilateral PKP group (mean difference 0.09/10 — not clinically significant) LOE I. A separate study found that bilateral PVP was associated with better long-term VAS when cement distribution was accounted for, suggesting a link between cement fill quality and sustained relief LOE II.

Radiographic Restoration

Most pooled data indicates no significant difference in anterior vertebral height restoration or kyphotic angle correction between approaches LOE I. However, evidence is less consistent:

  • One meta-analysis found unilateral PKP associated with greater recovery of kyphosis angle LOE I.
  • Another found the post-kyphotic angle significantly lower (better correction) in the unilateral group LOE I.
  • A multi-level PVP study found bilateral technique superior in maintaining coronal Cobb angle correction over 2 years, suggesting better long-term stability LOE II.

The Clinical-Biomechanical Paradox Explained

If bilateral cement fill provides superior biomechanical stability, why does this not translate into better pain or function scores? Three hypotheses emerge:

  1. Insufficient sensitivity of outcome tools: VAS/ODI capture dramatic improvement from severe to mild pain but may not detect subtle differences related to long-term micro-instability.
  2. "Sufficient stabilization" threshold: Unilateral augmentation may cross the threshold needed to eliminate micromotion-related pain, rendering additional bilateral stability redundant for immediate symptom relief.
  3. Positive confounding from procedural benefits: Reduced surgical trauma and shorter anesthesia from single puncture may lead to faster recovery, potentially masking long-term bilateral advantages in short-term studies.

A Critical Review of Safety & Complication Profiles

Cement Extravasation (Leakage)

Cement leakage is the most common PVA complication, though usually asymptomatic LOE II. Evidence is inconsistent: several meta-analyses show no significant difference in leakage rates LOE I, while a comprehensive meta-analysis of 35 RCTs found significantly lower leakage with unilateral approach LOE I.

🔑 Key Confounders for Leakage
  • Unilateral approach consistently uses smaller cement volume, reducing intravertebral pressure and available material for extravasation.
  • High-viscosity cement (HVC) independently reduces leakage rates regardless of approach LOE I.
  • Leakage into critical areas (canal, foramina) can cause neurologic injury; venous leakage can cause pulmonary embolism LOE II.

Subsequent and Adjacent Vertebral Fractures

New vertebral fractures post-PVA occur in 8–20% of osteoporotic cohorts LOE III. Most meta-analyses show no significant difference in subsequent fracture rates between approaches LOE I. However, stratification by cement distribution reveals critical nuances:

  • Recurrence of treated-level and adjacent-level fractures was significantly more pronounced in unilateral PVP compared to bilateral PVP cohorts (both connected and disconnected patterns) LOE II.
  • Injection of higher cement volume (>3 mL) in unilateral PVP was associated with significantly increased incidence of new fractures at other levels LOE III, supporting the biomechanical stress-riser theory.

Systemic Complications

Serious systemic complications (symptomatic pulmonary cement embolism, spondylodiscitis, major neurologic injury) occur at rates well below 2% LOE II. Asymptomatic PCE may be detected in up to 26% on imaging but rarely requires intervention LOE II. Evidence does not suggest differential risk between approaches.

💡 Critical Insight
The risk of subsequent fracture is not simply a function of surgical approach but a consequence of the biomechanical characteristics of the final augmented vertebra. An asymmetrically filled vertebra risks uneven loading and re-collapse; an overfilled vertebra creates a stress riser endangering adjacent levels. The clinical goal must be a symmetrical, adequately-volumized but not overfilled, stable construct.

The Biomechanical Foundation: Cement Distribution & Spinal Stability

Finite Element Analysis (FEA) Studies

FEA studies consistently demonstrate biomechanical advantages of bilateral PVP (BPVP) over traditional unilateral PVP (UPVP) LOE II–III:

  • BPVP results in more balanced, homogenous stress distribution across the treated vertebral body, without high-stress concentration regions seen with asymmetric fills.
  • BPVP leads to lower maximum von Mises stresses on adjacent vertebrae and intervertebral discs under flexion, extension, and lateral bending loads.
  • BPVP shows significantly less maximum displacement of the fractured vertebra under load, indicating a more stable construct.

Radiological Assessment with 3D-CT

Three-dimensional computed tomography (3D-CT) enables precise quantitative evaluation of cement volume, distribution patterns, and occult leakage LOE II. Key findings:

  • In unilateral PKP where cement failed to cross the midline, patients had significantly higher follow-up VAS scores compared to bilateral PKP LOE II.
  • Purely unilateral cement distribution was associated with more significant kyphotic angle aggravation over 2-year follow-up, indicating gradual structural failure LOE II.

Quantitative Analysis with Voxel-Based Morphometry (VBM)

A groundbreaking VBM study comparing unilateral extrapedicular vertebroplasty (UEV) with bilateral transpedicular vertebroplasty (BTV) produced surprising results LOE II:

  • Despite a single entry point, UEV achieved a spatial, symmetric, and even cement distribution not statistically different from BTV.
  • The BTV group received a significantly higher relative cement volume (BCV/VBV ratio), a known risk factor for subsequent adjacent segment fractures.
  • UEV achieved a more "optimal" and sufficient fill without overfilling risk.
✅ Implication
With proper technique, a unilateral approach can achieve a biomechanically favorable cement construct comparable to bilateral techniques — supporting the concept of the "Advanced Unilateral" approach.

Advanced Unipedicular Techniques for Achieving Bilateral Fill

Foundational Technique: Optimizing Needle Trajectory

The most fundamental principle for cross-midline fill from a single side is precise needle trajectory planning. The cannula must be advanced through the pedicle with a more oblique, lateral-to-medial angle. The target is the vertebral body midline on AP view and the junction of the anterior one-fourth and posterior three-fourths on lateral view LOE V / Expert. This often requires a parapedicular or extrapedicular approach with a more lateral skin entry point LOE V / Expert.

Specialized Instrumentation

Curved Needles and Steerable Trocars

Systems such as the Stryker AVAflex and Zavation CurvePlus feature curved needles or cannulae passed through a straight outer trocar LOE V. Once docked in the pedicle, the curved instrument can be advanced and rotated under fluoroscopy to steer the tip across the midline. A meta-analysis comparing unilateral curved PVA with bilateral straight PVA found comparable clinical outcomes with significantly reduced operative time, cement volume, and leakage rate LOE I.

Steerable Curettes and Curved Balloons

Steerable curettes can create a bilateral cavity through a single approach. Novel curved balloon systems (e.g., Stryker OmniCurve) are designed to deploy across the midline from a single entry point LOE V.

⚠️ Instrument Risks
Case reports document instrument failure: handle breakage, entrapment in hardening cement, and instances where instruments required cutting and retention as foreign bodies LOE V.

High-Viscosity PMMA Cement

High-viscosity cement (HVC) reaches a dough-like consistency shortly after mixing, providing a longer working time during which it maintains stable high viscosity LOE II. This enables:

  • More controlled, forceful injection — pushing and molding cement into trabecular voids rather than passively watching it flow.
  • Dramatically reduced leakage rates compared to low-viscosity cement, confirmed by multiple meta-analyses LOE I.
  • Improved cross-midline spread from unipedicular approach due to ability to inject under higher pressure LOE II.
🔬 The "Advanced Unilateral" Concept
The convergence of optimized trajectory + curved instrumentation + high-viscosity cement constitutes the "Advanced Unilateral" approach. Most large meta-analyses group all "unilateral" procedures together, failing to differentiate basic from advanced techniques — this methodological limitation likely dilutes the true efficacy profile of the modern approach.

Synthesis & Evidence-Based Recommendations for the Lumbar Spine

Proposed Algorithm for Approach Selection

  1. Default to Advanced Unilateral Approach: For the majority of single-level, uncomplicated lumbar OVCFs, use an Advanced Unilateral approach (optimized trajectory + curved/steerable instrumentation + HVC). This offers equivalent clinical efficacy with superior procedural metrics and reduced complication risk LOE I.
  2. Bilateral Approach Indications:
    • Severe Vertebral Collapse (>75% height loss) — bilateral balloon tamps may be necessary for maximal symmetrical height restoration LOE V.
    • Complex Fracture Morphology — significant comminution, biconcave deformity, or intravertebral clefts may benefit from two-access controlled fill.
    • Significant Spinal Deformity — pre-existing scoliosis with vertebral body rotation may make bilateral approach technically easier LOE II.
    • Intraoperative "Bailout" — if unilateral attempt fails to achieve midline-crossing distribution, place contralateral needle to complete augmentation LOE II.

Technical Pearls

  • Prioritize Symmetrical Distribution: The primary goal is a stable, symmetrical cement mass crossing the midline and interdigitating with healthy trabecular bone bilaterally.
  • Utilize High-Viscosity Cement: Strongly recommended in all cases to minimize extravasation risk and maximize injection control LOE I.
  • Avoid Overfilling: Inject the minimum volume necessary for stability. For unilateral approach, typically 3–5 mL LOE II. Overfilling (>3 mL) increases subsequent fracture risk LOE III.

Future Research Imperatives

Future trials should be designed as three-arm comparisons: (1) Traditional Unilateral, (2) Traditional Bilateral, (3) Advanced Unilateral. Long-term follow-up (≥2 years) with quantitative radiological analysis (3D-CT, VBM) is needed to correlate specific cement distribution patterns with clinical outcomes, loss of correction, and adjacent segment disease rates.

🔬 Key Evidence Gaps

  • No RCTs directly comparing Advanced Unilateral (curved + HVC) vs bilateral approaches
  • Most meta-analyses group all unilateral techniques, diluting modern technique efficacy data
  • Long-term outcomes (>2 years) with quantitative cement distribution analysis are sparse
  • No consensus guidelines on approach selection from major societies
