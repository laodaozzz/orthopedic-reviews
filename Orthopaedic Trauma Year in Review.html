Orthopaedic Trauma Advancements 2025

Year in Review · Evidence Summary

1. Introduction: The Era of Precision & Physiological Optimization

The year 2025 marks a paradigm shift in orthopaedic trauma surgery—moving from an "implant-centric" focus to one defined by physiological optimization and precision protocolization. While the early 2000s saw the advent of locking compression plates and the 2010s refined intramedullary nailing, 2025 is characterized by the rigorous interrogation of perioperative biology and the resolution of long-standing dogmatic debates.

The overarching theme is a transition from binary clinical decisions—such as "operate or non-operate" or "nail versus plate"—toward a stratified approach based on specific patient phenotypes, physiological biomarkers, and large-scale registry data. Key catalysts include:

  • The PREP-IT portfolio, fundamentally altering infection prevention protocols
  • Widespread validation of Early Appropriate Care (EAC), refining timing of intervention in polytrauma
  • Integration of robotics and AI, graduating from experimental novelty to clinically validated adjuncts
Scope of This Review

Data synthesized from Journal of Orthopaedic Trauma (JOT), JBJS, Injury, Bone & Joint Journal, and The Lancet, covering Polytrauma & Resuscitation, Infection Prevention, Fracture-Specific Management, Geriatric Orthotrauma, Biologics, and Emerging Technologies.

2. Polytrauma & Resuscitation: Redefining the "Second Hit"

2.1 Early Appropriate Care (EAC) — Physiological Rationale

The historical pendulum between Early Total Care (ETC) and Damage Control Orthopaedics (DCO) has been arrested in 2025, with EAC established as the dominant evidence-based standard. The binary "24-hour rule" has been replaced by a physiological triad for determining safe definitive surgery LOE II:

  • Serum Lactate: Clearance to < 4.0 mmol/L
  • pH Status: Maintenance of pH ≥ 7.25
  • Base Excess: Levels ≥ −5.5 mmol/L

This approach allows "borderline" patients—neither stable enough for ETC nor in extremis requiring DCO—to undergo early fixation, avoiding complications of prolonged external fixation and immobility LOE II.

2.2 TBI and Fracture Fixation

A pivotal 2025 national cohort analysis of combined TBI and femoral fractures challenged the dogma that early intramedullary nailing exacerbates secondary brain injury LOE II:

Paradigm Shift

Fracture treatment strategy (DCO vs. ETC) did not statistically impact mortality rates in the TBI subgroup. The "orthopaedic insult" of reamed nailing appears less detrimental than prolonged supine positioning and traction required by DCO. Guidelines now allow early definitive fixation in head-injured patients when ICP and CPP are monitored and stable.

2.3 Biomarkers and MicroRNA

A prospective randomized investigation found significantly higher immuno-inflammatory responses (IL-6, Procalcitonin, CRP) in ETC compared to DCO patients LOE I. However, groundbreaking microRNA research revealed a nuanced trade-off:

  • DCO: More balanced systemic immune response
  • ETC: Significantly more advanced local fracture healing response, characterized by upregulation of osteogenic microRNAs at the fracture site LOE II

This molecular insight supports the EAC approach: optimize the patient rapidly to permit early fixation, capturing biological benefits of ETC without incurring the systemic penalties of the "second hit."

Key Mechanistic Insight

Iodine Povacrylex forms a durable, water-insoluble film. In closed fracture surgery—where the soft tissue envelope is compromised by edema but the skin barrier is intact—this film-forming property may offer superior protection against the specific lower-extremity microbiome.

3.1 TOBRA Trial: Intrawound Antibiotics

The TOBRA trial evaluated the safety of intrawound Vancomycin (1000 mg) and Tobramycin (1200 mg) powder in high-risk tibial fractures LOE I:

  • Nephrotoxicity: 97% of patients experienced no acute kidney injury
  • Serum levels remained well below toxic thresholds (< 2 mcg/mL at 24 hours)
  • Transient creatinine elevations returned to baseline within two weeks

3.2 Antimicrobial Resistance (AMR)

A 2025 secondary analysis of PREP-IT data found no detectable differences in AMR rates between fractures treated with or without local antibiotic strategies LOE II, providing reassurance for local antibiotic stewardship protocols.

4. Fracture-Specific Management

4.1 Distal Femur Fractures: Nail vs. Plate

Synthesis: Nailing virtually guarantees union but risks alignment; plating ensures alignment but risks healing. This has led to "Dual Fixation" (nail + plate) protocols and "Extreme Nailing" with blocking screws LOE II.

4.1.1 Fixation vs. Replacement (DIFFIR Trial)

For geriatric distal femur fractures, the DIFFIR trial suggests a trend toward lower 1-year mortality with Distal Femoral Replacement (DFR) (13.8%) vs. fixation (22.6%), attributed to immediate full weight-bearing LOE II. DFR is preferred for severe comminution, pre-existing arthritis, or extreme frailty.

4.2 Tibial Fractures: "Extreme Nailing"

"Extreme nailing"—intramedullary nailing of fractures within the most proximal or distal 25% of the tibia—has been standardized in 2025 LOE II. Despite a 32% all-cause complication rate, the technique allows load-sharing and soft-tissue preservation that plating often compromises. Success relies on blocking screws (Poller screws) to narrow the effective canal diameter LOE III.

A 2025 decision analysis found that operative management of Lateral Compression Type 1 (LC1) pelvic fractures is cost-effective for pain relief at 2 weeks and functional recovery (Majeed Score) at 12 weeks LOE II, challenging the traditional non-operative default for active patients.

5. Geriatric Orthotrauma

5.1 Care Gaps (NHFD 2025 Report)

Despite 94% survival at 30 days, the National Hip Fracture Database 2025 Report identifies persistent failures LOE III:

  • Admission Delays: Average 15-hour ED wait before ward admission, correlating with increased delirium and mortality
  • Bone Protection: Only 58% receive bone-strengthening medication; some hospitals fail to treat >75%
  • "Weekend Gap": Hospitals with weekend physiotherapy reduced LOS by 2.3 days, saving £676/patient—yet many lack 7-day therapy services

6. Emerging Technologies

6.1 Robot-Assisted Fracture Fixation (RAFF)

A 2025 scoping review confirms RAFF achieves high accuracy in fracture reduction (mean error 1.3–3.4 mm) and percutaneous screw placement, with significant reduction in intraoperative radiation exposure LOE II. The learning curve requires 10–15 cases for proficiency; current utility is highest in high-volume pelvic trauma centers.

6.2 Artificial Intelligence in Diagnostics

Deep learning algorithms for fracture detection now achieve pooled sensitivity and specificity exceeding 90% LOE II. AI assistance improves radiologists' sensitivity by 6–8% without compromising specificity. In pediatric wrist fractures, AI acts as an effective "second set of eyes." Barriers include the need for diverse training datasets to ensure equity.

6.3 Augmented Reality (AR)

AR head-mounted displays can overlay 3D anatomical models onto patients, facilitating "heads-up" surgery LOE III. Current limitations include setup time and registration difficulties in the acute trauma setting.

In-house 3D printing for preoperative planning and patient-specific instrumentation reduces operative time and blood loss in complex articular fractures. Regulatory hurdles regarding quality assurance of in-hospital manufacturing remain a barrier LOE II.

7.3 Biological Augmentation

A network meta-analysis compared biological agents for delayed unions LOE I:

  • Both PRP and BMP shorten healing time vs. standard treatment
  • The combination of PRP + BMAC showed the highest probability of improving healing rates, suggesting a synergistic "cocktail" approach

9. Conclusion

The orthopaedic trauma landscape of 2025 is defined by a nuanced maturation of the field. The "metal vs. no metal" debates have evolved into sophisticated discussions on which metal, which approach, and which timing.

The definitive findings from PREP-IT regarding skin antisepsis represent a rare "closed case" in surgical science. The SSRF mortality data provides the ethical imperative to intervene in the vulnerable geriatric chest trauma population. Conversely, the distal femur and polytrauma data suggest that "equivalence" is not "failure"—it enables patient-specific decision-making.

The Path Forward

The integration of robotic precision and bioabsorbable materials will likely accelerate in 2026. However, the core remains physiological optimization—ensuring the "Second Hit" is avoided not by delaying care, but by delivering Early Appropriate Care with the highest degree of precision.
