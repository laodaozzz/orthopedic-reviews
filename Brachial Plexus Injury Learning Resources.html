Executive Summary

The clinical management of Brachial Plexus Injury (BPI) represents one of the most intellectually demanding and surgically complex frontiers in modern orthopedics and neurosurgery. Historically viewed with pessimism, the field has undergone a radical transformation over the past half-century, anchored in the intellectual heritage of pioneers Algimantas Narakas and Hanno Millesi, whose foundational principles of careful observation and tension-free repair continue to govern surgical strategy.

The last decade—specifically 2020–2025—has witnessed a paradigm shift toward distal nerve transfers (neurotization) and a renewed focus on evidence-based outcomes, including the critical integration of patient-reported outcomes alongside surgeon-graded metrics.

Part I: Canonical Resources and Historical Foundations

To navigate modern brachial plexus surgery, one must first master the classic literature that defined nomenclature, anatomy, and surgical philosophy. The works of Narakas, Millesi, and Green's Operative Hand Surgery form the "trivium" of essential learning resources.

1.1 Algimantas Narakas (1927–1993): The Philosopher-Surgeon

Narakas's contribution was not merely technical but philosophical, emphasizing deep understanding of lesion patterns and their natural history. Severely wounded at age eleven by an exploding grenade, he spent a decade (1937–1947) in a Swiss sanatorium—a period that cultivated the intellectual reflection and manual dexterity that defined his surgical career.

Seminal Text: Brachial Plexus Lesions

Published posthumously by Springer in 2002, this unique pedagogical tool features 60 detailed drawings of lesion types and reconstruction procedures—chosen deliberately over intraoperative photography. Narakas believed only through the abstraction of drawing could essential anatomical distortions be isolated from surgical chaos. The "Narakas Club" continues to preserve this heritage.

1.2 Hanno Millesi (1927–2017): The Principle of Tension-Free Repair

Millesi revolutionized the physiology of nerve repair by establishing that tension at a repair site is the primary enemy of regeneration. He demonstrated that peripheral nerves possess intrinsic redundancy (spiral "Bands of Fontana") that allows excursion; when repaired under tension, these bands are obliterated, causing ischemia and fibrosis.

🔑 Core Millesi Principles
  • Aggressive resection of damaged nerve ends until healthy fascicular structure is visible
  • Accept the resulting nerve gap rather than attempt tensioned primary repair
  • Interfascicular nerve grafting (typically sural nerve) to bridge defects
  • Classification into supraganglionic (avulsion) vs. infraganglionic (rupture) injuries

1.3 Green's Operative Hand Surgery (8th Edition, 2021/2022)

Edited by Scott W. Wolfe, William C. Pederson, Scott H. Kozin, and Mark S. Cohen, this multi-volume set is the contemporary "Bible" of upper extremity surgery. Chapter 34 covers the entire spectrum of BPI care: diagnosis (electrodiagnostic/imaging), surgical techniques (neurolysis, repair, grafting, transfers), and secondary reconstructions (Free Functioning Muscle Transfer, tendon transfers), drawing on expertise from leaders such as Robert Spinner and Alexander Shin.

Part III: Contemporary Diagnostic Guidelines

Accurate diagnosis requires triangulation of clinical examination, advanced imaging, and electrophysiology. The primary challenge: distinguishing pre-ganglionic avulsions (requiring early transfer) from post-ganglionic ruptures (may be grafted or observed).

3.1 Clinical Examination: Bedside Biomarkers

Guidelines from the American Society for Surgery of the Hand (ASSH) emphasize specific physical signs for lesion localization.

  • Horner's Syndrome: Triad of ptosis, miosis, anhydrosis—pathognomonic for T1 avulsion where the sympathetic chain exits the cord. N/A
  • Medial Scapular Winging: Indicates Serratus Anterior paralysis (Long Thoracic Nerve, C5–C7); suggests very proximal or pre-ganglionic injury. N/A
  • Lateral Scapular Winging: Indicates Rhomboid paralysis (Dorsal Scapular Nerve, C5 only); localizes to C5 root level. N/A
  • Tinel's Sign (supraclavicular): Absent Tinel in flail arm suggests avulsion (proximal stump inaccessible). Positive Tinel advancing distally indicates regenerating post-ganglionic lesion. N/A

MRI is the preferred first-line imaging study. CT Myelography is reserved for equivocal cases where surgical planning depends critically on the status of a specific root and MRI is inconclusive.

3.3 Electrodiagnostic Studies (EMG/NCS)

Recommended 3–4 weeks post-injury to allow Wallerian degeneration. The most critical concept: preservation of Sensory Nerve Action Potentials (SNAPs) in pre-ganglionic injuries.

🔬 The SNAP Paradox

In pre-ganglionic avulsion: sensory cell bodies in the DRG remain intact → distal axons viable → normal SNAP. But the patient has complete anesthesia (signal cannot reach the brain). This dissociation is pathognomonic for root avulsion and mandates nerve transfer.

In post-ganglionic rupture: injury separates axon from DRG → Wallerian degeneration → absent SNAP.

Part IV: Surgical Indications and Timing

The 3-to-6 Month Window

For closed traction injuries (the majority of adult BPI), the standard of care mandates a period of observation with serial examination.

0–3 Months
Observation period. Neurapraxic injuries show recovery signs within 3 months. Serial EMG and clinical examination. N/A
3 Months — Decision Point
If no clinical (muscle contraction) or electrical (EMG) recovery is evident → surgical exploration is indicated.
6 Months — Critical Threshold
Outcomes degrade significantly if surgery delayed beyond 6 months. Distal motor endplates begin degenerating.
12–18 Months — Biological Deadline
After this window, even a successful nerve repair may fail because the muscle can no longer accept the signal. Muscle fibrosis becomes irreversible.

Injury-Specific Protocols

  • Gunshot Wounds: Treated expectantly unless vascular injury present. Exploration delayed 3–4 months. N/A
  • Open Sharp Lacerations: Immediate primary repair (within 24 h) if clean wound.
  • Blunt Lacerations: Tag and repair in 2–4 weeks for zone-of-injury demarcation.
  • Pan-Plexus Injuries: Early intervention favored to utilize available donor nerves before muscle fibrosis. N/A
🏥 Multidisciplinary Approach
Leading centers (e.g., Mayo Clinic) emphasize a "team approach" where orthopedic surgeons, neurosurgeons, and plastic surgeons operate simultaneously—enabling efficient harvesting of donor grafts/muscles while the plexus is explored, reducing operative time and improving outcomes.

Part V: Systematic Review of Reconstructive Outcomes (2020–2025)

The most significant development: the shift from proximal reconstruction (grafting) to distal nerve transfers (neurotization), particularly for C5-C6 injuries.

5.1 Elbow Flexion: The Oberlin Revolution

Restoration of elbow flexion is the highest priority in BPI reconstruction. The Oberlin transfer involves transferring redundant fascicles from the ulnar nerve to the motor branch of the biceps. The "Double Oberlin" adds a transfer from the median nerve to the brachialis branch.

✅ Key Finding (2025 Meta-Analysis)

The double fascicular transfer is statistically superior to single transfer. Associated with significantly greater likelihood of achieving MRC Grade 3 (M3) and Grade 4 (M4) strength. I

  • 94% of patients receiving double transfers achieved ≥M4 strength I
  • Overall success rate (≥M4) of 75% for partial BPI patients across 1,335 adults I
  • No significant functional deficit in donor ulnar/median nerve territories—safety validated I

5.2 Shoulder Reanimation: Somsak & Spinal Accessory Transfers

Shoulder abduction and external rotation are critical for positioning the hand in space.

The Somsak Procedure (Triceps → Axillary Nerve)

  • Transfer of the radial nerve branch (to long head of triceps) to the anterior division of the axillary nerve via posterior approach
  • Provides superior abduction strength compared to intercostal or pectoral transfers—attributed to high axon count and proximity to deltoid I
  • Described as the "method of choice" for deltoid reinnervation

Dual Transfer Strategy (Gold Standard for C5-C6 Avulsions)

✅ Recommended Combination

SAN → SSN (supraspinatus/infraspinatus) + Somsak (Triceps → Axillary for deltoid) = reliable abduction and improved external rotation I

Recent reviews highlight that the posterior approach for SAN → SSN allows more direct coaptation and better visualization, potentially improving recovery of external rotation. I

🧬 Biological Advantage of Transfers

Grafting requires axons to traverse 20–30 cm at ~1 mm/day. Transfers are performed 3–5 cm from the muscle, ensuring reinnervation before irreversible motor endplate atrophy. N/A

Role of Grafting: Remains standard for pan-plexus injuries (no local donors) and for bridging gaps when the SAN is too short to reach the SSN directly.

5.4 Patient-Reported Outcomes (PROs): The "Hidden" Failure

A critical insight: the disconnect between surgeon-graded success (MRC Scale) and patient perception.

🚨 The MRC–DASH Disconnect

A systematic review of 21 studies (464 participants) using the DASH score found that despite "successful" motor recovery, patients reported high levels of residual disability. I

  • Only 29.3% of patients were "pain-free" post-operatively — neuropathic pain remains a dominant factor in QoL I
  • 66% returned to work, but many changed to lower-demand occupations I
  • "Success" definitions need recalibration to include pain relief and vocational reintegration

6.2 Free Functioning Muscle Transfer (FFMT)

For pan-plexus injuries where no local muscles remain, FFMT (usually gracilis muscle) is harvested from the leg, connected to the clavicle, and vascularized via microsurgery—powered by intercostal or spinal accessory nerves. Detailed in Chapter 52 of Green's Operative Hand Surgery as the definitive salvage for restoring prehension in the flail arm.

Part VII: Pediatric & Obstetric Considerations

The Narakas classification originated in obstetric palsy, and principles overlap with adult management.

  • Group 1 (C5-C6 Erb's): Best prognosis
  • Group 2 (C5-C7): Intermediate
  • Group 3 (C5-T1): Total
  • Group 4 (C5-T1 + Horner's): Worst prognosis N/A

Recent studies show no significant difference in Mallet scores between nerve grafting and transfers in children—suggesting the "distance advantage" of transfers is less pronounced in the small pediatric limb. II

Part VIII: Summary of Key Recommendations

📋 Evidence-Based Recommendations (Source Document)
  1. Diagnosis: Use MRI as primary screening; rely on electrodiagnosis (SNAPs) to confirm pre-ganglionic avulsion
  2. Timing: Adhere strictly to the 3-to-6 month window for closed injuries
  3. Elbow Flexion: Prioritize Double Fascicular Transfer (Ulnar/Median → Biceps/Brachialis) — 94% ≥M4 I
  4. Shoulder: Prioritize Somsak Transfer (Triceps → Axillary) + SAN → SSN I
  5. Grafting: Reserve for pan-plexus injuries or bridging gaps
  6. Outcome Measurement: Integrate PROs (DASH score) and pain assessments into follow-up I
  7. Salvage: Prefer trapezius transfer over arthrodesis for flail shoulder I
