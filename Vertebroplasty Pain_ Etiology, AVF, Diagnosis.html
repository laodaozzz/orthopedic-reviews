Residual Back Pain & Adjacent Vertebral Fracture After Percutaneous Vertebral Augmentation

Evidence-Based Review

1. Introduction: The Clinical Paradox

Percutaneous vertebroplasty (PVP) and percutaneous kyphoplasty (PKP) are minimally invasive procedures that inject polymethyl methacrylate (PMMA) bone cement into collapsed osteoporotic vertebral compression fractures (OVCFs). They achieve immediate mechanical stabilization, vertebral height restoration, and rapid analgesia—returning most patients to ambulatory independence.

However, a substantial subset of patients (prevalence ≈ 19%) continues to suffer from severe, refractory residual back pain (RBP).1 Additionally, adjacent vertebral fractures (AVFs) and new vertebral compression fractures (NVCFs) represent devastating biomechanical complications that can negate the initial therapeutic benefit.

The etiology of RBP is remarkably complex and bifurcates into two primary pathophysiological domains:

  1. Bone cement–related biomechanical failures — stress concentrations, pseudoarthrosis, or mechanical impingement from the rigid PMMA construct.
  2. Intrinsic musculoskeletal and soft-tissue deterioration — paraspinal muscle degradation, fascial injury, and global sagittal imbalance generating chronic nociceptive feedback independent of the stabilized osseous structures.
Clinical Significance: Historically, clinical assessments have struggled to accurately stratify these risk factors, often leading to inappropriate revision surgeries or over-prescription of analgesics. Recent advancements in multi-modal radiomics, machine learning, and sagittal balance analysis have provided unprecedented insights into the micro-architectural and biomechanical underpinnings of RBP and AVF.

2. Etiology: Bone Cement–Related Biomechanical Mechanisms

2.1 Cement Volume and Rheological Dynamics

Both inadequate and excessive cement volumes disrupt biomechanical equilibrium through distinct mechanisms.

Inadequate cement volume is one of the most potent and direct risk factors for RBP (OR = 5.58).1 Level II Insufficient volume fails to interdigitate with surrounding cancellous bone, permitting persistent micromotion at the fracture cleft during axial loading—leading to pseudoarthrosis (nonunion), which itself carries an OR of 4.67 for chronic pain.1

  • Mild–moderate thoracolumbar fractures: Volumes of 4–6 ml are generally required.1
  • Thoracic/lumbar regions: ≈4 ml is often sufficient.
  • Thoracolumbar junction (T11–L1): Up to 8 ml may be required to restore structural integrity under the highest transitional biomechanical stress.1

Excessive cement volume renders the augmented vertebra overly rigid compared to highly osteopenic neighboring segments. This modulus-of-elasticity mismatch shifts the mechanical burden directly to the intervertebral discs and adjacent endplates, precipitating subsidence and accelerating AVFs.

2.2 Spatial Distribution Patterns: "O-shape" vs. "H-shape"

Unsatisfactory cement distribution is an independent risk factor for RBP (OR = 2.79–2.82).1 Level II

"O-shape" distribution — a single, dense, unilateral mass in one hemi-vertebra — facilitates unilateral load transfer, causing extreme stress concentration on the unsupported side, asymmetric endplate damage, and compensatory facet arthropathy. Multivariate logistic regression confirms an OR of 2.225 for RBP.5 Level III In unipedicular PKP, the prevalence of O-shape distribution is notably higher.4

"H-shape" distribution — bilateral, spongy, briquette-like cement with connecting trans-midline bridges — represents a biomechanically superior configuration. Achieved via bilateral bipedicular approaches or precise unipedicular trajectories crossing the midline, the H-shape maximizes cement–trabecular contact surface area, maintains bilateral biomechanical balance, minimizes internal fretting, and substantially reduces both residual nociception and long-term risk of ipsilateral recompression.34 Level II

2.3 Intravertebral Vacuum Cleft (IVC) and Kümmell's Disease

An intravertebral vacuum cleft (IVC) — a radiolucent shadow or gas-filled void within the collapsed vertebra — is a hallmark of ischemic avascular necrosis (Kümmell's disease) and one of the most powerful predictors of RBP (OR = 3.52–3.79).1 Level II

The internal margins of the IVC are lined with a dense, avascular fibrocartilaginous membrane that acts as an impenetrable barrier, preventing cement from interdigitating with surrounding trabecular bone. The cement cures into a smooth-walled mass encased within a biological fibrous capsule, acting as a loose foreign body that generates intense, activity-dependent internal friction and micromotion.1

2.4 Cement Leakage, Height Restoration, and Mechanical Mismatch

Intradiscal cement leakage infiltrates the pliable disc tissue, accelerating degeneration of disc architecture and destroying the disc's hydraulic shock absorption capacity. The full, unmitigated axial load is transferred directly onto the adjacent vertebral endplate, vastly increasing AVF probability.7 Level III

Over-aggressive height restoration (e.g., extreme balloon inflation in PKP) can over-stretch degenerated paraspinal soft tissues, joint capsules, and the anterior longitudinal ligament. A lower anterior vertebral height recovery rate is paradoxically protective, whereas an excessively high local kyphosis angle recovery rate (LKARR) is a significant AVF risk factor (OR = 1.94).8 Level II

3. Etiology: Musculoskeletal and Neuromyofascial Factors

3.1 Paraspinal Muscle Fatty Degeneration and Systemic Sarcopenia

Sarcopenia — the systemic, progressive loss of skeletal muscle mass, quality, and physical function — frequently coexists with severe osteoporosis. The prevalence of clinically diagnosed sarcopenia in patients with post-PKP RBP is ≈48.89%, nearly double the rate in asymptomatic controls (27.42%).9 Level II

Skeletal Muscle Mass Index (SMI) diagnostic thresholds: < 42.6 cm²/m² (males) and < 30.6 cm²/m² (females), measured at the T12 pedicle level via CT.9 Sarcopenia independently doubles the likelihood of persistent pain (OR = 2.146).9

Goutallier Classification System (GCS) on axial T2-weighted MRI at L4–L5 grades fat infiltration from Grade 0 (healthy) to Grade 4 (severe fatty replacement). Goutallier Grades 3–4 represent one of the strongest overall predictors of RBP, with an extraordinary OR of 6.25–13.76.510 Level II

Additional metrics: diminished absolute functional cross-sectional area (fCSA, OR = 3.06) and elevated fat-to-functional muscle ratio (fCSA/CSA ratio, OR = 14.38).10

Pathophysiological mechanisms:

  • Biomechanical: Loss of active muscular support destabilizes the spinal column; compensatory chronic isometric contraction leads to muscle exhaustion, ischemia, lactic acid accumulation, and chronic myofascial pain.9
  • Biochemical: Sarcopenic muscle is metabolically active with a chronic low-grade systemic inflammatory state. Degenerating adipocytes and stressed myocytes release elevated pro-inflammatory cytokines (IL-6, histamine, prostaglandins) that directly sensitize peripheral nociceptors.9

3.2 Thoracolumbar Fascia Injury and Iatrogenic Trauma

The percutaneous surgical approach requires penetration of the multi-layered thoracolumbar fascia. Posterior fascia injury — detectable as hyperintense fascial edema on MRI — is a powerful independent risk factor for RBP (OR = 3.21–5.23).1 Level II

The fascial layers are densely innervated with proprioceptive mechanoreceptors and nociceptive free nerve endings. Iatrogenic trauma induces localized inflammation, micro-hemorrhaging, and fibrotic scarring that impairs descending pain inhibitory pathways and creates a self-sustaining cycle of pain and immobility.1

Facet joint violation by the cement introducer cannula initiates acute traumatic arthritis and is a leading iatrogenic cause of RBP (OR = 7.71–8.54).2 Level II

3.3 Disruption of Global Sagittal Balance

The compounding effects of sarcopenia, muscular fatigue, and sequential wedge fractures lead to severe sagittal imbalance, forcing patients into a forward-leaning, hyper-kyphotic posture. Key radiographic parameters evaluated on full-length standing lateral radiographs:11 Level II

  • C7-Sagittal Vertical Axis (C7-SVA): Averaging 10.6 cm in symptomatic cohorts vs. 4.5 cm in controls.
  • T1 Pelvic Angle (TPA): 20.8° in RBP patients vs. 11.5° in controls.
  • Lumbar Lordosis (LL): Dropping to 26.8° in RBP patients vs. 39.2° healthy.
  • PI-LL mismatch significantly increases the incidence of intractable RBP in elderly populations.

When global sagittal balance is disrupted, the center of gravity shifts anteriorly. To prevent toppling forward, degenerated paraspinal muscles must exert continuous, exhaustive isometric contraction, causing severe ischemic muscle pain that masquerades as surgical or hardware failure. While PVP/PKP addresses the localized fracture, it cannot independently reverse severe global sagittal imbalance.

4. Advanced Prediction Models for Adjacent Vertebral Fracture (AVF)

4.1 Multi-Modal Radiomics and Micro-Architectural Analysis

Standard DEXA provides macroscopic, 2D areal data that is frequently artificially elevated by aortic calcifications and osteophytes. Radiomics bridges this diagnostic gap by converting routine medical images into high-dimensional quantitative datasets.1214 Level II

State-of-the-art predictive models simultaneously extract features from preoperative CT scans (trabecular bone density, cortical thickness) and T2-weighted MRI (bone marrow composition, edema, lipid-to-water ratios).12

From precisely delineated regions of interest, hundreds of features are extracted. Highly predictive models rely on wavelet-transformed features (e.g., wavelet-HHL_gldm_GrayLevelVariance, wavelet-LLH_glszm_ZonePercentage) that isolate subtle micro-fractures, sub-trabecular buckling, and minute texture irregularities imperceptible to the human eye. Matrices such as the Gray Level Dependence Matrix (GLDM) and Gray Level Size Zone Matrix (GLSZM) with Laplacian of Gaussian (LoG) filters quantify non-uniformity of the bone matrix.14

4.2 Machine Learning Algorithm Architectures

Advanced ML classifiers used include SVM, KNN, Random Forest, ExtraTrees, XGBoost, LightGBM, MLP, and deep learning TabNet frameworks.12 Level II To counter severe class imbalance, sophisticated models utilize SMOTE or SMOTE-TomekLink to synthesize minority class examples.12

Performance hierarchy:

  • Standalone clinical models (age, BMI, DEXA via KNN): AUC ≈ 0.74
  • Standalone radiomic models (imaging textures via SVM): AUC ≈ 0.798 (accuracy 83.9%, sensitivity 85.7%, specificity 83.3%)
  • Combined multi-modal models (MRI/CT radiomics + clinical factors): AUC = 0.886 — significantly superior.12
⚠ Overfitting Warning: Complex deep tree-based models (XGBoost, ExtraTrees) can achieve near-perfect (1.000) training AUCs but fail dramatically on external test sets (dropping to AUCs as low as 0.565) due to over-sensitivity to noise in osteoporotic bone data.12

4.3 Clinical Nomograms for Real-Time Risk Stratification

Multicenter Prediction Nomogram (Cheng et al., 2025)

Built from nearly 500 patients across multiple institutions using LASSO regression, this model incorporates seven highly independent drivers:8 Level II

  • Severely low BMD (OR 0.56 per unit drop)
  • History of diabetes (OR 3.29)
  • Higher total number of fractured vertebrae at baseline (OR 1.63)
  • Presence of IVC sign (OR 3.55)
  • High LKARR (OR 1.94)
  • Protective: regular postoperative aerobic exercise (OR 0.20)
  • Protective: strict lumbar brace use (OR 0.32)

Performance: Training AUC = 89.21%; External validation AUC = 98.33% for two-year fracture prediction.

Three-Year NVCF Prediction Nomogram (Nie/Zhao et al., 2024)

Five core risk factors:8 Level II

  • Older age
  • Lower absolute BMD
  • Lack of standardized anti-osteoporosis treatment (OR = 2.214)
  • Postoperative smoking (OR = 1.761) — nicotine induces microvascular constriction and impairs osteoblast function
  • Minor postoperative trauma (OR = 2.281)

Validation AUC: 0.786.

4.4 CT Hounsfield Unit (HU) Thresholds for Precision Assessment

Routine preoperative CT provides an accessible, precise metric for AVF prediction via opportunistic HU measurement, avoiding DEXA artifacts.1920 Level II

🔴 High-Risk HU Thresholds (Actionable):
• Superior adjacent vertebra: < 79.3 HU
• Inferior adjacent vertebra: < 61.0 HU
• Global L1 average: < 50 HU (OR = 15.26 for secondary fracture)19

These thresholds provide an immediate rationale for prophylactic interventions — extending augmentation to adjacent levels, initiating anabolic bone agents, or customizing cement volume downward to reduce adjacent stress risers.

5. Differential Diagnosis: Cement Failure vs. Musculoskeletal Failure

5.1 Clinical Physical Examination

Identifying Biomechanical/Cement Failure

Pain is fundamentally mechanical: specifically and instantly exacerbated during ambulation or posture transitions (axial load shift). It is described as sharp, highly localized, and dramatically worsened by weight-bearing. Patients typically report a "honeymoon period" of immediate relief followed by sharp recurrence weeks later, or new pain slightly above or below the surgical site.2 Level II Severe intolerance to extended walking/sitting with normal inflammatory markers (WBC, ESR) is highly indicative of structural etiology.

Identifying Musculoskeletal/Sarcopenic Issues

Pain is characterized by continuous, intractable fatigue, burning, and dull, widespread aching — often present even at rest and worsening cumulatively throughout the day.2 Level II Physical examination reveals significantly lower BMI, visible frailty, hypertonic trigger points, palpable muscle spasms, and diffuse tenderness spanning multiple dermatomes. Iatrogenic posterior fascia injury presents with localized superficial edema and extreme point tenderness directly over the surgical needle trajectory.2

5.2 Targeted Radiological Evaluation

Biomechanical failure signs: "O-shape" cement mass or IVC shadow on radiographs/CT; progressive failure of anterior height maintenance or iatrogenic over-correction; cement leakage into disc space or canal; new endplate depression/cortical buckling in adjacent vertebrae; CT evidence of facet cortical breach.2

Musculoskeletal failure signs: Goutallier Grade 3–4 fatty infiltration on T2-MRI at L4–L5/T12; critically low SMI on opportunistic CT; hyperintense fascial edema on T2-STIR MRI; severe global sagittal imbalance (C7-SVA > 10.6 cm, TPA > 20.8°, PI-LL mismatch) with structurally intact augmented spine.91011

6. Key Findings: Executive Summary

RBP: Bone Cement Factors

  • Inadequate volume (<4–6 ml): OR 5.58
  • "O-shape" distribution: OR 2.79
  • IVC (Kümmell's disease): OR 3.52–3.79
  • Intradiscal leakage → AVF precursor
  • High LKARR → OR 1.94 for AVF

RBP: Musculoskeletal Factors

  • Sarcopenia: OR 2.146
  • Goutallier Grade 3–4: OR 6.25–13.76
  • Fascia injury: OR 3.21–5.23
  • Facet violation: OR 7.71–8.54
  • Sagittal imbalance: C7-SVA > 10.6 cm

AVF Prediction Models

  • Combined ML (radiomics + clinical): AUC 0.886
  • Cheng 2025 Nomogram: AUC 0.983
  • Nie 2024 Nomogram: AUC 0.786
  • HU thresholds: <79.3 (sup), <61.0 (inf), <50 (L1)

Differential Diagnosis

  • Cement failure: mechanical, load-dependent, localized
  • Sarcopenia: constant, burning, rest pain, frailty
  • Imaging distinguishes: O-shape vs H-shape, SMI, Goutallier, fascia edema, global alignment
  • Misdiagnosis leads to catastrophic outcomes
