1. Executive Summary

The management of complex fractures complicated by severe soft tissue compromise, infection, or non-union represents one of the most arduous challenges in modern orthopedic trauma surgery. Traditional treatment algorithms have relied heavily on a dichotomy between internal fixation—which offers superior mechanical stability and patient comfort but carries high risks of infection in compromised tissue envelopes—and external fixation, which prioritizes soft tissue biology but is often associated with pin tract infections, joint stiffness, and poor patient tolerance.

"Supercutaneous Plating", also referred to as Externalized Locking Plating (ELP), Supracutaneous Plating, or the Locking Plate External Fixator (LPEF), has emerged as a hybrid technique that bridges this gap. This technique utilizes standard internal locking compression plates (LCPs) applied externally to the limb, secured via locking screws that pass through the skin into the bone. This configuration leverages the angular stability of locking head screws to provide a stable mechanical construct without the bulk of traditional external fixators or the devascularization associated with internal plating. 2. Evolution of Biological Fixation

The history of fracture management has been defined by a pendulum swing between mechanical stability and biological preservation. In the mid-20th century, the Arbeitsgemeinschaft für Osteosynthesefragen (AO) pioneered the principles of anatomic reduction and rigid internal fixation. While this revolutionized the treatment of simple fractures, the extensive soft tissue dissection required to place plates directly on the bone often proved disastrous in high-energy trauma, leading to high rates of necrosis and osteomyelitis. Historical

This failure of "mechanical" fixation in compromised hosts led to the rise of "biological" fixation, primarily through external fixators. Devices like the Ilizarov frame or monolateral rail fixators allowed surgeons to stabilize bone from a distance, preserving the delicate hematoma and periosteal blood supply critical for healing. Yet, these devices introduced their own morbidity: pin tract infections (20–50%), tethering of muscles leading to joint stiffness, and significant psychological and physical burdens on patients.

The introduction of the Locking Compression Plate (LCP) in the early 2000s marked a paradigm shift. By locking the screw head into the plate, the implant functioned as an "internal fixator," eliminating the need for the plate to compress the periosteum. It was a short intellectual leap for trauma surgeons to realize that if an LCP functions mechanically as an internal fixator, it could also function physically as an external fixator if placed outside the skin. Level II–III [1]

💡 Conceptual Synthesis Supercutaneous plating represents not a novel device, but a novel application of existing technology. It synthesizes the low-profile comfort of plating with the biological safety of external fixation—occupying the vital middle ground between the nail and the frame.

3. Indications & Clinical Scenarios

The versatility of the externalized locking plate allows it to be deployed across a spectrum of pathology, from acute trauma to chronic reconstructive challenges. The literature identifies distinct clinical scenarios where this technique offers tangible advantages over conventional methods.

3.1 Infected Non-Unions and Chronic Osteomyelitis

This represents the most robust and frequent indication for supercutaneous plating in the literature. Managing an infected non-union requires a two-pronged approach: eradication of infection and stabilization of the bone. Internal fixation is often contraindicated due to the presence of biofilm and active bacteria, while traditional external fixators are poorly tolerated for the long durations required. Level III [3]

  • Biofilm Avoidance: Unlike intramedullary nails, the screws do not traverse the medullary canal, preventing longitudinal spread of bacteria.
  • Plastic Surgery Access: The low profile (~20 mm off skin) simplifies application of VAC devices or rotation of muscle flaps.
  • Definitive Potential: The construct is often stable enough to remain in place until bony union, sparing the patient a secondary conversion procedure.

3.2 Acute High-Energy Tibial Fractures (Gustilo-Anderson Types II, IIIA, IIIB)

The subcutaneous nature of the anteromedial tibia makes it uniquely vulnerable to soft tissue stripping. Supercutaneous plating avoids an incision over the fracture site, preserving periosteal blood supply and adhering to the principles of "minimally invasive osteosynthesis." Level III [4, 5]

  • Metadiaphyseal Fractures: At the junction of shaft and joint (distal tibia/pilon), difficult to treat with IMNs due to the wide canal. An anatomically contoured LCP allows multiple locking screws in the short metaphyseal fragment. [6]
  • Polytrauma Patients: Speed of application makes it attractive for Damage Control Orthopedics—rapid stabilization without the physiological hit of canal reaming.

3.3 Septic Arthritis and Joint Arthrodesis

When a joint is destroyed by infection, the goal is often fusion. Supercutaneous plating across the knee or ankle provides a rigid, low-profile alternative that permits the patient to remain mobile and potentially weight-bear sooner than with traditional frames. Level IV [7]

3.4 Pediatric Trauma and Physeal Sparing

The technique has been described in pediatric populations. The primary advantage is the ability to place pins strategically to avoid the physis under fluoroscopic guidance. The lightweight nature of the plate is better tolerated by smaller patients, and removal in clinic avoids a second anesthetic induction. Level IV [8]

3.5 Veterinary Applications as Translational Models

Robust adoption in veterinary orthopedics (bovine, feline, canine fractures) provides "proof of concept" for its mechanical viability, as postoperative weight-bearing restriction is impossible in animals. Studies on bovine metatarsal fractures [7] and feline tibial fractures [9] demonstrate that the construct can withstand significant physiological loads and lead to union. Level III (Translational)

4. Biomechanical Characterization

To understand the efficacy of supercutaneous plating, one must analyze it not as a plate, but as a structural beam. The biomechanics differ fundamentally from both internal plating and traditional external fixation.

4.1 The Cantilever Beam Effect and Axial Stiffness

The defining biomechanical feature is the offset from the bone. In engineering terms, this creates a cantilever beam where the screws function as the beams. The bending moment (M) exerted on the screws is the product of the axial load (F) and the distance from the bone to the plate (d). Since stiffness is inversely proportional to the cube of the length in cantilever bending, even small increases in "plate elevation" can drastically reduce axial stiffness. Level II [1, 10]

⚠️ Clinical Consequence: Double-Edged Sword
  • Positive: Reduced axial stiffness promotes "dynamization" and interfragmentary motion, stimulating callus formation (secondary bone healing).
  • Negative: In heavy patients or large bone defects, the construct may be too flexible, leading to screw bending or fatigue failure.

4.2 Torsional Stiffness and Rotational Control

While axial stiffness may be lower, torsional stiffness is a distinct strength. The plate acts as a broad "shear wall." Biomechanical studies demonstrate that externalized LCPs exhibit significantly higher torsional stiffness than unilateral external fixators. Level II [12, 13]

  • Externalized stainless steel LCP: 0.686 Nm/degree
  • Standard unilateral external fixator: 0.512 Nm/degree

4.3 The Screw-Plate Interface

In supercutaneous plating, the weak link shifts to the screw-bone interface. Bicortical fixation is mandatory to distribute the load across both cortices, effectively doubling the resistance to toggling. Level II [14, 15]

4.4 Material Science: Titanium vs. Stainless Steel

  • Titanium (Ti-6Al-4V): Lower modulus (~110 GPa). More flexible, promoting more callus but potentially risking deformation under load.
  • Stainless Steel (316L): Stiffer and more fatigue-resistant (~200 GPa). Biomechanically preferable for heavy patients or femoral applications. [13]

5. Clinical Outcomes & Evidence Synthesis

The body of evidence has grown from sporadic case reports to sizable prospective cohorts. A synthesis of the literature from the last decade reveals consistent themes regarding union, infection, and patient quality of life.

5.1 Union Rates and Healing Dynamics

Healing is predominantly by secondary intention. The formation of bridging callus is a hallmark of the technique, driven by the interfragmentary motion allowed by the plate's elasticity. Level III [3, 4, 5]

  • Infected non-unions (n=19): 88% union rate [3]
  • High-energy tibial fractures (n=18): 94.7% union rate [4]
  • Distal tibial fractures: 90% union rate, mean time to union 12.1 weeks [5]

5.2 Infection Profile

The most compelling argument for supercutaneous plating is its infection profile. Level III

  • Pin Tract Infections: Superficial infection rates as low as 6.6% [5] to 12% [17], compared to 20–50% with conventional external fixators.
  • Deep Infection Clearance: 94% in cases of active osteomyelitis [3].
  • Mechanism: The locking screw does not toggle, and the lack of bulky clamps against the skin prevents moisture/bacteria trapping.

5.3 Complications

  • Screw Loosening: Most frequent mechanical complication (up to 31% in some series). Rarely leads to catastrophic failure—often results in "auto-dynamization" [3].
  • Malalignment: Low rates but notable in early learning curve cases. The LCP fixes the bone exactly as held during drilling—imperfect reduction is maintained. [3]
  • Re-fracture: Risk after plate removal if callus is not fully mature. Cautious protection after frame removal is essential. [3]

5.4 Patient Tolerance and Quality of Life

Quality of life is a major differentiator. The supercutaneous plate, sitting only ~20 mm off the skin, can be concealed under loose-fitting trousers. Patients report less interference with walking and sleeping. Level III [3, 18]

  • Pain scores: NRS 0 at rest, 2 while walking [3]
  • Device is monolateral, preventing it from hitting the contralateral leg during gait.

6. Surgical Technique & Pearls

The application of a supercutaneous plate is technically demanding, requiring the visualization skills of internal fixation combined with the planning of external fixation. [6, 19, 21]

6.1 Pre-Operative Planning

  • Tibia: The LISS-DF (Less Invasive Stabilization System – Distal Femur) plate is a workhorse. Its curvature matches the proximal tibial metaphysis and shaft.
  • Distal Tibia: The 3.5 mm LCP Distal Tibia Metaphyseal Plate is ideal for pilon fractures.
  • Length Rule: Plate ratio of 2:1 or 3:1 (plate length to fracture length).

6.2 The "Reduction First" Rule

🔑 Critical Principle Locking screws lock the bone in situ. They cannot be used to pull the bone to the plate (unlike non-locking screws). Use a temporary external fixator, distractor, or pointed reduction clamps to achieve and hold reduction. Confirm alignment in AP and lateral views before drilling a single hole.

6.3 The "Safety Zone": Plate-to-Skin Distance

This is the critical variable that determines success or failure. Level II [19, 21]

  • Optimal Range: 15 mm to 20 mm (1.5–2.0 cm)
  • Why > 15 mm? Allows for post-operative swelling without skin necrosis; allows space for cleaning.
  • Why < 30 mm? Minimizes the cantilever moment arm. Beyond 30 mm, stiffness drops dramatically, risking screw breakage.
  • Pearl: Use a spacer intra-operatively (large needle holder or stack of sterile gauze pads of known thickness) between skin and plate while inserting first screws.

6.4 Screw Insertion and Configuration

1
Fix the plate to the bone with K-wires through pin holes at the desired offset.
2
Insert a screw in the most proximal and most distal hole first—secures the "working length."
3
Check alignment again (AP, lateral, mechanical axis via cable technique).
4
Insert remaining screws. Aim for 3–4 bicortical locking screws per main fragment. Leave empty holes to distribute strain ("dot-dash" pattern).
5
Place a "drop screw" just below the fracture line in the distal fragment to minimize shear forces. [19]

6.5 Soft Tissue Management

  • Use a scalpel to release any skin tension around the screw shaft after insertion—"tented" skin will necrose.
  • Apply simple, dry dressing around the screw base. The gap between plate and skin facilitates easy daily cleaning.

Economic and Logistical Considerations

While the upfront cost of a locking plate and screws is higher than reusable external fixator components, the "Total Cost of Care" argument favors supercutaneous plating in specific scenarios: it eliminates second surgeries (definitive treatment), reduces readmissions for pin site infections, and removal does not require OR time or an anesthesia team.

9. Limitations & Contraindications

Absolute and Relative Contraindications

  • Morbid Obesity: Thick subcutaneous fat requires plate elevation >40 mm, creating excessive lever arm that exceeds mechanical limits of screws. [3]
  • Severe Osteoporosis: Screw pullout strength is the limiting factor—screws may cut out rapidly under cantilever load. [15]
  • Complex Deformity Correction: Not suitable for gradual deformity correction (e.g., bone transport). Ring fixators remain the gold standard.
  • Critical Size Defects (>20 mm): Generally require bone grafting or transport. The plate can bridge the defect but puts immense strain on the construct if healing is prolonged. [4]

Troubleshooting

  • Screw Loosening: Remove if causing pain/drainage. If healing is progressing, construct usually remains stable. Exchange for larger diameter screw if needed.
  • Skin Impingement (<10 mm): Emergency—skin necrosis requires plate elevation or soft tissue release. Prevention (15–20 mm offset) is the only cure.
  • Knee Stiffness: Avoid placing screws too close to pes anserinus or patellar tendon. Careful placement with knee flexed during surgery.

10. Future Directions

The field of externalized plating is evolving. Current research focuses on:

  • Antibiotic Coatings: Integration of antibiotic-impregnated cement coatings on the plate surface for local delivery of high-dose antimicrobials in infected non-unions. [22]
  • 3D Printed Guides: Patient-specific instrumentation (PSI) to guide screw insertion and ensure perfect reduction and offset, lowering the technical barrier to entry.
  • Dedicated Systems: Development of plates specifically designed for external use, with adjustable offsets or variable-angle locking mechanisms more robust than current internal plates.

11. Key Takeaways for the Clinician

✅ Summary of Evidence-Based Recommendations
  1. Indication: Consider for infected non-unions and high-grade open tibial fractures where soft tissue is the limiting factor. Level III
  2. Technique: Respect the biomechanics—use bicortical screws, maintain a 15–20 mm offset, and achieve perfect reduction before locking. Level II
  3. Expected Healing: Union by secondary intention (callus formation). Level III
  4. Patient Experience: Expect comfortable patients who can wear trousers and sleep well. Pain scores typically NRS 0 at rest. Level III
  5. Complication Management: Expect occasional loose screws (auto-dynamization). Deep infection is rare. Level III
