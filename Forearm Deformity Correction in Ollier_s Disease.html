Ulnar Lengthening & Radial Bowing Correction in Ollier's Disease

Interactive Evidence-Based Educational Summary · Comprehensive Clinical Report on Management with External Fixation

1. Pathoanatomy of Forearm Deformity

The management of skeletal deformities in Ollier's disease necessitates a profound understanding of the underlying pathoanatomy. The clinical presentation of a shortened and bowed forearm is the culmination of a complex biological and biomechanical cascade initiated at the cellular level.

1.1 Pathogenesis of Enchondromatosis and Physeal Growth Disruption

Ollier's disease (multiple enchondromatosis) is a rare, non-hereditary skeletal dysplasia defined by the presence of multiple benign cartilaginous tumors—enchondromas—within the medullary cavity of affected bones, with a prevalence of approximately 1 in 100,000 individuals.[1] Level IV

The disease is not inherited; its etiology is linked to somatic mosaic mutations in IDH1, IDH2, and less frequently PTHR1.[3] These post-zygotic mutations are present in only a percentage of the body's cells, explaining the characteristic asymmetric and often unilateral distribution of lesions.[4]

The fundamental pathology is a disruption of normal endochondral ossification.[8] The genetic mutations lead to abnormal proliferation of ectopic islands of hyaline cartilage within the metaphyses, near the physes. These enchondromatous lesions interfere directly with the orderly process of longitudinal bone growth by acting as a mechanical and biological tether, leading to progressive shortening of the affected bone.[1] This growth inhibition is the primary driver of both limb length discrepancy (LLD) and complex angular deformities.

1.2 Biomechanical Cascade: Ulnar Shortening, Radial Bowing, and Wrist Instability

When the forearm is affected, enchondromas demonstrate a predilection for the distal ulna.[11] The tethering effect on the distal ulnar physis results in progressive, relative ulnar shortening. The radius, often less affected, continues normal growth. This differential growth creates a significant length mismatch between the two forearm bones.[11]

This length discrepancy initiates a predictable biomechanical cascade:

  1. Ulnar shortening – primary pathology from enchondromatous physeal tethering
  2. Radial bowing – secondary deformity as the constrained radius outgrows the tethered ulna[11]
  3. Wrist deformity – ulnar deviation of the hand, increased radial articular angle, and carpal slip indicating instability[7]
  4. Radial head dislocation – in severe cases, proximal tethering can lead to subluxation or dislocation at the elbow, compromising rotation[12]

1.3 Pseudo-Madelung Deformity: A Critical Distinction

The forearm appearance in Ollier's disease can closely resemble Madelung deformity, classified as a "pseudo-Madelung" variant.[15] Understanding the distinction is the fundamental principle guiding surgical intervention.

Table A: True Madelung vs. Pseudo-Madelung in Ollier's Disease
Feature True Madelung Deformity Pseudo-Madelung (Ollier's)
Primary pathology Vickers ligament tethers distal radial physis[17] Enchondroma-induced distal ulnar growth arrest[15]
Primary bone affected Radius Ulna
Ulnar variance Positive (relatively long ulna)[16] Negative (short ulna)[15]

⚠ Critical Surgical Principle

Procedures designed for true Madelung deformity (e.g., Vickers ligament resection) would be entirely ineffective in Ollier's disease. The logical surgical approach must address the shortened ulna as the root of the biomechanical cascade.[17]

2. Clinical Evaluation & Pre-operative Strategy

2.1 Diagnostic Imaging and Radiographic Analysis

Diagnosis is primarily established through radiographic evaluation showing multiple, asymmetric, lucent, expansile enchondromatous lesions within metaphyses.[1] For pre-operative planning, standardized AP and lateral radiographs of the entire forearm (including wrist and elbow) are mandatory.

Table 1: Radiographic Parameters for Pre- and Post-operative Assessment[23]
Parameter Definition Normal Range Clinical Significance in Ollier's
Ulnar Variance (UV) Vertical distance between radial lunate fossa line and distal ulnar head -2.5 to +0.5 mm Quantifies primary ulnar shortening. Large negative value = significant shortening[13]
Radial Articular Angle (RAA) Angle between perpendicular to radial shaft and distal articular surface (AP view) 21–25°[20] Measures ulnar tilt. Increased angle = hallmark deformity & carpal instability[11]
Carpal Slip (CS) % of lunate lying ulnar to long axis of radius on AP view < 50%[11] Quantifies ulnar translocation = direct measure of wrist instability[11]

Radiocapitellar Alignment Line through radial shaft center should pass through capitellum center (AP & lateral) Centered Assesses radial head subluxation/dislocation[13]

Advanced imaging: CT with 3D reconstructions provides precise planning of osteotomy levels/angles and patient-specific guides.[21] MRI assesses cartilaginous lesion extent, physeal relationship, and soft tissue status (TFCC, ligaments).[28]

2.2 Surgical Indications and Timing

Surgery is not universally required. Indications include:[7,12]

  • Functional impairment – progressive loss of forearm rotation or wrist motion[7]
  • Pain – wrist pain from ulnocarpal impaction or instability[12]
  • Instability – progressive wrist subluxation or radial head dislocation[12]
  • Cosmetic deformity – visible, progressive bowing/deviation of concern to patient and family
ℹ Timing Considerations

Debate exists on timing. One philosophy delays surgery until near skeletal maturity to minimize recurrence risk.[24] However, for progressive radial head subluxation, earlier intervention is strongly recommended to preserve elbow congruity and prevent irreducible dislocation.[31,32] Surgery is typically deferred until the deformity becomes pronounced (generally after age 6).[30]

Pre-operative planning must be predictive, accounting for remaining growth. The Paley Multiplier Method can predict final LLD at skeletal maturity, enabling a staged "reconstructive life plan" with multiple procedures throughout childhood and adolescence.[1]

2.3 Formulating the Surgical Plan

Osteotomy Site Selection

  • Proximal ulna – if radial head dislocation exists; provides direct corrective force for reduction[14]
  • Distal ulna – if deformity is confined to wrist/distal forearm without elbow pathology[31]
  • At the CORA (Center of Rotation of Angulation) – if the ulna has angular deformity; allows simultaneous lengthening and angular correction[31]

External Fixator Choice

3. Principles of Distraction Osteogenesis

3.1 Biology of Callotasis

Distraction osteogenesis (DO), pioneered by Gavriil Ilizarov, leverages the body's innate bone healing under controlled mechanical tension.[35] The process proceeds through four phases:

  1. Osteotomy – low-energy cut preserving periosteum and blood supply
  2. Latency phase (5–7 days) – no distraction; allows hematoma, inflammation, and soft callus formation[7,36]
  3. Distraction phase – 1 mm/day in 0.25 mm increments (q6h); stimulates collagen scaffold formation and intramembranous ossification[7,36]
  4. Consolidation phase – 1.5–2× the distraction duration; mineralization and remodeling of woven bone into cortical lamellar bone[36]

3.2 Unique Considerations in Ollier's Disease

✦ Key Biological Insight

Patients with Ollier's disease exhibit an accelerated rate of bone formation during DO compared to patients with other conditions.[1] This vigorous healing can be advantageous but introduces a specific risk: premature consolidation – new bone bridging the gap before target length is achieved.[6]

Intralesional osteotomy: Performing the osteotomy directly through an enchondromatous lesion has a therapeutic effect. The controlled mechanical tension stimulates conversion of abnormal enchondroma cartilage into healthy lamellar bone.[7,8] This remarkable phenomenon means the procedure simultaneously corrects deformity and treats the underlying lesion—eliminating the need for separate tumor removal.[8]

This suggests that mechanical forces of distraction can override the aberrant genetic signaling promoting enchondroma formation, making DO a uniquely suitable treatment modality—not merely a lengthening procedure, but a form of localized mechanotherapy that regenerates normal bone from pathological tissue.

4. Surgical Technique

4.1 Ulnar Osteotomy

The ulna is approached through a longitudinal incision along its subcutaneous posterior border, between FCU and ECU compartments.[27] Minimally invasive technique preserves periosteal blood supply. A common technique uses small-diameter drill holes connected with a narrow osteotome.[32] The osteotomy may initially be left incomplete, with the final cortical bridge manually fractured after fixator pin placement.[40]

Key principle: intralesional osteotomy is performed directly through the enchondromatous lesion, leveraging the therapeutic effect of DO and mitigating risk of uncontrolled fracture through weakened bone.[7,30]

4.2 External Fixator Application

Pins/wires are inserted through anatomically "safe zones" using small incisions and blunt dissection to avoid neurovascular injury.[40] A minimum of two fixation points per bone segment is required for stability. For circular frames applied to the proximal forearm, specialized step-cut half-rings accommodate elbow flexion.[11]

4.3 Management of Radial Bowing

This represents a critical intraoperative decision point:

Table B: Decision Framework for Radial Bowing Management
Strategy Indications Rationale
Ulnar lengthening alone Children <10–12 years with moderate, flexible bowing[31] Releases ulnar tether; leverages natural remodeling potential of growing radius. Avoids morbidity of radial osteotomy
Combined: ulnar lengthening + radial osteotomy Older adolescents with diminished growth; severe, rigid radial bow[11] Radial osteotomy (closing/opening wedge) with internal plate fixation provides immediate, definitive correction

4.4 Intraoperative Assessment

Fluoscopy guides osteotomies, confirms pin placement, and assesses correction. If radial head dislocation exists, intraoperative distraction can demonstrate radial head reduction with the capitellum.[11] Stability is tested through full ROM. Adjunctive measures may include temporary K-wire fixation across the DRUJ to enhance wrist stability during initial lengthening.[11]

5. Post-operative Management & Rehabilitation

5.1 The Three Phases of Management

  • Latency phase (Days 1–7): Pain control, elevation, neurovascular monitoring, patient/family education on fixator adjustments and pin care[38]
  • Distraction phase (begins ~1 week): Family performs 1 mm/day distraction in 4 increments. Weekly/bi-weekly clinical and radiographic monitoring. In Ollier's: vigilant for premature consolidation; may need to increase rate to 1.25–1.5 mm/day[30]
  • Consolidation phase: Fixator remains as stabilizing frame for 1.5–2× distraction duration. Removed when ≥3 cortices visible on radiographs. Limb protected in cast/brace 4–6 weeks after removal[6]

5.2 Pin Site Care Protocol

Daily cleaning of pin-skin interface with sterile swabs and saline/mild antiseptic removes crust and drainage harboring bacteria.[38] Routine antibiotic ointment application is generally not recommended (risk of maceration).[42]

5.3 Critical Role of Physical/Occupational Therapy

🚨 Essential Principle

The surgery lengthens bone, but therapy preserves function. Without aggressive concurrent therapy, soft tissues will contract, leading to severe, potentially permanent joint stiffness—rendering an anatomically successful lengthening a functional failure.[6]

Therapy begins immediately post-op (gentle finger AROM day 1) and continues relentlessly:[43]

  • Distraction phase: Daily multiple sessions for full passive/active ROM of wrist, forearm, and elbow. Dynamic/static-progressive splinting between sessions[11]
  • Consolidation phase: Maintaining flexibility + gradual strengthening introduction[48]
  • Post-fixator removal: Focus on full motion restoration, strength rebuilding, and return to function[48]

6.2 Functional Outcomes

Most studies report forearm and elbow ROM is well-preserved or improved.[11] Specific improvements in pronation/supination have been documented.[31] Excellent pain relief from joint incongruity/instability.[35]

The Bone Healing Index in Ollier's reflects robust healing potential: reported values range from 18.3 to 50.5 days/cm.[6]

6.3 Long-Term Results

Results are generally well-maintained into adolescence/adulthood, though mild loss of correction can occur with remaining growth.[11] A significant long-term benefit: stable conversion of pathological enchondroma into healthy bone with no lesion recurrence in the regenerate.[34]

Patient satisfaction is overwhelmingly positive. DASH scores show significant functional/quality-of-life improvements.[50] The vast majority of patients state they would undergo the same treatment again.[50]

7. Complications & Mitigation Strategies

7.1 Pin Tract Infections

The most frequent complication.[46] Majority are superficial (redness, swelling, drainage) and managed with intensified local care + short course oral antibiotics.[11] Rare deep infections may require surgical debridement or pin relocation.[6]

7.2 Bone-Related Complications

  • Premature consolidation – particular risk in Ollier's due to accelerated healing[6]. Managed by increasing distraction rate or percutaneous osteoclasis[30]
  • Nonunion/delayed union – less common in Ollier's (the same robust healing that risks premature consolidation likely protects against nonunion). Risk factors: excessive distraction rate, poor nutrition, smoking, infection[6]
  • Fracture – through weak regenerate or through other enchondroma-weakened areas[29]. Mitigated by post-removal casting and gradual return to activity[6]
ℹ Inverse Complication Relationship

The hyperactive healing response in Ollier's disease creates an interesting paradox: it increases risk of premature consolidation while likely protecting against nonunion. The primary biological concern for the surgeon is too much healing—a challenge relatively unique to this patient population.

7.3 Joint Contractures

The most significant threat to functional outcome.[6] Generated by muscle tethering, immobilization, pain, and edema during lengthening. Prevention: dedicated, intensive therapy from day one with daily ROM exercises and dynamic/static splinting.[30] Severe contractures may require serial casting or surgical release.[6]

8. Alternative & Adjunctive Surgical Modalities

8.1 Internal Lengthening Nails

Fully implantable, motorized telescopic nails (e.g., PRECICE) lengthened by an external remote controller via magnetic mechanism.[30] Advantages: no pin infections, improved comfort/cosmesis, reduced pain, no muscle tethering. Current limitation: existing nails are often too large for the pediatric ulnar medullary canal, restricting use to older adolescents/adults.[1]

8.2 Epiphysiodesis and Acute Correction

Epiphysiodesis (growth arrest of longer limb) is effective in lower extremities but rarely used in the upper limb where precise length equality is less critical.[1] Acute deformity correction (osteotomy + internal fixation) addresses pure angular deformity but is insufficient when significant shortening coexists, as is typical in Ollier's.

8.3 Evolution of Treatment Philosophy

A clear progression has occurred:

  1. Ablative – curettage & bone grafting (removes pathology, doesn't address deformity)[8]
  2. Regenerative with external fixation – distraction osteogenesis (corrects deformity + transforms pathology)[30]
  3. Less invasive regenerative – internal lengthening nails (same biology, minimal morbidity)

9. Expert Recommendations & Treatment Algorithm

Synthesized Treatment Algorithm

✦ Six-Step Management Framework

Step 1: Confirm diagnosis; comprehensive radiographic quantification (UV, RAA, CS, radial bow, radiocapitellar alignment).
Step 2: Determine surgical indications (functional impairment, pain, instability, significant cosmetic deformity).
Step 3: Predict final LLD at skeletal maturity (Paley Multiplier); develop staged "reconstructive life plan."
Step 4: Plan osteotomy site (proximal if radial head reduction needed), fixator choice, and radial bow strategy (remodeling vs. osteotomy by age/severity).
Step 5: Strict post-operative protocol; monitor for premature consolidation; mandatory intensive therapy.
Step 6: Follow until skeletal maturity; lifelong surveillance for malignant transformation (up to 50% risk of chondrosarcoma in Ollier's).[1]

Future Directions

  • Smaller internal lengthening nails for pediatric ulnae – a potential paradigm shift
  • Biological augmentation – agents to modulate healing rate (accelerate slow consolidation or prevent premature consolidation)[36]
  • Mechanotransduction research – understanding how distraction force overrides IDH1/IDH2 genetic signaling in enchondromas
  • Targeted pharmacotherapy – IDH1/IDH2 pathway inhibitors to slow/prevent enchondroma growth
  • Long-term (30+ year) outcomes research as patients advance into middle and older age
