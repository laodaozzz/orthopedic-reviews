1. Introduction to Contemporary ARCR Rehabilitation

Arthroscopic rotator cuff repair (ARCR) has firmly established itself as the gold standard in the surgical management of symptomatic, full-thickness rotator cuff tears. Over the past two decades, significant advancements in surgical techniques—from simple single-row anchors to biomechanically superior double-row transosseous-equivalent (TOE) suture-bridge constructs—have improved anatomical restoration of the rotator cuff footprint. Level I/II

Despite intraoperative innovations, clinical outcomes and structural healing remain heavily dependent on the postoperative rehabilitation protocol [1]. The rehabilitation paradigm represents a highly nuanced balance between two competing imperatives:

  • Protecting the tenuous tendon-to-bone repair construct to ensure biological healing
  • Applying progressive, controlled mechanical stress to prevent glenohumeral stiffness, muscle atrophy, and capsular adhesions [4]

A landmark Level II study by Galetta et al. (2021, JSES) analyzed public rehabilitation protocols across academic institutions and found highly divergent recommendations regarding the duration of immobilization, initiation of passive range of motion (PROM), onset of active range of motion (AROM), and timing of resistance exercises [6]. This variability often stems from differing surgical philosophies and an overall lack of universal standardization.

🔑 Key Paradigm Shift

Contemporary evidence dictates that a uniform "one-size-fits-all" approach to postoperative rehabilitation is fundamentally flawed. Instead, rehabilitation must be highly customized based on tear size, tissue quality, chronicity, patient age, metabolic comorbidities (e.g., diabetes), and the specific surgical construct utilized [1].

2. Biomechanical and Biological Foundations of Tendon Healing

Following ARCR, the reattachment of the rotator cuff tendons to the tuberosity relies on a structured, chronological biological cascade. The healing process does not regenerate the native transitional zones; rather, it heals via a scar-tissue-mediated process characterized by three overlapping phases:

Inflammatory Phase
Days 1–14
Hematoma formation; macrophage/neutrophil infiltration; cytokine release. Repair relies entirely on surgical construct strength — the biological interface provides zero tensile strength [4].
Reparative & Proliferative Phase
Weeks 2–8
Fibroblast migration; disorganized Type III collagen synthesis. Controlled mechanotransduction aligns collagen fibrils along the stress axis. Immobilization beyond this phase risks adhesive capsulitis [1].
Remodeling & Maturation
Weeks 8–50+
Type III collagen replaced by organized Type I collagen. At 12 weeks: ~25% native strength. At 26 weeks: ~85%. Full histological remodeling may require 40–50 weeks [1][12].
⚠️ Critical Biological Threshold

Studies tracking tissue biomechanics indicate that at 12 weeks postoperatively, the healing tendon has achieved only approximately 25% of its normal native strength. It may take up to 26 weeks to reach 85% strength. Full histological remodeling continues for 40–50 weeks [1][12]. This underscores the necessity for a highly delayed approach to heavy resistance training.

Tear size is universally recognized as the single most influential prognostic factor determining repair integrity, tendon healing rates, and the required aggressiveness of rehabilitation [10].

Intrinsic biological variables such as fatty infiltration (Goutallier staging), muscle atrophy, and tendon retraction severely compromise tissue quality. Massive tears often present with advanced fatty infiltration, inelastic tendinous tissue, and a completely disrupted anterior cable [14]. A robust, healthy supraspinatus in a 45-year-old can withstand vastly different biomechanical forces than a retracted, fatty-infiltrated massive tear repaired under high tension in a 70-year-old [4].

4. Early vs. Delayed Postoperative Passive Motion — The Evidence

4.1 Definitions

  • Early Passive ROM (EPM): Initiation of guided PROM within postoperative days 1–7, often with early sling discontinuation.
  • Delayed Passive ROM (DPM): Strict sling immobilization with absolute PROM restriction for 3–6 weeks; only distal joints (elbow, wrist, hand) allowed [18].

4.2 Impact on Stiffness, ROM, and Functional Outcomes Level I

A comprehensive Level I meta-analysis demonstrated statistically significant improvements in forward flexion and external rotation at 6 weeks, 3 months, and 6 months favoring EPM cohorts (anterior flexion MD 1.40–1.57°, ER MD 1.59°) [18][19]. EPM also shows a moderate short-term VAS pain advantage during weeks 6–12 [21].

However, by 12–24 months, there are no clinically significant differences in global shoulder function, final ROM, or long-term pain between groups [17].

4.3 Impact on Repair Integrity — Tear Size Matters Most Level I

A landmark Level I systematic review of 28 studies (1,729 repairs) investigated retear risk stratified by tear size [27]:

  • Small/Medium (< 3 cm): EPM does not significantly increase retear risk. The benefits of preventing stiffness outweigh the negligible mechanical failure risk [22].
  • Large/Massive (> 3 cm): Aggressive early motion leads to a statistically significant increase in structural failure. For tears > 5 cm with DR repairs: 56.4% retear (EPM) vs. 20.0% (DPM); p = .002 [27].
✅ Current Consensus — Individualized, Pathology-Driven Approach

Small–Medium (< 3 cm): Early Motion Protocol — PROM at 1–2 weeks is safe and minimizes stiffness [22].

Large–Massive (> 3 cm), chronic tears, or high-tension repairs: Delayed Motion Protocol — strict immobilization 4–6 weeks is imperative [10].

5. Special Protocol: Massive Rotator Cuff Tears (MRCT)

MRCTs represent a distinct, highly complex pathological entity. Defined as a tear > 5 cm or complete rupture of ≥ 2 tendons [13], MRCTs feature severe disruption of glenohumeral force couples, superior humeral head migration, and clinical "pseudoparalysis" [14].

⚠️ MRCT Failure Window

Clinical data shows that MRCTs fail most commonly within the first 12 weeks. Applying load prior to this window is strictly contraindicated [3]. For MRCTs, the primary surgical goal is often restoration of pain-free ADLs rather than return to heavy labor or competitive overhead sports [15].

📋 Return-to-Play Criteria (Objective, Not Time-Based)

Before clearance for unrestricted activity, patients must demonstrate: full symmetric AROM, negative impingement signs, and isokinetic strength ≥ 85–90% of the uninvolved extremity [1]. For MRCTs, recovery of abduction/ER strength may be permanently blunted, particularly if repair tension exceeded 35 N [44].

7. Adjustments for Concomitant Procedures

7.1 Biceps Tenodesis or Tenotomy

If a biceps tenodesis is performed, active elbow flexion and active forearm supination are strictly prohibited for 4 weeks. Passive elbow ROM is permitted. Resisted biceps curls and supination are delayed until 8 weeks [4].

7.2 Subscapularis Repair

When the subscapularis is repaired concurrently:

  • ER restricted to neutral (0°) or max 10° for the first 4–6 weeks [2]
  • Extension past coronal plane prohibited for 6 weeks [2]
  • Pendulum exercises universally contraindicated (patients frequently use active IR to swing) [4]
  • Resisted IR strengthening delayed until 8–12 weeks [2]

7.3 Biological Augmentation & Spacers

For subacromial balloon spacers, PROM/AAROM initiated weeks 3–6; full strengthening by weeks 12–16 [1]. For superior capsular reconstruction (SCR), protocol aligns with the delayed massive tear pathway: 6 weeks strict sling, delayed strengthening to protect allograft [1].

8. Conclusion

Postoperative rehabilitation following ARCR is a highly complex, scientifically driven process requiring a sophisticated, evidence-based approach that respects the biological timeline of tendon-to-bone healing.

  • Small/Medium tears (< 3 cm) with robust tissue quality benefit safely from early passive motion, mitigating stiffness and achieving early pain reduction without compromising structural integrity.
  • Large/Massive tears (> 3 cm) demand a delayed, highly conservative protocol — minimum 6 weeks strict immobilization, delayed PROM, and significantly delayed strengthening — to protect fragile constructs against catastrophic structural failure.

By adhering to the precise phase-by-phase milestones and strictly respecting concomitant procedural restrictions, orthopedic surgeons and physical therapists can optimize the delicate balance between structural protection and functional restoration [1][4][5].
