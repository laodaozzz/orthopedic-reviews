Proximal Humerus Fracture-Dislocations: Anterior vs. Posterior

Interactive Evidence-Based Clinical Summary

Executive Summary

Proximal humerus fracture-dislocations (PHFDs) represent a severe subset of proximal humerus fractures (PHFs), combining articular incongruity with osseous instability. A critical error in management is the failure to differentiate between the two primary vectors: anterior and posterior. While often grouped under a single classification, these are fundamentally distinct clinical entities with divergent mechanisms, unique diagnostic challenges, and entirely separate treatment algorithms.

Synthesis

🔵 Anterior PHFD

  • High-energy traumatic injury
  • A reconstructive challenge
  • Primary risk: axillary artery injury
  • Surgical decision driven by patient age & bone quality

🔴 Posterior PHFD

  • Convulsive mechanism (seizure / shock)
  • A diagnostic challenge
  • Primary risk: missed diagnosis
  • Surgical decision driven by RHS defect size
⚠ Critical Time-Sensitive Finding
Surgical delay beyond 48 hours increases avascular necrosis (AVN) rates from 20% to 100% — a five-fold increase.[47] The failure to correctly diagnose a posterior PHFD is not merely a classification error; it is a clinical catastrophe that commits the patient to a poor outcome.

I. Anatomical and Epidemiological Context

Proximal humerus fractures are the third most common non-vertebral fracture in individuals over 65 years old, trailing only distal radius and proximal femur fractures, accounting for 4–10% of all adult fractures.[1] Incidence is rising with the aging population.[2] The vast majority occur in elderly patients with osteoporotic bone (2:1 female-to-male ratio) from low-energy ground-level falls.[1]

A severe and distinct subset involves not only fracture but also dislocation of the glenohumeral joint.[4] PHFDs are far less common, identified in 5.5% of all PHFs in one study.[6] Unlike low-energy geriatric fractures, PHFDs typically arise from high-energy mechanisms in younger individuals and carry a greater degree of comminution, displacement, and concomitant soft tissue injuries.[1, 4]

The Neer Classification (1970)

The most enduring classification system was developed by Dr. Charles Neer.[3] It categorizes fractures by displacement of four anatomical segments — the humeral head, greater tuberosity, lesser tuberosity, and humeral shaft — where "displaced" means separation >1 cm or angulation >45°.[1, 11] Fracture-dislocations are designated as Group VI, the highest severity category.[12]

💡 Key Limitation of Neer Classification
By grouping all PHFDs into a single "Group VI," the system fails to highlight the most immediately relevant clinical variable: the direction of dislocation. An anterior and a posterior PHFD have non-overlapping diagnostic and therapeutic pathways.[13]

Prevalence Disparity

Simple anterior dislocations account for 95% of all shoulder dislocations; posterior dislocations are rare at 2–5%.[14, 15, 16] This rarity is amplified in fracture-dislocations: true posterior PHFDs have an incidence of only 0.6 per 100,000 per year.[19]

II. Comparative Pathomechanics and Injury Patterns

A. Anterior Fracture-Dislocation: High-Energy, High-Risk

Mechanism: Force applied to the arm in forced abduction, external rotation, and extension levers the humeral head anteriorly, inferiorly, and medially, often resting in a subcoracoid position.[1]

Associated high-risk injuries: The defining feature is the immediate threat to the axillary neurovascular bundle:

  • Axillary artery injury — 5–6% overall risk in PHFs, but specifically elevated in displaced surgical neck fractures and subcoracoid dislocations.[1, 4]
  • Axillary nerve injury — the most commonly injured nerve in all shoulder trauma, placed under extreme tension by the dislocated head.[1, 4]

B. Posterior Fracture-Dislocation: The "Triple E" Syndrome

Mechanism: Rarely from a simple fall. Pathognomonic for violent, uncontrolled muscle contraction — the "Triple E Syndrome":[22]

  1. Epilepsy (or any convulsive seizure)[22]
  2. Electric Shock[24]
  3. Extreme Trauma (e.g., high-voltage electrical work, severe convulsion)[22]

Seizures are the most common cause,[15] and bilateral posterior shoulder dislocation is pathognomonic for a recent seizure.[22]

🧠 The Mechanism IS the Diagnosis
For a posterior PHFD, the mechanism (e.g., "patient had a seizure") is the diagnosis.[22] It should immediately trigger a high-suspicion workup for a missed injury. For anterior PHFDs, the mechanism is less diagnostically specific but highly prognostic — predicting neurovascular and soft-tissue injury risk.[1, 4]

Muscular pathophysiology: The powerful internal rotators (subscapularis, pectoralis major, latissimus dorsi) generate a convulsive force that overwhelms the weaker external rotators, driving the humeral head posteriorly.[22]

Associated "Red Flag" fractures:

  • Lesser tuberosity avulsion — violent subscapularis contraction avulses the lesser tuberosity. An isolated lesser tuberosity fracture should be considered a posterior dislocation until proven otherwise.[1]
  • Reverse Hill-Sachs (RHS) lesion — the hallmark osseous lesion of posterior dislocation: an impaction fracture of the anteromedial humeral head from impact against the posterior glenoid rim.[17, 22, 30]

III. The Diagnostic Chasm

The diagnostic pathways for anterior and posterior PHFDs diverge dramatically. The anterior injury is typically obvious; the posterior injury is notoriously subtle and frequently missed, leading to devastating long-term complications.[13]

A. Clinical Presentation and Physical Examination

🔵 Anterior PHFD

  • Deformity often clinically obvious
  • Arm held in slight abduction and external rotation
  • Prominent mass (humeral head) palpable anteriorly
  • "Hollow" felt posteriorly beneath the acromion
  • Priority: Vascular exam — check distal pulses

🔴 Posterior PHFD

  • Anterior shoulder may appear "flattened"
  • Coracoid and acromion unusually prominent
  • Arm locked in adduction and internal rotation
  • Cannot passively externally rotate — rigid mechanical block[27]
  • Priority: Suspicion — mechanism is the key

Mandatory neurovascular exam: For all PHFDs, axillary nerve function must be documented by testing deltoid contraction and lateral shoulder "badge patch" sensation.[1] For anterior PHFDs, the vascular exam is paramount — distal pulses must be compared, though presence can be masked by collateral circulation.[1]

B. The "Missed" Diagnosis: A Cognitive Crisis

Up to 79% of posterior dislocations are missed on initial evaluation.[15] This stems from three converging factors:

⚠ Why Posterior PHFDs Are Missed
  1. "Satisfaction of Search" phenomenon: The fracture is obvious on X-ray, so the evaluator stops searching — the dislocation is overlooked.[13]
  2. Inadequate imaging: Over-reliance on a single AP radiograph. One study found 80% of missed posterior dislocations did not have an axillary view.[13]
  3. Rarity bias: Because the injury is so rare, clinicians "do not think of the condition as a possible cause."[14]

C. The Radiological Protocol

The Mandatory Trauma Series (Three Views):

  1. True AP (Grashey) view
  2. Scapular-Y (Trans-scapular) view
  3. Axillary view[1]
⚡ The Axillary View Controversy
Some studies suggest the axillary view may be optional for simple PHFs without dislocation.[42] However, this is dangerously misapplied to fracture-dislocations: 80% of missed posterior dislocations lacked an axillary view.[13] In any patient where a dislocation cannot be definitively ruled out clinically, the axillary view or CT scan is mandatory.

D. Radiographic Signs of Posterior Dislocation (AP View)

E. The Essential Role of CT Scanning

For any PHFD, CT is invaluable for pre-operative planning: assessing comminution, tuberosity fragment location, and intra-articular involvement.[1]

For posterior PHFDs, CT is not just helpful — it is diagnostic and prescriptive:[26]

  1. Confirming the diagnosis — unambiguously demonstrates posterior head-glenoid relationship[30]
  2. Quantifying the RHS lesion — measures defect as percentage of articular surface[39]
  3. Driving the treatment algorithm — percentage involvement dictates the surgical plan[16]

IV. Divergent Therapeutic Algorithms

A. Principles of Initial Management

All PHFDs are orthopaedic emergencies. The first and most urgent goal is prompt closed reduction of the glenohumeral joint to relieve neurovascular tension and restore humeral head perfusion, decreasing AVN risk.[46]

Non-operative management (sling immobilization and rehabilitation) is typically reserved only for patients with minimal displacement after reduction or those medically unfit for surgery.[1]

B. Surgical Armamentarium for Anterior PHFDs

The central question: "Can this fracture be fixed, or does it need to be replaced?" The decision is based on patient factors and fracture complexity.

Table 3: Surgical Options for Anterior PHFD — Patient-Factor Algorithm
ProcedureIndicationKey Considerations
ORIF Younger patients (<65) with good bone quality; displaced 2-, 3-, 4-part fractures[1] Goal: anatomic reduction, restore medial calcar support to prevent varus collapse[1]
Hemiarthroplasty (HA) Younger, active patients (~40–65); non-reconstructible pattern (head-splitting, severely impacted)[1] Outcome entirely dependent on tuberosity healing; historically unpredictable[50]
Reverse TSA (rTSA) Low-demand, elderly (>65–70); poor bone stock; highly comminuted, non-reconstructible[1] Does not depend on rotator cuff; lower complication/revision rates vs. ORIF and HA in elderly[50]

C. Specialized Management of Posterior PHFDs — Stability-Based Algorithm

The treatment algorithm completely diverges. Decision-making is driven by a single, critical injury factor: the size of the Reverse Hill-Sachs (RHS) defect measured on pre-operative CT.[16]

Table 4: Treatment Algorithm for Posterior PHFD Based on RHS Defect Size
RHS Defect Size Joint Stability Recommended Intervention Rationale
< 20–25%[44] Stable Closed or Open Reduction; Fixation of associated fractures[44] Defect too small to engage on glenoid rim
25% – 45%[36, 44, 56] Unstable (Engaging) Modified McLaughlin Procedure — lesser tuberosity transfer into defect[35, 36, 55] Fills osseous defect; converts deforming subscapularis into stabilizer[34]
Alternatives: McLaughlin (tendon)[17], bone grafting[57], arthroscopic reverse remplissage[59]
> 45–50%[44] Non-salvageable Arthroplasty (HA in young; rTSA in elderly)[1] Articular surface too damaged for joint-preserving procedure

Age + Bone Quality → ORIF vs. HA vs. rTSA

The rTSA is the one procedure on both flowcharts — reserved for the "end-stage" version of each injury.

V. Clinical Outcomes and Complications

A. Functional Outcomes

Functional recovery is measured using standardized scores (Constant-Murley, DASH, ASES).[9] The expectation is a stable, pain-free shoulder, but "excellent" functional results are not the norm:

  • ORIF for 3- and 4-part fracture-dislocations in young adults: 71.4% "good or excellent" results, but 21.4% significant shoulder stiffness.[9]
  • Patients >60 years: median Constant-Murley Score of 76 for ORIF vs. 42 for arthroplasty.[62]
  • Chronic locked posterior dislocations after modified McLaughlin: median CMS of 70 ("fair-to-good").[63]

B. Avascular Necrosis (AVN) — The Primary Complication

AVN is the most feared complication, occurring when the blood supply (primarily posterior humeral circumflex artery) is severed.[1, 47] It leads to progressive humeral head collapse, severe pain, and often revision to arthroplasty.[23]

🔴 The 48-Hour Clock — Schnetzke et al. (2018)

A study of 30 patients with PHFDs found:[47]

  • Early surgery (≤48 hours): AVN rate = 20% (5 of 25 patients)
  • Late surgery (>48 hours): AVN rate = 100% (5 of 5 patients)

This represents a five-fold increased risk and a 3.3-fold increased risk of revision surgery.

C. Neurovascular Complications

Axillary Nerve Injury

Most common nerve injury for all PHFs/dislocations. Incidence: 35–42%.[20] Most often transient neuropraxia resolving after reduction, but persistent functional impairment can remain.[67] Risk increases with age.[68]

Axillary Artery Injury (Anterior PHFD Specific)

Limb-threatening emergency. 5–6% risk specifically associated with anterior (subcoracoid) displacement.[1] Not a recognized feature of posterior PHFDs.[4]

Chronic Instability & Pain

Primary sequela of a missed posterior dislocation. Duration of dislocation (time from injury to diagnosis) is the single most important factor for poor outcome.[63]

D. The Causal Chain of Catastrophe

⛓ The Devastating Sequence
"Satisfaction of search" cognitive error[13]
→ Missed diagnosis of posterior PHFD[15]
→ Surgical delay beyond 48 hours[63]
→ Five-fold increase in AVN (20% → 100%)[47]

The initial diagnostic failure is the clinical catastrophe.

VI. Expert Synthesis and Clinical Recommendations

These are two separate, non-overlapping clinical entities erroneously grouped by traditional classifications. The anterior pattern represents a high-energy reconstruction problem; the posterior pattern represents a high-stakes diagnostic problem.

✅ The "Never-Miss" List — Actionable Clinical Mandates
  1. SUSPECT POSTERIOR: Any PHF after seizure, electric shock, or convulsive trauma → posterior PHFD assumed present until proven otherwise.[22]
  2. EXAMINE THE ARM: Locked in internal rotation, cannot passively externally rotate → locked posterior dislocation, regardless of X-ray.[27]
  3. CHECK "RED FLAGS" ON AP FILM: Search for "Lightbulb Sign,"[33] "Trough Sign,"[30] or isolated lesser tuberosity fracture.[1]
  4. GET THE AXILLARY VIEW OR CT: Never accept an incomplete trauma series — mandatory to rule out occult dislocation.[1, 13]
  5. TRIAGE ANTERIOR VASCULAR INJURY: In anterior PHFD, vascular check is the first step after neurologic assessment. Asymmetrical pulses, cool hand, or expanding hematoma → emergent vascular surgery consultation.[4]
  6. RESPECT THE 48-HOUR CLOCK: Both anterior and posterior PHFDs are surgical emergencies. Delay beyond 48 hours commits the humeral head to AVN.[47]
