Evidence-Based Guidelines for NPWT Discontinuation & Delayed Primary Closure in Severe Open Fractures

Interactive clinical decision support — synthesising meta-analyses (2020–2025), landmark trials, and emerging diagnostic technologies

1. Introduction: The Clinical Imperative

The management of severe open fractures remains among the most demanding challenges in orthopaedic trauma. Unlike closed injuries, open fractures—particularly Gustilo-Anderson Type III—involve catastrophic disruption of the local biological environment: bacterial contamination, desiccating necrosis, and a profound inflammatory response. The primary objective extends beyond osseous stabilisation; it requires meticulous restoration of the soft tissue envelope to prevent Fracture-Related Infection (FRI), non-union, and amputation.

Over the past two decades, Negative Pressure Wound Therapy (NPWT) has transformed the open-fracture algorithm, acting as a “bridge” between initial debridement and definitive closure. Level I However, the convenience of NPWT has introduced clinical ambiguity—“timing drift”—where definitive closure is delayed beyond the physiological window, increasing the risk of nosocomial infection and flap failure.

Key Principle — The Race for the Surface
Coined by Gristina, this concept describes the competition between host tissue cells and bacteria for adherence to the bone/implant surface. Once biofilm matures within 24–72 hours, bacteria become phenotypically distinct, exhibiting extreme resistance to antibiotics and immune defences. Level III

1.1 Classification as a Prognostic Framework

The Gustilo-Anderson classification remains the universal language, predicting feasibility of closure and complication risk. Level III

  • Type I: Clean wound <1 cm. Minimal soft tissue damage. DPC within 48 hours. Level IV
  • Type II: Wound >1 cm without extensive soft tissue damage. Primary closure after debridement if no tension. Level IV
  • Type IIIA: Extensive lacerations but adequate soft tissue coverage. DPC or split-thickness skin grafting (SSG). Level IV
  • Type IIIB: Extensive soft tissue loss, periosteal stripping, bone exposure. Mandates flap coverage (rotational or free). Level IV
  • Type IIIC: Arterial injury requiring repair. Highest risk of amputation and infection. Level IV
Critical Distinction
Type IIIA vs IIIB — Misclassification or forcing DPC in a IIIB injury inevitably leads to wound dehiscence, necrosis, and deep infection. Level III

2. Negative Pressure Wound Therapy: Mechanisms & Evidence

2.1 Mechanisms of Action

NPWT (sub-atmospheric pressure, typically −125 mmHg) prepares the wound bed through four mechanisms: Level II

  1. Macro-deformation: Foam collapse physically draws wound edges together, reducing defect surface area. Mechanism
  2. Micro-deformation & Mechanotransduction: Localised 5–20% cytoskeletal strain stimulates VEGF, FGF expression, and fibroblast/endothelial proliferation. Mechanism
  3. Fluid Management & Oedema Reduction: Evacuation of interstitial fluid reduces inter-capillary distance, improves perfusion, and eliminates inflammatory cytokines (TNF-α, IL-1) and proteases (MMPs). Mechanism
  4. Environmental Stabilisation: Sealed system provides warm, moist, nosocomially isolated environment. Mechanism

2.2 Efficacy Data

The WOLLF Trial (2018) Level I — A landmark multicentre UK RCT comparing NPWT with standard dressings for Grade II–III open fractures found no statistically significant difference in self-rated disability at 12 months. Deep SSI rates: NPWT 7.1% vs standard 8.1% (ITT analysis). This challenged the dogma of NPWT as a panacea.

Liu et al. Meta-analysis (2024) Level I — Analysis of 18 trauma studies found NPWT significantly reduced deep SSI (OR 0.64; 95% CI 0.52–0.80) compared to conventional dressings. NPWT is thus effective for acute infection reduction in high-risk wounds, even if long-term functional outcome (driven by injury severity) is unaltered.

Clinical Synthesis
NPWT is a valuable temporary adjunct for wound bed preparation and infection risk reduction. It “buys time” but does not independently solve coverage. Prolonged use erodes its benefit. Level II

2.3 Contraindications

  • Untreated Osteomyelitis: NPWT acts as an incubator, sequestering bacteria. Level IV
  • Necrotic Tissue: Cannot revive dead tissue; placing foam over eschar prevents debridement. Level IV
  • Exposed Neurovascular Structures: Foam can erode vessels/nerves; a non-adherent interface (silicone, petroleum gauze) is mandatory. Level IV
  • Malignancy: Enhanced proliferation contraindicated with malignant cells. Level V

3. Discontinuation of NPWT: Evidence-Based Criteria

3.1 The “Grade A” Wound Status

Formalised by Stannard et al., the Grade A status is the primary benchmark for NPWT discontinuation. Level II

  • Abundant Granulation: 100% wound bed covered with “beefy red” tissue that bleeds on contact.
  • Absence of Exposed Structures: No exposed bone, tendon, or hardware not covered by granulation tissue.
  • Absence of Necrosis: No slough, eschar, or fibrin debris.
  • Readiness for Coverage: Wound is ready for DPC, SSG, or flap.

Under the Stannard protocol, serial irrigation and debridement occur every 48–72 hours with NPWT exchanges. NPWT is discontinued immediately upon Grade A achievement. Continuing therapy on a Grade A wound risks maceration and pathogen introduction.

3.2 The “Seven-Day Rule”

Critical Temporal Boundary
Retrospective analysis of Gustilo IIIB tibia fractures: Level III
  • Closure < 7 days → deep infection rate ~12.5%
  • Closure > 7 days → deep infection rate ~57%
NPWT must be viewed as a bridge with a maximum span of 7 days. If the wound is not ready by Day 7, the strategy is failing—aggressive re-debridement and definitive flap reconstruction are required.

3.3 Signs of Efficacy Plateau

Clinicians must monitor for plateau indicators that signal further NPWT will not yield improvement: Level IV

  • Stagnant Dimensions: No measurable reduction in wound surface area/depth over two consecutive dressing changes (~1 week).
  • Fibrosis: Granulation tissue changes from bright red to pale, dusky, or fibrotic.
  • Hyper-granulation: Friable overgrowth above skin level preventing epithelialisation.
  • Biofilm Indicators: Increased exudate, odour, or slough despite negative pressure.

4. Indications of “Ready for Closure”

4.1 Intraoperative Visual Criteria — The “Second Look”

Typically performed 48 hours after initial debridement, the surgeon evaluates: Level III

  1. Tissue Viability:
    • Muscle: Contractile with “Paprika Sign” (pinpoint bleeding upon shaving).
    • Skin: Margins must bleed bright red; dusky/cyanotic edges = ischaemia.
    • Bone: White/yellow cortical bone; gray/black = necrotic, requires decortication to bleeding bone.
  2. Absence of Tension: Cardinal rule—wound edges must approximate without tension. Tension → local ischaemia → margin necrosis → breakdown. If edges cannot be brought together easily, the wound is not ready for DPC. Level IV
  3. Absence of Gross Infection: No purulence, fibrin slough, or foul odour.

4.2 The GHOISS Scoring System

The Ganga Hospital Open Injury Severity Score (GHOISS) provides a validated, objective framework for determining closure method. Level III

Table 1 — GHOISS Skin Score and Closure Indications
ScoreClinical DescriptionClosure Indication
1No skin loss. Margins approximate without tension.Primary / Delayed Primary Closure (DPC)
2No skin loss. Margins approximate without tension. Fracture exposed.DPC (if tension-free)
3Skin loss present. Fracture not exposed. Adequate soft tissue bed.Split-Thickness Skin Graft (SSG)
4Skin loss with exposed bone, tendon, or neurovascular structures.Flap Coverage (Rotational / Free)
5Circumferential degloving or extensive loss.Complex Reconstruction / Amputation
Clinical Guardrail
A Score 3 or 4 wound is, by definition, not ready for primary closure. Attempting to close these wounds leads to inevitable failure.

4.3 Fluorescence Imaging (Point-of-Care)

Traditional clinical signs of infection (erythema, warmth, purulence) have sensitivity <15% for detecting high bacterial loads (≥104 CFU/g) in acute trauma. Level II Fluorescence imaging (e.g., MolecuLight) addresses this gap:

  • Red Fluorescence: Porphyrins from most pathogens (S. aureus, E. coli, anaerobes). Correlates with ≥104 CFU/g. Level II
  • Cyan Fluorescence: Pyoverdine, specific to Pseudomonas aeruginosa. Level II

Clinical Workflow: Scan → Negative (high NPV ~99%) → proceed to closure. Positive → targeted debridement of fluorescing areas → re-scan → delay closure if persistent, apply topical antimicrobials.

5.2 Diagnostic Modalities

  • Quantitative Tissue Biopsy (Gold Standard): Excise → weigh → homogenise → culture. Definitive but invasive, expensive, and slow (48+ h). Level III
  • Semiquantitative Swab (Levine Technique): Fast, non-invasive, but correlates poorly with deep tissue bioburden; may miss biofilm. Level III
  • Fluorescence Imaging (Surrogate): Real-time proxy for quantitative biopsy. Detection of fluorescence correlates with ≥104 CFU/g. Level II

7. Integrated Clinical Protocols

7.1 Gustilo Type I, II, and IIIA

  1. Day 0: Antibiotics (Cephalosporin ± Aminoglycoside) within 1 h. Aggressive debridement & lavage. Skeletal stabilisation (IM nail or plate). Wound: open + NPWT.
  2. Day 2 (“Second Look”): Evaluate Paprika sign, tension-free approximation, GHOISS ≤ 2, fluorescence check if available.
  3. Closure Decision: Criteria met → DPC. Criteria not met → re-debride, re-apply NPWT, target closure by Day 5.

7.2 Gustilo Type IIIB — “Fix and Flap”

  1. Day 0: Aggressive debridement. Skeletal stabilisation (Ex-Fix if unstable; definitive fixation if stable). GHOISS Score 4.
  2. Target <72 h: Definitive fixation + flap coverage in the same sitting. NPWT used strictly as logistical bridge.
  3. Late Scenario (>7 days): Do NOT close primarily. Serial debridement → Grade A. Deep tissue cultures/biopsy. NPWT for granulation. Flap only when bacterial control confirmed (<104 CFU/g or negative fluorescence).

7.3 The Masquelet Technique (Bone Loss)

  1. Stage 1: Radical debridement. Antibiotic-impregnated cement spacer. Close soft tissue (flap) over spacer.
  2. Interval: 6–8 weeks for induced membrane formation.
  3. Stage 2: Remove spacer. Pack defect with bone graft inside the biological membrane.

9. Conclusions

  • NPWT is a vital adjunct, not a cure. Its benefits are strictly time-limited. The “Seven-Day Rule” is critical; extending NPWT beyond one week results in a precipitous rise in infection. Level III
  • Readiness for Closure requires multi-dimensional assessment: “Grade A” status, GHOISS scoring, and fluorescence imaging provide objective data to prevent premature closure. Level II
  • Early Closure (<72 h) remains the gold standard—lowest rates of deep infection, non-union, and flap failure. “Fix and Flap” within 72 h offers the highest probability of limb salvage. Level I
Summary Recommendations
  • Stop NPWT immediately upon “Grade A” status or by Day 7.
  • Prioritise coverage within 72 hours for Type III fractures.
  • Utilise objective tools (Fluorescence, GHOISS) to support clinical judgment.
  • Beware the “clean-looking” wound: if delay >7 days, presume critical colonisation and verify bioburden before sealing.
