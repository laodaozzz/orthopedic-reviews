1. Pathophysiology & Biomechanical Complexity

Medial compartment knee osteoarthritis (OA) is characterized by a self-propagating cycle of biomechanical degradation. As the structural integrity of the medial articular cartilage and the medial meniscus fails, the joint space narrows, causing the mechanical axis of the lower extremity to shift medially. This medial shift increases the adduction moment and eccentric load transferred across the medial tibial plateau. The resulting concentration of focal mechanical stress accelerates cartilage apoptosis, subchondral bone sclerosis, and further joint space collapse, exacerbating varus malalignment in a continuous destructive feedback loop.1

For decades, total knee arthroplasty (TKA) was the definitive surgical intervention for end-stage disease. However, TKA application in younger, active patients with isolated medial disease has been associated with suboptimal patient-reported outcome measures (PROMs), restricted high-impact activity, perception of an "unnatural" joint, and premature sacrifice of pristine lateral cartilage, patellofemoral cartilage, and cruciate ligaments.2

Two primary modalities now dominate treatment of isolated medial compartment OA:

High Tibial Osteotomy (HTO) A joint-preserving, extra-articular realignment procedure that reorients the coronal alignment of the lower extremity. By creating controlled valgus alignment, HTO unloads the pathological medial compartment, shifting the mechanical weight-bearing axis laterally to redistribute contact stresses across the preserved lateral compartment.1 Level II Evidence
Unicompartmental Knee Arthroplasty (UKA) An intra-articular focal resurfacing procedure that resects damaged medial articular cartilage and underlying subchondral bone, replacing these with metallic and polyethylene prosthetic constructs. UKA preserves anterior/posterior cruciate ligaments and lateral/patellofemoral compartments.1 Level II Evidence

Modernization of surgical technologies has disrupted traditional boundaries. Angle-stable locking plate fixators and digital planning for medial opening-wedge HTO (MOWHTO) have facilitated earlier weight-bearing and reduced non-union rates.7 Concurrently, highly cross-linked polyethylene and advanced UKA designs have radically improved implant survivorship in younger demographics. This has created significantly overlapping indications—a complex clinical "grey zone."8

2. Radiographic Thresholds & Anatomy of Varus Deformity

The precise anatomical locus of the varus deformity is the most critical deterministic variable. A varus knee can stem from intra-articular cartilage loss, extra-articular metaphyseal bowing, lateral soft-tissue laxity, or a combination. Matching the surgical intervention to the exact anatomical source is the cornerstone of contemporary joint preservation guidelines.8

Distinguishing Intra-articular from Extra-articular Deformity

⚠ Critical Clinical Principle UKA operates by replacing absent medial cartilage—it can only correct intra-articular varus. Attempting UKA in severe extra-articular tibial varus forces impossible biomechanical compromise: either under-correction (leaving pathological mechanical varus causing rapid polyethylene wear) or over-resection with extensive soft tissue release (destabilizing the knee and guaranteeing premature failure).10
⚠ HTO on Purely Intra-articular Deformity Performing HTO on a patient with purely intra-articular varus (normal bone, severe cartilage/meniscal loss) creates a secondary extra-articular deformity, resulting in unphysiologic joint line obliquity (JLO). This increases pathological coronal shear forces, strains the ACL, and accelerates lateral/patellofemoral compartment degeneration.14, 15

Medial Proximal Tibial Angle (MPTA)

The MPTA represents the medial angle between the mechanical axis of the tibia and the tangential line of the tibial plateau. In a healthy, anatomically neutral knee, the physiological MPTA ranges from 85° to 90°.11

  • MPTA < 85°: Indicates structural, extra-articular varus deformity within the proximal metaphyseal bone → HTO is indicated.16
  • MPTA > 85°: Indicates structurally normal proximal tibia → varus is driven by intra-articular cartilage loss or lateral ligamentous laxity → UKA is indicated.16
⚠ Joint Line Obliquity Warning Performing HTO on a knee with normal MPTA (e.g., 87°) pushes the postoperative MPTA into excessive valgus (>93°–94°), creating catastrophic shear joint line obliquity. Retrospective studies with 5-year follow-up confirm patients with MPTA >80° have excellent outcomes following UKA.16

Joint Line Convergence Angle (JLCA)

The JLCA is formed by the convergence of tangential lines of the distal femoral condyles and proximal tibial plateau. Normal physiological JLCA is 0° to 2°.11

  • JLCA > 3°–5° (correctable on valgus stress): Varus driven by intra-articular space loss and lateral soft-tissue stretch → UKA indicated. Implant insertion re-tensions lateral ligaments, parallels the joint line.16
  • JLCA < 2° (rigid) with extra-articular deformity > 5°–10°: Varus driven by fixed bony bowing → HTO strictly indicated.10

3. The AKUMA Framework & Clinical "Grey Zone"

The AKUMA framework integrates the Hip-Knee-Ankle (HKA) angle, MPTA, mLDFA, JLCA, and proportional calculations of deformity distribution to categorize patients into three groups:8

1

Osteotomy Group

Dominant extra-articular tibial deformities necessitating structural bony realignment.

2

UKA Group

Dominant intra-articular deformities with anatomically normal extra-articular bony architecture.

3

Grey Zone Group

Overlapping features of both intra-articular cartilage loss and moderate extra-articular deformity. Neither procedure is anatomically perfect; both may be technically feasible depending on functional goals.

⚠ Clinical Implication for Grey-Zone Patients While HTO can salvage a grey-zone knee for daily activities, surgeons must set highly conservative expectations regarding return to impact sports. In grey-zone patients who prioritize high athletic function, a simple HTO may not suffice. If an isolated proximal tibial correction cannot achieve a physiological joint line without inducing over-correction, the surgeon should pivot to Double Level Osteotomy (DLO), addressing both femur and tibia.21

4. Cartilage Wear Severity & the "Bone-on-Bone" Paradigm

UKA and the Necessity of End-Stage Disease

The Oxford Criteria strictly mandate full-thickness, bone-on-bone anteromedial OA (Kellgren-Lawrence Grade 4 or Ahlbäck Grade III/IV) for UKA.22 Performing UKA in partial-thickness cartilage loss (KL Grade 2 or 3) results in significantly poorer PROMs, higher unexplained postoperative pain, and elevated early revision rates.

⚠ Biomechanical Rationale for "Bone-on-Bone" Requirement In partial cartilage preservation, the MCL has not fully contracted. Inserting a standard UKA construct acts as a mechanical wedge—over-tensions the MCL, forces unphysiologic valgus, alters patellofemoral tracking, and shifts immense load onto the pristine lateral compartment. This iatrogenic over-stuffing induces rapid, painful lateral compartment degeneration.23

HTO and Disease Severity Tolerance

HTO is optimally indicated for early-to-moderate disease (KL Grade 2–3, Ahlbäck I–II).22 The UK Knee Osteotomy Consensus Group (KOG) clarified that bone-on-bone OA is not an absolute contraindication for HTO, based on the observation that unloading can induce fibrocartilage formation.22

⚠ Long-Term Caution In long-term follow-up averaging 16 years, 74% of HTOs performed on Ahlbäck Grade 3 knees ultimately failed and required revision to TKA.27 While technically permissible, the algorithm dictates that UKA provides far superior, predictable longevity for patients with isolated bone-on-bone pathology.10

5. Demographics, Physical Demands & Return to Sport

The transition from chronological age to physiological demand and activity expectations represents a massive evolution in the algorithm. Chronological age alone is an insufficient and deeply flawed standalone metric.4

Age & Comorbidities

  • UKA can yield excellent mid-term survival, superior early functional scores, and rapid pain relief in patients under 60.16
  • In the "early elderly" cohort (65–74 years), UKA consistently demonstrates lower complication profile, superior early mobilization, and reduced operative burden vs HTO.6
  • Advanced age is an independent risk factor for early HTO revision. Biological healing capacity of metaphyseal bone diminishes with age, increasing delayed union/non-union risk.23
  • International consensus strongly favors UKA in the older demographic due to immediate weight-bearing, rapid rehabilitation, and shorter hospital stay (often outpatient).18

The Return to Sport Paradox

According to 2024–2025 meta-analyses (2,673 patients), the pooled relative risk for overall return to sport favors UKA (RR: 1.42, 95% CI: 1.20–1.67, p<0.001).35

The Paradox Explained
  • UKA: Faster RTS (weeks to months) for low-to-moderate impact activities (cycling, swimming, golf). However, Tegner scores gradually decline over 2 years (mean: 2.78).35
  • HTO: Prolonged rehabilitation (>6–9 months) but ultimately sustains higher functional ceiling. Tegner scores maintained at 4.25 over 2 years. >50% of appropriately selected UKA patients can return to high-impact sports, but HTO remains dominant for sustaining intense athletic performance.35, 37

Kneeling Ability

A frequently overlooked functional variable. UKA patients frequently report discomfort or inability to kneel due to prosthetic components and capsular tensioning. MOWHTO patients demonstrate significantly superior postoperative kneeling ability as native tibiofemoral and patellofemoral articulations are preserved.39

6. Comparative Clinical Outcomes & Complications

Meta-analyses published in 2024–2025, encompassing upwards of 56,686 pooled patients, provide definitive comparisons.41

Functional Outcome Scores

  • UKA superiority: Reduced postoperative pain (MD = −0.33; 95% CI: −0.64 to −0.03; p = 0.03),42 superior KSS and HSS scores (MD = 2.63; 95% CI: 0.52–4.74; p = 0.01).3
  • HTO superiority: Superior KSS Function scores (MD = 3.74; 95% CI: 1.37–6.11),3 preserved or improved ROM (+8.6° average).3

Complication Profiles

⚠ Stark Difference in Complication Rates
  • Overall complication risk favors UKA: RR = 0.37 (95% CI: 0.25–0.54; p<0.0001).42
  • Odds of complication nearly 5× higher following HTO: OR = 4.88 (95% CI: 2.92–6.86).32

7. Long-Term Survivorship & Revision Arthroplasty Burden

10- and 15-Year Survivorship

  • UKA: 83.5% survivorship at 10 years; 81.9% at 15 years with strict selection.25 Primary revision driver: aseptic loosening (83.4% of revisions).45
  • HTO: Excellent 5-year survivorship (>97.2%),17 but declining to 56%–82% at 10 years and ~61% at 15 years.25

Conversion to Total Knee Arthroplasty

The most profound paradigm shift concerns the consequences of eventual failure. Meta-analyses (11 studies, 10,045 patients) demonstrate that converting HTO to TKA is significantly safer and functionally superior.25

8. Integrated Decision-Making Algorithm

Synthesized from ESSKA/ISAKOS consensus directives (2024–2025), the UK KOG, and the Vipulendran algorithm:22, 23

1

ACL Integrity Assessment

UKA requires an intact ACL. If ACL-deficient with manageable tibial slope, HTO is favored—it can neutralize anterior tibial translation and stabilize the knee while unloading the medial compartment.4

2

Disease Severity & Deformity Locus — The UKA Pathway

If bone-on-bone OA (Ahlbäck III/IV, KL 4) + intact cruciates + purely intra-articular deformity (MPTA >85°, JLCA >3° correctable) + low-moderate demand (Tegner <5) → UKA.4

3

Disease Severity & Deformity Locus — The HTO Pathway

If physiologically young (<60), partial-thickness wear (Ahlbäck I/II), measurable metaphyseal bowing (MPTA <85°) → MOWHTO.10

4

Resolving the AKUMA Grey Zone

If mixed, borderline features → pivot to patient-specific athletic expectations:
• Priority = rapid pain relief, swift return to work, low-impact activity → UKA
• Priority = return to high-impact sports (Tegner >5), or preserving bone stock for uncomplicated future TKA → HTO1

ESSKA & ISAKOS Specific Directives

  • ESSKA (2024): HTO recommended when MPTA <85°. Postoperative JLO must not exceed 5°.54 Prior HTO is not an absolute contraindication for UKA, but over-corrected valgus makes UKA absolutely contraindicated.56
  • ISAKOS: Both HTO and UKA are equally viable for delaying TKA. Choice driven by shared decision-making on long-term physical expectations. HTO for young, high-demand patients (Tegner >5); UKA for aging populations (>60) with lower demands.46, 35

Decision Flowchart Summary

Step 1: ACL intact? → No → Consider HTO (can stabilize ACL-deficient knee)

↓

Step 2: Bone-on-bone (Ahlbäck III/IV)? → Yes + MPTA >85° + Tegner <5 → UKA

↓

Step 3: Partial wear (Ahlbäck I/II) + MPTA <85° + age <60 → HTO (MOWHTO)

↓

Step 4: Grey Zone (mixed features) → Shared Decision:

• Fast recovery, low demand → UKA

• High sport demand, bone stock preservation → HTO

9. Key Studies Summary

📋 Note on Evidence Base The source document references multiple meta-analyses, registry studies, and consensus guidelines. Below are the most impactful studies identified in the source. The source did not provide explicit Level of Evidence classifications for each individual study; levels are inferred from study design.

1. Meta-Analysis: HTO vs UKA for Medial Knee OA (56,000+ patients)

Design: Systematic review & meta-analysis Level I
Key Finding: UKA achieves superior pain reduction, lower overall complication rates (RR = 0.37), and shorter operative time. HTO preserves ROM and demonstrates superior KSS Function scores.41, 42

2. Meta-Analysis: Return to Sport After UKA vs HTO (2,673 patients)

Design: Meta-analysis & meta-regression Level I
Key Finding: UKA achieves faster RTS (RR = 1.42). However, HTO sustains higher long-term activity levels (Tegner 4.25 vs 2.78 at 2 years).35

3. AKUMA Grey-Zone Outcomes Study (8.0-year follow-up)

Design: Multicenter retrospective cohort Level III
Key Finding: 72.0% grey-zone HTO patients achieve WOMAC ≥80 for daily function, but only 6.8% return to Tegner ≥5. Ahlbäck Grade 3 is a strong negative predictor (OR = 0.20).8, 19

4. Comparative Outcomes of Revision TKA: HTO-TKA vs UKA-TKA (10,045 patients)

Design: Systematic review & meta-analysis (11 studies) Level I
Key Finding: HTO-TKA requires fewer revision implants (OR = 0.11), shorter operative time (MD = −9.15 min), superior function, and drastically lower re-revision risk (HR 2.3–2.7 for UKA-TKA).25, 48, 50

5. ESSKA Formal Consensus on Osteotomy (2024)

Design: Formal consensus guideline Level V
Key Finding: HTO mandated when MPTA <85°. Postoperative JLO must not exceed 5°. Prior HTO not absolute contraindication for UKA.7, 54, 56

6. KOG Statement on HTO in Bone-on-Bone OA

Design: Consensus statement Level V
Key Finding: Bone-on-bone OA is not an absolute contraindication for HTO, but long-term data shows 74% failure rate at 16 years for Ahlbäck Grade 3.22, 27

7. Kneeling Ability: MOWHTO vs UKA (2025 ISAKOS)

Design: Comparative clinical study Level III
Key Finding: MOWHTO patients demonstrate significantly superior postoperative kneeling ability compared to UKA.39
