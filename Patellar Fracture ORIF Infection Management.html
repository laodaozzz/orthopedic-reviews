Surgical Site Infection Following Patellar ORIF

Comprehensive Clinical Review of Risk, Diagnosis, and Management — Evidence-Based Educational Summary

1. Clinical Significance & Incidence of SSI

Open Reduction and Internal Fixation (ORIF) is the established standard of care for displaced patellar fractures, essential for anatomically realigning fractured bone segments and restoring the integrity of the knee's extensor mechanism.1,2 While ORIF is critical for achieving good functional outcomes, Surgical Site Infection (SSI) stands out as a particularly devastating complication.

⚠ Incidence Range The reported incidence of SSI following patellar ORIF ranges from 1% to 10%.5 A detailed retrospective cohort study identified a specific incidence of 9.3% in patients undergoing operative management for patella fractures.7 Level III

Consequences of established SSI include:5,7

  • Prolonged hospitalization and multiple revision surgeries
  • Significant increase in healthcare expenditures — a ninefold increase in direct hospital costs compared to non-infected cases7 Level III
  • Permanent functional impairment, chronic pain, and diminished quality of life
  • Reoperation rates already high (34–57% even without infection), dramatically worsened by FRI60

Pathophysiology: Why the Patella Is Uniquely Vulnerable

The patella's susceptibility to infection is profoundly linked to its unique anatomy. As a subcutaneous bone with a very thin soft-tissue envelope, the patella has minimal protective layers separating the surgical field from the external environment.12 This vulnerability is often exacerbated by high-energy direct trauma, which compromises overlying soft tissues and their vascular supply.2

Orthopedic implants (wires, screws) introduce an avascular surface susceptible to bacterial adhesion and biofilm formation.14,15 The biofilm shields bacteria from the immune system and reduces antibiotic susceptibility, potentially leading to a persistent, chronic infectious state — termed Fracture-Related Infection (FRI).14,17

2. Risk Stratification for Surgical Site Infection

2.1 Patient-Specific Risk Factors

Diabetes Mellitus Level III

One of the most consistently identified independent risk factors. In elderly patients, 44.4% of the SSI group had diabetes vs. 21.0% in the non-SSI group (p=0.017).5 Mechanisms include impaired leukocyte function, compromised microcirculation, and diminished wound healing capacity.8

Body Mass Index (BMI) Level III

The SSI group had a significantly higher median BMI (26.42 kg/m²) compared to the non-SSI group (24.22 kg/m², p=0.016).5 An optimal predictive cutoff of BMI > 25.39 kg/m² was identified.18 Obesity (BMI >30) is a well-established risk factor due to poor vascularity of adipose tissue and chronic systemic inflammation.8

Nutritional & Hematological Status Level III

Low serum albumin (ALB) and low hematocrit (HCT) are confirmed independent risk factors:18

  • Hypoalbuminemia (ALB < 39.3 g/L): indicates malnutrition, impairs collagen synthesis and immune cell production
  • Anemia (HCT < 35.62%): reduces oxygen-carrying capacity critical for oxidative bacterial killing. SSI group mean HCT was 35.21% vs. 37.86% (p=0.020)5

Age & General Health Level III

Advanced age is associated with immunosenescence and higher prevalence of comorbidities. ASA score >2–3 (significant systemic disease) is a confirmed risk factor.19

Lifestyle Factors Not quantified in source

Smoking impairs wound healing via vasoconstriction and reduced tissue oxygenation. Chronic alcohol consumption is also linked to increased postoperative complications.8

2.2 Injury- and Procedure-Specific Risk Factors

Open Fractures: Direct gross contamination dramatically increases infection risk. While one patellar study did not find statistical significance (likely due to small numbers), large meta-analyses confirm open fracture as a major predictor.5

Surgical Delay (Time to Surgery): A critical, modifiable independent risk factor. The SSI group had a median delay of 7 days vs. 4 days in the non-infected group (p=0.002). A delay of >6 days was identified as a predictive cutoff.5 Level III

High-Energy Trauma: Results in more widespread tissue devitalization and disrupted local blood supply, creating a larger zone of compromised tissue.8

Prolonged Surgical Duration: Increased tissue exposure, handling, and desiccation elevate contamination risk.5

3. Diagnostic Algorithm for Suspected Postoperative Infection

3.1 Clinical Evaluation

The CDC criteria form the basis for SSI diagnosis:9,28

  • Superficial Incisional SSI: Purulent drainage from the incision, or ≥1 sign of inflammation (pain/tenderness, localized swelling, erythema, heat)5
  • Deep Infection: Persistent pain disproportionate to expected recovery, wound dehiscence, systemic symptoms (fever, chills, night sweats), or gross motion at fracture site23

3.3 Advanced Imaging

  • Plain Radiographs: Initial modality; may show peri-implant lucency, periosteal reaction, hardware loosening, sequestra in subacute/chronic settings9
  • MRI: Excellent soft-tissue contrast, 98% sensitivity but 78% specificity; limited by metal artifact23
  • CT: Superior bony detail, less affected by metal artifact; useful for preoperative planning in infected non-union23
  • WBC-labeled Scans: Valuable for differentiating infection from aseptic loosening when MRI is non-diagnostic23

3.4 Microbiological Diagnosis — The Gold Standard

🔬 Intraoperative Cultures: Definitive Diagnosis The gold standard is aseptic collection of minimum 3–5 deep tissue/fluid samples during surgery using separate, sterile instruments.9 Superficial wound swabs are notoriously unreliable.23 Antibiotic therapy should be discontinued ≥2 weeks prior to sampling when clinically safe.15 The most commonly isolated organism is Staphylococcus aureus.9
⚡ Diagnostic-Therapeutic Imperative Clinical signs and rising CRP may raise suspicion for deep infection, but non-invasive imaging is often not definitive early on. Because the gold standard requires deep intraoperative cultures, a high index of clinical suspicion creates a necessity to operate — simultaneously obtaining a definitive diagnosis and performing the critical first step of management: thorough debridement.

4. Classification & Impact on Treatment Strategy

4.1 Anatomical Classification (CDC Criteria)28

  • Superficial Incisional SSI: Confined to skin and subcutaneous tissue. Managed with local wound care and antibiotics.5
  • Deep Incisional SSI: Involves fascia and muscle layers. May present with purulent drainage, spontaneous dehiscence, or abscess. Mandates aggressive surgical debridement.9
  • Organ/Space SSI: Most severe — involves structures deeper than the fascia (septic arthritis, patellar osteomyelitis). Requires extensive surgical intervention.9

4.2 Chronological Classification (Biofilm Maturity Proxy)

Time of onset serves as a direct proxy for biofilm maturity, the fundamental reason management strategies differ:9

  • Early Onset (<2–4 weeks): Florid signs; virulent organisms (e.g., S. aureus); immature biofilm — DAIR viable7
  • Delayed Onset (2–10 weeks): Subacute presentation; maturing biofilm — DAIR has lower success
  • Late Onset (>10 weeks): Chronic subtle symptoms; low-virulence organisms (e.g., coagulase-negative staphylococci); mature biofilm — implant removal nearly always required9

5. Management Protocols for Established SSI

5.1 Superficial Incisional SSI

Conservative management: meticulous local wound care (sterile cleansing, dressing changes), possible bedside incision and drainage for localized fluid collections, and systemic antibiotics targeted to the causative organism or empirically covering S. aureus.18,25 Vigilant monitoring for progression to deeper infection is essential.

5.2 Deep SSI / FRI: The Imperative for Surgical Debridement

⚠ Surgical Debridement Is Non-Negotiable For infections involving deep fascia, muscle, or bone, systemic antibiotics alone are ineffective. Debridement must be radical — complete removal of all purulent material, unhealthy tissue, and non-viable bone. Multiple staged procedures are often required before definitive reconstruction.26,40

5.3 The Hardware Dilemma: DAIR vs. Implant Removal

DAIR (Debridement, Antibiotics, and Implant Retention):38

  • Best indications: Early-onset infection (<2–4 weeks), mechanically stable implant, intact soft-tissue envelope, no draining sinus, susceptible pathogen7
  • Risk factors for failure: S. aureus (especially MRSA), sinus tract present, symptoms >8 days before debridement42
  • Anti-biofilm antibiotics (e.g., rifampicin for staphylococci) are critical adjuncts7

Implant Removal / Exchange:

  • If fracture healed: Remove all hardware — no longer needed for stability, only perpetuates infection7
  • If unhealed (infected non-union): Multi-stage approach:
    1. Radical debridement and hardware removal
    2. Antibiotic-impregnated spacer (PMMA) for local delivery
    3. External fixator for stability
    4. Prolonged systemic antibiotics per culture results
    5. Delayed definitive fixation and bone grafting once infection eradicated26

5.4 Antibiotic Therapy

  • Systemic: Broad-spectrum IV initially, tailored to cultures in consultation with infectious disease. Often 4–6 weeks IV, sometimes followed by long-term oral suppression.26
  • Local: Antibiotic-laden PMMA beads/spacer or absorbable carriers (calcium sulfate) deliver high local concentrations with minimal systemic toxicity.16

5.5 Salvage Procedures

  • Total Patellectomy: Salvage for refractory patellar osteomyelitis; compromises extensor mechanism power but can yield pain-free limb7
  • Knee Arthrodesis: Limb-salvage for chronic uncontrolled infection; eliminates motion but provides stable, pain-free ambulation48
  • Amputation: Last resort for life-threatening, uncontrolled infection with extensive loss25

6. Evidence-Based Prevention: The Bundle Approach

6.1 Preoperative Phase

  • Risk factor modification: Optimize glycemic control in diabetes, smoking cessation counseling, nutritional supplementation for malnourished patients18
  • Preoperative cleansing: Shower/bathe with antiseptic soap night before or morning of surgery49
  • Decolonization: Intranasal mupirocin for known S. aureus carriers50
  • Antibiotic prophylaxis: First-generation cephalosporin (Cefazolin) IV within 60–120 min before incision; discontinue within 24 hours8,50 For severe open fractures (Gustilo III), add Gram-negative coverage (aminoglycoside)22

6.2 Intraoperative Phase

  • Skin preparation: Alcohol-based antiseptic (chlorhexidine gluconate) — superior to povidone-iodine50
  • Surgical technique: Gentle soft-tissue handling, minimize desiccation, careful hemostasis, minimize operative time5
  • Wound irrigation: Thorough irrigation with sterile normal saline (AAOS guideline)53
  • Local antibiotics: Vancomycin powder in wound may reduce SSI in high-risk fractures16
  • Adhesive incise drapes: Not recommended — may increase SSI risk52

6.3 Postoperative Phase

  • Wound dressings: Sterile dressing clean, dry, intact for ≥48 hours. Negative pressure wound therapy for high-risk incisions37,53
  • Patient education: Teach recognition of infection signs — fever >38.6°C, chills, increasing pain, swelling, redness, drainage37
  • Rehabilitation: Structured physical therapy per protocol for optimal functional outcome1

7. Long-Term Sequelae & Functional Outcomes

7.1 Cascade to Chronic Complications

SSI acts as a potent negative multiplier, amplifying the risk and severity of all other complications:4,6

  • Nonunion: Infection disrupts the biological environment required for bone healing
  • Arthrofibrosis: Multiple surgeries and inflammation promote excessive scar tissue and severe knee stiffness
  • Chronic Osteomyelitis: Persistent bone infection with sequestra harboring bacteria, inaccessible to antibiotics, often requiring multiple debridements40,61

7.2 Functional Impact

Even in uncomplicated cases, long-term deficits persist at mean 6.5-year follow-up: chronic pain, restricted ROM (38% with >5° flexion loss), and significant quadriceps weakness (mean 26% isometric extension deficit).12

FRI further worsens outcomes: a 3.3-point reduction in VR-12 Physical Component Score11 and markedly lower KOOS subscale scores across all domains (pain, symptoms, ADL, quality of life).7
