I. Pharmacological Framework: GI Mechanism of GLP-1 Agonists

1.1 The "Enterogastrone Effect" LOE II

GLP-1 receptor agonists (GLP-1RAs), such as semaglutide, are synthetic mimetics of the natural incretin hormone GLP-1. A primary physiological function of GLP-1 is to act as an "enterogastrone" — a factor that slows gastric emptying and inhibits gastric acid secretion.[2] This gastric-slowing effect may even outweigh the drugs' direct insulinotropic and glucagonostatic effects on glycemic control.[2]

Mechanically, exogenous GLP-1 relaxes the proximal stomach, reduces antral and duodenal motility, and increases pyloric tone.[2] This process is mediated through myenteric neuronal mechanisms and appears dependent on intact vagal nerve function.[5]

⚠ Critical Perioperative Conflict The primary therapeutic action (delayed gastric emptying) is mechanistically identical to the primary perioperative hazard (retained gastric contents / gastroparesis).[3] A patient deriving therapeutic benefit is, by definition, at increased risk for anesthesia-related complications.

1.2 Pharmacokinetics and Pharmacodynamics LOE II

The perioperative concern is magnified by the pharmacokinetic profile of modern long-acting agents. Semaglutide has a half-life of approximately 7 days.[7]

  • Long Washout: The drug remains in circulation for approximately 5 weeks after the final dose.[6]
  • Delayed Steady State: Steady-state plasma concentrations are reached only after 4–5 weeks of consistent weekly administration.[7]
  • Pharmacodynamic Uncertainty: It takes approximately 23 days (3.3 half-lives) for semaglutide plasma levels to drop below 10% of initial concentration. However, the actual recovery of gastric motility is not perfectly correlated with plasma concentration.[8]
📌 Clinical Implication Short-term holds are pharmacologically futile. Holding a weekly agent for 1–2 days has negligible impact. Even a 7-day hold reduces plasma concentration by only ~50%. This provides the rationale for the 14-day hold recommendation for TJA patients.[10]

1.3 The Tachyphylaxis Hypothesis LOE II

The effect of GLP-1RAs on gastric emptying is not static. The phenomenon of tachyphylaxis — a waning of the pharmacodynamic effect with prolonged, continuous exposure — is central to understanding divergent perioperative guidelines.[4]

  • Short-Acting Agents (Lixisenatide, Exenatide): Profound and sustained deceleration of gastric emptying with little evidence of tachyphylaxis — effect remains strong even after 8 weeks.[12]
  • Long-Acting Agents (Semaglutide, Liraglutide): Subject to tachyphylaxis — the delay in gastric emptying attenuates with chronic use.[4][11]
🔬 Key Insight Risk of gastroparesis is highest in new users in the "escalation phase" and attenuates in stable, long-term "maintenance" users.[15] This is the single most important concept for reconciling divergent guidelines.

II. The Evolving Guideline Landscape

The rapid adoption of GLP-1RAs has outpaced high-quality evidence, resulting in a chaotic and contradictory landscape of recommendations from major anesthesiology and surgical societies.[16]

2.1 ASA 2023: "Hold the Medication" LOE IV (Consensus)

In June 2023, the ASA issued precautionary guidance in response to anecdotal case reports of pulmonary aspiration.[11]

  • Recommendation: Hold daily agents on day of surgery; hold weekly agents (e.g., semaglutide) for one week prior.
  • Rationale: Reduce risk of regurgitation and pulmonary aspiration.
  • Mitigation: If not held, or if active GI symptoms → treat patient as "full stomach" (consider delay or rapid sequence intubation).[11]

2.2 October 2024 Multi-Society Reversal: "Continue for Most, Risk-Stratify" LOE III

ASA, AGA, ASMBS, and others issued a significant reversal in October 2024.[14]

  • Recommendation: Most patients should continue GLP-1RAs before elective surgery.
  • Risk Stratification — Low-Risk (Continue): Stable maintenance dose, asymptomatic.
  • Risk Stratification — High-Risk (Mitigate/Delay):
    1. Patients in the "escalation phase" (new users)
    2. Active GI symptoms (nausea, vomiting, bloating)
    3. Higher doses
    4. Comorbidities that slow gastric emptying (gastroparesis, Parkinson's, severe diabetes)
  • High-Risk Mitigation: 24-hour liquid diet, point-of-care gastric ultrasound, or delay surgery.[14]

2.3 European / UK Divergence

ESAIC (Europe): Recommends holding weekly agents for at least one week, and two weeks if the indication is obesity — directly conflicting with the 2024 ASA reversal.[18]

CPOC (UK, 2025): Aligns with the 2024 ASA guidance — patients should continue these drugs, managed with full risk assessment and anesthetic mitigation techniques.[25]

2.4 AAOS 2025: Orthopedic-Specific "Hold 14 Days" LOE III

🦴 Orthopedic Landmark Data A large TriNetX study of TKA/THA patients found that any GLP-1RA use within 7 days of TJA remained an independent risk factor for anesthesia complications (aspiration, aspiration pneumonitis, conversion to intubation). The 14-day cessation period was optimal — 14-day and 30-day cohorts had no independent risk factor.[10]

III. Risk Analysis I: Gastroparesis & Retained Gastric Content

3.1 Quantifying the Risk of Retained Gastric Content LOE II (Meta-analysis)

A 2024 systematic review and meta-analysis (14 studies, 2,143 patients) quantified the risk:[29]

  • GLP-1RA use associated with OR 7.66 (95% CI: 3.42–17.17) for pre-procedural GI symptoms.
  • OR 6.08 (95% CI: 2.86–12.94) for elevated retained gastric content (RGC).[29]

Corroborating observational evidence:

  • Retrospective EGD analysis: RGC in 24.2% of semaglutide users vs. 5.1% of controls.[13]
  • Prospective gastric ultrasound study: 40% prevalence of RGC in semaglutide users (within 10 days) vs. 3% in non-users.[30]
  • Cross-sectional study: 30.5% increase in RGC after controlling for confounders.[30]

3.2 Aspiration Risk: Low-Frequency, High-Severity LOE II

⚡ The Clinical Disconnect While the risk factor (RGC) is unequivocally increased, the adverse outcome (pulmonary aspiration) link is less clear. The 2024 meta-analysis found no significant difference in aspiration event rates.[29] A 2025 systematic review confirmed RR 1.00 (95% CI: 0.76–1.30) for elective surgery aspiration.[34] However, the AAOS 2025 study was among the first to link GLP-1RA use to aspiration pneumonitis in the TJA population specifically.[10]

IV. Risk Analysis II: Ileus and Bowel Obstruction

4.1 Functional vs. Mechanical Obstruction

A critical clinical distinction:[35][37]

  • Paralytic (Functional) Ileus: Reduction/cessation of bowel motor activity without physical blockage — an expected pharmacologic extension of the GLP-1RA mechanism.
  • Mechanical Bowel Obstruction: Physical blockage of the intestinal lumen (adhesions, volvulus, intussusception).

4.2 Conflicting Evidence LOE II–III

The literature is highly contradictory:

  • Increased Risk: Large real-world study: HR 4.22 (95% CI: 1.02–17.40) for bowel obstruction in GLP-1RA users vs. bupropion-naltrexone users.[40] Pharmacovigilance studies report 3.5–4.5× increase in intestinal obstruction rates.[12]
  • No Significant Risk: 2024 meta-analysis concluded that GLP-1RAs as a class do not significantly increase bowel obstruction or ileus risk.[38] Danish nationwide cohort study in IBD patients found no increased risk.[30]
  • Decreased Risk: Mendelian randomization study suggested GLP-1RAs are causally associated with decreased risk of paralytic ileus in T2D patients.[35]

4.3 Agent-Specific Risk LOE II

🔍 Critical Subgroup Finding The same 2024 meta-analysis that found no class-wide risk performed a subgroup analysis showing liraglutide was associated with significantly increased bowel obstruction/ileus risk (OR 3.0, 95% CI: 2.03–4.45), while semaglutide risk was not significant.[41] A Bayesian network meta-analysis of 48 RCTs confirmed different GLP-1RAs have significantly different GI adverse event profiles.[46]

V. Clinical Focus: The Orthopedic Paradox

GLP-1RAs are simultaneously associated with increased acute anesthetic risk and decreased subacute surgical risk in orthopedic patients.

Phase-Dependent Effects

Phase 1: Pre-Op (>3 mo) 🟢 SURGICAL BENEFIT
Phase 2: Day of Surgery (0–14 d) 🔴 ANESTHETIC LIABILITY
Phase 3: Post-Op (Days 1–10) 🟡 DIAGNOSTIC RISK

5.1 Anesthetic Risk: AAOS 2025 "Hold 14 Days" LOE III

As detailed above, the AAOS 2025 data confirms that any GLP-1RA use within 7 days of TJA is an independent risk factor for anesthesia complications. 14-day cessation is optimal.[10]

5.2 The Counter-Narrative: Improved Orthopedic Outcomes

Total Joint Arthroplasty (TJA)

  • 2023 retrospective study (n=7,051 TKA): Semaglutide cohort had lower odds of 90-day sepsis (OR 0.23), 2-year PJI (OR 0.70), and 90-day readmissions (OR 0.71).[47] (Also noted higher odds of MI and AKI — complex risk-benefit profile.)
  • 2023 cohort study: GLP-1RA users had equal or lower rates of overall perioperative complications and 90-day readmissions.[48]
  • 2024 study: Minimum 2–3 months preoperative semaglutide exposure required for significant reduction in adverse events.[49]

Spine Surgery

  • 2025 spinal fusion study: GLP-1RA use associated with lower infection rates, fewer revisions, better QoL.[50]
  • 2025 lumbar fusion study: Statistically significant reduction in postoperative length of stay.[52]
  • 2025 lumbar spine study: No increase in short/intermediate-term complications; reduced pseudarthrosis at 1 and 3 years.[53]
  • 2025 cervical study: GLP-1RA use not associated with increased short-term adverse outcomes.[54]

5.3 Postoperative Diagnostic Dilemma LOE IV (Case Report)

⚠ Case Report: The GLP-1 Mimic A 55-year-old woman resumed her GLP-1RA on postoperative day 1 after major abdominal surgery. Ten days later, she presented with nausea, vomiting, and abdominal pain. Imaging showed delayed enteric transit, interpreted as small bowel obstruction, and exploratory laparotomy was scheduled. Surgery was cancelled after clinical improvement with conservative management. Final diagnosis: severe GLP-1RA pharmacological effect, not surgical SBO.[55]

VI. Synthesis & Evidence-Based Recommendations

6.1 Reconciling the Paradox

GLP-1RAs have distinct, phase-dependent effects during the perioperative journey:

  • Phase 1 (Pre-Op Optimization, >3 months prior): Powerful surgical benefit — improved glycemic control, reduced BMI, mitigation of major risk factors. Benefits accrue with ≥2–3 months of therapy. May include direct immunomodulatory effects (enhanced neutrophil migration, reduced PJI).[47][49][58]
  • Phase 2 (Day of Surgery, 0–14 days prior): Residual pharmacodynamic effect is a significant anesthetic liability. AAOS data confirms TJA patients should hold for 14 days.[10]
  • Phase 3 (Post-Op, Days 1–10): Re-initiation before full bowel recovery creates a diagnostic mimic, confounding ileus vs. mechanical obstruction diagnosis.[55]

6.2 Proposed Orthopedic Protocol

✅ Evidence-Based Framework for Orthopedic Surgery
  1. Preoperative Optimization: For elective TJA and major spine surgery, initiate GLP-1RA therapy at least 3 months preoperatively to achieve metabolic and infection-reduction benefits.[49]
  2. Preoperative Cessation: For elective TJA, hold weekly-dosed GLP-1RAs (e.g., semaglutide) for 14 days pre-procedure (superseding the generalist 2024 ASA guideline). For daily-dosed agents (e.g., liraglutide), hold for ~3 days (~5 half-lives).[10]
  3. Day of Surgery Management: For trauma or patients who did not adhere to the 14-day hold: treat with full stomach precautions (rapid sequence intubation). Point-of-care gastric ultrasound is strongly recommended.[19]
  4. Postoperative Re-initiation: Do not resume GLP-1RAs until unambiguous clinical evidence of full return of bowel function (tolerating diet, consistent bowel movements, resolution of any ileus) to avoid the diagnostic trap of pseudo-obstruction.[55]

6.3 Key Evidence Gaps & Future Directions

🔬 Unanswered Questions
  1. What is the precise mechanism (metabolic, immunologic, or bone-related) for the observed reduction in PJI and pseudarthrosis?[58]
  2. Does the 14-day "hold" period required for anesthetic safety negate any of the long-term surgical benefits?
  3. What is the precise, evidence-based timing for safe postoperative re-initiation?

Prospective, randomized controlled trials are urgently needed to validate these retrospective findings.
