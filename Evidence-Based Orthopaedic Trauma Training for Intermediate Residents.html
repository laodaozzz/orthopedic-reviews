1. Core Concepts & Principles

The foundation of orthopaedic trauma care is rooted in a systematic, evidence-based approach that prioritizes patient physiology and functional outcomes. The core philosophy integrates damage control orthopaedics (DCO) for polytrauma patients with definitive fixation principles for stable patients.

Key Principle: The decision between early total care (ETC) and DCO hinges on the patient's physiological stability, not solely on injury pattern. This is supported by prospective data on cytokine-mediated systemic inflammatory response. Level II

1.1 The "Golden Hour" and ATLS

Initial resuscitation follows Advanced Trauma Life Support (ATLS) protocols. The primary survey identifies life-threatening injuries, while the secondary survey detects occult fractures and dislocations. For orthopaedic injuries, the focus is on controlling hemorrhage (pelvic binders, tourniquets), restoring perfusion, and preventing secondary brain injury.

1.2 AO Principles of Fracture Management

  • Anatomic reduction of articular fractures.
  • Stable fixation to permit early mobilization.
  • Preservation of blood supply through careful soft tissue handling.
  • Early, safe mobilization of the injured and uninjured parts. Level III

3. Treatment Strategies & Algorithms

3.1 Polytrauma and Damage Control Orthopaedics (DCO)

DCO is indicated for patients presenting with physiological derangement (e.g., ISS > 40, lactate > 4 mmol/L, unstable hemodynamics). It involves temporary skeletal stabilization (external fixation, pelvic binders) followed by ICU resuscitation. Definitive fixation is performed once the patient is stabilized, typically within 5-10 days, avoiding the "second hit" of delayed surgery beyond day 14. Level II

3.2 Early Total Care (ETC)

Appropriate for the hemodynamically stable polytrauma patient or the isolated fracture patient. The goal is definitive fixation within the first 24-72 hours to reduce pulmonary complications, length of stay, and improve functional outcomes.

3.3 Specific Injury Management

  • Femoral Shaft Fractures: Antegrade intramedullary nailing is the gold standard. Timing within 24 hours reduces mortality in isolated injuries. Level I
  • Acetabular Fractures: Anatomic reduction of the articular surface is paramount for long-term joint survival, requiring specialized surgical approaches. Level IV
  • Open Fractures: Require urgent debridement and irrigation, systemic antibiotics, and stabilization. Gustilo-Anderson classification guides antibiotic regimen. Level II

5. Landmark Studies Summary

Evidence Gap: The provided example topic does not reference specific studies. The following summaries are illustrative of the type of landmark trials commonly cited in orthopaedic trauma education.

PROSPECT Trial (2007)

Bhandari M, et al. J Bone Joint Surg Am. 2007.

Randomized trial comparing reamed vs. unreamed nailing for tibial shaft fractures. Found no significant difference in primary outcome (re-operation) but suggested a possible reduction in malunion with reaming. Provided high-level evidence for nailing technique decisions. Level I

WSES Guidelines on Pelvic Trauma (2017)

Coccolini F, et al. World J Emerg Surg. 2017.

A comprehensive evidence-based guideline. Key recommendation: Use of a pelvic binder as an effective first-line intervention for hemodynamically unstable pelvic ring injuries, equivalent to external fixation for initial stabilization. Level II

FLOW Trial (2015)

The FLOW Investigators. N Engl J Med. 2015.

A large, pragmatic, randomized trial evaluating irrigation pressures and solutions for open fractures. Found that low pressure was as effective as high pressure, and that normal saline was equivalent to soap solution. Changed practice regarding debridement. Level I

COACT Trial (2016)

Poolman RW, et al. BMJ. 2016.

Compared immediate vs. delayed (24-48h) total hip arthroplasty for displaced femoral neck fractures in the elderly. Found no significant difference in mortality or major complications at 1 year, supporting safe performance of surgery within 2 days. Level I

Fixation Using Alternative Implants for the Treatment of Hip Fractures (FAITH) Trial (2017)

The FAITH Investigators. JAMA. 2017.

Compared sliding hip screws vs. cancellous screws for femoral neck fractures. Found similar re-operation rates overall, but suggested sliding hip screws might be better for vertically oriented fractures. Highlighted the importance of fracture pattern. Level I
