1. Introduction: The Clinical Imperative of the Ankylosed Spine

Clinical Alert: Minor trauma that would cause simple soft-tissue strain in a healthy individual can produce catastrophic, three-column fractures ("chalk-stick" fractures / Andersson lesions) in patients with Ankylosing Spondylitis (AS). Mortality and morbidity rates are significantly higher than in the general trauma population.

The management of cervical spine trauma in patients with Ankylosing Spondylitis represents one of the most technically demanding and high-stakes challenges in modern spinal surgery. AS, a chronic inflammatory spondyloarthropathy, fundamentally alters the biomechanical and structural properties of the vertebral column. Through a progressive cascade of syndesmophyte formation, ligamentous ossification, and facet joint ankylosis, the spine is transformed from a flexible, segmented organ into a rigid, monolithic tube — the "bamboo spine."

This transformation paradoxically renders the spine exceptionally brittle. The rigid column acts as a long lever arm, concentrating stress forces at any point of disruption. Consequently, even minor trauma can cause three-column fractures, and due to the inherent instability of these injuries and the poor bone quality associated with the disease, neurological deficits are common and often devastating.

The Fixation Evolution

Historically, posterior cervical fixation relied on wiring techniques or Lateral Mass Screws (LMS). However, biomechanical analyses and clinical outcomes have demonstrated the insufficiency of these methods in the osteoporotic, ankylosed spine:

  • LMS rely on purchase of the relatively thin lateral mass cortex and frequently fail due to screw loosening and pull-out under the high torque and flexion moments of the AS spine I (Refs 2, 3)
  • Anterior approaches alone have failure rates approaching 50%, largely due to inability of anterior screws to hold in osteoporotic vertebral body against lever arm forces II (Ref 4)
  • Subaxial Cervical Pedicle Screws (CPS) provide approximately twice the pull-out strength of LMS (~1200 N vs ~300–350 N) and superior fatigue resistance I (Refs 2, 3)
Key Principle
The goal of surgical intervention in AS fractures is not merely stabilization but the provision of immediate, rigid fixation that can withstand the immense shear and cantilever forces exerted by the long, fused spinal segments above and below the injury site.

2. Pathoanatomy & Biomechanics of the Ankylosed Cervical Spine

2.1 The "Bamboo Spine" and Altered Landmarks

In the standard subaxial cervical spine, the surgeon relies on a constellation of bony landmarks: the lateral mass, superior/inferior articular processes, facet joint line, and lamina. In advanced AS, these structures are homogenized into a continuous sheet of bone:

  • Facet Joint Fusion: Synovial joints are completely ankylosed. The joint line is obliterated, replaced by smooth or irregular bony ridge N/S (Ref 1)
  • Ligamentous Ossification: Supraspinous, interspinous ligaments, and ligamentum flavum ossify — eliminating the tactile "give" surgeons rely on and creating a rigid posterior tension band that, once fractured, snaps violently N/S (Ref 5)
  • Lateral Mass Distortion: Syndesmophytes bridging lateral margins extend posteriorly, distorting perception of lateral mass boundaries N/S (Ref 9)

2.2 Osteoporosis and Screw Purchase

Critical Paradox: "Sclerotic Osteoporosis"

Abundant heterotopic ossification coexists with severe osteopenia within the vertebral body. While the spine appears robust on plain radiographs due to calcified ligaments, the internal bone quality is poor.

  • Hounsfield Units (HU): Quantitative CT analysis reveals low HU values in cancellous bone, correlating with low bone mineral density. The probe may encounter very little resistance once the cortex is breached N/S (Ref 5)
  • CPS Pull-out Strength: Even in osteoporotic bone, engagement of dorsal and ventral cortices of the pedicle and vertebral body provides necessary stability I (Ref 2)

2.3 Pedicle Morphology in the Subaxial Spine

Despite external distortions, the internal morphology of the cervical pedicle in AS typically remains conserved, although cortical thickness may vary III (Ref 7).

2.4 The "Danger Zones"

Critical Neurovascular Structures at Risk

  • Lateral: Vertebral artery in foramen transversarium — within 1–2 mm of lateral cortex in subaxial spine
  • Medial: Spinal cord — subarachnoid space buffer of only a few millimeters
  • Superior/Inferior: Nerve roots exit through neural foramina; superior margin of pedicle creates floor for exiting root

3. Preoperative Planning & Imaging Protocols

3.1 Computed Tomography (CT) Analysis

A thin-cut (1 mm) CT scan with multi-planar reconstruction (MPR) is mandatory. The surgeon must meticulously analyze each level intended for instrumentation III (Refs 7, 8).

Critical Preoperative Measurements

  • Pedicle Width (Isthmus): Inner cortical diameter must be measured at every level. Minimum 3.5 mm required for standard 3.5 mm screw. Levels measuring 2.5–3.5 mm require smaller screws (2.7 mm), extreme caution, or skip constructs III (Ref 8)
  • Trajectory Mapping: Specific transverse angle measured per vertebra — note angle relative to posterior laminar surface (accessible intraoperatively)
  • Vertebral Artery Course: Must trace for "high-riding" arteries or anomalous loops entering foramen at C4/C5 instead of C6 III (Ref 7)

3.2 The "Pedicle Axis View" Simulation

3D reconstruction software can simulate the "pedicle axis view" — rotating the model to look directly down the barrel of the pedicle from a posterior-oblique angle. This "mind's eye" view is crucial for the freehand technique N/S (Ref 11).

4. Surgical Technique: Freehand CPS Placement

Definition
"Freehand" implies the absence of real-time computer navigation. It does not imply lack of guidance. Guidance is derived from surface anatomy, tactile feedback, and direct visualization of the internal pedicle walls via laminoforaminotomy.

4.1 Positioning and Exposure

  • Positioning: Prone position. In AS patients with fixed cervical kyphosis, the head must be supported in neutral position relative to the deformity — attempting to force extension can cause iatrogenic fracture or cord compression. Mayfield head holder preferred N/S (Ref 13)
  • Exposure: Standard midline posterior approach. Subperiosteal dissection laterally to lateral margins of lateral masses. In AS, requires careful use of Cobb elevators, monopolar cautery, and possibly high-speed burrs to remove osteophytes N/S (Ref 13)

4.2 Anatomical Landmarks and Entry Points

Several landmark systems have been described. In AS, these must be applied with the understanding that the "surface" may be distorted by bone growth.

4.3 The Laminoforaminotomy Technique III

🎯 The "Gold Standard" for Safety in Freehand CPS

The laminoforaminotomy technique converts the "blind" passage of the pedicle probe into a visually verified procedure. It is the single most effective risk-mitigation strategy in freehand CPS surgery.

  1. Create the Window: Using a high-speed diamond or cutting burr (2–3 mm), create a small laminotomy at the junction of the superior lamina of the target vertebra and the inferior lamina of the cranial vertebra. In AS, the ossified ligamentum flavum may require burring through a continuous bony bridge.
  2. Expose the Dura: Deepen the window until the dura or underlying root axilla is visualized.
  3. Palpate the Medial Wall: Introduce a small nerve hook, Penfield #4, or micro-dissector. Hook laterally to palpate the medial cortex of the pedicle. This tactile step confirms the exact medial limit — even with a 5 mm osteophyte distorting the surface, the internal medial wall remains a constant boundary III (Ref 11).
  4. Define the Borders: Palpate the superior and inferior borders of the pedicle to define the safe vertical zone and prevent nerve root impingement III (Ref 7).

4.4 The "Medial Funnel" and "Slide" Techniques IV

Biomechanical Basis

The medial wall of the cervical pedicle is significantly thicker (~2.0 mm) and stronger than the lateral wall (~0.8 mm). The "Medial Funnel" technique exploits this cortical strength disparity to guide the probe N/S (Ref 16).

  1. Cortical Breach: Entry point (confirmed relative to laminotomy) is breached with a burr. Hole is widened slightly to create a "funnel" shape N/S (Ref 12).
  2. Probe Insertion: A 2 mm curved, blunt-tipped pedicle probe (gear shift) is inserted with curve facing medially.
  3. The "Slide": Surgeon aims probe with steep medial angle (40–50°). Goal: gently contact the thick medial cortical wall. As the blunt tip hits this hard wall, the probe naturally "slides" off and is deflected laterally into the softer cancellous isthmus.
  4. Advancement: The probe advances with moderate resistance (tight isthmus). If it plunges effortlessly, suspect lateral breach (vertebral artery) or anterior breach.

⚠️ AS-Specific Warning

In AS, the cancellous bone may be extremely soft. The "hard stop" of the medial wall is the critical tactile confirmation. If this hard stop is not felt, the trajectory is likely too lateral — imminent danger of vertebral artery injury IV (Refs 18, 19).

4.5 Screw Insertion and Stabilization

  • Tapping: All holes must be tapped. The bone is brittle ("chalk-stick"). Inserting a screw without tapping can cause the pedicle to burst. Use a line-to-line tap (same diameter as screw) N/S
  • Screw Selection: Polyaxial screws mandatory — heads must be mobile to align with rod, as trajectories vary significantly level to level (C3 vs. C7)
  • Washer Use: In severely osteoporotic lateral masses, washers distribute load and prevent screw head subsidence
  • Long-Segment Fixation: Standard is ≥3 levels above and below the fracture to distribute stress across the lever arm N/S (Ref 1)
  • Rod Cantilever: The high pull-out strength of CPS allows forceful cantilever reduction — this would rip out LMS I (Ref 2)

5.2 The "AS Paradox" in Navigation

⚠️ Dynamic Instability: Navigation's Achilles' Heel in AS

  • Reference Frame Shift: Navigation relies on a frame clamped to a spinous process (e.g., C2). The system assumes a fixed relationship between C2 and the target vertebra. In a "chalk-stick" fracture, the spine is unstable. If the patient moves or reduction maneuvers shift the vertebrae after CT acquisition, navigation data becomes invalid — potentially leading to catastrophic cord injury III (Ref 25)
  • Registration Challenges: Point-matching registration requires touching specific landmarks obliterated by AS fusion. Intraoperative CT (O-arm) solves automatic registration but the instability issue remains II (Ref 24)

5.3 The Case for Freehand (with Visualization)

In cases of gross instability, freehand technique with laminoforaminotomy is often safer because it is local. The surgeon references anatomy at that specific level (the medial wall of C6), not a remote reference point (C2). There is no "drift" in direct visualization.

6.2 AS-Specific Complications

  • Hardware Failure: The lever arm of the bamboo spine is immense. Risk includes screw loosening, rod breakage, or fracture at construct endpoints. Mitigation: Long-segment fixation (≥3 levels above/below) N/S (Ref 1)
  • Iatrogenic Fractures: Forceful insertion into "chalk-stick" bone can burst the pedicle or lamina. Mitigation: Use sharp burrs; tap all holes
  • Epidural Hematoma: Extensive bone removal in patients with engorged epidural veins

6.3 Meta-Analysis Data on Safety

A systematic review by Shin et al. found a relative risk of 0.39 favoring navigation over freehand for perforation I (Ref 23). However, looking at clinical complications (permanent deficit), many studies find no significant difference between expert freehand surgeons and navigated groups. This suggests that while navigation reduces minor (asymptomatic) breaches, expert freehand technique with medial wall palpation is clinically robust.

7. Key Landmark Studies

Study 1: Jo et al. — Laminoforaminotomy Accuracy III

  • Design: Retrospective review of 104 subaxial screws
  • Accuracy: 91.3% Grade 0 (perfectly contained)
  • Breaches: 9 screws — 6 Grade 2, 3 Grade 3
  • Complications: Zero neurovascular complications
  • Key Insight: Medial pedicle cortex used as safe guide; even with minor breaches, the technique prevents cord-level violations III (Ref 17)

Study 2: Lee et al. — Medial Funnel Technique IV

  • Design: 88 CPS in 28 patients
  • Accuracy: 94.3% correct placement (Grade 0/1)
  • Breach Distribution: Of 34 perforated screws, 30 were medial and only 4 were lateral
  • Interpretation: Technique biases errors toward the medial side (benign grazing of thick medial cortex) while actively protecting against catastrophic lateral (vertebral artery) breaches IV (Refs 18, 19)

Study 3: Shin et al. — Navigation Meta-Analysis I

  • Data: 8,539 screws (4,814 navigated vs. 3,725 non-navigated)
  • Result: RR 0.39 favoring navigation for perforation
  • Nuance: Neurological complications rare in both groups (0 navigated vs. 3 non-navigated), suggesting clinical safety of expert freehand placement is comparable I (Ref 23)

Study 4: Duff & Johnston et al. — Biomechanics of CPS vs. LMS I

  • Pull-out Strength: CPS ~1200 N vs. LMS ~300–350 N
  • Fatigue Testing: LMS showed "rapid loosening" under cyclic loading; CPS maintained purchase
  • Clinical Implication: In AS, where fracture healing takes months and lever arm forces are constant, fatigue resistance is the primary determinant of construct survival. LMS are programmed to fail; CPS are designed to endure I (Ref 2)

Study 5: Abumi et al. (1994) — Foundational CPS Technique N/S

  • Described the foundational technique for subaxial CPS insertion
  • Entry point: slightly lateral to center of articular mass, close to posterior margin of superior articular surface N/S (Ref 12)
  • Established the basis for all subsequent freehand techniques
Evidence Gap Note: The source document identifies these as the primary studies. No large, multicenter RCTs specifically comparing freehand vs. navigated CPS in AS trauma were identified. Evidence is primarily from retrospective case series, biomechanical studies, and meta-analyses of mixed populations.

8. Conclusions & Best Practices

Domain Expert Summary

  • Preoperative: Thin-cut CT mandatory. Measure every pedicle. If <3.5 mm → skip the level. Map vertebral artery course III
  • Technique: Utilize Laminoforaminotomy to visualize the medial wall. Do not trust surface landmarks on an ankylosed lateral mass III
  • Tactile Feedback: Use the Medial Funnel / Slide technique. Rely on the "hard stop" of the medial wall. If the probe plunges without resistance → STOP IV
  • Fixation: Long-segment constructs (minimally 2–3 levels above/below). Polyaxial screws. Tap all holes to prevent pedicle burst N/S
  • Philosophy: In the unstable AS spine, the surgeon's hand, guided by direct visualization, remains the ultimate fail-safe against the limitations of technology
Clinical Decision Algorithm

Rigid Deformity Correction (Stable): Navigation (O-arm/CT-based) is preferred — highest accuracy, complex trajectory planning.

Unstable "Chalk-Stick" Fractures: Freehand CPS with Laminoforaminotomy is safer — no reference frame drift, local anatomical verification.
