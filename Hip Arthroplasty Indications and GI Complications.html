Advanced Clinical Analysis: Hip Arthroplasty & GI Sequelae

Evidence-Based Education

Executive Summary

The management of proximal femoral fractures in the geriatric population is a critical challenge. This document synthesizes evidence on two interconnected domains: clinical indications for hip arthroplasty and the pathogenesis of fatal postoperative gastrointestinal complications.Level V

Part I: Indications for Hip Arthroplasty in Geriatric Trauma

1. Anatomical & Biomechanical Divergence

Femoral neck fractures (intracapsular) and intertrochanteric fractures (extracapsular) present fundamentally different challenges:Level IV

Key Distinction: Femoral neck fractures threaten the vascularity of the femoral head, while intertrochanteric fractures are primarily a biomechanical challenge to fixation stability.

Femoral Neck Fractures

  • Intracapsular location
  • Risk of avascular necrosis (30-50% in displaced fractures)
  • Blood supply via retinacular vessels from MFCA
  • Healing potential severely compromised

Intertrochanteric Fractures

  • Extracapsular location
  • Well-vascularized cancellous bone
  • Biomechanical challenge: posteromedial cortex (calcar)
  • Fixation failure: cutout, varus collapse

2. Indications for Arthroplasty in Femoral Neck Fractures

For displaced fractures (Garden III & IV) in elderly patients (>65 years), hip arthroplasty is the undisputed gold standard.Level I Internal fixation carries a 35-48% reoperation rate due to nonunion or avascular necrosis.

Non-Displaced Fractures (Garden I & II) - Special Indications

  • Pathologic bone with dubious screw purchaseLevel III
  • Posterior tilt >20 degrees (higher failure rate)Level III
  • Concomitant symptomatic osteoarthritis

3. Indications for Arthroplasty in Intertrochanteric Fractures

The use of arthroplasty for intertrochanteric fractures is more controversial, with internal fixation (cephalomedullary nails) remaining the standard of care.Level IV

Primary Indications for Arthroplasty

  • Hostile Bone: Severe osteoporosis (Singh Index < 3) where screw purchase is insufficient
  • Hostile Anatomy:
    • Comminuted posteromedial cortex (calcar loss)
    • Lateral wall compromise (<20.5mm)
    • Reverse obliquity patterns
  • Salvage: Failed internal fixation (nonunion, implant failure)
  • Tumor: Pathologic fractures from metastatic disease
  • Immediate Mobilization: Contralateral limb compromise, severe Parkinson's

Part II: Pathogenesis of Postoperative GI Complications

4. Acute Mesenteric Ischemia (AMI)

AMI involves abrupt cessation of blood flow to the intestines, leading to cellular hypoxia, necrosis, and perforation. The pathogenesis is multifactorial.Level IV

Pathogenesis Triad

  • Acute Mesenteric Arterial Embolism (40-50%): Cardiogenic source, lodges in SMA at Griffith's point
  • Non-Occlusive Mesenteric Ischemia (20-30%): Functional vasoconstriction from hypotension/vasopressors
  • Fat Embolism/Microvascular Occlusion: From intramedullary reaming during arthroplasty

The Ischemic Cascade

  • Hypoxic injury → anaerobic metabolism → lactate production
  • Mucosal barrier failure (3-6 hours) → bacterial translocation
  • Cytokine release → septic shock → MODS
Diagnostic Challenge: "Pain out of proportion to physical exam" is often masked by analgesia or dementia. Systemic lactate is a LATE marker.

5. Ogilvie's Syndrome (Acute Colonic Pseudo-Obstruction)

Massive colonic dilation without mechanical obstruction, linked to autonomic dysregulation from surgical stress and retroperitoneal inflammation.Level IV

Critical Threshold: Cecal diameter >12-14 cm exceeds capillary perfusion pressure, leading to ischemia, gangrene, and perforation (40-50% mortality).

6. Mechanical & Thermal Bowel Perforation

Iatrogenic injuries from hardware migration or bone cement thermal effects.Level V

  • Intrapelvic Hardware Migration: Acetabular screws, K-wire migration
  • Delayed Migration: Protrusio acetabuli causing pressure necrosis → colo-articular fistula
  • Thermal Injury: Bone cement exothermic reaction (80-100°C) → coagulative necrosis → delayed perforation (3-5 days)

7. The Mortality Cascade

Immunosenescence in elderly patients blunts typical febrile/leukocytic responses. Peritonitis triggers cytokine storm → vasodilation → MODS (80-90% mortality once established).Level IV

Dementia Impact: Increases mortality 1.5-2-fold due to diagnostic delay. Patients cannot articulate pain, presenting with delirium or refusal to eat.

  • Abdominal pain out of proportion: Suspect AMI
