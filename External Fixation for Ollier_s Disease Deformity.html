Multiple enchondromatosis (Ollier's disease) is a rare, non-hereditary skeletal dysplasia characterized by multiple benign cartilaginous tumors (enchondromas) within the medullary cavity of bones.Level V While the lower limbs are more commonly affected, forearm involvement leads to significant functional and cosmetic deformities through a predictable mechanism: enchondromas affecting the distal ulnar physis cause progressive ulnar shortening, which tethers the radius and produces a secondary compensatory radial bow, ulnar deviation of the wrist, and distal radioulnar joint (DRUJ) instability.Level IV

The primary treatment for these complex deformities — particularly in skeletally immature patients — is gradual correction using external fixation, founded on the principles of distraction osteogenesis.Level IV This approach enables simultaneous correction of limb length discrepancy and multiplanar angular deformities. However, management presents unique challenges: the procedure is technically demanding, requires meticulous planning, and is associated with high complication rates including pin-tract infections, joint stiffness, and a disease-specific tendency for accelerated bone healing leading to premature consolidation.Level IV

⚠ Critical Disease-Specific Consideration
Patients with Ollier's disease exhibit a paradoxically accelerated rate of bone healing during distraction osteogenesis. While this can shorten external fixation time, it creates the most common disease-specific complication: premature consolidation of the regenerate bone, which can halt the lengthening process and may necessitate re-osteotomy.Level IV

II. Pathoanatomy of Forearm Deformity

Etiology and Pathophysiology

Ollier's disease is caused by somatic mosaic mutations in the IDH1 and IDH2 genes, and less frequently in PTHR1.Level IV These post-zygotic mutations disrupt normal chondrocyte proliferation and differentiation at the physis, leading to persistent cartilaginous islands (enchondromas) in the metaphysis that fail endochondral ossification.

Key pathological features:Level IV

  • Non-hereditary condition — evidence points to somatic mosaicism
  • Clinical presentation typically apparent by age 10, as growth accentuates deformities
  • Enchondromas render bone structurally weaker, prone to pathological fractures
  • Lesions tend to become quiescent and may partially ossify after skeletal maturity
  • Estimated 25–50% lifetime risk of malignant transformation to chondrosarcoma — necessitates lifelong surveillanceLevel V

Characteristic Forearm Deformity Pattern

Forearm involvement in Ollier's disease follows a predictable cascade rooted in differential growth:Level IV

  1. Primary event: Enchondromas involve the distal ulnar physis → premature physeal closure or retarded growth → progressive ulnar shortening
  2. Secondary event: The radius continues normal growth but is constrained by the interosseous membrane and radioulnar joints → forced into progressive, multiplanar bowing (typically lateral and dorsal)
  3. Tertiary consequences: Positive ulnar variance, ulnar deviation of the hand, increased radial inclination, potential radial head subluxation/dislocation, DRUJ instability
💡 Central Therapeutic Principle
The logical surgical approach is not to primarily correct the bowed radius, but to address the root cause: the shortened ulna. Ulnar lengthening releases the tethering force on the radius, allowing spontaneous radial bow remodeling (in immature patients) or creating space for concurrent radial osteotomy (in older patients).Level IV

Functional and Clinical Implications

The structural derangement translates into:Level IV

  • Marked loss of forearm rotation (pronation/supination) — the most profound impairment
  • Pain from DRUJ instability, soft-tissue impingement, or tendon irritation over protuberant enchondromas
  • Cosmetic deformity and compromised functional reach

III. Principles of Gradual Correction with External Fixation

Biological Basis of Distraction Osteogenesis (DO)

The surgical management relies on distraction osteogenesis (DO), pioneered by Gavriil Ilizarov, based on the "law of tension-stress": gradual, controlled traction stimulates tissue regeneration and growth.Level III The process follows four phases:Level III

  1. Osteotomy — A low-energy surgical cut preserving periosteum and endosteal blood supply to maximize osteogenic potential
  2. Latency Phase (5–7 days) — Rest period allowing initial inflammatory cascade, hematoma formation, and fibrovascular callus development to provide a biological scaffold
  3. Distraction Phase (~1 mm/day) — Gradual separation at approximately 0.25 mm × 4 times daily. New "regenerate" bone forms as longitudinally oriented trabecular columns parallel to the distraction vector. Too slow (<0.5 mm/day) risks premature consolidation; too fast (>1.5 mm/day) compromises vascularity
  4. Consolidation Phase — Distraction ceases; the fixator provides static stability while the regenerate mineralizes, remodels, and matures into load-bearing lamellar bone. Typically lasts ≥2× the distraction phase duration

Biomechanical Considerations for Forearm Constructs

Key factors affecting construct stability:Level III

  • Pin diameter — Bending stiffness ∝ radius⁴; small diameter increases yield substantial stability gains
  • Construct geometry — Greater pin spread, more fixation points, minimized bone-to-frame distance
  • Fixator type — Monolateral for simple lengthening; circular/hexapod frames (e.g., Taylor Spatial Frame) for complex 3D deformities with computer-guided correctionLevel IV
  • Safe corridors — Ulna: subcutaneous posterior border is relatively safe. Radius: higher risk; requires careful dissection to protect neurovascular structuresLevel V
⚠ Disease-Specific Biology: Accelerated Healing
Patients with Ollier's disease consistently exhibit paradoxically accelerated bone healing during distraction compared to other conditions.Level IV This creates a clinical double-edged sword:
  • Benefit: Potentially shorter fixation duration
  • Risk: High rate of premature consolidation — the regenerate can fuse faster than the distraction rate, halting lengthening. Management requires vigilant radiographic monitoring and willingness to increase distraction rates proactively.Level IV

IV. The Surgical Management Pathway

Pre-operative Assessment and Strategic Planning

Clinical Evaluation

A thorough, quantitative physical examination is essential:Level IV

  • Objective measurement of active/passive range of motion (elbow, forearm, wrist), with particular attention to pronation and supination
  • DRUJ stability assessment (e.g., positive "piano key" sign)
  • Collaborative discussion with patient/family regarding functional goals, expectations, and the compliance demands of prolonged treatment

Radiographic Analysis

Standard Radiographs: PA and lateral views of the entire forearm (including elbow and wrist), with comparison views of the contralateral arm for templating.Level IV

Key Measurements: Ulnar variance, magnitude/apex of radial bow, radial articular angles (radial inclination, volar tilt).Level IV

Advanced Imaging (3D-CT): For complex multiplanar deformities, CT with 3D reconstruction enables precise virtual surgical planning, osteotomy simulation, and fabrication of patient-specific guides.Level IV

Operative Technique

Osteotomy

  • Site: Metaphyseal-diaphyseal junction of the ulna (proximal or distal) — broad cancellous area with excellent osteogenic potentialLevel IV
  • Intralesional Osteotomy: Contemporary evidence supports performing the osteotomy directly through an enchondromatous lesion. This approach is safe and may stimulate conversion of pathologic cartilage into normal lamellar bone via DO. It also preserves limited healthy bone segments for stable pin fixation.Level IV

External Fixator Application

  • Monolateral fixation — effective for primarily ulnar lengthening with minimal angular deformity; applied along the subcutaneous ulnar border with ≥2 half-pins per fragmentLevel IV
  • Circular/Hexapod fixation — for complex multiplanar deformities; computer software enables precise, simultaneous correction of angulation, translation, rotation, and lengthLevel IV

Distraction Protocol

After 5–7 day latency, distraction begins at 0.75–1.0 mm/day divided into 3–4 increments.Level IV Close follow-up with serial radiographs (weekly/bi-weekly) monitors alignment, regenerate quality, and screens for premature consolidation.Level IV

Post-operative Care and Rehabilitation

  • Pin-site Management: Meticulous daily cleaning with sterile solutions — the single most important measure to prevent pin-tract infectionLevel IV
  • Occupational Therapy: Intensive, therapist-led rehabilitation is non-negotiable. Daily sessions focus on maintaining full passive and active ROM of all adjacent joints to prevent contracturesLevel IV
  • Consolidation and Frame Removal: Fixator maintained until ≥3 of 4 cortices show adequate mineralization. Optional "dynamization" to stimulate final healing. Post-removal cast immobilization for 4–6 weeks to protect regenerate from fractureLevel IV

V. Clinical Outcomes and Complications

Efficacy of Deformity Correction

Available literature (primarily case series and case reports due to disease rarity) demonstrates that external fixation is highly effective for correcting forearm deformities in Ollier's disease.Level IV

Evidence Limitation: Only 2 cases with detailed quantitative forearm data were identified in the source document. High-level evidence (RCTs, prospective cohorts) is not available. This represents an evidence gap inherent to the rarity of the condition.

Functional Restoration

Ulnar lengthening successfully improves forearm rotation. Case 1 reported a 15-degree increase in forearm rotation and a 21.6-degree improvement in the arc of ulnar-radial deviation following 20 mm of ulnar lengthening.Level IV The procedure re-establishes more normal kinematics at the proximal and distal radioulnar joints.

VII. Expert Insights and Recommendations

Synthesized Clinical Recommendations

  • Timing: Intervention generally deferred until ~age 6, when deformity becomes functionally or cosmetically significant. Earlier intervention for rapidly progressing deformity to prevent irreversible joint changes.Level V
  • Patient Selection: Ideal candidates are well-motivated patients with robust family support. Compliance demands are substantial. Pre-operative counseling must set realistic expectations and outline the high likelihood of staged procedures.Level V
  • Technique Choice: Young children + complex deformities → circular/hexapod external fixation (versatility, safety). Older adolescents/adults with primarily LLD → monolateral EF or internal lengthening nails.Level IV
  • Surgical Pearl — Intralesional Osteotomy: Perform osteotomy directly through the enchondromatous lesion. Safe, may promote biological conversion of pathologic tissue, preserves bone stock.Level IV
  • Proactive Complication Management: Frequent radiographic monitoring for premature consolidation (be prepared to increase distraction rate). Aggressive therapist-led rehabilitation from day one.Level IV

Future Directions

  • Internal Device Advancements: Development of smaller, more versatile internal lengthening nails may expand application to younger patients and complex deformities
  • Biologic/Genetic Therapies: Deeper understanding of molecular pathways (IDH1/IDH2) may yield pharmacological agents modulating abnormal chondrocyte activity at the physisLevel V
  • Collaborative Research: Multi-center registries and prospective cohort studies are essential given disease rarity. Systematic data collection needed for optimal treatment algorithms and long-term outcome tracking
✅ Key Takeaway
Management of forearm deformity in Ollier's disease is a complex, long-term reconstructive challenge. Success hinges on understanding the unique pathoanatomy (ulnar-driven deformity), applying distraction osteogenesis principles with careful attention to the disease's accelerated healing biology, and maintaining a proactive, multidisciplinary post-operative rehabilitation program. External fixation remains the gold standard for growing children and complex deformities.
