Evidence-Based Closure Strategies for the Previously Infected Orthopedic Wound

Comprehensive EBM Report — Synthesis of Levels I–V Evidence

⚠ Disclaimer: This summary is for informational and educational purposes only. For medical advice or diagnosis, consult a qualified professional.

1. Introduction to the Orthopedic Infection Paradigm

The surgical management of the previously infected orthopedic wound—particularly following periprosthetic joint infection (PJI) or severe fracture-related infection (FRI)—represents one of the most formidable biomechanical and microbiological challenges in modern musculoskeletal surgery. Even when clinical infection control is achieved through rigorous surgical debridement, targeted systemic antimicrobial therapy, and optimization of host factors, the subsequent decision regarding soft-tissue closure remains critical. Failure to secure an optimal biomechanical environment during wound closure can lead to persistent wound drainage, delayed healing, and the catastrophic recurrence of deep surgical site infection (SSI).Ref 1

In contemporary orthopedic practice, surgical wound complications—including dehiscence, necrosis, and seroma formation—are the leading causes of hospital readmission within the first 90 days postoperative. Persistent wound drainage alone affects 1% to 10% of total joint arthroplasty cases and increases the risk of secondary deep infection by up to 20%. Furthermore, treating a single PJI costs between $60,000 and $100,000, and the five-year mortality rate for PJI patients rivals or exceeds that of several common malignancies.Ref 5

For a previously infected bed that has reached "clinical infection control"—defined by normalized inflammatory markers, negative intraoperative cultures, and the presence of healthy granulation tissue per EBJIS and MSIS criteriaRef 7—two primary closure strategies dominate clinical discourse:

  • Immediate Layered Closure: Meticulous obliteration of deep anatomical dead space with absorbable sutures, followed by definitive epidermal closure.
  • Delayed Primary Closure (DPC): Often utilizing Full-Thickness Interrupted (Retention) Sutures; the wound is left partially open for a specified temporal window to allow continuous drainage and bacterial clearance.Ref 10

2. The Pathophysiology of Dead Space

Dead space is the residual tissue void resulting from tissue necrosis, high-energy trauma, or aggressive surgical debridement of nonviable infected tissues.Ref 12 According to classical surgical principles, failure to obliterate this dead space allows rapid accumulation of plasma, lymphatic fluid (seroma), or whole blood (hematoma).Ref 13

2.1 Immunological Cost of Seroma Formation

Fluid collections localized within a closed cavity act as a highly nutritious, avascular culture medium for residual microscopic bacterial loads. The local microenvironment is characterized by severe hypoxia and marked acidosis. Physiological measurements indicate that oxygen tension within foreign-debris-contaminated dead spaces drops to approximately 20 mmHg, compared to the 40 mmHg typically observed in normally healing, well-perfused tissue.Ref 16 This hypoxic state severely impairs the microbicidal capacity of polymorphonuclear leukocytes (PMNs) and macrophages, preventing the respiratory burst necessary to clear residual pathogens.

A retrospective case-control study (Level III evidence) examining soft-tissue tumor resections demonstrated that patients receiving deep, dead space-obliterating sutures had seroma rates dropping from 74% to 26% (P < 0.001).Ref 17 Expert consensus guidelines consistently emphasize that dead space obliteration remains a fundamental pillar of Halsted's principles of surgery.Ref 18

2.2 The Foreign Body Effect in a Contaminated Bed

Conversely, the primary method of obliterating dead space—extensive implantation of buried absorbable sutures—introduces a profound immunological risk. The foreign body reaction triggers "frustrated phagocytosis," where PMNs exhaust their microbicidal capacity by indiscriminately expelling enzymes into surrounding tissue.Ref 16

⚠ Landmark Finding — Elek & Conen Suture Abscess Model

The classic in vivo models demonstrated that while 10⁷ CFU of Staphylococcus aureus were required to produce clinical infection in unobstructed tissue, the introduction of a single silk suture fragment reduced the required infective inoculum to merely 10² CFU—a 100,000-fold decrease in the threshold for clinical infection strictly due to the foreign body effect.Ref 16

Retained sutures in previously infected beds are highly susceptible to becoming nidi for chronic inflammation and foreign body granulomas, manifesting as sterile abscesses or delayed-onset infectious sinuses months to years after index surgery.Ref 19, 23

2.3 Alternative Dead Space Management Strategies

The evidence indicates a strict physiological dichotomy. For large, deep osseous and soft-tissue dead spaces, Level IV–V evidence strongly supports the use of antibiotic-loaded polymethylmethacrylate (PMMA) beads or absorbable calcium sulfate preparations.Ref 12 PMMA beads effectively obliterate dead space while simultaneously eluting supra-therapeutic concentrations of local antibiotics (e.g., tobramycin, vancomycin), maintaining local antimicrobial elution for up to 28 days.Ref 6

3. Suture Material & Biofilm Dynamics

If layered closure is elected, the selection of buried suture material is paramount. Bacterial adhesion, propagation, and biofilm formation are heavily dictated by the macroscopic physical configuration and microscopic chemical composition of the suture polymer.Ref 25

3.1 Physical Configuration: Capillarity and Surface Area

Multifilament sutures (e.g., Polyglactin 910 / Vicryl) exhibit superior handling but their braided architecture creates microscopic interstices that confer high capillarity, drawing wound exudate and bacteria deep into the suture core via wicking action.Ref 25 Once inside, bacteria are physically shielded from host immune cells and systemically administered antibiotics, rapidly forming mature, recalcitrant biofilm.Ref 26

Monofilament sutures (e.g., Polydioxanone / PDS) possess a smooth, contiguous surface lacking capillary interstices, significantly reducing the surface area for bacterial adhesion and preventing deep colonization.Ref 26

3.2 Comparative Evidence: PDS vs. Vicryl in Contaminated Models

A rigorous in vitro study (Level V) using Columbia blood agar plates inoculated with MSSA, MRSA, S. epidermidis, and E. coli quantified bacterial propagation across sutures over 7 days.Ref 31

⚠ Critical Finding — Suture-Mediated Bacterial Propagation

Vicryl (multifilament) facilitated bacterial propagation in 100% of MRSA trials, 90% of MSSA trials, and 80% of S. epidermidis trials. In stark contrast, PDS (monofilament) demonstrated zero propagation for MSSA and S. epidermidis, and limited propagation for MRSA (66%).Ref 31

Confocal microscopy confirmed dense biofilm clusters deep between braided Vicryl filaments, while bacteria struggled to adhere to smooth monofilament surfaces. Bacterial survival halos were up to 3.6 times smaller on monofilament sutures (P < 0.001).Ref 26

3.3 Antimicrobial Coatings & Knotless Technology

Triclosan-coated sutures (PDS Plus, Vicryl Plus) create an active zone of inhibition preventing initial bacterial colonization. Extensive Level I meta-analyses confirm these sutures reduce overall SSI risk by up to 29% compared to uncoated sutures.Ref 34 WHO strongly recommends triclosan-coated sutures for layered closures (Level I / Strong Recommendation).Ref 18

Barbed knotless monofilament sutures provide equivalent or superior tensile strength while entirely eliminating the need for knots—the densest foreign material concentration in a wound. This drastically reduces total polymer volume and eliminates the focal ischemia and granuloma formation associated with suture knots.Ref 2

✅ EBM Recommendation — Suture Selection

If layered closure is necessary, braided multifilaments (Vicryl) are strictly contraindicated. Use triclosan-coated monofilament (PDS Plus) or barbed knotless monofilament to minimize synergistic risks of biofilm propagation and foreign body granulomatosis.Level I–V

4. Surgical Strategy: Layered Closure vs. Delayed Primary Closure

4.1 Rationale for Delayed Primary Closure (DPC)

DPC involves aggressive debridement, leaving the wound open for 3–7 days to develop healthy granulation tissue, then surgically approximating margins.Ref 10 The rationale is that an open wound creates a highly aerobic environment suppressing anaerobic bacteria, allows unimpeded exudate drainage, and stimulates neoangiogenesis.

In visceral surgery, Level I–II evidence strongly supports DPC for heavily contaminated wounds. A high-quality RCT of Class III/IV abdominal laparotomies demonstrated SSI rates of 7.4% with DPC versus 42.9% with primary closure (P = 0.004).Ref 38

4.2 The Orthopedic Dichotomy: Evidence for Immediate Closure

Orthopedic wounds present a vastly different paradigm. Extremity wounds frequently expose bradytrophic structures—cortical bone without periosteum, tendons, ligaments, and implanted hardware. Prolonged exposure leads to rapid desiccation, secondary nosocomial colonization, and irreversible tissue necrosis.Ref 24

📊 Key Study — Propensity-Matched Open Fracture Cohort (n = 349)

After rigorous statistical adjustment, the deep infection rate was significantly lower with immediate closure (4.1%; 95% CI 0.86–11.5) compared to propensity-matched DPC (17.8%; 95% CI 9.8–28.5).Ref 43

A systematic review demonstrated that early soft-tissue coverage within 72 hours is associated with significantly lower infection rates than delayed protocols.Ref 44 Historical microbiological data indicate that only 18% of infections following open orthopedic injuries are caused by organisms present at the time of injury; the vast majority are resistant nosocomial organisms acquired during prolonged open-wound periods.Ref 45

4.3 Full-Thickness Interrupted (Retention) Sutures

For catastrophic wounds where deep fascial layers cannot hold layered sutures, full-thickness interrupted closure using heavy, non-absorbable monofilament (e.g., Nylon or Polypropylene) passes through all tissue planes in a single wide bite.Ref 11 While this eliminates buried foreign material, it introduces massive focal tension points causing localized skin ischemia, pressure necrosis, and "railroad track" scarring. Full-thickness closures without deep dermal support often fail to achieve a watertight seal—a critical necessity to prevent ingress of normal skin flora into a joint space.Ref 18

5. Bridging the Gap: Negative Pressure Wound Therapy (NPWT)

NPWT fundamentally altered the decision-making algorithm, bridging the gap between DPC drainage benefits and immediate closure's protective benefits. Standard NPWT applies controlled subatmospheric pressure (typically −125 mmHg) via reticulated polyurethane-ether foam.Ref 50

5.1 Mechanisms of Action

  1. Macro-deformation: Vacuum draws wound edges together, reducing tissue defect size by up to 80% and functionally obliterating dead space without buried sutures.Ref 50
  2. Micro-deformation: Mechanical strain on individual cells upregulates proliferation, stimulates angiogenesis, and increases VEGF and FGF-2 expression.Ref 50
  3. Fluid Evacuation: Continuously removes third-spaced interstitial fluid, reducing edema and evacuating hematoma/seroma.Ref 51
  4. Bacterial Control: Occlusive adhesive drape provides an impenetrable seal against exogenous pathogens while aspirating infected exudate.Ref 50

5.2 NPWT as Bridge to DPC

A systematic review (Level III) of 34 studies and 804 patients demonstrated that NPWT safely maintains a healthy wound bed and protects exposed bone and tendon from desiccation without worsening nonunion or amputation rates.Ref 41

5.3 Prophylactic Incisional NPWT (iNPWT) Over Closed Incisions

✅ Level I Evidence — iNPWT Meta-Analysis

A comprehensive meta-analysis of 32 studies (12 RCTs, 20 cohorts) encompassing 7,454 patients demonstrated that prophylactic iNPWT significantly lowers deep SSI incidence: pooled OR 0.64 (95% CI 0.52–0.80, P = 0.0001).Ref 53

A focused orthopedic trauma meta-analysis confirmed: OR 0.60 for deep SSI (P = 0.0005), OR 0.34 for superficial SSI (P = 0.003), and OR 0.41 for mechanical dehiscence (P = 0.009).Ref 56

iNPWT acts as an external dynamic splint relieving ischemic tension, actively evacuates residual dead space fluid, and provides an impenetrable barrier against environmental contamination.Ref 55

6. Expert Consensus Guidelines

The AAOS Clinical Practice Guidelines emphasize that prophylactic iNPWT for high-risk surgical incisions represents a highly viable, evidence-backed option to decrease postoperative SSI rates.Ref 58, 59

A comprehensive Delphi consensus from the Spanish Orthopaedic Society recommends continuous barbed sutures for deep surgical wound closure in joint arthroplasty (Level: High, Grade: Strong), citing faster, tighter, watertight closures with reduced foreign material and eliminated knot-related infection risk.Ref 2

The MSIS and EBJIS emphasize the necessity of a watertight seal to prevent prolonged wound drainage, universally recognized as an independent major risk factor for deep PJI.Ref 1

8. Clinical Decision-Making Algorithm

Based on the synthesis of Level I–V evidence, the following structured EBM framework is recommended:

  1. Dead Space Management Level IV/V — Deep osseous/soft-tissue voids should be managed with antibiotic-loaded PMMA beads or calcium sulfate carriers, not buried sutures.Ref 12
  2. Suture Material Selection Level I/V — Avoid braided multifilaments (Vicryl). Use triclosan-coated monofilament (PDS Plus) or barbed knotless monofilament.Ref 31
  3. Closure Timing Level I/III — If infection control is achieved, Immediate Layered Closure is preferred (4.1% vs 17.8% deep infection). Reserve DPC for gross uncontrolled contamination or impossible tension-free closure.Ref 43
  4. Adjunctive NPWT Level I/III — If DPC is needed, NPWT bridges the gap. If immediate closure is achieved, apply prophylactic iNPWT (OR 0.60–0.64 for deep SSI).Ref 53, 56
