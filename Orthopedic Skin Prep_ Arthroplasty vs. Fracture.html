Preoperative Skin Preparation for SSI Prevention in Orthopaedic Surgery

Evidence-Based Interactive Review

§ Executive Summary

Surgical Site Infection (SSI) remains a devastating complication in orthopaedic surgery. This report provides a comparative analysis of skin preparation protocols for two distinct scenarios: elective joint arthroplasty (preemptive optimization) and fracture surgery (emergent decontamination).

The elective protocol leverages multi-day, patient-administered chlorhexidine gluconate (CHG) cleansing followed by intraoperative alcohol-based antiseptic application. In contrast, fracture surgery prioritizes rapid surgical debridement and high-volume irrigation, with the intraoperative antiseptic choice guided by the fracture type (closed vs. open).

Recent landmark trials from the PREP-IT master protocol Level I have reshaped antiseptic selection in trauma: for closed fractures, 0.7% iodine povacrylex in 74% isopropyl alcohol outperforms 2% CHG in 70% isopropyl alcohol (SSI: 2.4% vs. 3.3%, OR 0.74, p = 0.049). For open fractures, no significant difference was found between any tested agents, underscoring the primacy of mechanical decontamination.

1 The Foundational Role of Skin Antisepsis

1.1 Pathophysiology of SSI

The vast majority of SSIs following clean orthopaedic procedures are caused by microorganisms originating from the patient's own skin. Staphylococcus aureus and coagulase-negative staphylococci (e.g., S. epidermidis) are the most common pathogens. Level I [1,2,3]

The surgical incision breaches the skin barrier, creating a pathway for endogenous bacteria to reach deep tissues. Prosthetic implants further elevate risk, as bacteria can form resistant biofilms on their surfaces. The primary objective of skin preparation is therefore to reduce the microbial bioburden below the patient's immune threshold, not to achieve sterilization. Level II [4]

1.2 Core Principles: A Multi-Modal Approach

Effective skin preparation is a systematic, multi-step process. The AAOS delineates four core components Level I [5,7]:

  1. Hair Removal – Clippers only; razors create microscopic wounds that increase SSI risk.
  2. Preoperative / Pre-admission Cleansing – Patient-led efforts to reduce skin bioburden at home.
  3. Intraoperative Skin Preparation – Definitive antiseptic application in the OR.
  4. Sterile Draping – Isolating the prepared field from non-sterile areas.

Skin preparation must be combined with other evidence-based practices (perioperative antibiotics, hand hygiene, sterile environment, patient optimization) for maximum efficacy. [1]

2 Elective Joint Arthroplasty: Preemptive Optimization

2.1 Pre-Admission CHG Cleansing

The cornerstone of the elective protocol is patient-administered CHG cleansing for multiple days before surgery. Patients apply 2% CHG cloths or washes the night before and morning of surgery, allowing the solution to air dry without rinsing. Level II [15,17]

Rationale: CHG binds to the stratum corneum, creating a persistent antimicrobial reservoir effective for up to 24 hours per application. Repeated applications achieve a cumulative reduction in bacterial load. [15,16]

Evidence: A key study demonstrated a significantly lower SSI rate in THA patients using pre-admission CHG cloths (0.5%) vs. standard preparation (1.7%). CHG-impregnated cloths may be superior to CHG showers, achieving greater microbial count reduction and better compliance. Level II [18,19,20]

Implementation Challenge: Patient compliance is critical but variable. Confusion, physical limitations, or forgetfulness can compromise efficacy. Robust, multi-format patient education is essential.

2.2 In-Hospital Preparation

  • Hair Removal: Electric clippers only, same day as surgery. Razors are explicitly contraindicated — they create microscopic wounds that harbor bacteria. Level I [7]
  • Final Cleansing: Preliminary wipe of the surgical field with alcohol or CHG solution to remove residual soils. [7]
  • Surgical Marking: Surgeon marks the site before skin prep. Some solutions erase standard pen markings — coordination with solution choice is needed. [7]

2.3 Intraoperative Antisepsis and Draping

An alcohol-based antiseptic is applied starting at the incision line, moving outward in concentric circles ("clean-to-dirty"). Level I [11,22]

Critical Safety Point: The solution must be fully air-dried before draping. Incomplete drying reduces antimicrobial efficacy and creates a fire hazard from pooling alcohol near electrosurgical devices. Level II [7]

Adhesive drapes (some iodine-impregnated) are applied. Compatibility between the antiseptic and drape adhesive must be confirmed — certain prep solutions impair drape adhesion, risking sterile field compromise. [7]

3 Fracture Surgery: Emergent Decontamination

3.1 Elevated and Altered Bioburden

Fracture patients present with no pre-admission preparation. A prospective study confirmed that trauma patients have a 3.6× greater bioburden in the trochanteric region and 2.7× greater in the inguinal region compared to elective patients. The frequency of pathogenic bacteria was 38% in trauma vs. 6% in elective. Level II [26]

3.2 Emergency Department and Surgical Debridement

ED Care: Gross debris is gently removed; the wound is covered with a sterile, saline-soaked dressing; the limb is stabilized. IV antibiotics and tetanus prophylaxis are administered immediately. Level I [27,28]

Surgical Debridement & Irrigation: This is the single most important infection prevention measure for open fractures. Meticulous removal of all foreign contaminants and devitalized tissue is followed by copious low-pressure irrigation with sterile saline. Level I [27,28,29]

Irrigation Volume Recommendations (per Gustilo-Anderson Type):
Type I: 3 liters  |  Type II: 6 liters  |  Type III: 9+ liters [27]

3.3 Intraoperative Skin Preparation: "From-Out-to-In" Technique

The antiseptic application is reversed compared to elective surgery: prep begins on the peripheral intact skin and moves inward, with the contaminated wound prepped last using a separate, discarded sponge. This containment strategy prevents spreading contaminants across the surgical field. Level II [30]

4.2 Meta-Analytic Evidence: CHG vs. PVP-I

A network meta-analysis of 17 studies (>14,000 patients) found alcoholic CHG (4–5%) halved SSI risk vs. both aqueous and alcoholic PVP-I. Level I [38] However, these analyses pooled heterogeneous surgical procedures, limiting applicability to specific orthopaedic contexts. [39,40]

4.3 The PREP-IT Trials: Definitive Evidence for Fracture Surgery

The PREP-IT master protocol resolved the evidence gap through two large, cluster-randomized crossover trials. Level I [42]

Key Insight: The PREP-IT findings demonstrate that high-quality, pragmatic trials in specific populations can — and should — supersede generalized conclusions from heterogeneous meta-analyses. The unique pathophysiology of closed fracture surgery creates conditions where iodine povacrylex's properties provide a distinct advantage. [44,46,47]

4.4 Anatomic Considerations

Different body sites harbor distinct microbial communities. The shoulder region is heavily colonized by Cutibacterium acnes, a major pathogen in shoulder arthroplasty infections. Evidence suggests CHG-based solutions are more effective against C. acnes than iodophors, supporting the preference for CHG-alcohol for shoulder surgery. Level II [7,49]

5.2 Elective Joint Arthroplasty Protocol

  1. Pre-Admission: Mandate multi-application, patient-administered 2% CHG-impregnated cloths (night before + morning of). Support with multi-format education. Level II
  2. Hair Removal: Same-day electric clippers only; razors banned. Level I
  3. Intraoperative Antiseptic: Alcohol-based solution standardized institution-wide. CHG-alcohol preferred for shoulder surgery. Confirm compatibility with adhesive drapes. Level II
  4. Application & Safety: Proper "clean-to-dirty" technique; strict adherence to full drying time to prevent fires and ensure efficacy. Level II

5.3 Fracture Surgery Protocol

  1. Initial Wound Management: Prompt IV antibiotics + tetanus; sterile dressing for open fractures; rapid transfer to OR. Level I
  2. Debridement & Irrigation: Meticulous debridement is the single most important infection prevention measure. Irrigate with copious low-pressure saline (3L / 6L / 9L+ by Gustilo type). Level I
  3. Closed Fractures — Antiseptic: 0.7% iodine povacrylex in 74% isopropyl alcohol is the evidence-based standard (PREPARE trial). Level I
  4. Open Fractures — Antiseptic: Clinical equipoise. Institutional choice based on cost, availability, and surgeon preference. Aqueous solutions preferred directly in the wound. Level I
  5. Application Technique: "From-out-to-in" is mandatory for open fractures. Level II

5.4 Unresolved Issues & Future Research

  • Optimal dose-response for pre-admission CHG applications in elective TJA
  • Role of CHG bathing between staged trauma surgeries
  • Large-scale pragmatic RCT comparing CHG-alcohol vs. iodine povacrylex-alcohol specifically for primary hip/knee arthroplasty
  • Anatomic-specific protocols based on local microbiome research (e.g., foot/ankle, spine)

6 The Future of SSI Prevention

6.1 Advanced Irrigation Solutions

Novel citrate-based irrigation solutions chelate biofilm-essential metal ions (Ca²⁺, Mg²⁺), disrupting biofilm structure while providing bactericidal activity with lower cytotoxicity than traditional antiseptics. Early evidence suggests improved outcomes in revision arthroplasty and severe open fractures. Level III [50,51]

6.2 Antimicrobial Coatings & Implant Technologies

Defensive Antibacterial Coating (DAC®) hydrogels create a physical barrier against bacterial adhesion and can be loaded with local antibiotics (e.g., vancomycin, gentamicin) for sustained release. Early clinical data show reduced infection rates in revision arthroplasty and trauma. Research into nanotechnology and antimicrobial peptide coatings aims to create inherently infection-resistant implants. Level III [52,53,54]

6.3 AI, Robotics, and Augmented Reality

Robotic-assisted surgery (MAKO, ROSA) improves implant positioning accuracy, reduces tissue trauma, and may shorten operative times — all factors influencing SSI risk. AI algorithms can predict high-risk patients and optimize preoperative planning. Augmented reality overlays enhance surgical precision. While not directly involving skin preparation, these technologies contribute to SSI prevention by optimizing the surgical procedure itself. Level IV [55,56,57,58,59]

Evidence Level Legend

Level I: High-quality RCTs, systematic reviews/meta-analyses of RCTs
Level II: Prospective cohort studies, lesser-quality RCTs
Level III: Case-control, retrospective cohort studies
Level IV: Case series, expert opinion
