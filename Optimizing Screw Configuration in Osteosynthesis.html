1. Introduction — The Evolution of Plate Osteosynthesis

The operative treatment of fractures has evolved from an exclusive focus on absolute mechanical stability toward a nuanced appreciation of mechanobiology, vascular preservation, and biological osteosynthesis.[1] Historically, the foundational philosophy relied on precise anatomical reduction and rigid internal fixation using devices such as the Dynamic Compression Plate (DCP), aiming to completely neutralize mechanical forces at the fracture site to facilitate primary bone healing without periosteal callus.

However, longitudinal clinical analyses revealed that the iatrogenic soft-tissue stripping required for such exact reduction frequently devascularized the bone, leading to delayed unions, non-unions, and heightened susceptibility to infection.[1,4]

This catalyzed the development of internal fixator implants:

  • Locking Compression Plate (LCP) — the archetype of modern locked plating
  • Less Invasive Stabilization System (LISS) — for minimally invasive application
  • Proximal Humerus Interlocking System (PHILOS) — anatomically specific designs

Unlike conventional plates that rely on friction from plate-to-bone compression, locking plates feature threaded screw heads that engage directly with threaded plate holes, creating a fixed-angle construct independent of plate-to-bone contact. This profoundly mitigates periosteal damage and enables Minimally Invasive Plate Osteosynthesis (MIPO).[1]

🎯 Contemporary Primary Objective
Engineer an optimal biomechanical environment that fosters rapid, robust biological healing while rigorously minimizing the risk of implant failure (plate fatigue breakage, plastic deformation, or screw pullout).

Five surgical variables interact dynamically with fracture morphology to achieve this balance:

  1. Working length of the plate
  2. Plate span ratio
  3. Plate-screw density
  4. Plate-bone stand-off distance
  5. Proximity of screws to the fracture gap

2. The Mechanobiological Imperative — Strain Theory

The foundational framework governing screw configuration and plate selection is grounded in the strain theory of tissue differentiation, postulated by Perren.[2,21] Strain represents the relative deformation of reparative tissue within the fracture gap under physiological loading:

📐 Strain Equation
ε = ΔL / L₀
where ΔL = change in gap length under load, L₀ = original unloaded gap length.

The tolerance limits of fracture gap strain dictate the biological pathway of bone regeneration:

  • < 2% strain: Direct (primary) bone healing — osteoclasts create cutting cones that cross the fracture line, followed by osteoblasts depositing Haversian systems. No external callus.[3] LOE II
  • 2% – 10% strain: Secondary bone healing — robust periosteal callus formation via endochondral ossification.[3] LOE II
  • > 10% strain: Healing failure — microvascular disruption, fibrous tissue formation, delayed union or hypertrophic non-union.[3] LOE II

Table 1 — Fixation Stability, Strain, and Biological Response

Fixation Stability Gap Width Local Tissue Strain Biological Bone Reaction
Rigid (Absolute) Small / none Very Low (< 2%) Direct primary healing without callus
Flexible (Relative) Small gap Extremely High (> 10%) Delayed union / non-union (tissue rupture)
Flexible (Relative) Large comminuted gap Moderate (2% – 10%) Callus healing (secondary bone formation)
Rigid (Absolute) Large comminuted gap Very Low (< 2%) No mechanical stimulus; high non-union risk
Adapted from source document [3, 6]. Strain thresholds from established mechanobiological principles.

Implications for Fracture Morphology

Simple fractures (transverse, short oblique) lack a large gap to absorb deformation. Even microscopic motion across a minuscule gap produces strain levels that breach the 10% threshold. These strictly demand absolute stability via compression plating.[4] LOE III

Comminuted fractures possess multiple fracture lines and intermediate fragments that distribute deformation across a much larger cumulative gap. Because L₀ is large, a greater ΔL is tolerated before reaching the 10% threshold. These respond optimally to relative stability via bridge plating.[5] LOE III

3. Biomechanics of the Plate Span Ratio

The plate span ratio is the quotient of total plate length divided by the overall fracture zone length. Maximizing this ratio is one of the most effective structural strategies for ensuring stable fixation and averting early implant failure.[1,7] LOE III

Mechanical Rationale

A high plate span ratio increases the lever arm of the fixation construct, distributing bending forces, axial loads, and rotational torques over a substantially longer working distance. This dramatically attenuates stress on the plate itself and decreases pullout forces on individual screws at the construct extremities.[8]

⚠️ Osteoporotic Bone Consideration
In osteoporotic bone, the screw-thread bone interface is the weakest link. A high plate span ratio provides critical mechanical protection by reducing per-screw pullout forces. A too-short plate translates loads into severely high strain levels, leading to plate bending, plate fracture, or catastrophic screw pullout.[8]

Table 2 — Recommended Plate Span Ratios by Fracture Morphology

Fracture Morphology Recommended Span Ratio Biomechanical Rationale
Simple fractures (transverse, oblique) > 8 to 10 Narrow gap requires very long plate to increase lever arm and mitigate stress concentration at the single failure locus
Comminuted / multifragmentary > 2 to 3 Broad fracture zone; moderate extension beyond comminution provides adequate leverage while sparing diaphyseal soft tissue
Based on biomechanical testing and retrospective clinical analyses [1, 7, 10].

Clinical Evidence — Distal Femoral Fractures

In comprehensive analyses of distal femoral fractures stabilized with bridging LCPs, the absolute medial fracture distance and the plate span ratio are critical prognostic risk factors.[10] When medial fracture distance exceeds 5 mm, the non-union rate escalates significantly because lack of medial cortical support places the entire bending moment on the lateral plate.[10] LOE III

4. Plate-Screw Density and Configuration Geometries

Plate-screw density is the ratio of inserted screws to total available holes in the plate.[6] In the era of non-locking plates, filling every hole was standard practice. In the locking plate era, high screw densities are recognized as biologically and biomechanically detrimental.

Adverse Effects of High Screw Density

  • Suppressed interfragmentary micromotion — inhibits secondary bone healing, increasing non-union risk[7]
  • Iatrogenic periosteal disruption — compromises cortical vascularity, raising avascular necrosis and infection risk[1]
  • No incremental biomechanical benefit — beyond 4 screws per fragment, construct strength plateaus[7]
✅ Evidence-Based Target
Maintain plate-screw density at < 0.4 to 0.5 (fewer than half of available holes occupied by screws). Clinical studies show lower densities are functionally non-inferior for stability while markedly superior for biological preservation.[1,14] LOE III

The 2 vs. 3 Screws Per Fragment Debate

This is one of the most scrutinized topics in MIPO biomechanics. Comprehensive quasi-static and cyclic testing on cadaveric humeri and synthetic bone models provides a definitive consensus.[15,16,17,19]

Initial stiffness: Under non-destructive loading (lateral bending, axial compression, torsion), no statistically significant difference exists between 2-screw and 3-screw configurations.[15] LOE III

Cyclic fatigue and secondary stability: The 3-screw construct is vastly superior. Using a 10-hole 3.5 mm stainless steel LCP on synthetic bone with a 47 mm gap:[16,17] LOE III

  • 2-screw constructs exhibited 13.3%–25% reduction in cycles to failure under compression/bending
  • Maximum load before plastic deformation was up to 23.5% lower in 2-screw models
  • 3-screw constructs maintained stiffness of 19.73 N/mm; 2-screw constructs suffered 20% stiffness reduction and ~40% increase in relative fragment displacement

Failure Mechanism

When locking plate constructs fail, fracture of screws occurs sequentially, initiating with the innermost screw closest to the fracture gap. This screw absorbs the maximum stress, shielding the outer screws. Without a third inner screw, the entire burden shifts to the remaining two screws, causing rapid screw head unlocking, screw breakage, or severe plate bending.[19]

Table 3 — Screw Configuration Characteristics and Indications

Configuration Biomechanical Properties Optimal Clinical Indication
2 + 2 screws Sufficient initial stiffness; lower fatigue resistance; 20% reduced construct stiffness under cyclic loads Simple fractures with very long plates; minimal physiological loading expected
3 + 3 screws Superior cyclic fatigue life; handles peak stresses; maintains secondary stability Standard requirement for bridge plating of comminuted, complex, or multi-fragmentary fractures
4 + 4 screws Maximum biological limit; peak stabilization in compromised bone Severe osteoporosis; massive segmental defects. Exceeding 4 screws is contraindicated.
Derived from biomechanical cadaveric and synthetic bone model studies [15, 16, 17, 19].

5. The Working Length Paradigm and the Strain Paradox

Working length is the unsupported span of the plate between the two innermost screws bordering the fracture zone.[6,21] In locking plates, this is dictated by the distance between innermost locking screws. Modulation of working length has a profound, multi-faceted impact on construct stiffness, plate deformation, and interfragmentary gap motion.[22]

Classical Beam Theory Predictions

Based on uniform beam theory, increased working length decreases construct stiffness, distributing deformation over a wider area and reducing peak strain, thereby minimizing fatigue failure risk.[13] Conversely, a very short working length creates a highly stiff construct where maximal strain develops precipitously in the middle of the plate.[13]

The Strain Paradox

The strain paradox demonstrates that unilaterally increasing working length does not universally reduce plate strain. In specific contexts, a longer working length can precipitate higher strains and catastrophic failure.[6] Resolution requires understanding the bone-plate construct as a dynamic composite beam.[24]

🔍 Small Gap Model (Simple Fractures)

With a small gap (e.g., 1 mm), a long working length creates a flexible construct that permits the gap to close upon weight-bearing, achieving transcortical contact. Once cortical bone shares the load, plate strain is arrested. Paradoxically, a longer working length decreases ultimate plate strain by facilitating load-sharing.[13]

With a short working length, the construct resists deformation so fiercely that the bone ends do not make contact. Without transcortical contact, all load remains on the plate, causing fatigue failure.[13]

🔍 Large Gap Model (Comminuted Fractures)

With a large gap or segmental defect, transcortical contact is physically impossible. The plate bears the entirety of the load. Increasing working length linearly increases interfragmentary motion and pathological lateral shear movement. In synthetic femurs with a 10 mm gap, long working length constructs (175 mm) induced 2.81 mm shear movement vs. 0.89 mm for short constructs (95 mm).[3] This shear destroys capillary buds, guaranteeing non-union.

Clinical guideline: For comminuted fractures, an intermediate, meticulously calculated working length is required — long enough to prevent acute stress concentration, but short enough to prevent excessive shear instability.[27]

6. Screw Proximity to Fracture Gap and Empty Holes

The distance of the innermost screw from the fracture gap directly regulates construct flexibility and defines the functional working length.[6]

Absolute Stability (Simple Fractures)

The proximity rule is absolute: middle screws must be inserted as close as physically possible to the fracture line. A critical prerequisite is deliberate plate overbending at the center (1–2 mm stand-off). When eccentric cortical screws are tightened, the overbend drives far cortices into compression, preventing splaying.[30] Zero empty holes over the fracture zone.[13]

Relative Stability (Comminuted Fractures)

Placing screws too close to the comminuted gap creates a precipitously short working length without load-sharing, leading to rapid fatigue failure.[8] Guidelines recommend leaving at least 2–3 plate holes empty over the fracture zone to generate adequate working length.[13]

Screw Hole Inserts

Biomechanical testing with 42 seven-hole LC/DCP plates on synthetic blocks with a 1 cm gap demonstrated that filling empty central holes with inserts significantly increases axial load to failure, bending load to failure, and resistance to displacement.[32] LOE IV

Medial Fracture Distance — The Critical Override

Retrospective analyses show that absolute medial fracture distance is a far more critical prognostic factor than the number of empty holes. When the medial gap exceeds 5 mm, no configuration of empty holes adequately compensates, necessitating bone grafting or dual-plate configurations.[10] LOE III

7. Plate-Bone Stand-Off Distance

A revolutionary advantage of locking plates is their ability to provide rigid angular stability without compressing the plate against bone, preserving periosteal blood flow. However, excessive stand-off distance carries severe biomechanical penalties.[6]

Biomechanical Effects of Increased Stand-Off

  • Plate operates on a functionally longer lever arm relative to the bone's neutral axis
  • Physiological loads exert exponentially higher bending moments on locking screw shafts
  • Lower total construct stiffness under axial compression and torsion[6]
  • Earlier plastic deformation and lower ultimate load to failure
  • Studies on both 2.0 mm and 4.5 mm LCP constructs uniformly report increased plate strain[33]
✅ Recommended Stand-Off
1–2 mm clearance is biologically advantageous for periosteal sparing. Avoid > 5 mm stand-off distances. This caution is paramount when combined with long working lengths, as compounded elasticity leads to catastrophic shear movement.[6]

8. Innovations in Controlled Dynamization

Standard locking plates can suppress interfragmentary motion to levels insufficient for robust secondary healing.[35] Tracking demonstrates highly asymmetrical motion: near cortex motion of merely 0.03 mm vs. far cortex motion of 0.16 mm.[36] This falls below the required 0.2–1.0 mm motion envelope for optimal callus stimulation.

Far Cortical Locking (FCL) Screws

FCL screws selectively reduce axial stiffness by 80%–88% compared to standard locked constructs.[34,35] LOE II

  • Feature a reduced-diameter smooth shaft that bypasses the near cortex entirely
  • Threads located exclusively at the tip (far cortex) and at the head (plate engagement)
  • Creates a flexible cantilevered shaft that mimics external fixator biomechanics
  • Induces symmetric, nearly parallel interfragmentary motion across the entire fracture gap[36]
✅ Clinical Results — FCL Screws
Randomized controlled trial results:[39] LOE I
  • Significantly more symmetric callus proliferation
  • Higher RUST scores at critical intervals
  • Faster total healing: 6.5 months (FCL) vs. 8.8 months (standard locking)
  • Elimination of dangerous stress risers at end-most screws

Active Plate Osteosynthesis

Active plates feature elastically suspended screw holes that slide within the plate frame, creating controlled elastic suspension for locking screws.[40] LOE IV (animal model)

In comparative in vivo ovine models:[12]

  • Active plates produced ~6× more periosteal callus area by week 9 vs. rigid compression plating
  • Tibiae recovered 54%–64% native torsional strength by week 9 (failure through distant screw hole = healed osteotomy)
  • Standard rigid plates recovered only 17%–24% of native strength with catastrophic failure through the poorly healed osteotomy site

9. Anatomical Considerations and 2025 Clinical Guidelines

Distal Femur

When placing a condylar VA-LCP, the central 7.3 mm screw in the articular block must be perfectly parallel with the tibiofemoral joint surface to guarantee appropriate varus/valgus alignment.[42]

Humeral Shaft

The anterolateral, anterior, and posterior surfaces are acceptable plate positions. For optimal compression, the plate should form a tight "axilla" with the major fragment. Modern MIPO techniques favor a long bridging plate twisted into a helix: proximal end laterally, distal end curving anteriorly to avoid radial nerve pathology.[43]

Proximal Ulna

Finite element models simulating forearm self-weight demonstrate that while diaphyseal screws can safely be omitted, securing the coronoid and apical fragments is non-negotiable to prevent pathological interfragmentary motion.[45] LOE IV (FEA)

2025 AO Innovation — Cannulated Headless Screw System

Approved September 2025 by the AO Hand Expert Group, the Cannulated Headless Screw (Fully Threaded) System targets high-precision reconstruction in hand and wrist procedures (2.0–4.5 mm diameters).[46]

  • Cannulation permits exact placement over a guidewire, eliminating trajectory errors
  • Fully threaded headless design provides absolute interfragmentary compression while burying within cortical bone
  • Eliminates soft tissue irritation, tendon fraying, and stress shielding from prominent screw heads

Future — AI-Guided Screw Configuration

Modern 2025 techniques increasingly utilize finite element analyses paired with Artificial Neural Networks (ANN). By processing CT data of complex fractures, ANN models virtually test thousands of screw orientations preoperatively, detecting potential internal screw collisions and assessing bone strain around each proposed screw track.[48]
