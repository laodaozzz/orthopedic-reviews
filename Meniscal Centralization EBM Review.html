1. Introduction: The Biomechanical Imperative of Meniscal Preservation

The medial and lateral menisci are crescent-shaped, fibrocartilaginous structures responsible for load transmission, shock absorption, secondary joint stabilization, and proprioception within the tibiofemoral articulation. Ref 1

The ultrastructural integrity of the meniscus depends on an organized extracellular matrix: thick, circumferentially oriented type I collagen fibers convert axial compressive loads into radial hoop stresses, while interspersed radial "tie" fibers prevent longitudinal splitting during dynamic loading.

When a medial meniscus posterior root tear (MMPRT) occurs, the anatomical anchor securing the posterior horn to the tibial plateau is severed. This disruption abolishes hoop tension, rendering the construct biomechanically equivalent to a total meniscectomy. Ref 2 The disrupted body displaces peripherally—a condition known as meniscal extrusion (ME)—leading to exponentially elevated peak contact pressures, chondral degradation, subchondral insufficiency fractures, and accelerated osteoarthritis. Ref 1

While transtibial pull-out root repair reestablishes structural continuity, postoperative MRI has consistently demonstrated the persistence or worsening of meniscal extrusion. Ref 2 Hideyuki Koga conceptualized the arthroscopic meniscal centralization technique to physically tether the extruded meniscal body back to the tibial plateau rim, directly addressing peripheral laxity that isolated root repairs ignore. Ref 7

Core Concept

Meniscal centralization is an advanced adjunctive procedure designed to restore the meniscotibial (coronary) ligament tension, reinstating the meniscus's anatomical footprint and its ability to manage radial hoop stresses. It addresses the peripheral constraint failure that isolated root repair cannot correct.

2. Patient Selection and Clinical Boundaries

Arthroscopic meniscal centralization is a specialized biomechanical intervention that relies on viable articular cartilage and a favorable biological environment. Operating outside strictly defined clinical boundaries predictably leads to construct failure.

2.1 Meniscal Extrusion Thresholds

Diagnostic quantification of meniscal extrusion is achieved through high-resolution coronal MRI, measuring the maximum perpendicular distance from the peripheral margin of the meniscal body to the outermost margin of the tibial plateau. Peripheral osteophytic formations must be excluded from the tibial margin reference point. Ref 1

Recent literature suggests assessing extrusion on the coronal slice corresponding to the apex of the medial tibial spine, as this correlates most reliably with true perpendicular extrusion. Ref 11

Key Threshold

Pathological extrusion warranting surgical intervention: ≥ 3 mm. Meniscal centralization is explicitly indicated at this threshold. Ref 1
Prophylactic centralization may be indicated at < 3 mm in the presence of a complete radial tear or MMPRT, as structural loss of hoop tension indicates inevitable progression. Ref 1

2.2 Kellgren-Lawrence (K-L) Grade Contraindications

The overall degenerative status is assessed using the K-L radiographic classification. The degree of joint space narrowing and osteophytosis determines whether the knee can biologically support a centralization construct.

  • K-L Grade 0, 1, 2: Optimal candidates for isolated centralization or combined root repair + centralization. Preserved joint space allows the repositioned meniscal body to re-assume its load-bearing position. Ref 14
  • K-L Grade 3 (moderate osteophytes, definite narrowing) and Grade 4 (large osteophytes, marked narrowing, severe sclerosis): Absolute contraindication. The narrowed joint space crushes the centralized meniscus, causing massive shear forces → rapid suture pull-through and anchor avulsion. Ref 12, 14

2.3 Outerbridge Classification Thresholds

MRI and diagnostic arthroscopy enable precise evaluation of chondral integrity using the Outerbridge classification.

  • Grade 1 (softening/swelling) and Grade 2 (focal fragmentation/fissuring ≤ 1.5 cm): Appropriate candidates—cartilage retains sufficient structural integrity. Ref 1
  • Grade 3 (deep fissuring to subchondral bone, > 1.5 cm) and Grade 4 (complete cartilage loss, exposed bone): Strict exclusion. Abrasive chondral surfaces induce severe frictional shear stress, rapidly degrading meniscal tissue. Ref 14
Caveat

Outerbridge Grade 3–4 lesions may be considered only if highly localized to the meniscal footprint, executed concurrently with a joint-leveling osteotomy or biological cartilage restoration procedure (e.g., ACI, microfracture). Ref 1

2.4 Physiological vs. Chronological Age

Modern sports medicine consensus emphasizes that isolated chronological age is an archaic and scientifically flawed metric. Ref 18 The concept of "physiological age"—accounting for biological tissue quality, BMI, activity level, comorbidities, and functional demands—must completely supersede chronological limits. Ref 17, 19

No absolute upper age limit exists, provided that the biological boundaries of K-L Grade ≤ 2 and Outerbridge Grade ≤ 2 are strictly respected. A physiologically robust, active 65-year-old with a traumatic MMPRT, preserved joint space, and neutral alignment is a superior candidate compared to a sedentary, morbidly obese 45-year-old with K-L Grade 3 OA and varus malalignment. Ref 17

3. The Osteotomy Dilemma: Addressing Coronal Malalignment

Meniscal extrusion and root tears are intimately linked to coronal alignment. In varus malalignment, the mechanical axis shifts medially, causing the weight-bearing load vector to pass directly through the medial compartment. Ref 12 This asymmetrical loading exponentially increases peak contact pressures, generating massive radial displacement forces that drive the meniscus out of the joint.

3.1 The Varus Angle Threshold

According to extensive clinical cohort evaluations, a mechanical varus angle (HKA angle deviation) exceeding 3° to 5° is the critical threshold mandating a concurrent Medial Open-Wedge High Tibial Osteotomy (MOWHTO) alongside meniscal root repair and centralization. Ref 24

Above this limit, compressive loads through the medial compartment exceed the yield strength of meniscotibial suture anchors, leading to recurrent extrusion. MOWHTO shifts the mechanical axis laterally, redistributing loads and providing the low-stress environment necessary for healing. Ref 12

Critical Decision Point

HKA mechanical varus > 3°–5° → Concurrent MOWHTO mandatory. Uncorrected varus guarantees rapid destruction of the centralization construct. Ref 24

3.2 Weight-Bearing Line (WBL) Ratio Targets

The primary metric for coronal correction is the Weight-Bearing Line (WBL) ratio, measured on standing full-length lower extremity radiographs (medial border of proximal tibia = 0%; lateral border = 100%). Ref 23

  • Historical "Fujisawa point": WBL ratio of 62.5%—significant valgus overcorrection, originally designed for end-stage medial OA without preservation options. Ref 12
  • Modern individualized alignment (ESSKA consensus): WBL ratio of 57.0% to 62.5%, tailored to patient characteristics: Ref 12
    • Younger, low-BMI, high-activity patients → target closer to 57.0% (neutral to slight valgus)
    • Older patients with advanced chondral wear → target toward 62.5%
Paradigm Shift

Extreme valgus overcorrection to the Fujisawa point is now considered unnecessary and potentially detrimental when combining osteotomy with meniscal centralization. Overcorrection places unnatural loads on the healthy lateral compartment, potentially precipitating lateral meniscal tears and OA. Ref 12

4. Surgical Technique & Biomechanical Efficacy

The centralization procedure physically advances the lax capsular attachments and secures them to the tibial plateau margin, re-tensioning the meniscotibial (coronary) ligament. Ref 8

4.1 The Koga Technique: Anatomical Landmarks

First introduced in 2012 by Hideyuki Koga for lateral meniscus centralization, the technique was subsequently adapted for the medial compartment. Ref 7, 9

Step-by-Step Protocol:

  1. Preparation of the Tibial Footprint: The medial gutter is cleared of scar tissue. The true margin of the medial tibial plateau is identified, differentiating from peripheral osteophytes (which must be conservatively resected). Ref 1
  2. Anchor Placement Boundaries: 1.8-mm knotless soft suture anchors (e.g., FiberTak) are inserted. The first anchor targets the most posteromedial aspect, just posterior to the MCL posterior border. Bone tunnels are placed exactly 3 mm inward from the anteromedial tibial rim for adequate cortical bone bridge stability. Ref 30, 31
  3. Sequential Fixation Spacing: Up to three anchors are used. The second anchor is placed 1 cm anterior to the first; if needed, a third is placed another 1 cm anteriorly. Ref 9, 30
  4. Capsular Suturing (Not Meniscal Body): Sutures pass strictly through the capsulomeniscal junction—never through the main fibrocartilaginous body. This horizontal mattress configuration advances the meniscotibial ligament, pulling the extruded body centrally while preserving required physiological anterior-posterior translation. Ref 9, 34
Critical Technical Point

Never suture the meniscal body directly to bone. Doing so over-constrains the meniscus, eliminating its required physiological translation during flexion/extension and leading to severe joint stiffness. Ref 34

5. Level I Clinical Evidence: The Tollefson & LaPrade RCT (2025)

Landmark RCT — Level I Evidence

Tollefson, Kajabi, LaPrade et al. (2025)
A randomized controlled clinical trial evaluating isolated posterior medial meniscus root repair vs. root repair combined with a centralization suture. Level I Ref 6

Key Findings:

  1. Universality of Postoperative Extrusion: Even with technically successful root repairs, medial meniscal extrusion increased in the vast majority of patients at 6-month MRI follow-up. Isolated root repair fundamentally fails to restore meniscotibial peripheral constraints. Level I Ref 6
  2. Efficacy of Centralization Augmentation: The centralization group demonstrated a significantly lower increase in extrusion (~0.7 mm, 18% increase) vs. the control group (~1.6 mm, 48% increase). P = 0.017 vs. P = 0.002. Level I Ref 6
  3. Construct Stability: Variance in extrusion was significantly constrained in the centralization group vs. the non-centralization group (P = 0.039). Zero failures of centralization sutures were observed at final follow-up. Level I Ref 41, 42
Clinical Implication

Centralization may not perfectly reverse existing severe extrusion to zero, but it radically halts progressive displacement. By locking the tissue in a functionally viable position, it prevents the massive 48% extrusion increases seen with isolated repair, preserving joint space and protecting articular cartilage.

6. Evidence-Based Rehabilitation Protocol

The postoperative rehabilitation protocol is as critical as the surgical execution. The advanced and anchored meniscotibial capsule requires time to biologically integrate. Premature loading, uncontrolled shear, or excessive flexion will rapidly avulse anchors or cause suture pull-through. Ref 14, 43

Phase I: Maximum Protection (Weeks 0–6)

  • Weight-bearing: Strict Non-Weight-Bearing (NWB) or touchdown weight-bearing with bilateral crutches for full 6 weeks. Ref 43
  • Brace: Rigid hinged brace locked in full extension (0°) during ambulation and sleep. Ref 45
  • ROM: Passive ROM limited to 0°–90°. Flexion beyond 90° strictly prohibited (drives femoral condyles posteriorly, placing devastating tension on anchors). Ref 43, 44
  • Neuromuscular: NMES + BFR training for quadriceps reactivation. All active hamstring exercises prohibited (semimembranosus attachments jeopardize posteromedial anchors). Ref 43, 44

Phase II: Transitional Loading (Weeks 6–12)

  • Weight-bearing: Graduated PWB → WBAT. Crutches weaned only with normal gait pattern. Ref 14, 43
  • ROM: 90° restriction lifted; full functional passive ROM targeted. Loaded flexion restricted to 0°–90°. Deep loaded squatting avoided for 4–6 months. Ref 43
  • Kinematic control: Hip abductor and core strengthening to prevent dynamic valgus. All twisting/pivoting prohibited. Ref 43

Phase III: Remodeling & Return (Months 3–6+)

  • Strength: Progressive resistance; isokinetic hamstring exercises introduced (if no effusion). Quadriceps LSI target ≥ 80%. Ref 14, 44
  • Cardio: Stationary biking, elliptical, swimming permitted. Ref 44
  • Jogging: 4–6 months, contingent on absent swelling and LSI ≥ 80%. Ref 14
  • Return to sport: 9–12 months for high-impact, multi-directional, pivoting sports. Ref 14

7. Conclusion

Arthroscopic meniscal centralization represents a paradigm shift in the treatment of meniscal pathology. Historically, surgical techniques focused purely on root repair, leaving capsular laxity and progressive extrusion unaddressed—guaranteeing eventual osteoarthritis.

Through robust Level I and Level II Evidence-Based Medicine, the clinical boundaries are clearly established:

  1. Indications: Pathological extrusion ≥ 3 mm with K-L Grade ≤ 2 and Outerbridge Grade ≤ 2. Physiological age supersedes chronological limits.
  2. Alignment: Uncorrected mechanical varus > 3°–5° mandates concurrent MOWHTO. An individualized WBL target of 57.0%–62.5% provides superior cartilage protection vs. historical overcorrection.
  3. Technique: Knotless anchors placed 3 mm from tibial margin, 1 cm apart, capturing only the capsule. While SA fixation is biomechanically superior in isolation, TP + robust centralization creates a stable construct.
  4. Level I Evidence: Centralization drastically mitigates the severe extrusion progression seen with isolated root repair (18% vs. 48% increase at 6 months).
  5. Rehabilitation: A disciplined, multiphasic protocol with restricted early weight-bearing and prohibited deep flexion is non-negotiable.

By comprehensively addressing the peripheral constraints of the knee joint, arthroscopic meniscal centralization effectively halts the rapid progression of medial compartment osteoarthritis, permanently altering the trajectory of joint-preserving sports medicine.
