Severe Lower Limb Degloving & Avulsion Injuries

Comprehensive Evidence-Based Management · Interactive Decision Support Tool

1. Executive Summary

Severe degloving soft tissue injuries (DSTIs) of the lower extremity represent a complex spectrum of trauma characterized by the extensive avulsion of skin and subcutaneous tissue from the underlying investing fascia, muscles, and skeletal structures. These injuries, predominantly resulting from high-energy mechanisms such as run-over traffic accidents or industrial machinery entanglements, pose a profound reconstructive challenge due to the disruption of the fasciocutaneous vascular perforators that supply the integument. Evidence Level II

Central Clinical Dilemma
The primary therapeutic dilemma is the accurate assessment of flap viability and the strategic decision between radical excision with delayed reconstruction versus immediate salvage via defatting and full-thickness skin grafting (FTSG) using the patient's own avulsed tissue as a "spare part."

Systematic reviews and meta-analyses integrated into this report indicate that while Negative Pressure Wound Therapy (NPWT) significantly enhances graft take rates and reduces infection compared to conventional compression dressings Level I Meta-Analysis, the use of defatted full-thickness grafts from the avulsed flap offers superior long-term aesthetic and functional outcomes. Specifically, FTSGs demonstrate lower Vancouver Scar Scale (VSS) scores, reduced secondary contracture, and better durability compared to split-thickness skin grafts (STSG). Evidence Level II

Key NPWT Finding
Optimal pressure for graft fixation: −75 mmHg (continuous) is advocated over the standard −125 mmHg, based on molecular and microcirculatory evidence.

2. Epidemiology, Anatomy & Pathophysiology

2.1 Burden of Degloving Injuries

DSTIs are a distinct and devastating subset of trauma. The "run-over" mechanism, where a rotating tire passes over a limb, generates powerful shearing forces that break the connective tissue septa anchoring the skin to the deep fascia, creating a large dead space and immediately disrupting the vascular supply to the overlying skin. Level III Cohort

  • Male predominance, reflecting occupational and behavioral risk factors.
  • Economic burden: Prolonged hospitalizations, serial surgical interventions, and long-term rehabilitation.
  • Psychological impact: High rates of PTSD and body image disturbance due to disfiguring scarring and functional deficits.

2.2 Anatomical Considerations

  • Fasciocutaneous Perforators: In the thigh and calf, shearing forces typically occur at the suprafascial plane, disconnecting the skin from its blood supply. The avulsed flap becomes dependent on the random-pattern dermal plexus.
  • Glabrous vs. Non-Glabrous Skin: The plantar surface possesses specialized fibrous septa tightly anchoring thick dermis to the calcaneus. Degloving of the heel pad is particularly catastrophic; replacing this specialized tissue with standard skin grafts often results in unstable coverage that ulcerates under load. Level IV Case Series
  • The "Zone of Injury": Microvascular thrombosis can propagate days after the initial insult, leading to delayed necrosis of skin that initially appeared viable—underscoring the necessity for objective viability testing.

2.3 Pathophysiology of Avulsion and Ischemia

The fundamental pathology is ischemic necrosis. When a flap is avulsed, arterial inflow is severed. In incompletely detached flaps (Arnez Type 2), some arterial inflow may persist through the skin bridge, but venous outflow—compromised due to thin-walled veins' susceptibility to compression and thrombosis—creates a cycle of venous congestion, increased hydrostatic pressure, and progressive ischemia. Level IV

In closed degloving injuries (Morel-Lavallée lesions), the accumulation of lymph and blood in the subcutaneous dead space creates a toxic milieu containing inflammatory cytokines and free radicals, which can cause secondary necrosis of the overlying skin.

3. Classification Systems

Accurate classification is the cornerstone of effective management, allowing the surgeon to predict tissue viability and choose the appropriate reconstructive strategy.

3.1 Arnez Classification System

Categorizes soft tissue injuries based on the pattern of degloving and the integrity of the vascular supply. Level III

Table 1: Arnez Classification of Degloving Injuries
TypeDescriptionPathophysiologyTreatment Implication
Type 1 Abrasion / Avulsion — limited degloving with localized abrasion Circulation largely intact Conservative management or minor debridement
Type 2 Non-circumferential Degloving — elevated flap with continuous skin bridge Venous drainage preserved through bridge Re-suturing in situ often successful
Type 3 Circumferential Single-Plane — complete circumferential degloving Perforators universally disrupted Re-suturing contraindicated. Radical excision + grafting required.
Type 4 Circumferential Multi-Plane — multiple levels with muscle avulsion Highest risk of compartmental ischemia & infection Serial debridement required
Critical Decision Point
The distinction between Type 2 and Type 3 is paramount. Arnez et al. concluded that avulsed flaps resutured in situ survived only in non-circumferential (Type 2) cases. Failure to recognize a Type 3 injury leads to catastrophic re-suturing of a dead flap. Level III

3.2 Yan Classification System

Based on a cohort of 102 patients, focusing on the depth of injury and associated skeletal trauma. Level III Cohort

Table 2: Yan Classification of Degloving Injuries
PatternDescriptionPrognosisKey Finding
Pattern 1 Pure Degloving — skin & subcutaneous tissue only; deep fascia intact Highest rate of primary healing with immediate FTSG Best prognosis overall
Pattern 2 Deep Soft Tissue Involvement — damage to muscles, tendons, or neurovascular bundles Immediate grafting feasible; higher infection risk Aggressive muscle debridement required
Pattern 3 Associated Fractures — degloving complicated by long bone fractures Poorest prognosis Advanced age (>60 years) is a significant predictor of flap failure and mortality

4. Pre-operative Assessment

4.1 Resuscitation & Hemodynamic Stabilization

A "closed" degloving injury of the thigh can sequester up to 2 liters of blood in the potential space between the fascia and the skin, leading to hypovolemic shock without external hemorrhage. Level IV

  • Fluid Resuscitation: Aggressive crystalloid administration accounting for "third-spacing" into the degloved area.
  • Blood Products: Early transfusion protocols for hemodynamically unstable patients, particularly in Pattern 3 injuries where bone bleeding contributes to volume loss.

4.2 Diagnosis of Compartment Syndrome

Compartment syndrome is a known complication whose diagnosis is obscured in degloving injuries. The classic "5 Ps" are unreliable in obtunded trauma patients. Level V Expert Opinion

  • Objective Monitoring: Intracompartmental pressure monitoring recommended for high-energy mechanisms with tense limb or altered mental status.
  • Threshold: Delta pressure (Diastolic BP − Compartment Pressure) of <30 mmHg is the indication for fasciotomy.
  • Fasciotomy: Performed urgently; incisions planned to preserve viable skin bridges. In circumferential degloving, fasciotomy is through the degloved fascia, managed with NPWT.

5. The Defatting Technique

Paradigm Shift
The technique of defatting the avulsed flap and replanting it as an FTSG converts the "damaged part" into a "spare part" for reconstruction—eliminating donor site morbidity while preserving native tissue architecture.

5.1 Rationale

The avulsed flap's subcutaneous fat has high metabolic demand and acts as a barrier to revascularization. Without arterial flow, fat necroses before new vessels can grow. The skin (epidermis + dermis) has low metabolic rate and can survive 48–72 hours via plasmatic imbibition until inosculation occurs. Level IV / Mechanistic

Removing the fat converts the composite flap into an FTSG that retains complete dermal architecture—providing superior durability, elasticity, and suppression of wound contracture compared to STSG.

5.2 Step-by-Step Protocol

Step 1: Radical Debridement & Bed Preparation

  • Pulsatile Lavage: 9–12 liters of saline to reduce bacterial load.
  • Hemostasis: Absolute hemostasis mandatory (bipolar cautery preferred). Hematoma prevents plasmatic imbibition and serves as a culture medium.

Step 2: Harvesting & Viability Marking

  • Using fluorescein/ICG guidance, mark the demarcation line on the skin.
  • Excise the non-viable portion from the limb.
  • In extensive Pattern 3/4 injuries, even the "viable" bridge may be sacrificed and converted to a graft due to high risk of delayed pedicle thrombosis.

Step 3: The Defatting Procedure

  • Setup: Flap laid epidermal-side down on a dermatome board or convex drum.
  • Instrumentation: Large scalpel (#20/#22 blade) or humby knife preferred over Mayo scissors for large flaps—sweeping tangential motion to shave fat lobules.
  • Endpoint: Remove all subcutaneous fat until white, glistening reticular dermis is visible. Remaining fat pearls will act as a barrier and undergo liquefactive necrosis. Avoid "buttonholing" the dermis.

Step 4: Fenestration & Meshing

  • Meshing (1:1 or 1.5:1): Increases surface area, excellent drainage, but leaves permanent "pebbled" pattern.
  • Pie-Crusting: Manual fenestration with #11 blade (slits every 1–2 cm) for high-aesthetic areas (e.g., female calf)—preserves smoother skin texture.

Step 5: Replantation & Fixation

  • Graft sutured to wound margins with absorbable sutures or staples.
  • When NPWT is used, quilting sutures are less critical as negative pressure provides uniform fixation.

6. Negative Pressure Wound Therapy (NPWT)

6.1 Mechanism of Action

  • Macro-strain: Suction draws wound edges together and provides mechanical stability.
  • Micro-strain: Foam pores transmit negative pressure to tissue, causing micro-deformations that upregulate VEGF, FGF—promoting angiogenesis and granulation. Level II
  • Fluid Management: Actively removes excess interstitial fluid, improving microvascular perfusion.

6.2 NPWT as Graft Bolster

NPWT addresses limitations of traditional "tie-over" bolsters by providing:

  1. Uniform Compression: Splints graft against irregular contours, preventing shear forces.
  2. Active Drainage: Continuously removes serum/blood, preventing hematoma/seroma—the most common cause of graft failure. Level II

6.3 Optimal Pressure: −75 mmHg vs. −125 mmHg

Table 4: NPWT Pressure Settings Comparison for Skin Grafts
Parameter−75 mmHg (Continuous)−125 mmHg (Continuous)
Indication Skin graft fixation (recommended) Open wound bed preparation / granulation stimulation
Gene Expression Reduced pro-inflammatory genes (S100A8, Tenascin C); increased fibrinogen production Higher pro-inflammatory response
Graft Adherence Fibrinogen acts as biological glue, enhancing adherence Risk of pulling graft into foam mesh
Microcirculation Preserves delicate new capillaries May compromise microcirculation of new graft
Evidence Level II Level II

Source: Referenced studies on NPWT pressure settings for skin grafts [refs 27, 25].

Important
Continuous pressure is mandatory for graft fixation. Intermittent therapy is contraindicated for grafts as pressure cycling can cause shear and disrupt early vascular connections.

6.4 Application Technique

  • Interface Layer: A non-adherent, porous contact layer (e.g., silicone mesh, Mepitel, Adaptic) must be placed between graft and foam to prevent tissue ingrowth and graft damage upon removal.
  • Duration: Dressing left in place for 5–7 days. Removing <4 days risks disrupting early vascular connections; 5–7 days allows the inosculation phase to complete undisturbed.

7.1 Graft Survival & Take Rates

A systematic review of 18 studies involving 1,098 patients demonstrated that NPWT significantly improves graft take rates compared to conventional dressings. Level I Meta-Analysis

  • Statistical Significance: NPWT group showed significantly higher graft survival (Mean Difference = 11.04%, 95% CI [5.57, 16.51], p < 0.0001).
  • Subgroup Analysis: Benefit most pronounced in lower limb injuries (MD = 13.31) compared to upper limb.
  • Defatted FTSG: Average necrosis rate of only 8.4% in properly selected patients with viability protocols. Necrosis, when present, was usually partial and manageable.

7.2 Infection Rates

Meta-analyses confirm that NPWT reduces postoperative infection risk compared to conventional dressings (p < 0.05). The closed system provides a barrier to exogenous bacteria while suction removes bacterial load. Level I

Clinical Nuance
In heavily contaminated wounds (e.g., agricultural injuries), a staged approach using NPWT with instillation (intermittent irrigation with antiseptic solution) for 3–5 days prior to grafting is recommended to sterilize the bed.

7.3 Aesthetic & Functional Outcomes

FTSGs demonstrate clear superiority over STSGs in aesthetic outcomes. Level II

  • Vancouver Scar Scale (VSS): FTSGs consistently score better than STSGs. STSGs are prone to hyperpigmentation, hypertrophy, and a "shiny" appearance.
  • Contracture: STSGs have significant secondary contracture, which can limit ROM at joints. FTSGs have minimal secondary contracture due to the full dermal lattice.
  • Durability: The thicker dermis of FTSG provides better protection against minor trauma.

7.4 Cost-Effectiveness

Health economic analyses support NPWT use. A probabilistic sensitivity analysis found NPWT "dominated" standard care in 88% of simulations—savings from reduced LOS, fewer dressing changes, and avoidance of repeat surgeries for graft failure. Level II RCT-Based Economic Analysis

8. Treatment Algorithm

Based on synthesis of the Arnez/Yan classifications and systematic review data, the following management algorithm is proposed.

Phase 1: Emergency & Assessment (0–6 Hours)
  1. Trauma Survey: ATLS protocols. Stabilize hemodynamics.
  2. Wound Assessment:
    • Closed Degloving (Morel-Lavallée): Diagnosis via physical exam + Ultrasound/MRI. Percutaneous drainage/sclerotherapy for small lesions; I&D for large/necrotic.
    • Open Degloving: Immediate transfer to OR.
  3. Compartment Check: Measure compartmental pressures if limb is tense/obtunded (threshold: delta <30 mmHg).
Phase 2: Intra-operative Decision Making
  1. Debridement: Radical excision of non-viable muscle/fascia.
  2. Viability Test: IV Fluorescein (15 min) or ICG Angiography.
  3. Classification (Arnez):
    • Type 1/2 (Non-circumferential + Viable Bridge): Primary suture of viable flap. Defat only marginal necrosis.
    • Type 3/4 (Circumferential or Non-Viable): EXCISE. Do not attempt re-suturing. Harvest the entire avulsed segment.
Phase 3: Reconstruction Strategy
Decision Node: Is the wound bed clean and patient stable?
✓ Yes — "Immediate Salvage" Pathway
  1. Defatting: On sterile back table, radically defat harvested flap to reticular dermis.
  2. Preparation: Fenestrate (cosmetic areas) or mesh (1:1.5) the graft.
  3. Application: Suture graft to wound bed.
  4. Fixation: Apply NPWT over non-adherent layer at −75 mmHg continuous.
  5. Post-Op: Immobilize 5–7 days. Take down dressing to inspect.
✗ No — "Staged" Pathway (Contaminated/Unstable)
  1. Preservation: Cryopreserve harvested skin (if available) or discard if heavily damaged.
  2. Temporizing: Apply NPWT to raw wound bed at −125 mmHg continuous (granulation stimulation).
  3. Re-evaluation: Return to OR in 48–72 hours.
  4. Definitive Closure:
    • If bed clean → graft using banked skin or harvest STSG from thigh.
    • If bed dirty → repeat debridement + NPWT. Consider Dermal Regeneration Template (Integra) if bone/tendon exposed.

9. Special Considerations

9.1 The Plantar Surface (Heel Pad)

Degloving of the heel pad presents a unique challenge. The specialized septate architecture of the heel fat pad is irreplaceable. Level IV Case Series

  • STSG is contraindicated for the weight-bearing heel as it will ulcerate.
  • The defatted FTSG from heel skin is the best option if available—glabrous skin is inherently durable.
  • If plantar skin is lost, reconstruction requires free muscle flaps (e.g., Latissimus Dorsi) covered with skin grafts.
  • Replanting the heel pad without defatting is doomed to failure due to fat thickness.

9.2 Long-Term Sequelae & Quality of Life

  • Functional: Range of motion may be limited by scarring. Aggressive physiotherapy is required.
  • Psychosocial: High rates of PTSD and depression; altered body image from extensive scarring. Early psychological intervention is essential. Level III
  • Chronic Pain: Neuropathic pain from damaged cutaneous nerves is common. Neurotization of the graft occurs slowly (6–12 months) and is rarely complete.

10. Key Studies from the Source Document

The following summarizes the most important studies referenced in the source document. Evidence level is inferred from study design as the source does not assign explicit levels.

1. Yan et al. — Classification & Outcomes of Degloving Injuries
Retrospective cohort, n = 102 · Level III
Proposed the Yan classification (3 patterns). Advanced age (>60) is a significant predictor of flap failure and mortality in Pattern 3 (fracture-associated) injuries. Pure degloving (Pattern 1) has highest primary healing rates.
2. Arnez et al. — Classification of Soft-Tissue Degloving
Prospective clinical series · Level III
Proposed the 4-type Arnez classification. Demonstrated that avulsed flaps resutured in situ survived only in non-circumferential (Type 2) cases with intact skin bridges.
3. Systematic Review: NPWT Efficacy on Limb Grafts (n = 1,098)
Systematic review & meta-analysis of 18 studies · Level I
NPWT significantly improved graft take rates (MD = 11.04%, 95% CI [5.57, 16.51], p < 0.0001). Benefit most pronounced in lower limb injuries (MD = 13.31). Significant reduction in infection rates (p < 0.05).
4. Lim & Han — Flap Viability Prediction with Fluorescein
Prospective clinical study · Level II
IV fluorescein under Wood's lamp reliably delineates necrotic boundaries. Defatted FTSG showed average necrosis rate of only 8.4% when viability protocols strictly followed. Mottled areas usually progress to necrosis.
5. NPWT Pressure Studies: −75 mmHg vs. −125 mmHg for Grafts
Comparative studies · Level II
−75 mmHg continuous is optimal for skin graft fixation: reduced pro-inflammatory gene expression (S100A8, Tenascin C), increased fibrinogen production enhancing graft adherence, and preserved delicate microcirculation. −125 mmHg reserved for open wound bed preparation.
