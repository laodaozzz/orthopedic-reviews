1. Introduction: The Evolving Paradigm of Hemorrhage Control

Hemorrhage remains a formidable challenge in orthopedic surgery, representing both the leading cause of preventable mortality in trauma patients and a significant source of morbidity in elective procedures. The management of blood loss is not merely a technical necessity for maintaining visualization of the surgical field; it is a fundamental determinant of patient survival, infection risk, functional recovery, and long-term viability of implants. The orthopedic surgeon's responsibility extends beyond the stabilization of the skeletal framework to the meticulous preservation of the intravascular volume.II

Historically, the prioritization of trauma care followed the Airway, Breathing, Circulation (ABC) model established by the Advanced Trauma Life Support (ATLS) guidelines. However, evidence derived from military conflicts and advanced civilian trauma centers has precipitated a paradigm shift toward the C-ABC algorithm, where the control of catastrophic hemorrhage (C) takes precedence.[1] II

Key Paradigm Shift
This shift acknowledges the austere physiological reality that exsanguination from major vascular or pelvic injuries can occur within minutes—faster than the sequelae of airway compromise—rendering subsequent resuscitation efforts futile if the primary vascular breach is not sealed.

In the elective setting, such as total joint arthroplasty (TJA) or complex spine reconstruction, the focus is distinct but equally critical. Here, the objective is to minimize intraoperative and postoperative blood loss to obviate the need for allogenic blood transfusions. Allogenic transfusions are associated with immunomodulation, increased rates of surgical site infection (SSI), prolonged hospital length of stay, and increased healthcare costs.[2] II Modern blood management has evolved into a multimodal discipline integrating preoperative optimization (e.g., management of anemia and anticoagulants), intraoperative techniques (e.g., tranexamic acid, hypotensive anesthesia, advanced topical hemostats), and postoperative strategies (e.g., restrictive transfusion thresholds).

2. Pathophysiology of Hemorrhage and Coagulopathy

2.1. The "Diamond of Death"

The classic "Lethal Triad" of hypothermia, acidosis, and coagulopathy has long guided resuscitation strategies. Recent insights have expanded this concept to the "Diamond of Death", incorporating hypocalcemia as a fourth critical pillar.[4] II

2.2. Mechanism of Trauma-Induced Coagulopathy (TIC)

TIC is not simply a depletion of clotting factors due to blood loss; it is a distinct, multifactorial pathology driven by tissue injury and shock.

  • Hyperfibrinolysis: Severe tissue injury triggers massive release of tissue plasminogen activator (tPA) from the endothelium, activating plasmin and rapidly breaking down fibrin clots.[1] II
  • Acidosis: Hypoperfusion leads to anaerobic metabolism and lactic acidosis. An acidic environment (pH < 7.2) drastically impairs the enzymatic efficiency of the coagulation cascade, reducing thrombin generation and accelerating fibrinogen degradation.[4] II
  • Hypothermia: A core temperature below 34°C significantly inhibits fibrinogen synthesis and platelet aggregation.[4] II
  • Hypocalcemia: Citrate in transfused blood products binds calcium; shock states deplete ionized calcium. Calcium is a necessary cofactor for the assembly of coagulation complexes on cell surfaces.[4]
Clinical Implication
The presence of TIC dictates that hemostasis cannot be achieved solely by surgical means if the physiological substrate for clotting is absent. This necessitates Damage Control Resuscitation (DCR), which prioritizes the early administration of plasma and platelets in a balanced ratio (e.g., 1:1:1) with red blood cells, minimizing crystalloid infusion to prevent dilutional coagulopathy.[6] III

3. Level I: Basic Mechanical Hemostasis & Patient Positioning

LEVEL I Mechanical Compression & Tourniquet Systems

3.1. Mechanical Compression and Splinting

3.1.1. Pelvic Circumferential Compression

In patients with suspected unstable pelvic fractures (Antero-Posterior Compression / "Open Book" types), the volume of the true pelvis increases significantly, creating a low-pressure reservoir that can accommodate liters of blood from the presacral venous plexus and fractured bony surfaces.

  • Mechanism: Application of a pelvic binder reduces the pelvic ring volume, re-approximates the bony ends, and increases retroperitoneal tissue pressure, promoting a tamponade effect.[1]
  • Protocol: The binder must be centered over the greater trochanters to exert compressive force on the femoral heads and acetabula. Misplacement over the iliac crests or abdomen is a common error.[6]
  • Limitations: Pelvic binders are a temporizing measure. Prolonged application (>24 hours) poses a significant risk of skin necrosis and pressure ulceration. In lateral compression fractures, binders may be contraindicated.[6]

3.1.2. Traction Splinting for Femoral Fractures

The thigh compartment can conceal 1–2 liters of blood following a femoral shaft fracture. Muscle spasm causes shortening and overriding of bone fragments, distorting cylindrical geometry and preventing self-tamponade. Application of a traction splint (e.g., Thomas splint) restores limb length and cylindrical volume, increasing interstitial tissue pressure to tamponade venous bleeding. Prehospital application has been associated with reduced transfusion requirements and a lower incidence of pulmonary embolism.[1] III

3.2. Tourniquet Systems

3.2.1. Physiological Impact of Tourniquet Ischemia

  • Metabolic Acidosis: Within 2 hours of ischemia, intracellular energy stores (creatine phosphate and ATP) are depleted, resulting in accumulation of lactate, hydrogen ions, and CO2.[9]
  • Reperfusion Injury: Deflation releases metabolic byproducts into the systemic circulation, causing transient hypotension, rising ETCO2, and hyperkalemia—in physiologically compromised patients, this can precipitate cardiac arrhythmias.[9]
  • Neuromuscular Injury: Histological changes in nerve fibers can occur after as little as 2 hours; irreversible damage is typically cited at 8 hours. Direct mechanical crush from excessive pressure can cause neurapraxia instantaneously.[9]

4. Level II: Local Hemostatic Agents

LEVEL II The "Wax and Wadding" Phase

4.1. Bone Wax: Efficacy vs. Toxicity

Bone wax is a non-absorbable mixture of beeswax and softening agents (paraffin or isopropyl palmitate). It acts purely through a mechanical mechanism—plugging Haversian canals and marrow spaces to create physical tamponade.[14]

⚠ Significant Biological Downsides
  • Osteogenesis Inhibition: Biologically inert and non-resorbable; acts as a physical barrier preventing osteogenic hematoma formation. Use is strictly contraindicated where bone fusion is desired (spinal fusion beds, fracture non-unions).[14]
  • Infection Promotion: Reduces the bacterial inoculum required to cause osteomyelitis by a factor of 10,000 (from 107 to 103 organisms).[16]
  • Chronic Inflammation: Long-term retention can incite foreign body giant cell reactions, forming "waxomas" or granulomas.[14]

Soluble polymer alternatives (e.g., alkylene oxide copolymers like Ostene) dissolve within 24–48 hours, do not inhibit bone healing, and have significantly lower infection rates in contaminated models compared to beeswax-based products.[16] I

5. Level III: Active and Flowable Hemostasis

LEVEL III Supplying Clotting Factors

5.1. Thrombin and Fibrin Sealants

  • Thrombin: Available in bovine, human, and recombinant forms. Directly converts fibrinogen to fibrin. Bovine thrombin carries a historical risk of inducing antibodies against Factor V, though modern purifications have reduced this risk.[19]
  • Fibrin Sealants (Tisseel, Evicel): Two-syringe kits—one with fibrinogen, one with thrombin—mixed at the nozzle to instantly form a stable fibrin clot. Indicated for vascular anastomoses, dural tears, and sealing large raw surfaces in coagulopathic patients.[20]

5.2. Flowable Hemostats

Flowable hemostats (e.g., Floseal, Surgiflo) represent "third generation" hybrid technology: a passive gelatin matrix (granules) mixed with active thrombin. The gelatin granules swell to provide mechanical tamponade, while high-concentration thrombin activates the patient's fibrinogen at the bleeding site.[19] III

They are particularly useful in orthopedic surgery for bleeding from irregular bony surfaces, spinal epidural bleeding, or deep pelvic wounds where precise placement of a sponge is difficult.

7. Level V: Systemic Pharmacological Interventions

LEVEL V Tranexamic Acid & Cell Salvage

7.1. Tranexamic Acid (TXA) Protocols

TXA is a synthetic lysine analogue that competitively inhibits the activation of plasminogen to plasmin, preventing fibrinolysis. It has become a cornerstone of blood management in arthroplasty and trauma.[26] I

7.1.1. Efficacy and Safety

Meta-analyses of RCTs have unequivocally demonstrated that TXA reduces total blood loss and transfusion requirements in THA and TKA without increasing VTE risk, even in patients with cardiovascular disease.[26] I

7.2. Intraoperative Cell Salvage (Cell Saver)

Cell salvage involves aspiration of shed blood from the surgical field, which is then washed, filtered, and concentrated for reinfusion.[32]

  • Indications: Anticipated blood loss >1000 mL (e.g., revision arthroplasty, scoliosis correction, pelvic fractures) or patients who refuse allogenic blood.[32]
  • Absolute Contraindications: Aspiration of hypotonic solutions (water), alcohol, or hydrogen peroxide (causes hemolysis).
  • Relative Contraindications:
    • Malignancy: Modern leukocyte depletion filters effectively remove cancer cells; increasingly used in oncologic surgery with informed consent.[32]
    • Infection: Washing and filtration significantly reduce bacterial load—viable in life-threatening hemorrhage even with contaminated fields.[34]
    • Bone Cement: Liquid methylmethacrylate must not be aspirated.

8. Level VI: Catastrophic Hemorrhage & Pelvic Ring Injuries

LEVEL VI Pelvic Ring & Damage Control

Pelvic fractures represent a unique and lethal hemostatic challenge due to complex vascular anatomy (presacral venous plexus, internal iliac arterial branches) and the "chimney effect", where disruption of the pelvic floor prevents natural tamponade.

8.1. Hemodynamic Algorithm

  1. Trauma Bay Management:
    • Apply pelvic binder centered over greater trochanters to close pelvic volume.[1]
    • Initiate Massive Transfusion Protocol (MTP): balanced 1:1:1 ratio (Plasma:Platelets:RBCs).
    • FAST Exam: differentiate intraperitoneal bleeding (→ laparotomy) from retroperitoneal bleeding (→ pelvic stabilization).[6]
  2. Surgical Decision Making:
    • Hemodynamically Unstable + FAST Positive: Immediate Laparotomy + Preperitoneal Pelvic Packing (PPP).
    • Hemodynamically Unstable + FAST Negative:
      • Angioembolization (AE): Gold standard for arterial bleeding (blush on CT). Requires time and specialized team.
      • Preperitoneal Pelvic Packing (PPP): Indicated if SBP < 70 mmHg, AE unavailable, or as bridge to AE. PPP addresses venous bleeding (85% of pelvic hemorrhage) that AE cannot treat.[8]

8.2. Preperitoneal Pelvic Packing (PPP) Protocol

PPP is a damage control technique designed to directly compress the retroperitoneal space.[35] III

  1. Incision: 6–8 cm vertical midline from pubic symphysis extending cranially.
  2. Approach: Linea alba incised; rectus retracted laterally. The peritoneum is NOT opened—dissection remains in the preperitoneal space (Space of Retzius).
  3. Dissection: Bladder retracted medially; blunt dissection posteriorly along the pelvic brim toward the sacroiliac joints.
  4. Packing: Three large laparotomy sponges on each side:
    • Sponge 1: Deep posterior (adjacent to sacroiliac joint) — tamponades presacral plexus.
    • Sponge 2: Middle of the pelvic brim.
    • Sponge 3: Anterior retropubic space.
  5. Counter-Pressure: Anterior external fixator or C-clamp to provide rigid counter-force.
  6. Second Look: Patient transferred to ICU for resuscitation; packs removed/exchanged after 24–48 hours.

9. Level VII: Management of Major Vascular Injuries

LEVEL VII Iatrogenic & Traumatic Vascular Injuries

9.1. Iatrogenic Vascular Injuries (IVIs)

IVIs are rare but devastating complications of TKA, THA, and spine surgery. The popliteal artery is at highest risk during TKA.[37]

  1. Recognition: Intraoperative hard signs (pulsatile bleeding, expanding hematoma, loss of distal pulses) or postoperative signs (pain out of proportion, pallor, paresthesia, excessive drain output >1000 mL).
  2. Immediate Control: Stop procedure. Apply manual pressure or re-inflate tourniquet. Consult vascular surgery.
  3. Imaging: Digital Subtraction Angiography (DSA) preferred over CTA in post-TKA patients due to metal artifacts.[38]
  4. Repair: Endovascular (covered stents) or open repair (contralateral saphenous vein bypass). Prophylactic fasciotomy mandatory if ischemia >4–6 hours.[39]

9.2. Traumatic Vascular Injuries

9.2.1. Diagnosis: Hard vs. Soft Signs

  • Hard Signs (mandate immediate exploration): Active pulsatile hemorrhage, expanding pulsatile hematoma, palpable thrill/bruit, overt ischemia.
  • Soft Signs (mandate imaging): History of significant bleeding, wound proximity to major vessel, small non-pulsatile hematoma, neurologic deficit.
  • ABI Protocol: Ankle-Brachial Index < 0.9 is a sensitive indicator of vascular injury; if ABI < 0.9, CTA is mandatory.[39]

9.2.2. The Shunt Priority: Temporary Intravascular Shunts (TIVS)

Surgical Sequence Protocol
  1. Shunt Insertion: Immediately restores limb perfusion, stopping the ischemic clock.
  2. Orthopedic Stabilization: External fixation applied. The shunt allows manipulation for reduction without disrupting a delicate vascular anastomosis.
  3. Definitive Vascular Repair: Once bone is stable and length established, the vascular surgeon performs definitive graft or repair.[41]

10. Level VIII: Damage Control Orthopedics (DCO)

LEVEL VIII Physiological Survival Over Anatomical Reconstruction

In the polytrauma patient, the physiological reserve is depleted by the "first hit" of trauma. Extensive definitive surgery acts as a "second hit", releasing inflammatory mediators (IL-6, TNF-α) that can precipitate MODS or ARDS.[47]

10.1. Principles of DCO

  1. Phase 1 (Initial Surgery): Rapid (<60 min), minimally invasive stabilization. Long bone fractures stabilized with external fixators. Hemorrhage controlled via packing or ligation.
  2. Phase 2 (ICU Resuscitation): Optimization of physiology. Correction of the Lethal Triad. Warming, correction of acidosis, coagulopathy management.
  3. Phase 3 (Definitive Surgery): Conversion of external fixators to internal fixation (nails/plates) once physiologically stable—the "window of opportunity" (typically days 5–10), avoiding the hyper-inflammatory window of days 2–4.[47]

10.2. Indications for DCO vs. Early Total Care (ETC)

DCO Indication Thresholds (from Source Document)

The decision to pursue DCO rather than ETC is driven by physiological markers:

  • pH < 7.24
  • Temperature < 35°C
  • Serum Lactate > 4.0 mmol/L
  • Coagulopathy (INR > 1.5)
  • ISS > 40
  • Presence of bilateral pulmonary contusions[48]

11. Conclusion

The management of hemorrhage in orthopedic surgery is a highly stratified discipline demanding fluency in a wide array of techniques. From the routine application of a tourniquet in elective hand surgery to the chaotic resuscitation of a pelvic fracture in the trauma bay, the orthopedic surgeon must adhere to a strict hierarchy of interventions:

  • At the basic level, optimization of tourniquet pressures and transition to soluble bone hemostats prevent iatrogenic morbidity.
  • In elective arthroplasty, integration of TXA and appropriate topical agents has drastically reduced transfusion needs.
  • In trauma, the shift to C-ABC, adoption of Preperitoneal Pelvic Packing, and strategic use of DCO reflect deeper understanding of coagulopathy physiology.

The ultimate goal is not merely the cessation of bleeding, but the preservation of the physiological milieu necessary for recovery and regeneration.
