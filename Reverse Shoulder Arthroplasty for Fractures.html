Reverse Shoulder Arthroplasty for Proximal Humeral Fractures

A Comprehensive Interactive Clinical & Technical Guide for the Elderly Population

1. Introduction & Evolving Treatment Paradigm

The Challenge of Proximal Humeral Fractures in the Geriatric Population

Proximal humeral fractures (PHFs) are the third most common fracture type in patients over 65 years, accounting for approximately 5% of all adult fractures.Level II[1] The majority result from low-energy trauma such as a fall from standing height. With an aging global population, their incidence is projected to rise substantially.[3]

These injuries are more than isolated fractures—they are often sentinel events signaling underlying frailty. Termed "fragility fractures," they carry a notable 1-year mortality rate of 10% irrespective of treatment modality.[3] The clinical challenge is compounded by multiple negative prognostic indicators common in elderly patients:

  • Severe fracture comminution and significant fragment displacement
  • Poor bone quality secondary to osteopenia/osteoporosis
  • Pre-existing rotator cuff pathology or glenohumeral arthritis[6]
Key Concept
A "complex" fracture in the geriatric setting is defined not merely by the Neer classification pattern, but as an aggregate of mechanical and biological variables—bone quality, cuff status, and comminution collectively predict treatment failure.[7]

Limitations of Traditional Treatments

Non-Operative Management: Sling immobilization remains standard for non-displaced PHFs, yielding satisfactory outcomes in these cases.[8,14] However, when applied to complex displaced 3- and 4-part fractures, it frequently leads to malunion, nonunion, AVN, subacromial impingement, chronic pain, and stiffness.[8]

Open Reduction & Internal Fixation (ORIF): While locking plate technology expanded ORIF indications, its use in complex geriatric fractures is associated with high failure rates. Common failure modes include loss of fixation with varus collapse and screw cutout through the humeral head.[6] Reoperation rates reach up to 26% in some series.[15]

Hemiarthroplasty (HA): Provides reliable pain relief but yields bimodal, unpredictable functional outcomes. Success critically depends on anatomic tuberosity healing—failure leads to persistent cuff deficiency and pseudoparalysis.[2,6]

Paradigm Shift Toward Reverse Shoulder Arthroplasty

The fundamental advantage of RSA lies in its non-anatomic, semiconstrained design. Based on Grammont principles, the prosthesis reverses normal anatomy, medializing and distalizing the center of rotation, which lengthens the deltoid and increases its lever arm.[1] This allows the deltoid to power arm elevation independently of the rotator cuff.[2]

Central Value Proposition
RSA directly addresses the primary failure mechanisms of both ORIF (post-AVN cuff dysfunction) and HA (tuberosity nonunion), providing outcome predictability. Patients achieve functional goals more quickly with less dependence on intensive rehabilitation.[11] Recent literature confirms an accelerating trend toward RSA as the emerging standard of care for appropriately selected elderly patients.[2,3]

⚠ Critical Contraindication
A permanent axillary nerve palsy or global deltoid dysfunction is an absolute contraindication to RSA. Performing RSA without a functional deltoid is biomechanically futile and will result in a poorly functioning, unstable shoulder.[2]

Comprehensive Preoperative Evaluation

  • History: Baseline function, independence, ambulatory status, living situation, cognitive function, and prior shoulder surgeries.[5]
  • Neurovascular Assessment: Meticulous axillary nerve integrity testing (deltoid contraction assessment). EMG/nerve conduction studies if any suspicion of injury.[7]
  • General Health: Optimization of cardiovascular disease, diabetes, smoking cessation, nutritional status, and obesity before surgery.[26,27]

Advanced Imaging Protocols

  • Radiography: Trauma series with true AP (Grashey), scapular-Y, and axillary views.[4]
  • CT with 3D reconstructions: Standard of care for surgical planning—details comminution, tuberosity displacement, head-split components, medial calcar integrity, glenoid version/inclination, and bone loss.[4,34]
  • MRI: Not routine for acute fractures; useful for confirming pre-existing cuff tears or evaluating fatty infiltration in chronic fracture sequelae.[30]

Digital Templating & Virtual Surgical Planning

Modern 3D CT-based planning platforms allow surgeons to simulate the entire procedure virtually. Key benefits include preoperative determination of optimal glenoid baseplate size, position, and orientation.[36,39]

Contralateral Templating Technique
A CT scan of the uninjured contralateral shoulder is used for planning to accurately estimate native humeral head size and appropriate stem dimensions, overcoming the challenge of destroyed ipsilateral anatomy.[41]

3. Surgical Technique: Step-by-Step

Patient Positioning & Anesthesia

The modified beach chair position (head elevated 30–60°) is almost universally preferred. The patient is moved to the table edge on the operative side to free the scapula.[24] Anesthesia typically combines general anesthesia with an interscalene nerve block for postoperative analgesia, though some surgeons omit the block to allow immediate neurological assessment.[7,46]

Surgical Approaches

Feature Deltopectoral Approach Deltoid-Splitting Approach
Incision Along deltopectoral groove (10–15 cm) Anterolateral acromion, split distally
Key Interval Between deltoid & pectoralis major (internervous plane) Between anterior & middle deltoid thirds (intramuscular)
Nerve at Risk Musculocutaneous nerve (excessive medial retraction) Axillary nerve (if split > 5 cm distal to acromion)
Glenoid Exposure Good to excellent Excellent, especially superior glenoid
Tuberosity Exposure Good Excellent, direct GT access
Outcomes Workhorse approach; low axillary nerve risk[48] May have shorter OR time; no significant PROM difference[51]

Humeral Preparation

  1. Evacuate fracture hematoma; identify the long head of biceps tendon (bicipital groove landmark)
  2. Tenotomize/tenodesis the biceps to improve exposure and remove pain source[22]
  3. Extract humeral head fragment; save for autologous bone graft[2]
  4. Mobilize and tag tuberosities with heavy non-absorbable sutures through tendon-bone junction[2]
  5. Sequentially ream humeral canal (caution in osteoporotic bone)[44]

Glenoid Preparation

Circumferential labrum/capsule release from glenoid rim. Guide pin placed at glenoid center with 0–10° inferior tilt to minimize scapular notching risk. Cannulated reaming, baseplate impaction, and glenosphere attachment complete glenoid preparation.[44]

Determining Humeral Implant Height & Version

This is one of the most critical steps—normal landmarks are lost in comminuted fractures.[22]

Technique 1: Pectoralis Major Landmark
Measure 5.0 cm proximally from the superior border of the pectoralis major tendon insertion; position the superior aspect of the metallic humeral implant at this level.[52]
Technique 2: Intraoperative Trial Reduction
Assess stability and deltoid tension with trial components. Arm lengthening should not exceed 2.5 cm relative to the contralateral side. Exchange trial liners of different thicknesses to fine-tune tension.[8,44]

For humeral version, a consensus range of 0–20° retroversion (most surgeons prefer 10–20°) is recommended.[21,44]

Final Implantation

In osteoporotic bone, cemented fixation is often necessary for immediate stability. Cement should be limited to the diaphyseal portion to avoid impeding tuberosity healing in the metaphysis.[43,44]

4. The Critical Role of Tuberosity Management

Tuberosity management should be viewed as complex osteosynthesis, not merely soft-tissue reattachment. Anatomic healing is strongly correlated with superior clinical results.Level II[6]

Biomechanical Importance

  • Active external rotation: GT healing restores infraspinatus/teres minor function—critical for rotational strength[7]
  • Deltoid wrapping: Healed tuberosities restore the rounded proximal humeral contour, enhancing deltoid efficiency[43]
  • Joint stability: Soft-tissue buttress significantly diminishes dislocation risk[43]
  • A systematic review & meta-analysis confirmed significantly better forward flexion, external/internal rotation, and Constant scores in patients with healed GTs[59]

Surgical Repair Techniques

  • Horizontal fixation: Mattress/figure-of-eight sutures from GT to LT around prosthetic neck[44]
  • Vertical fixation: Sutures through drill holes in humeral shaft to prevent superior migration[43]
  • Prosthesis integration: Fracture-specific stem suture eyelets/fins create direct bone-to-implant linkage[61]
  • Circumferential cerclage: Powerful compression preventing posterior GT gapping[50]
  • "7 sutures & 8 knots" technique: Intricate multi-point construct achieving ~90% union rates[57]

Bone Grafting

The excised humeral head provides an excellent source of cancellous autograft. Morselized graft is packed between tuberosity fragments and the porous-coated stem surface, promoting osseointegration. Studies show significantly higher union rates with autograft augmentation.[20,63]

5. Implant Considerations

Fracture-Specific Humeral Stem Design

  • Enhanced suture management: Multiple proximal suture holes/eyelets/fins for robust tuberosity repair[20]
  • Biologically active surfaces: Porous coatings or HA coatings to promote osseointegration[20]
  • Fenestrations/windows: Allow packing of bone graft directly into the implant[20]
  • Lateral flange: Buttoress for GT positioning; smooth polished neck to prevent suture fraying[20,22]

The Critical Impact of Neck-Shaft Angle (NSA)

The original Grammont design featured a 155° NSA ("inlay"), maximizing deltoid tension but hindering tuberosity healing. Modern evidence demonstrates that a 135° NSA is superior for fracture treatment, associated with significantly higher tuberosity healing rates compared to 145° or 155° implants.[20,63]

Evidence-Based Implant Selection
A 135° NSA with "onlay" design allows tuberosity repositioning with less tension, creating a more favorable mechanical environment for bone healing. Patients in the 135° group demonstrate superior forward flexion and improved active external rotation.Level II[63]

Short Stems & Convertible Platforms

Short metaphyseal-fitting stems preserve diaphyseal bone stock, simplifying future revision. Early studies show comparable outcomes to long stems.[66]

Convertible/platform stems allow conversion from HA to RSA without stem exchange, resulting in shorter operative times, less blood loss, and fewer intraoperative complications.[68]

6. Strategies for Complication Avoidance

Neurological Injury Prevention

  • Palpate axillary nerve before deep retractor placement[7]
  • Limit deltoid split to ≤5 cm distal to acromion[45]
  • Arm lengthening ≤ 2.5 cm; avoid extreme traction positions[8,72]
  • Consider intraoperative nerve monitoring (IONM) for high-risk cases[71]

Intraoperative Fracture Prevention

  • Gentle reaming technique in osteoporotic bone[44]
  • Cemented fixation preferred in severe osteoporosis—avoids hoop stresses[77]
  • Complete circumferential capsular release for glenoid exposure; "sphere-in-socket" technique[75]
  • If fracture occurs: stem acts as intramedullary splint + cerclage augmentation[76]

Dislocation Prevention

  • Accurate humeral height restoration (pectoralis major landmark or trial reduction)[46]
  • Glenoid baseplate with slight inferior overhang and tilt[55]
  • Appropriate deltoid tension—not overstuffed[44]
  • Anatomic tuberosity healing provides critical anterior buttress[50]

Infection Prevention

  • Preoperative: Optimize diabetes, smoking cessation, nutritional status, treat infection sources[27]
  • Intraoperative: Strict sterile technique, laminar airflow, prophylactic antibiotics (Cefazolin), minimize OR time[22]
  • Postoperative: Short IV antibiotic course, close wound monitoring[26]

7. Postoperative Rehabilitation & Recovery

Rehabilitation differs fundamentally from elective RSA—it must balance early motion to prevent stiffness with protecting the tuberosity repair for 6–8 weeks.[80]

Typical Recovery Timeline

  • 0–6 weeks: Most intense pain subsides[86]
  • 6–12 weeks: Bony tuberosity healing occurs[86]
  • 3–6 months: Majority of functional improvement (motion and strength)
  • 12–24 months: Continued gradual improvement; full recovery may take ≥1 year[87]

"Timing Penalty"
RSA performed acutely (within 4 weeks) yields significantly better clinical scores, superior ROM, and lower complication rates compared to delayed RSA as salvage for failed initial treatment. This supports definitive primary RSA in appropriately selected high-risk patients.Level II[96]

Complication Rates

Overall complication rate: 7–21%.[10] Key complications include:

  • Tuberosity failure: 15–25% (nonunion, malunion, resorption)[43]
  • Infection: 1–4%[43]
  • Dislocation: 0.8–2.8%[59]
  • Scapular notching: Up to 73% radiographically (clinical significance debated)[39]
  • Acromial/spine fractures: 1–11%[43]
  • Neurologic injury: Rare but potentially devastating[72]

Long-Term Survivorship

Grammont-style prostheses demonstrate 10-year survival free from revision of 91–93%.[101] For fracture sequelae, the 10-year rate is approximately 84%.[101] Gradual functional decline (particularly strength) may occur beyond 10 years, reinforcing the consensus to reserve RSA for older patients with limited salvage options.[102]

1 RSA vs. HA for Acute Proximal Humeral Fractures in the Elderly
Multiple systematic reviews and RCTs consistently demonstrate RSA produces superior active forward flexion and better overall Constant/ASES scores compared to HA. Mean forward elevation after RSA ~125° vs. ~90° after HA.Level I[16,18,93]
2 RSA vs. Non-Operative Treatment: Randomized Controlled Trial
An RCT of displaced 3- and 4-part fractures in patients >70 years found RSA provided statistically superior Constant scores at 1 year, though the mean difference approached minimal clinical importance. Surgical group experienced major complications not seen in non-operative group.Level I[94]
3 Acute vs. Delayed RSA for Proximal Humeral Fractures: Meta-Analysis
Systematic review and meta-analysis demonstrating significantly better outcomes with acute RSA (within 4 weeks) versus delayed RSA as salvage procedure, including superior clinical scores, ROM, and lower complication rates.Level II[96]
4 Tuberosity Healing & Functional Outcomes After RSA: Meta-Analysis
Quantified that patients with healed GTs had significantly better postoperative forward flexion, external rotation, internal rotation, and Constant scores versus non-healed GTs. Patient-reported pain was largely unaffected by healing status.Level II[59]
5 135° vs. 145°/155° Neck-Shaft Angle: Impact on Tuberosity Healing
Clinical and biomechanical evidence demonstrating that 135° NSA is associated with significantly higher tuberosity healing rates and superior postoperative forward flexion and external rotation compared to 145° and 155° implants.Level II[20,63]
6 RSA vs. ORIF in Complex Geriatric Fractures
Comparative studies show RSA provides better forward flexion and higher Constant scores at 2-year follow-up in the most comminuted patterns (OTA/AO Type C2), with significantly lower reoperation rates than ORIF.Level II[93]

Describes a precise, intricate multi-suture, multi-knot fixation construct achieving tuberosity union rates approaching 90%, with improved short-term clinical results.Level III[57]

10. Conclusion

Reverse shoulder arthroplasty has fundamentally altered the treatment algorithm for complex proximal humeral fractures in the elderly. By leveraging the deltoid muscle and bypassing the need for a functional rotator cuff, RSA directly addresses the primary challenges of poor bone quality and tuberosity healing difficulty inherent in this population.

Successful application demands meticulous patient selection, advanced imaging-based planning, and precise surgical execution. The critical steps—determining humeral height/version without normal landmarks and achieving robust tuberosity reconstruction—are paramount. While RSA can provide good function even if tuberosities fail to heal, achieving excellent outcomes with restored rotational strength depends on their successful union.

Clinical outcomes consistently demonstrate RSA provides superior forward elevation and functional scores compared to HA, and more reliable results with lower re-operation rates compared to ORIF. However, surgeons and patients must understand the trade-offs: limited external rotation, unique complication profiles, and potential long-term functional decline. RSA remains a definitive, often final reconstructive procedure best reserved for appropriately selected elderly patients.
