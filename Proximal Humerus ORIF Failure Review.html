Locking Plate Failure in Proximal Humeral Fractures

Interactive Evidence-Based Educational Summary — Source: Comprehensive Research Report (2026)

1. Introduction & Clinical Significance

Proximal humeral fractures (PHFs) represent a profound challenge in contemporary orthopedic traumatology, constituting approximately 4–5% of all skeletal injuries and ranking as the third most common osteoporotic fracture in the elderly population, surpassed only by fractures of the distal radius and the proximal femur.1 While the majority of minimally displaced or nondisplaced fractures are effectively managed through conservative modalities, displaced and unstable two-, three-, and four-part fractures per the Neer classification routinely necessitate operative intervention.5

Over the past two decades, the surgical paradigm has overwhelmingly shifted toward open reduction and internal fixation (ORIF) utilizing angular stable locking plates, with the Proximal Humerus Internal Locking System (PHILOS) emerging as the ubiquitous clinical standard.6 However, despite profound biomechanical advantages over conventional compression plates, locking plate fixation is associated with an unacceptably high rate of post-operative complications, ranging from 17.9% to 49%, with subsequent reoperation rates approaching 14–17.3%.5

⚠ Key Clinical Reality: Failure manifests across a spectrum including progressive varus collapse, intra-articular screw penetration (screw cut-out), avascular necrosis (AVN) of the humeral head, and catastrophic plate breakage. Plate breakage, while less frequent, represents a catastrophic mechanical failure resulting from suboptimal fracture reduction, compromised bone biology, and cyclical material fatigue.11

Unlike conventional compression plates, locking plates function as internally placed external fixators, relying entirely on the threaded interface between the locking screw head and the plate itself. This design minimizes periosteal compression, preserves the periosteal blood supply, and provides superior pull-out strength in osteoporotic cancellous bone.1613

2. Epidemiology & Taxonomy of Construct Failure

The clinical reality of locking plate osteosynthesis is heavily influenced by the fragility of the host bone and the complex, multidirectional forces exerted by the rotator cuff musculature. Complication rates escalate dramatically in multi-part, complex fractures among osteoporotic patients, reaching 24–29.5%.5 Construct failures are stratified into biological, mechanical, and infectious etiologies, each possessing distinct pathophysiological origins and clinical timelines.13

2.1 Biological Failure

Biological failure is predominantly characterized by avascular necrosis (AVN) of the humeral head, occurring in 3–23% of cases.5 The vascular supply to the proximal humerus is precarious, relying heavily on the ascending branch of the anterior circumflex humeral artery and the intraosseous anastomosis with the posterior circumflex humeral artery.

Factors that correlate with AVN development include:13

  • Complete disruption of the medial capsular hinge
  • Severe comminution and displacement of the tuberosity segments
  • A short metaphyseal calcar segment
  • Initial degree of humeral head dislocation
⚠ Secondary Mechanical Failure from AVN: As the subchondral bone resorbs and collapses due to osteonecrosis, the locking screws—rigidly fixed to the plate—do not collapse with the bone. Consequently, screws previously placed in an extra-articular position penetrate the joint space, leading to rapid destruction of the glenoid articular cartilage.9

2.2 Mechanical Failure

Mechanical failure directly implicates the structural integrity of the implant and the quality of its bone purchase. The most frequent mechanical failure is varus collapse coupled with screw cut-out, reported at 4.1–11.6%.5 Catastrophic implant breakage is reported in 5–8% of all cases experiencing hardware failure.5

2.3 Infectious & Neurological Complications

Deep tissue infection occurs in approximately 1.3–4% of cases.9 Neurological deficits—specifically axillary nerve neurapraxia or axonotmesis—manifest secondary to either the initial high-energy trauma or iatrogenic injury during plate positioning and deltoid retractor placement.13

3. Biomechanical & Metallurgical Etiology of Plate Breakage

Catastrophic breakage of a titanium proximal humeral locking plate is rarely the result of a single acute traumatic overload. Rather, it is almost universally a classic fatigue failure caused by repetitive, sub-yield cyclical loading.11 The trajectory of a proximal humerus fracture towards union or failure is fundamentally a race between biological bone healing and the mechanical fatigue life of the implant.

3.1 Cantilever Bending Pathomechanics

In a healthy, intact proximal humerus, joint reaction forces and muscle vector loads are transmitted smoothly down the medial cortical column. Following a complex fracture with medial void or severe metaphyseal comminution, if the surgeon fails to reconstruct this medial buttress, a devastating phenomenon known as cantilever bending occurs.8

In this scenario, the humeral head is anchored to the superior portion of the plate via locking screws, while the intact shaft is anchored to the inferior portion. Without a competent medial bony hinge, every elevation of the arm creates a severe bending moment focused entirely on the unsupported titanium segment bridging the fracture gap.8

3.2 Metallurgical Retrieval Analysis

Retrieval analyses using optical microscopy and scanning electron microscopy (SEM) of fractured PHILOS plates have mapped the precise morphology of failures. Breakages overwhelmingly occur at "Section E"—the critical transition zone between the wider, multi-hole proximal spatulated section and the narrower distal shaft section.11

This specific region contains two horizontally adjacent combi-holes, which dramatically reduce the cross-sectional area of solid titanium, decreasing the plate's area moment of inertia and creating a profound structural stress riser.11

🔬 Fatigue Failure Morphology: Microscopic inspection consistently reveals a "globular waved structure" characteristic of fatigue failure. Initial crack origination occurs at the burnished peripheral edges of empty screw holes—the areas of maximum tensile strain. Classic fatigue striations propagate inward until sudden, catastrophic macroscopic failure occurs, marked by ductile dimples.11

3.3 Finite Element Analysis & Screw Optimization

Computational modeling via Finite Element Analysis (FEA) demonstrates that the number of cycles to failure correlates logarithmically with the average compressive principal strain surrounding the proximal locking screws.29 Optimized, divergent screw trajectories significantly lower peri-implant bone strain and create a more uniform von Mises stress distribution, theoretically mitigating the risk of cyclic cut-out and delaying fatigue breakage onset.15

4. Patient-Specific Risk Factors & Radiographic Predictors

4.1 Demographic & Systemic Comorbidities

Advanced chronological age and severely diminished bone mineral density (osteoporosis) remain the most profound demographic predictors of failure.23

Key systemic risk factors identified through multivariable regression analyses include:3637

  • Diabetes mellitus: OR 4.36 (95% CI 1.23–16.78; p = 0.025) — impairs microvascular perfusion and osteoblastic differentiation
  • Alcohol consumption & smoking: Consistently linked to nonunion and delayed union through vasoconstriction and cellular toxicity
  • BMI ≥ 30 kg/m²: Correlates with elevated complication rates due to exponentially greater gravitational torque during rehabilitation

4.2 Fracture Morphology

Three- and four-part fractures, particularly those involving a head-split component or true fracture-dislocation, inherently disrupt the medial hinge and strip the periosteal blood supply.6 The most significant anatomical predictor of catastrophic mechanical failure is the loss of the medial calcar support.8 Clinical studies confirm that medial cortical translation exceeding 5 mm significantly heightens the risk of secondary displacement and implant failure (OR 4.70, p = 0.039).36

5. Surgical Execution & Technical Prevention Strategies

Prevention of implant failure begins meticulously in the operating room. Surgeons must adhere to strict principles: the locking plate must be utilized strictly as a neutralization device, not as a tool to effect reduction.13

5.1 Surgical Approaches

Comparative studies evaluating Minimally Invasive Plate Osteosynthesis (MIPO) via deltoid-split approach versus traditional ORIF demonstrate that traditional ORIF patients face a 2.6× higher risk of soft tissue complications and a 4.5× higher risk (95% CI 1.1–19.5) of nonunion compared to the MIPO cohort.44 MIPO preserves the delicate periosteal envelope, favoring robust callus formation. The MIPO group demonstrated an 8.2× higher probability of achieving forward flexion above 90°.44

5.2 Reduction Principles

Anatomical or intentional valgus impaction reduction is critical. A "joy-stick" technique with temporary threaded pins allows gentle manipulation of the humeral head without crushing cancellous bone.13 Under no circumstances should the plate be applied to draw a varus head to the shaft via screw tightening, as this pre-loads the titanium plate with massive tensile stress before rehabilitation begins.

5.3 Tuberosity Fixation & Calcar Screws

Heavy, non-absorbable tension band sutures must be passed through the tendinous insertions of the supraspinatus, infraspinatus, and subscapularis, then tied into the suture eyelets of the PHILOS plate.13 This creates a tension-band effect that neutralizes the superior and posterior displacing forces of the rotator cuff.

The strategic insertion of calcar screws into the inferomedial quadrant is highly protective. Crucially, the screw must physically purchase both the proximal head fragment and the distal shaft fragment to bridge the medial hinge effectively. Failure to achieve this dual-purchase results in a statistically significant higher rate of fixation failure.26

6. Advanced Construct Augmentation Strategies

In clinical scenarios involving severe osteoporosis, metabolic bone disease, or significant metaphyseal comminution, standard locking plate fixation is often insufficient. Construct augmentation is absolutely mandatory to transform the biomechanical environment.5

6.1 Intramedullary Fibular Strut Allograft

When the medial hinge is completely devoid of host bone, insertion of an intramedullary structural graft—typically a fibular strut allograft—is heavily indicated.3 The rigid graft is placed within the medullary canal and countersunk superiorly into the cancellous void, effectively recreating an unyielding medial cortical buttress.40

Finite element models suggest that inserting the fibular graft at an oblique 30° angle relative to the humeral shaft provides superior biomechanical advantages under traction and varus forces compared to a standard 0° vertical insertion.3

💡 Alternative Graft Option: Comparative biomechanical studies indicate that cancellous femoral or hip allografts offer statistically equivalent stiffness and maximum failure loads (stiffness: 174 for fibular vs. 157 for femoral, p = 0.97). Cancellous grafts present advantages regarding lower cost, higher availability, and significantly easier canal preparation should future revision to arthroplasty be necessary.50

6.2 Cement Augmentation of Locking Screws

In geriatric patients with exceedingly low BMD, cement augmentation (CA) using PMMA or calcium phosphate cements injected through specially designed perforated locking screws significantly increases the surface area of the bone-implant interface.21

A comprehensive meta-analysis demonstrated:2153

  • Implant failure rate: 2.6% with cement augmentation vs. 14% without (OR 0.19, 95% CI 0.10–0.39, p < 0.05)
  • Overall complication rate: More than halved (OR 0.45)
  • AVN rate: Statistically identical between cohorts (4.3% with cement vs. 4.2% without, OR 1.01), proving augmentation does not introduce new biological hazards

7. Evaluation & Diagnostic Workup of Failed Osteosynthesis

When a patient presents with recurrent pain, sudden loss of shoulder motion, or radiographic evidence of implant failure, a highly systematic evaluation is mandated.13

History & Physical Examination

Early failure (within weeks post-operatively) typically suggests catastrophic technical error—unrecognized varus malreduction, tuberosity fixation failure, or screws penetrating the joint immediately upon subsidence. Late failure (months to years later) points toward biological non-union, progressive AVN, or titanium implant fatigue.13

Advanced Imaging

Standard orthogonal radiographs confirm macroscopic failure, but a high-resolution CT scan with metallic artifact suppression algorithms and 3D reconstructions is mandatory for pre-operative planning. The CT provides critical data on glenoid cartilage erosion, residual bone stock, and tuberosity integrity—all of which dictate whether a joint-preserving revision or arthroplasty is feasible.13

Infectious Workup

Indolent, low-grade deep infections must be ruled out. Cutibacterium acnes is notorious for forming biofilms on orthopedic hardware. Aspirated fluid cultures must be held for a minimum of 14 days to accurately assess for C. acnes proliferation.13

8. Management & Salvage of Failed Osteosynthesis

The management algorithm relies heavily on the patient's chronological and physiological age, functional demands, glenoid cartilage status, and tuberosity viability.13

8.1 Revision ORIF

Revision ORIF is reserved for a highly specific, narrow subset of patients: relatively young (<60 years), highly active individuals presenting with aseptic non-union or isolated hardware failure, who maintain preserved glenohumeral articular cartilage, a functional rotator cuff, and healed tuberosities.13

Technical demands include:13

  • Aggressive debridement of the non-union site down to punctate, bleeding bone
  • Extensive contracture release (complete capsular excision, subscapularis mobilization)
  • Frequent acceptance of significant intentional shortening for reliable metaphyseal-diaphyseal contact
  • A new, longer locking plate to span previously compromised screw holes
  • Voluminous autogenous bone grafting (typically iliac crest)

Successful revision ORIF in highly selected patients can achieve union rates of up to 98% in specialized centers, restoring native shoulder kinematics.13

8.2 Hemiarthroplasty (Fading Paradigm)

Hemiarthroplasty relies entirely on concentric healing of the tuberosities around the implant. In the setting of failed ORIF, tuberosities are frequently severely comminuted, osteopenic, or resorbed. If they fail to heal, the patient suffers anterosuperior escape, pseudoparalysis, and abysmal outcomes.34 It is now generally reserved strictly for younger patients with unreconstructable cartilage but intact, reliable tuberosities.13

8.3 Reverse Total Shoulder Arthroplasty (RTSA): The Salvage Gold Standard

For the vast majority of older patients (>65 years) or those with advanced AVN, severe metaphyseal bone loss, glenoid cartilage destruction, or tuberosity resorption, RTSA has firmly established itself as the definitive salvage workhorse.13

The RTSA design reverses the articular concavity, profoundly medializing and inferiorizing the center of rotation. This dramatically increases the deltoid lever arm, allowing arm elevation independently of the rotator cuff or tuberosity healing.2

Primary vs. Revision RTSA Outcomes

Functional outcomes following revision RTSA are measurably inferior to primary RTSA. Institutional data shows a complication rate of 8% for salvage RTSA vs. 5% for primary RTSA, including prosthetic dislocation and aseptic loosening.61 This deficit is largely attributed to soft tissue scarring, deltoid impairment from multiple approaches, and metaphyseal bone loss during hardware extraction.13

Technical Hazards of Revision to RTSA

  • Severe proximal humeral bone loss and sclerotic diaphyseal canal after hardware removal13
  • Asymmetrical glenoid erosion from previous intra-articular screw penetration—requires meticulous 3D CT planning22
  • Contemporary evidence suggests long-stem press-fit or cemented implants without bulk allograft yield comparable outcomes while avoiding allograft-related complications65
  • Long-term prosthetic risks include scapular notching, periprosthetic fractures, joint instability, and metallosis17

🔑 Key Clinical Takeaways

  • Locking plate breakage is a fatigue failure—a race between bone healing and implant endurance, not a single traumatic event.11
  • Medial calcar support is non-negotiable—its absence transforms the plate into a load-bearing cantilever.8
  • Post-operative HSA <125°, HHH <5 mm, and screw-calcar distance ≥12 mm are the critical radiographic predictors of failure.23
  • Cement augmentation reduces implant failure from 14% to 2.6% without increasing AVN risk.53
  • RTSA is the definitive salvage procedure for most patients with failed ORIF, offering superior complication and revision rates compared to revision ORIF.17
  • Consider primary RTSA in the elderly with complex fracture patterns to preempt the devastating cascade of mechanical failure.
