Executive Overview

The management of Musculoskeletal Infection (MSKI)—encompassing both Prosthetic Joint Infection (PJI) and Fracture-Related Infection (FRI)—has undergone a profound paradigm shift between 2023 and 2025. This period is characterized not merely by the refinement of existing protocols but by a fundamental restructuring of the taxonomic and therapeutic frameworks that govern the field. The era of relying solely on binary diagnostic criteria—simplistically categorizing patients as "infected" or "not infected"—is ceding ground to sophisticated probabilistic scoring systems, functional classifications, and highly personalized surgical strategies.

Major international bodies—ICM, EBJIS, MSIS, and BOA—have released pivotal updates. These documents collectively seek to resolve the "Grey Zone" of diagnostic uncertainty, standardize the description of complex fracture scenarios, and impose stricter governance on surgical decision-making. However, a distinct transatlantic divergence has emerged: the North American approach (MSIS/IDSA) emphasizes high specificity to prevent overtreatment, whereas the European approach (EBJIS) aggressively moves toward high sensitivity to capture low-grade and culture-negative infections.[1]

⚠ Notable Gap

The updated IDSA guidelines for PJI remained listed as "TBD" throughout 2024 and early 2025.[3] In this vacuum, the ICM and EBJIS have become the de facto standard-setting bodies for definitive management.[4]

Part I: The Evolving Taxonomy of PJI

1.1 The Battle of Definitions: EBJIS vs. MSIS/ICM

The EBJIS definition (2021) establishes a three-tiered categorization: "Infection Unlikely," "Infection Likely," and "Confirmed Infection"—acknowledging biological ambiguity in a way binary systems cannot. Level II

Recent comparative studies indicate the EBJIS criteria are significantly more sensitive. In a large-scale multicenter cohort, EBJIS diagnosed PJI in 41% of cases vs. 31% (MSIS) and 36% (ICM 2018).[2] Patients classified as "Likely/Confirmed" by EBJIS but "Not Infected" by MSIS carried a three-fold increased risk of subsequent failure if treated as aseptic loosenings. 1.3 The "Grey Zone" and Low-Virulence Pathogens

Low-virulence infections—particularly Cutibacterium acnes and Coagulase-Negative Staphylococci (CoNS)—often fail to trigger the systemic inflammatory markers (ESR/CRP) relied upon by older MSIS criteria.[9] Level II

The EBJIS definition has shown superior performance in diagnosing "low-grade" infections defined by WAIOT, attributed to its inclusion of sonication fluid analysis and nuclear imaging.[10]

The 2025 ICM consensus now recommends that elective arthroplasty be delayed until eradication is confirmed for C. acnes, regardless of its typically indolent nature—overturning previous laissez-faire attitudes.[14] Consensus

1.4 Biomarkers: D-Dimer and Alpha-Defensin

Serum D-Dimer is a promising adjunctive test for PJI diagnosis and for determining reimplantation timing, but it lacks the specificity of alpha-defensin in settings of recent surgery or inflammatory conditions.[16] Level III

The MSIS/EBJIS survey highlights that alpha-defensin is a cornerstone of the American workup, while European centers rely more heavily on sonication and histopathology.[11,12]

Part II: Pathogenesis and Biofilm – 2025 Scientific Consensus

2.1 Biofilm Mechanics and Persistence

The 2025 ICM has prioritized investigation of extracellular DNA (eDNA) release through autolysis, identified as critical for the stability of Staphylococcus aureus biofilms.[17] Understanding this mechanism is key to developing agents that disrupt the biofilm matrix.

The consensus is also exploring "Viable But Not Culturable" (VBNC) states—where bacteria reduce metabolic activity to survive antibiotic exposure—explaining the high rate of recurrence in "culture-negative" revisions.[18] This validates aggressive surgical protocols (e.g., DAIR with 12 weeks of antibiotics) designed to suppress dormant populations.

2.2 Surface Affinity and Novel Alloys

The 2025 ICM questions probe whether biofilm has different affinities for different implant surfaces and whether novel alloys can resist biofilm formation.[17,18] While silver coatings have been studied, a truly "anti-infective" surface remains elusive. The consensus is investigating whether ultrasonic debridement devices can effectively remove biofilm intraoperatively—a critical question for DAIR viability.

2.3 Host Immunity and Immunogenomics

Emerging research suggests genetic polymorphisms (e.g., in the DEFB1 gene) may predispose individuals to PJI by altering human beta-defensin expression.[19,20] This opens the door to "immunogenomic profiling", where patients could be stratified by genetic risk before a primary implant is placed, allowing personalized prophylactic protocols. There is growing global consensus that elective arthroplasty should be withheld in patients with unoptimized risk factors. North American surgeons are particularly aggressive: 92% of MSIS respondents mandate smoking cessation prior to elective surgery.[11] Level III

3.2 Dental Prophylaxis: The 2024 AAOS Guideline

The AAOS released a dedicated CPG in 2024 addressing hematogenous seeding from the oral cavity.[7] The guideline acknowledges that for high-risk hosts (immunocompromised, history of PJI), the risk-benefit calculus may differ from the general population. CPG

3.3 Intraoperative Environment and Pharmaceuticals

The 2025 ICM is reviewing evidence for Laminar Airflow systems, whose efficacy is increasingly challenged by registry data suggesting no benefit or even harm due to hypothermia or airflow turbulence.[18]

  • Tranexamic Acid (TXA): Under investigation for reducing infection rates by minimizing hematoma formation.[18]
  • Urinary Screening: Re-evaluation of whether asymptomatic bacteriuria actually increases PJI risk—pushing back against routine treatment that drives resistance.[18]

The EBJIS 2025 Position Paper has fundamentally rebranded DAIR as a "curative surgical strategy" for acute infections, provided strict criteria are met.[4,5] Consensus

🚫 The Demise of Arthroscopy for PJI

Both BOA 2024 and ICM 2025 formally classify arthroscopic washout as a "temporizing" measure only—intended to reduce septic load in unstable patients prior to definitive open surgery.[6] Prior ipsilateral knee arthroscopy is now recognized as a distinct risk factor for increased revision rates in subsequent TKA.

4.2 One-Stage vs. Two-Stage Exchange

Eligibility for One-Stage Exchange

Retrospective reviews indicate that only 23% of PJI patients meet strict criteria for one-stage exchange.[23] Level III

Two-Stage Exchange: Refinements

Two-stage remains the gold standard for failed DAIRs, culture-negative cases, fungal infections, and resistant organisms (MRSA, VRE).[22] The 2025 ICM discussions emphasize that reimplantation timing should not rely solely on serological markers (ESR/CRP)—synovial fluid analysis and repeat aspiration prior to the second stage are recommended.[24]

The concept of "1.5 stage" exchange (exchanging the spacer for another spacer) is gaining traction for patients who fail to clear infection after the first spacer.[18]

4.3 Shoulder PJI: A Distinct Entity

The 2025 ICM has carved out specific questions for shoulder PJI, recognizing its unique microbiology (C. acnes dominance) and smaller soft tissue envelope. Key questions address one-stage vs. two-stage indications in subacute shoulder PJI and management of "Unexpected Positive Cultures" (UPC) in revision shoulder arthroplasty.[18] Prior procedures (Latarjet) or fracture sequelae are significant risk factors due to altered anatomy and scarring.[25]

A patient classified as F2-R3-I4 has an unhealed fracture with good potential, ≥3 comorbidities, and requires a free flap—implying a massive limb reconstruction requiring orthoplastic collaboration. The classification is post-operative, incorporating surgical debridement findings for accuracy.

5.2 Diagnostic Criteria and Adoption Challenges

FRI diagnosis relies on Confirmatory Criteria (fistula/sinus tract, purulent drainage, exposed implant, ≥2 positive deep cultures) and Suggestive Criteria (clinical signs, radiological signs, elevated inflammatory markers, persistent wound drainage).[27]

Despite clarity, adoption remains sluggish. A 2024 bibliometric analysis of OTA abstracts found that only 7.7% of presentations used the formal FRI consensus definition.[29] Level III

5.3 Antibiotic Stewardship in FRI: The 6-Week Threshold

New evidence suggests that for FRI treated with appropriate surgical debridement, 6 weeks of antibiotics may be non-inferior to 12 or 24 weeks regarding recurrence rates. Prolonged courses (24 weeks) did not improve cure rates but significantly increased adverse events.[32] Level II

✅ Key Insight: PJI vs. FRI Antibiotic Durations

Retained implants (PJI DAIR): 12 weeks minimum (due to permanent hardware). Fracture hardware (FRI): 6 weeks may suffice (hardware may eventually be removed; fracture debrided and stabilized).

5.4 Global Applicability and LMIC Challenges

In LMICs, the timing of initial antibiotics relative to injury is the critical determinant. Patients receiving antibiotics >24 hours after injury had 2.66× higher odds of infection.[33] While the West debates molecular diagnostics, the global priority remains basic access to early antimicrobial prophylaxis. Part VII: Governance, Economics, and the MDT

7.1 The 2024 BOA Standards: Mandate for MDT

The British Orthopaedic Association released updated Speciality Standards in 2024, mandating Multidisciplinary Team (MDT) involvement for all confirmed PJI cases.[22] Standard of Care

The "Sepsis Six" and Acute Management

For acute presentations, the BOA mandates the immediate initiation of the "Sepsis Six" protocol for systemically unstable patients. Crucially, for non-septic patients, antibiotics must be withheld until deep tissue samples are obtained.[34] This "pause for diagnosis" is critical for stewardship—often overlooked in non-specialist centers.

Definitive Management Standards

Every confirmed PJI case must be discussed in an MDT comprising arthroplasty surgeons, infectious disease physicians, microbiologists, and specialist nurses. Administrative time must be reflected in job plans—shifting the MDT from informal gathering to funded, medicolegal necessity.[22]

7.2 Socioeconomic Impact and Mental Health

PJI carries a mortality rate up to 250% greater than uninfected arthroplasty patients, along with significantly higher incidence of anxiety, depression, and psychotic disorders.[7] This supports the push for MDTs to include psychological support as standard treatment.

Part VIII: Emerging Frontiers and Future Directions

8.1 Phage Therapy

Bacteriophage therapy has moved from fringe interest to central topic. The EBJIS 2025 annual meeting features dedicated sessions, signaling transition toward mainstream clinical trials.[36] With MDR organisms making standard antibiotics obsolete, phages offer targeted "search and destroy" capability for biofilm-embedded bacteria.

8.2 Artificial Intelligence in Diagnosis

The 2025 Parr et al. probability scoring represents the first wave of AI-integrated diagnostics.[13] Future workflows envision dynamic scoring integrated into the electronic medical record (EMR) that updates infection probability in real-time as new lab results become available.

Key Clinical Takeaways

  1. Adopt the MDT Model: The management of bone infection is no longer a solo surgical endeavor—the BOA 2024 standards make multidisciplinary input a standard of care.[22]
  2. Respect the Biofilm: The new 12-week antibiotic requirement for DAIR and the move away from arthroscopic washout reflect deeper respect for biofilm resilience.[4,6]
  3. Stratify the Fracture: Use the F-R-I classification to document all FRI cases for clarity in communication and treatment planning.[26]
  4. Embrace Sensitivity: For culture-negative presentations, the EBJIS definitions offer a safer framework than rigid binary criteria, preventing the disastrous consequences of missing low-grade infections.[2]
  5. Stewardship is Global: In LMICs, the priority remains early antibiotics (<24h post-injury)—not advanced molecular diagnostics.[33]
