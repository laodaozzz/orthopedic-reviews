1. Introduction: The Paradigm Shift in PJI Management

The management of Periprosthetic Joint Infection (PJI) following Total Hip Arthroplasty (THA) has undergone a profound transformation during the quinquennium spanning 2021 to 2026. This period has been characterized not merely by incremental updates to existing protocols, but by a fundamental restructuring of the diagnostic and therapeutic landscape.

The convergence of global expertise through the European Bone and Joint Infection Society (EBJIS), the International Consensus Meeting (ICM), the American Academy of Orthopaedic Surgeons (AAOS), and the Infectious Diseases Society of America (IDSA) has driven a transition from binary, often discordant definitions to nuanced, probabilistic models that acknowledge the biological complexity of implant-associated infections. EBJIS 2021

PJI remains the most challenging complication of total joint arthroplasty (TJA), accounting for a significant proportion of revision surgeries and imposing a devastating physical and psychological burden on patients. The historical reliance on "gold standard" definitions that prioritized specificity often left clinicians grappling with "culture-negative" or "low-grade" infections that failed to meet rigid criteria yet resulted in aseptic failure if untreated. ICM 2018

Key Shift (2021–2026) The era from 2021 onwards has addressed the diagnostic "grey zone" through the validation of the 2021 EBJIS definition and the ratification of the 2025 Unified Consensus Definition, harmonising EBJIS, MSIS, IDSA, and ICM criteria. Level I–II

Furthermore, this period witnessed the rigorous re-evaluation of long-held prophylactic dogmas. The 2024 AAOS Clinical Practice Guideline on dental prophylaxis represents a landmark pivot in orthopaedic stewardship, reversing decades of antibiotic over-prescription based on theoretical rather than empirical risk. Level I

Simultaneously, the 2025 ICM has codified the role of Debridement, Antibiotics, and Implant Retention (DAIR) and One-Stage Exchange arthroplasty, moving these procedures from "salvage" or "controversial" status to defined standard-of-care options for appropriately stratified patients. Level II–III

2. Evolution of Diagnostic Definitions: From Binary to Probability

The accurate diagnosis of PJI is the fulcrum upon which all management decisions rest. Between 2011 and 2018, the field was fragmented by competing definitions from the MSIS, IDSA, and ICM. The 2021–2026 period is defined by the harmonization of these criteria into systems that prioritize clinical utility and sensitivity without sacrificing the specificity required for research.

2.1 The EBJIS 2021 Definition: Recognizing the "Infection Likely"

Published in 2021, the EBJIS definition fundamentally altered the diagnostic philosophy by moving away from a "black and white" (infected vs. not infected) dichotomy to a three-tiered "traffic light" system. This approach was explicitly designed to capture low-grade infections caused by low-virulence organisms (e.g., Cutibacterium acnes, coagulase-negative staphylococci) that frequently evade detection by traditional criteria. 2.2 The 2025 ICM Unified Consensus Definition

The 3rd International Consensus Meeting held in Istanbul in 2025 achieved a historic milestone: the ratification of a Unified Definition endorsed by EBJIS, MSIS, IDSA, and the ICM delegates. This definition sought to merge the weighted scoring precision of the 2018 ICM criteria with the clinically pragmatic "Likely" concept of EBJIS.

Major Criteria (Definitive Diagnosis)

  • Sinus tract communicating with the joint
  • Two or more positive cultures of the same species
  • Gross intraoperative purulence — re-included as major criterion in 2025 (demoted in 2018), reflecting consensus that "pus is pus" with excessively high PPV. Consensus ≥90%

2.3 Key Differences and Harmonization

The harmonization between 2021 EBJIS and 2025 ICM lies in the management of uncertainty. Where EBJIS labels the grey zone "Likely" (implying a bias toward treatment), ICM labels it "Inconclusive" (implying a bias toward further testing). However, clinical practice guidelines from both bodies now align on the principle that probabilistic reasoning must replace binary checklists. A patient with a "Likely/Inconclusive" status should never receive a standard aseptic revision. Level I–II

3. Advanced Diagnostic Biomarkers and Molecular Testing

The refinement of diagnostic definitions has been paralleled by advancements in the biomarkers used to populate these criteria. The 2021–2026 period has seen the transition of D-dimer and Alpha-defensin from novel experimental markers to standard-of-care tools, alongside the cautious adoption of Next-Generation Sequencing (NGS).

3.1 Serum Biomarkers: The D-dimer Revolution

While C-Reactive Protein (CRP) and ESR remain primary screening tests due to high sensitivity and low cost, their lack of specificity has driven the search for better serum markers. Systematic Review

  • D-dimer: Measures fibrin degradation, reflecting the fibrinolytic activity inherent to the inflammatory response to infection. Systematic reviews presented at the 2025 ICM demonstrate comparable sensitivity to CRP but higher specificity for PJI.
  • Age Adjustment: Baseline D-dimer rises with age. The 2025 guidelines suggest adjusting the threshold (e.g., Age × 10 ng/mL in patients >50) or using a higher fixed cutoff (>860 ng/mL) to avoid false positives in the elderly THA population. Expert Consensus
  • Clinical Utility: The combination of normal CRP + normal D-dimer provides a Negative Predictive Value (NPV) approaching 100%, effectively ruling out PJI without aspiration in low-probability cases. Level II

3.2 Synovial Fluid Biomarkers

Synovial fluid analysis remains the "liquid biopsy" of the joint.

  • Alpha-Defensin (ELISA): Strongly endorsed by 2025 ICM and EBJIS. Less susceptible to interference from antibiotic administration than traditional culture. The lateral flow (rapid) test has shown lower sensitivity in meta-analyses and is recommended only when ELISA is unavailable. Level II
  • Leukocyte Esterase (LE): Solidified as a crucial point-of-care adjunct. A "++" or "+++" result correlates strongly with elevated synovial WBC counts. Particularly valuable in "dry tap" situations where only a drop of fluid is retrieved. Level II–III
  • Calprotectin: Emerging data from 2025 suggests synovial calprotectin may offer sensitivity equivalent to alpha-defensin at lower cost, though not yet incorporated into the weighted scoring system. Emerging Evidence

3.3 Molecular Diagnostics: Next-Generation Sequencing (NGS)

Consensus Position (2025 ICM) NGS should not be a routine screening tool. Its extreme sensitivity leads to detection of non-viable DNA or contaminants, complicating interpretation. Consensus ≥85%
  • Indications: Specifically for Culture-Negative PJI (CN-PJI) where there is high pre-test probability of infection (elevated biomarkers, purulence) but traditional cultures are negative.
  • Performance: In CN-PJI, NGS identifies a pathogen in 65–80% of cases compared to <20% for extended cultures. Particularly adept at detecting polymicrobial infections and fastidious organisms (Ureaplasma, Mycoplasma). Level II
  • Interpretation: Results must be correlated with clinical findings by the MDT. Isolation of a skin commensal via NGS without other inflammatory signs may represent contamination rather than true infection.

4. Prevention: The 2024 AAOS Dental Guideline & Host Optimization

Prevention strategies have evolved from broad, defensive medicine to targeted, evidence-based optimisation.

4.1 The 2024 AAOS Dental Guideline: A Paradigm Shift

Critical Recommendation Change (Level I) The 2024 AAOS/ADA Clinical Practice Guideline recommends against the routine use of prophylactic antibiotics for patients with hip or knee implants undergoing dental procedures. Level I — Strong Recommendation
  • The Evidence: Systematic reviews found no association between dental procedures and PJI risk. The magnitude of transient bacteremia induced by daily mastication and oral hygiene (brushing) dwarfs that of a single dental visit.
  • Risk-Benefit Analysis: Harms of prophylaxis — anaphylaxis, C. difficile colitis, antimicrobial resistance promotion — significantly outweigh unproven benefits.
  • Implications: Data suggest up to 78% of prophylaxis prescriptions were inappropriate under previous criteria. Exceptions are now extremely limited, reserved for severely immunocompromised hosts. Level I

4.2 Host Optimization and Surgical Site Management

Glycemic Control

While >8% HbA1c is a widely cited cutoff, the 2025 consensus suggests that perioperative glycemic variability (fructosamine, daily glucose logs) may be as important as static A1c. Elective surgery is generally contraindicated at A1c >8% due to exponential infection risk. Expert Consensus

Prior Ipsilateral Arthroscopy

New Risk Factor (2025 ICM) Patients undergoing TKA within 1 year of knee arthroscopy have significantly elevated PJI risk. Consensus: delay elective arthroplasty 6–12 months following any arthroscopic procedure on the same joint. Level III

Skin Decolonization

Divergence exists between US (MSIS) and European (EBJIS) practices. MSIS members favour universal decolonization (chlorhexidine wipes + nasal mupirocin/iodine), whereas EBJIS members often reserve this for proven S. aureus carriers. The 2025 ICM leans toward universal decolonization due to its low harm profile and potential to reduce MSSA/MRSA PJIs. Level III

5. Surgical Management: Refined Indications and Techniques

The management of established PJI has moved away from the dogma that "two-stage is the only gold standard." The 2021–2026 period has validated DAIR and One-Stage Exchange as primary treatment modalities for specific indications.

5.1 Debridement, Antibiotics, and Implant Retention (DAIR)

Success relies on intervening before biofilm maturity. Level II–III

  • Early Postoperative PJI: Within 4 weeks of index surgery
  • Late Acute (Hematogenous): Symptoms present for <3–4 weeks in a previously well-functioning joint
Time-Critical Threshold DAIR attempted >4 weeks after symptom onset has failure rates exceeding 50% and should be avoided. Level II

Modular Component Exchange

The 2025 consensus reaffirms the necessity of exchanging modular components (polyethylene liners, femoral heads). This allows access to the posterior capsule for debridement and removes biofilm-colonised surfaces. Consensus ≥90%

Antimicrobial Duration

A major shift: recommendation now extends to a 12-week minimum duration (IV + Oral), utilising biofilm-active agents (rifampin-combinations for staphylococci, fluoroquinolones for gram-negatives). This extended duration is associated with higher retention rates compared to older 6-week protocols. Level II

5.2 One-Stage Exchange Arthroplasty

Once controversial, now endorsed by 2025 ICM for non-immunocompromised patients provided strict criteria are met. Level II

Selection Criteria (The "Healthy" Host)

  • Patient: McPherson Host Class A (uncompromised) or B (mildly compromised); no systemic sepsis
  • Local Tissues: Good soft tissue envelope, no sinus tract, adequate bone stock
  • Microbiology: Organism identified preoperatively and susceptible to oral antibiotics with high bioavailability. Culture-negative PJI = absolute contraindication

Meta-analyses at the 2025 ICM indicate reinfection rates equivalent to two-stage exchange (~5–10%), with significantly better functional outcomes and lower healthcare costs.

5.3 Two-Stage Exchange Arthroplasty

Remains the workhorse for "complex" PJI: culture-negative cases, resistant organisms (MRSA, VRE, fungi), and patients with poor soft tissues (sinus tracts). Level II

  • The Interval: Moving from fixed intervals (3 months) to biology-driven timing. Reimplantation considered when soft tissue envelope has healed and inflammatory markers (CRP, ESR) show downward trend.
  • Reimplantation Testing: Synovial fluid analysis (WBC/PMN) from the spacer joint prior to reimplantation. Rotational thromboelastometry (ROTEM) and synovial D-dimer are emerging as potential markers. Emerging / Level IV
  • Spacer Holiday: Of debated utility; many experts now advocate reimplantation without a prolonged antibiotic holiday if markers are reassuring.

6. Medical Management: Antibiotics and Biofilm

Medical management has been refined by the acceptance of the OVIVA trial principles and a deeper understanding of biofilm pathophysiology.

6.1 Oral vs. Intravenous Therapy

OVIVA Trial Principle (Level I) Oral antibiotic therapy is non-inferior to IV therapy for bone and joint infections, provided the oral agent has high bioavailability (fluoroquinolones, linezolid, rifampin, doxycycline). Typical protocol: 7 days IV perioperative → immediate switch to oral. Level I — RCT

6.2 Pathogen-Specific Protocols

  • Staphylococci (Biofilm-Active): Rifampin remains the cornerstone in the presence of implants (DAIR, One-Stage). Must never be used as monotherapy. Combination with fluoroquinolone (e.g., levofloxacin) or cotrimoxazole. Level I–II
  • Cutibacterium acnes: Requires extended cultures (14 days). Penicillin G or Ceftriaxone → oral Amoxicillin or Clindamycin. The utility of rifampin for C. acnes in DAIR settings remains debated (weak consensus supporting use). Expert Consensus
  • Fungal PJI: Catastrophic diagnosis. Strongly favour Two-Stage exchange. Prolonged antifungal therapy (minimum 6 months) with azoles or echinocandins. Level III–IV

7. Future Directions

Machine Learning (ML) Diagnostics

Several groups have validated ML nomograms integrating serum and synovial markers (CRP, D-dimer, WBC, Alpha-defensin) to predict PJI probability. These algorithms consistently outperform the manual application of ICM criteria, reducing the rate of "inconclusive" diagnoses. Future guidelines may see replacement of scoring sheets with app-based algorithmic calculators. Level II — Validation Studies

Bacteriophage Therapy

With the rise of antimicrobial resistance (AMR), phage therapy has entered the clinical conversation. The 2025 ICM included dedicated sessions on phage therapy for "intractable" biofilm infections. Currently reserved for compassionate use in salvage cases, early case series suggest potential in eradicating biofilm where antibiotics fail. Level V — Case Series

8. Conclusion & Key Takeaways

Five Pillars of Modern PJI Management (2026)
  1. Adopt the Unified Definition: Use the 2025 ICM/EBJIS scoring system. Treat "Inconclusive/Likely" scores with extreme caution — do not revise aseptically without further investigation.
  2. Abandon Dental Prophylaxis: Follow the 2024 AAOS guideline. Stop prescribing antibiotics for dental procedures in TJA patients.
  3. Optimise the Host: Delay surgery for HbA1c >8%; enforce smoking cessation. Wait 6–12 months after ipsilateral arthroscopy before performing TJA.
  4. Targeted Surgery: DAIR for acute, well-fixed implants with susceptible organisms (12 weeks antibiotics). One-Stage for healthy hosts with known organisms. Two-Stage for complex/resistant cases.
  5. Trust Oral Antibiotics: Transition to bioavailable oral agents early (OVIVA principle) to improve quality of life without compromising cure rates.
