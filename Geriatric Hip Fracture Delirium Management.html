Postoperative Delirium in Geriatric Hip Fracture Patients

Comprehensive Clinical Review — Risk, Prevention & Management  |  Evidence-Based Educational Resource

Executive Summary

Postoperative delirium (POD) is the most frequent and consequential complication following surgical repair of hip fractures in the geriatric population. Incidence ranges from 20% to 65%[1], and each episode is an independent predictor of increased mortality, accelerated functional decline, persistent cognitive impairment, higher rates of institutionalization, and substantially increased healthcare costs.

Pathophysiology is multifactorial: neuroinflammation, neurotransmitter dysregulation (acetylcholine/dopamine), and cerebral metabolic insufficiency converge on the vulnerable aging brain[1]. The single most potent predisposing factor is pre-existing cognitive impairment (OR 3.21)[8].

The evidence mandates a paradigm shift from reactive treatment to proactive prevention. Multi-component non-pharmacological bundles (e.g., the Hospital Elder Life Program [HELP]) reduce delirium incidence by up to 40%[11]. In contrast, pharmacological prophylaxis with antipsychotics[15] or melatonin[14] has not demonstrated efficacy. Management of established delirium rests on urgent identification of underlying causes and intensive non-pharmacological supportive care.

1. Defining the Syndrome

Postoperative delirium is an acute neuropsychiatric syndrome defined by an acute onset and fluctuating course of disturbed consciousness, attention, perception, and cognitionNot specified. It typically develops within the first five days following surgery, peaking at 24–48 hours[1].

Core diagnostic features include: (1) a rapid decline from baseline neurocognitive function, (2) a primary deficit in attention, and (3) a change in awareness or level of consciousness[3].

Motoric Subtypes Not specified

The clinical presentation is heterogeneous, broadly classified into three subtypes with significant diagnostic and prognostic implications[3]:

  • Hyperactive Restlessness, agitation, excitability, hallucinations. Most conspicuous but not the most common subtype[21].
  • Hypoactive Lethargy, inactivity, reduced motor activity, daytime sleepiness. Most prevalent in older adults yet frequently missed — often misattributed to fatigue or depression[21]. Associated with equally poor or worse outcomes.
  • Mixed Fluctuation between hyper- and hypoactive features[3].
Critical Diagnostic Gap: Hypoactive delirium is the most prevalent subtype but is under-recognized. Routine, standardized screening with the CAM is essential as a patient safety measure — up to 60% of delirium cases go undetected[30].

Delirium vs. Dementia

While dementia is chronic and progressive, delirium is acute and typically reversible. However, they are closely linked: pre-existing dementia is the greatest risk factor for POD, and an episode of delirium can accelerate the progression of underlying dementia or unmask previously undiagnosed cognitive impairment[1].

2. Pathophysiology: Multifactorial Insult to the Aging Brain

POD arises when a vulnerable brain — compromised by age-related changes and pre-existing pathology — is subjected to noxious perioperative insult[1]. Delirium is not a primary brain disease but the brain's acute failure in response to systemic physiological stress.

Leading Hypotheses

a) Neuroinflammation Hypothesis Not specified

The trauma of the hip fracture and surgery trigger a potent systemic inflammatory response. Peripheral cytokines and neutrophils disrupt the blood-brain barrier, activate microglia and astrocytes within the CNS, leading to synaptic remodeling and neuronal dysfunction[1]. Clinical evidence: an elevated preoperative neutrophil-to-lymphocyte ratio (NLR) is associated with increased risk and duration of POD[6].

b) Neurotransmitter Imbalance Hypothesis Not specified

Delirium represents a "final common pathway" of disruption in critical neurotransmitter systems[7]. The most implicated is acetylcholine — relative cholinergic deficiency is strongly associated with delirium (explains why anticholinergic drugs are iatrogenic causes). Concurrent dopamine excess contributes to agitation, forming the rationale for antipsychotic use. Serotonin, norepinephrine, and GABA also play roles[1].

c) Cerebral Metabolic Insufficiency Hypothesis

Perioperative stressors (hypoxemia, hypotension, hypoglycemia) lead to decreased cerebral metabolites and an imbalance between oxygen supply and demand, impairing higher cortical function[2].

Integrative Model: These hypotheses are not mutually exclusive. Systemic inflammation can directly induce neurotransmitter changes and impair cerebral metabolism, creating a vicious cycle that culminates in delirium.

3. Epidemiology & Impact

POD incidence ranges from 16% to 65%[1]. Consequences extend far beyond the immediate postoperative period:

  • Increased Mortality: Independent predictor at 30 days, 90 days, 1 year, and 2 years. Adjusted hazard ratio of 1.62 for long-term all-cause mortality; each additional day of delirium further increases the hazard[25].
  • Prolonged Hospitalization & Cost: One of the most significant predictors of increased length of stay, often adding several days[1].
  • Functional & Cognitive Decline: Poorer rehabilitation outcomes; less likely to regain pre-fracture mobility. May mark the beginning of permanent cognitive decline[1].
  • Increased Complications & Institutionalization: Higher risk of in-patient falls, pressure ulcers, pneumonia, and UTIs. Far more likely to be discharged to a nursing home[2, 26].

4. Diagnosis: The Confusion Assessment Method (CAM)

The CAM is the global "gold standard" for bedside detection of delirium[32]. Sensitivity 94–100%, specificity 90–95%[30]. It is a validated diagnostic algorithm, not merely a screening tool — a positive CAM confirms the diagnosis with high certainty, warranting an immediate "delirium workup."

Table 1: The Confusion Assessment Method (CAM) Diagnostic Algorithm
FeatureAssessment Method
1. Acute Onset & Fluctuating Course Established by interviewing family/nurse: "Is there evidence of an acute change in mental status from baseline?" AND "Did this abnormal behavior fluctuate during the day?"
2. Inattention "Did the patient have difficulty focusing attention (e.g., easily distracted, difficulty tracking conversation, unable to count backward from 20 by 2s)?"
3. Disorganized Thinking "Was the patient's thinking disorganized or incoherent (e.g., rambling conversation, unclear flow of ideas, unpredictable subject switching)?"
4. Altered Level of Consciousness "Overall, how would you rate this patient's level of consciousness?" Options: Alert (normal), Vigilant (hyperalert), Lethargic (drowsy), Stupor, Coma. Any answer other than "Alert."
Diagnostic Rule: Features 1 AND 2, plus EITHER 3 OR 4 → Delirium

Adapted from Inouye et al. (1990); references [30, 32, 33].

Clinical Variants

  • CAM-ICU: For critically ill/non-verbal patients (e.g., mechanically ventilated). Uses non-verbal assessments[30].
  • FAM-CAM (Family CAM): Informant-based tool for establishing baseline cognitive function and detecting delirium when direct assessment is difficult[33, 35].
Best Practice: All at-risk older adults with hip fractures should be assessed daily using the CAM[6]. A positive result is the beginning of a thorough investigation, not the end of the assessment[30].

5. Risk Stratification: The Two-Hit Model

POD develops through the interplay between baseline vulnerability ("first hit") and precipitating insults ("second hit"). Predisposing factors lower the threshold; peri-/post-operative stressors overwhelm the brain's coping capacity. This mandates a two-pronged prevention strategy: mitigate vulnerabilities on admission and minimize insults throughout hospitalization.

Intra-Operative Factors

  • Anesthesia type: Multiple large RCTs and systematic reviews found no significant difference in POD incidence between general vs. spinal anesthesia[14]. I
  • Benzodiazepines: Strongly associated with POD; should be avoided[14].
  • Operative duration & blood loss: Potential risk factors[38].

Post-Operative Triggers

  • Prolonged pre-op waiting (>48 h): A powerful, modifiable systems-level risk factor. "Fast-tracking" to surgery within 48 h is a critical prevention strategy[46].
  • Uncontrolled pain: A major trigger. Paradoxically, undertreated pain (avoiding opioids entirely) carries greater delirium risk than judicious multimodal analgesia[47].
  • Acute complications: Infections (UTI, pneumonia), electrolyte imbalances, dehydration, hypoxia, constipation, urinary retention[14].
  • ICU stay ≥2 days: Strong association[4].

6. Proactive Prevention: The Cornerstone of Care

The evidence mandates a fundamental paradigm shift: from reactive treatment to proactive, systematic prevention. Up to 40% of delirium cases are preventable[2]. The success of non-pharmacological bundles versus the failure of prophylactic medications reinforces that delirium is a syndrome of system failure requiring optimization of the entire system of care.

Multi-Component Non-Pharmacological Bundles I

Systematic reviews and meta-analyses show bundled interventions reduce delirium incidence by approximately 40%[12]. Core components:

  • Orientation & Cognitive Stimulation: Frequent reorientation; clocks, calendars, reminiscence therapy[12].
  • Sleep Enhancement: Minimize noise/light; schedule procedures to avoid sleep disruption; avoid deliriogenic sedatives[11].
  • Early Mobilization: Active range-of-motion exercises progressing to ambulation as soon as surgically permissible[11].
  • Sensory Support: Ensure patients have and use personal eyeglasses and hearing aids[18].
  • Hydration & Nutrition: Encourage oral fluids; monitor for dehydration; IV fluids if necessary[18].
  • Medication Review: Minimize polypharmacy; discontinue non-essential deliriogenic drugs (anticholinergics, benzodiazepines)[3].

The Hospital Elder Life Program (HELP) II

HELP is the most well-studied and widely implemented evidence-based model[54]. A landmark controlled clinical trial demonstrated a 40% reduction in delirium incidence and was cost-neutral or cost-saving[13].

HELP combines an interdisciplinary team (Elder Life Nurse Specialist, Elder Life Specialist) with trained community volunteers to deliver standardized protocols:

  • Daily visits for companionship and cognitively stimulating activities
  • Early mobilization assistance
  • Feeding and meal assistance
  • Non-pharmacological sleep protocol
  • Hearing/vision protocol

Pharmacological Prophylaxis I — Consistent Failure

No pharmacological agent has demonstrated efficacy for delirium prevention. Current guidelines do not recommend routine pharmacological prophylaxis[17].
Summary of Pharmacological Prophylaxis Evidence
AgentStudy DesignKey FindingRef
Haloperidol (1.5 mg/d preop) RCT, n >400 No significant difference in delirium incidence (15.1% vs. 16.5% placebo). Reduced severity/duration if delirium occurred, but failed primary prevention goal. [15]
Melatonin (3 mg × 5 nights) Multicenter RCT, n ≈400 Did not reduce delirium incidence (29.6% vs. 25.5% placebo). [14, 16]
Cholinesterase Inhibitors (donepezil, rivastigmine) Systematic reviews Insufficient evidence for prevention or treatment. Safety concerns: one rivastigmine trial stopped early for increased mortality signal. [24]
Dexmedetomidine Limited evidence Some promise in ICU settings; not yet recommended for routine hip fracture prophylaxis. [45]

7. Management of Established POD

Once diagnosed, delirium must be treated as a medical emergency[7]. Two primary principles: (1) urgent identification and reversal of underlying causes, and (2) intensive non-pharmacological supportive care.

Identify and Treat Underlying Causes

A systematic search for precipitating factors is essential. The mnemonic I WATCH DEATH[65] provides a structured approach:

I — Infection (pneumonia, UTI, sepsis)
W — Withdrawal (alcohol, benzodiazepines)
A — Acute Metabolic (electrolytes, renal/liver failure)
T — Trauma (hip fracture, head injury)
C — CNS Pathology (stroke, hemorrhage)
H — Hypoxia (heart failure, PE, pneumonia)
D — Deficiencies (thiamine, B12)
E — Endocrinopathies (glucose, thyroid)
A — Acute Vascular (shock, hypertensive encephalopathy)
T — Toxins / Drugs (medication review)
H — Heavy Metals

Workup should include: history, physical exam, vital signs, pulse oximetry, medication review, CBC, CMP, urinalysis, and cultures as indicated[66].

Non-Pharmacological Supportive Care

  • Environmental Modifications: Well-lit during day, quiet/dark at night. Clocks, calendars, familiar objects. Avoid room changes[18].
  • De-escalation: Calm approach, simple language, reassurance. Use the T-A-DA framework: Tolerate non-dangerous behaviors, Anticipate needs, Don't Agitate[68].
  • Family Engagement: Familiar presence is profoundly reassuring. Educate families about delirium[17].
  • Minimize Restraints: Physical restraints increase agitation, prolong delirium, and cause complications. Use only as last resort for imminent harm risk[17, 19].

Pharmacotherapy for Severe Agitation

Medications do not treat the underlying pathophysiology of delirium. Use only for severe agitation/psychosis posing safety risk, after non-pharmacological strategies have failed[17]. No FDA-approved medications exist for delirium treatment[19]. Benzodiazepines are contraindicated (except in alcohol/benzo withdrawal).
Table 3: Pharmacological Management of Severe Agitation in Delirium
Agent ClassDrugStarting Dose (PO)Key Considerations
Atypical Risperidone 0.5 mg once or twice daily Fewer EPS than haloperidol. Monitor for EPS, orthostasis.
Olanzapine 2.5 mg once daily (bedtime) Can be sedating. Monitor for orthostasis, anticholinergic effects.

All antipsychotics carry an FDA black-box warning for increased mortality in older adults with dementia-related psychosis[19]. Start low, go slow; use lowest effective dose for shortest duration[64].

8. Prognosis & Long-Term Outcomes

An episode of POD is a watershed event marking a point of irreversible decline for many patients[5]:

  • Mortality: Large cohort study (n >270,000): POD patients had 62% higher adjusted hazard of long-term death. 5-year survival: 81.7% (delirium) vs. 89.8% (no delirium)[25].
  • Cognitive Decline: Accelerated rate of long-term cognitive decline; significantly increased risk of dementia diagnosis post-surgery[1, 2].
  • Functional Decline: Poorer functional recovery; less likely to regain pre-fracture mobility. Much higher likelihood of nursing home discharge[3, 26].
  • Healthcare Utilization: Higher readmission rates; increased long-term costs[2].
Care Gap: Current models focus almost exclusively on acute hospitalization. The profound lasting effects necessitate post-discharge follow-up, cognitive monitoring, and caregiver support.

9. Landmark Studies Summary

The source document references numerous studies. The following are key studies with the highest evidence levels that directly inform clinical practice:

Randomized Controlled Trial — Level I
Haloperidol Prophylaxis for Elderly Hip-Surgery Patients at Risk for Delirium

Double-blind, placebo-controlled RCT, n >400. Low-dose haloperidol (1.5 mg/d) started preoperatively did not significantly reduce delirium incidence (15.1% vs. 16.5%). Did reduce severity and duration if delirium occurred.

Ref [15] — Egberts et al. (2005)
Randomized Controlled Trial — Level I
Effect of Melatonin on Incidence of Delirium Among Patients with Hip Fracture

Multicenter, double-blind RCT, n ≈400. Melatonin 3 mg nightly for 5 days did not reduce delirium incidence (29.6% vs. 25.5% placebo).

Ref [16] — de Jonghe et al. (2014), CMAJ
Retrospective Cohort Study — Level III
Incidence and Risk Factors of POD Following Hip Fracture Surgery: Nationwide Retrospective Cohort in Taiwan

Large national dataset. Identified cognitive impairment (aHR 2.63), advanced age (aHR 3.52 for ≥95 years), and prolonged ICU stay (aHR 2.85) as key risk factors.

Ref [4]
Cohort Study — Level II–III
Long-Term Mortality Impact of Postoperative Hyperactive Delirium

Over 270,000 hip fracture patients. Adjusted HR 1.62 for long-term all-cause mortality. Each additional day of delirium further increased mortality hazard. 5-year survival significantly lower with delirium.

Ref [25]
Systematic Review & Meta-Analysis — Level I
Non-Pharmacological Interventions for Preventing Delirium in Hospitalized Non-ICU Patients

Cochrane review demonstrating that multi-component, non-pharmacological intervention bundles reduce delirium incidence by approximately 40%.

10. Clinical Recommendations

Based on the comprehensive evidence review, the following actionable recommendations are proposed:

  1. Universal Risk Screening on Admission: All geriatric hip fracture patients should undergo formal risk assessment, prioritizing detection of pre-existing cognitive impairment with a validated tool.
  2. Standardize Delirium Diagnosis with the CAM: Mandate daily screening throughout hospitalization. Train all frontline staff to ensure reliable use and combat under-recognition of hypoactive delirium.
  3. Adopt Multi-Component Prevention Bundles: Implement evidence-based non-pharmacological programs (e.g., HELP) as the standard of care — orientation, sleep hygiene, early mobilization, sensory support, hydration.
  4. Optimize Peri-operative Pathways: "Fast-track" to surgery within 48 hours. Establish orthogeriatric co-management models providing comprehensive interdisciplinary care from admission through discharge.
  5. Promote Pharmacological Stewardship: Medications do not treat delirium. Reserve antipsychotics for severe agitation posing safety risk. Use low-dose atypical agents; strictly avoid benzodiazepines.
  6. Develop Post-Discharge Pathways: Communicate delirium episodes to primary care providers. Recommend follow-up cognitive/functional assessment and caregiver support.
