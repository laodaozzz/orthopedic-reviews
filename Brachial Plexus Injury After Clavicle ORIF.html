Iatrogenic Brachial Plexus Injury Following Clavicle Fracture Fixation

Mechanisms, Diagnosis, and Management — Evidence-Based Educational Summary

1. Introduction: The Evolving Landscape of Clavicle Fracture Management

The management of clavicle fractures has undergone a significant paradigm shift over the past two decades. Historically viewed as a forgiving bone, the vast majority of midshaft fractures were managed conservatively with a simple sling or figure-of-eight bandage. Prevailing orthopedic wisdom suggested that malunion was functionally well-tolerated and nonunion rare. However, contemporary literature has challenged these axioms, revealing higher rates of nonunion, symptomatic malunion, and functional deficits in specific patient subsets treated non-operatively. Evidence Level II

Consequently, the incidence of operative fixation—specifically Open Reduction and Internal Fixation (ORIF) with plating—has risen exponentially. This surge has introduced a parallel increase in iatrogenic complications. While infection and hardware failure are commonly discussed, iatrogenic injury to the brachial plexus and adjacent subclavian vessels represents the most feared and potentially devastating complication of clavicle surgery.

⚠ Critical Clinical Pearl

The anatomical intimacy between the clavicle, first rib, and the neurovascular bundle creates an unforgiving surgical field where millimeter-level errors in drill trajectory or screw length can result in permanent neurological deficit or life-threatening hemorrhage.[1]

1.1 The Clinical Imperative

While the overall incidence is reported as 0.2% to 1.5% in varying series[1], the "black swan" nature of these events means they are statistically rare but clinically ruinous. A meta-analysis of 582 cases reported zero vascular complications[4], illustrating the difficulty of capturing these rare events. Yet, case reports and registry data persistently document instances of brachial plexopathy, subclavian artery pseudoaneurysms, and arteriovenous fistulas[5], suggesting that the true incidence may be obscured by underreporting or the delayed nature of some presentations.

2. Surgical Anatomy and "Safe Zones"

To understand the genesis of iatrogenic brachial plexus injury (BPI), one must possess a granular understanding of the costoclavicular space. The clavicle acts as a protective strut for the neurovascular bundle as it exits the thorax and enters the axilla. This relationship is not static—it changes dynamically with patient positioning, fracture displacement, and surgical manipulation.

2.1 The Costoclavicular Interval

The brachial plexus, subclavian artery, and subclavian vein traverse the narrow interval between the inferior surface of the clavicle and the superior surface of the first rib.[6]

  • The Vein: Most anterior structure, often lying directly against the posterior cortex of the medial clavicle. Tethering to the costoclavicular ligaments prevents it from moving away from a plunging drill bit.[6]
  • The Artery: Lies posterior to the vein, separated by the anterior scalene muscle medially but becoming closer in the midshaft.
  • The Plexus: Trunks and cords lie posteroinferior to the clavicle in the midshaft—the most common site of fracture and fixation.[6]

2.3 Impact of Patient Positioning

  • Supine Position: Neutral position relative to the thorax.
  • Beach Chair Position: Gravity pulls the arm and shoulder girdle inferiorly, potentially shifting neurovascular structures closer to the clavicle. The second medial screw hole has been identified as the "riskiest" trajectory.[10] Evidence Level III
  • Shoulder Manipulation: Abduction and external rotation can rotate the clavicle posteriorly, bringing the neurovascular bundle into closer contact with the posterior cortex—relevant in delayed presentations of screw impingement.[4]

3. Mechanisms of Iatrogenic Injury

The mechanisms of BPI in clavicle surgery are multifactorial, categorized broadly into direct penetrating trauma, indirect traction injuries, and compression phenomena.

3.1 Direct Penetrating Trauma

3.1.1 The "Plunging" Drill

The most acute mechanism of injury is inadvertent penetration of the drill bit beyond the far cortex. The clavicle's dense cortical bone requires significant force to penetrate; once breached, the sudden loss of resistance can cause the drill to accelerate forward into soft tissues.[9] Evidence Level IV

  • Anterior-to-Posterior (AP) trajectory: Used in anterior plating. Places the drill directly in line with the neurovascular bundle (posterior). High risk of plunging into the plexus or vessels.[9]
  • Superior-to-Inferior (SI) trajectory: Used in superior plating. Aims towards the first rib or pleura. The brachial plexus (posterior) is theoretically safer, provided the drill does not skid posteriorly.[11]
📌 Anatomical Variance Warning

In some patients, the neurovascular bundle is located more cephalad than usual, bringing it directly under the clavicle and negating the safety of the SI trajectory.[12]

3.1.2 Screw Impingement and the "Saw" Effect

A screw that is too long can cause injury through two mechanisms:[1]

  • Immediate: A screw tip protruding into a nerve can cause immediate paresthesia or motor loss (neurotmesis/axonotmesis).
  • Chronic ("Saw Effect"): A screw tip resting adjacent to the pulsating subclavian artery or moving brachial plexus acts as an abrasive surface. Over months/years of shoulder motion, this friction erodes the arterial wall (leading to pseudoaneurysm) or nerve sheath (chronic neuritis).[1]

Protrusion Threshold: Literature suggests screw prominence should be limited to <4 mm. Screws longer than 16–18 mm in the middle third are statistically more likely to endanger neurovascular structures.[9]

3.2 Traction and Positioning Injury

3.2.1 Surgical Retraction

Vigorous retraction of the clavipectoral fascia or bone levers placed blindly behind the clavicle can exert tensile stress on the plexus. A nerve already compromised by initial trauma is more susceptible to secondary ischemic injury—the "double crush" syndrome.[1]

3.2.2 Fracture Reduction and Lengthening

In chronic shortening (malunion or nonunion), the neurovascular bundle may have adaptively shortened or become encased in fibrosis. Acute intraoperative lengthening stretches these shortened nerves, leading to traction neurapraxia.[4]

3.3 Indirect Compression (Space-Occupying Effect)

Plate and screw implantation inherently reduces costoclavicular space volume. Rigid fixation eliminates physiologic clavicular micromotion, potentially sandwiching the plexus between hardware and the first rib.[15] Hypertrophic callus formation around the fracture site, combined with hardware, can critically narrow the thoracic outlet.[16]

4. Temporal Classification of Injury

Differentiating between immediate perioperative deficits and those that develop weeks, months, or years later is the cornerstone of accurate diagnosis and management.

Immediate (0–48 hrs) Direct trauma, traction, block effect, hematoma
Delayed (2 wks – 6 mo) Hypertrophic callus, scar tethering
Late (>1 year) Pseudoaneurysm, AV fistula, screw migration

4.1 Immediate Onset (Perioperative)

Symptoms present immediately upon awakening or within the first 24–48 hours. Evidence Level IV

Etiologies: (1) Direct laceration by drill or screw; (2) Traction neurapraxia during positioning/reduction; (3) Regional anesthesia block effect; (4) Acute hematoma.

Clinical Differentiation: Block vs. Injury

A successful regional block typically induces sensory and motor deficits that resolve within 12–24 hours. A deficit that persists beyond 24 hours, or involves nerves not typically covered by the block, raises suspicion of injury. Patients with nerve laceration often report severe, burning neuropathic pain distinct from somatic surgical pain.[18]

4.2 Delayed Onset (Weeks to Months)

Primary Etiology: Hypertrophic Callus — In delayed union/nonunion, abundant callus grows inferiorly into the costoclavicular space. Symptoms typically appear 2 to 6 months post-surgery, mimicking Neurogenic Thoracic Outlet Syndrome (nTOS): progressive C8-T1 paresthesias, worsening pain with arm elevation, and eventual motor weakness.[15,16] Evidence Level V

Secondary Etiology: Scar/Fibrosis — Post-surgical scarring tethers the plexus to the clavicle or subclavius muscle, leading to traction neuritis at 3–6 months post-op.[13]

4.3 Late Onset (Years)

Primary Etiology: Vascular Pseudoaneurysm — A medial screw tip protruding slightly (<3–4 mm) physically abrades the subclavian artery wall with every heartbeat, weakening the adventitia over years. Average presentation: 4.9 years (range up to 10 years).[4] Clinical picture includes a pulsatile supraclavicular mass, audible bruit, distal embolization (finger ischemia), and secondary brachial plexopathy.[5]

5. Incidence Rates and Epidemiology

Quantifying the exact incidence is challenging due to heterogeneity of reporting.[1]

  • General Incidence: Clinically significant neurovascular injury estimated between 0.2% and 1.5%.[1] Evidence Level II
  • Meta-Analysis Data: A meta-analysis of 582 cases found 0% vascular complications, highlighting the rarity and need for large sample sizes.[4]

Risk Stratification

  • Fracture Type: Comminuted midshaft fractures and medial third fractures carry higher risk.
  • Chronicity: Delayed fixation (>4 weeks) and nonunion repair have significantly higher complication rates due to fibrosis and anatomical distortion.[1]
  • Gender: Female patients, having smaller clavicular dimensions on average, may have reduced safety margins.[6]

6. Diagnostic Algorithms and Workup

6.1 Clinical Evaluation

1
Motor Mapping: Assess deltoid (axillary n.), biceps (musculocutaneous n.), triceps (radial n.), hand intrinsics (ulnar/median n.).[21]
2
Sensory Mapping: Differentiate dermatomal loss (root level) from peripheral nerve distributions.
3
Vascular Exam: Palpate radial pulses, compare bilateral blood pressure, auscultate for bruits in the supraclavicular fossa.[21]
4
Provocative Tests: Tinel's sign over the supraclavicular fossa. Adson's test or Wright's maneuver for positional compression.[23]

6.2 Imaging Modalities

  • Plain Radiography: Initial screening. Assess fracture reduction, hardware position, screw tip extension, exuberant callus, "butterfly" fragments.[15] Standard practice
  • CT & CT Angiography (CTA): Gold standard for bony and vascular anatomy. Accurately measures screw protrusion and callus volume. CTA mandatory if vascular compromise is suspected.[1] Evidence Level II
  • MRI with MARS: Essential for direct nerve visualization. Shows nerve edema (T2 hyperintensity), discontinuity, or encasement by scar/callus. Differentiates extrinsic compression from intrinsic injury.[2]
  • Ultrasound: Dynamic assessment, hematoma/mass identification, diaphragm movement assessment for phrenic nerve injury.[19]

6.3 Electrodiagnostic Studies (EMG/NCS)

⏱ Timing Is Critical
  • <3 weeks: Limited utility. Wallerian degeneration takes 3–4 weeks to manifest.
  • 3–4 weeks: Optimal time. Detects fibrillation potentials and positive sharp waves, confirming axonal loss. Differentiates neurapraxia from axonotmesis/neurotmesis.[25] Evidence Level II
  • 3–6 months follow-up: Monitors for reinnervation (nascent motor unit potentials). No reinnervation by 3–6 months in proximal muscles → surgical exploration indicated.[25]

7. Evidence-Based Management Algorithms

7.1 Conservative Management

Indications:[28,29]

  • Immediate postoperative deficits suspected to be neurapraxia (traction/positioning)
  • Partial deficits with no structural impingement on imaging
  • EMG evidence of conduction block with preserved axons

Protocol:[29]

1
Observation: "Watchful waiting" — many traction injuries resolve spontaneously as nerve edema subsides.
2
Pharmacotherapy: Neuropathic pain modulators (Gabapentin, Pregabalin, Amitriptyline) started early.[28]
3
Rehabilitation: Phase I (0–4 weeks): gentle PROM to prevent stiffness; avoid traction on the arm. Monthly clinical exams and EMG at 3 months if no recovery.[29]
Outcome Note

Conservative management yields good results for traction injuries with recovery often within 3–6 months. However, it is ineffective for mechanical compression (callus/hardware).[30] Evidence Level V

7.2 Surgical Management

Indications for Exploration:[17]

  • Structural impingement confirmed on imaging (screw, plate, or bone fragment)
  • Vascular injury (pseudoaneurysm, AVF, expanding hematoma)
  • No clinical or electrical recovery by 3–6 months
  • Progressive deficit (e.g., delayed callus compression)
  • Intractable neuropathic pain unresponsive to medication

Surgical Techniques:

  • Hardware Removal: Often the first step. Removing rigid plate allows clavicle remodeling and soft tissue re-expansion.
  • Callus Resection (Decompression): For delayed palsy, offending bone mass is excised, decompressing the costoclavicular space. May require re-stabilization.[15]
  • External Neurolysis: Nerve dissected free from scar, callus, and adhesions to restore gliding ability.[4]
  • Vascular Repair: Pseudoaneurysm treatment involves resection and interposition grafting (saphenous vein) or endovascular stenting. Requires multidisciplinary vascular surgery.[5]
  • Nerve Reconstruction: For neurotmesis/avulsion: primary repair, nerve grafting (sural nerve), or nerve transfers (e.g., Oberlin transfer for biceps).[27]

8. Prevention and Technical Pearls

8.1 Preoperative Planning

  • Identify high-risk cases: Nonunions, malunions, and medial third fractures carry the highest risk.[1]
  • Advanced imaging: Consider 3D CT for complex deformities to map vessel trajectory relative to bone fragments.

8.2 Intraoperative Technique

  • Positioning: Beach chair position may shift the neurovascular bundle closer to the clavicle. Avoid excessive traction on the arm.[10]
  • Drilling Safety:[9]
    • Use a drill stop
    • Drill the near cortex only; hand-tap the far cortex to feel the exit
    • Avoid "plunging" by bracing the drill hand against the patient's chest wall
    • Use oscillating drill mode rather than forward mode near the far cortex
  • Screw Selection:[9]
    • Measure carefully; subtract 1–2 mm from depth gauge reading (countersinking effect)
    • Aim for flush bicortical fixation
    • Avoid screws >16–18 mm in the middle third unless safety confirmed
  • Plating Choice:[11,12]
    • Superior plating (SI trajectory): Generally safer for the plexus (posterior); risks the vein (inferior)
    • Anterior plating (AP trajectory): Directs drill towards plexus and vessels; requires extreme caution
  • Dissection: Strictly subperiosteal. The periosteum acts as a barrier. In nonunions, peel carefully to avoid traction on adherent nerves.[1]

9. Conclusion

Iatrogenic brachial plexus injury following clavicle fracture fixation is a rare but potentially catastrophic complication that demands a high index of suspicion. The transition from conservative to operative management has necessitated a deeper appreciation of the perilous anatomy of the costoclavicular space. While immediate injuries often result from direct mechanical trauma or traction, the surgeon must remain vigilant for the insidious onset of delayed plexopathy caused by hypertrophic callus or vascular pseudoaneurysms.

Key Takeaways
  • Successful management hinges on early recognition
  • Differentiate between transient neurapraxia (conservative) and structural compression (surgical decompression)
  • Adhere to strict "safe zones" and protective drilling techniques
  • Employ rigorous diagnostic algorithms for postoperative deficits
  • Maintain vigilance for delayed presentations (months to years post-op)
