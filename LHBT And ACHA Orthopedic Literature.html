The Bicipital-Vascular Axis: LHBT & ACHA Intersection

Evidence-Based Interactive Educational Summary

1 Introduction to the Bicipital-Vascular Axis

The long head of the biceps tendon (LHBT) is a structurally complex anatomical entity serving as an anterior glenohumeral stabilizer, a humeral head depressor, and an active participant in shoulder flexion and supination. It is simultaneously one of the most prominent generators of anterior shoulder pain in both athletic and aging populations. Level V [1]

The pathogenesis of LHBT disorders—which exist on a histological continuum from acute inflammatory tenosynovitis to chronic degenerative tendinopathy, and ultimately, spontaneous rupture—cannot be fully comprehended without rigorous examination of its regional microvasculature. Central to this network is the anterior circumflex humeral artery (ACHA) and its critical terminal extension, the anterolateral ascending branch (ALAB), historically referred to as the arcuate artery of Laing. Level IV [5,6]

Clinical Context
Contemporary evidence-based orthopedic sports medicine has progressively shifted surgical management of refractory LHBT pathology away from simple tenotomy toward biceps tenodesis, which preserves the length-tension relationship, prevents "Popeye" deformity, and mitigates postoperative muscle cramping in young, active patients. Level II [8]

However, this shift has ignited a highly scrutinized debate regarding the optimal anatomical location for tendon fixation: all-arthroscopic suprapectoral tenodesis versus open or mini-open subpectoral tenodesis. While high-level evidence confirms both techniques yield uniformly high functional success, the spatial proximity of the ACHA and ALAB to the intertubercular sulcus introduces profound risks of iatrogenic vascular injury. Level I [11]

⚠ Vascular Complications Are Not Benign
Iatrogenic vascular injuries manifest as: postoperative hematomas (nidus for deep space infections), pseudoaneurysms (compressive neuropathic effects), persistent intraoperative hemorrhage causing "red out," and in devastatingly rare instances, focal avascular necrosis (AVN) of the humeral head from disruption of intraosseous retrograde perfusion. Level IV [16,17,18]

2 Anatomical Mapping: Bicipital Groove Microvasculature

2.1 Embryology and Morphology of the Intertubercular Sulcus

The bicipital groove and LHBT form simultaneously around the seventh to eighth week of gestation from the mesoderm of the upper limb bud. By the twelfth prenatal week, the depth of the sulcus becomes apparent through bone removal on its floor and deposition on the tubercle crests. Level IV [19]

In the mature adult skeleton, the bicipital groove forms a confined fibro-osseous tunnel with the following dimensions:

  • Length: 81.0 – 87.3 mm
  • Width: 7.74 – 12.9 mm
  • Depth: 3.70 – 6.00 mm

A bottleneck narrowing occurs approximately two-thirds of the way down the groove from its superior origin. The tendon is stabilized proximally by the biceps pulley (coracohumeral ligament + superior glenohumeral ligament) and distally by the transverse humeral ligament. Level IV [1,19]

2.3 ACHA Course and Branching Pattern

The proximal humerus and extra-articular LHBT receive perfusion predominantly from the ACHA and posterior circumflex humeral artery (PCHA), both arising from the third segment of the axillary artery. Recent high-resolution studies indicate the PCHA may account for up to 64% of humeral head blood supply, with the ACHA supplying the remaining 36%. Despite this proportional shift, the ACHA remains the critical vessel of interest due to its intimate proximity to the surgical field. Level IV [6]

The ACHA typically travels laterally, passing deep to the short head of biceps and coracobrachialis along the inferior border of the subscapularis. Upon reaching the surgical neck, it approaches the intertubercular groove. Before crossing the LHBT, the ACHA delivers a medial ascending branch. The most clinically critical branch is the anterolateral ascending branch (ALAB), which courses superiorly along the lateral border of the bicipital groove after the main ACHA trunk crosses the LHBT. Level IV [6]

2.4 Morphological Variations and the Arcuate Artery of Laing

⚠ Critical Anatomical Variation Alert
In a seminal Level IV cadaveric and angiographic study, the standard ACHA branching pattern was observed in only 36% of shoulders. In 22% of specimens, the ALAB passed anterior (superficial) to the LHBT rather than deep to it. This places it directly in the path of motorized shavers and radiofrequency probes during arthroscopic suprapectoral tenodesis. Level IV [6]

As the ALAB reaches the proximal extent of the intertubercular groove, just inferior to the transverse humeral ligament, it penetrates the cortical bone of the greater tuberosity. Once intraosseous, it is formally designated the arcuate artery of Laing—a major conduit responsible for retrograde perfusion of the humeral epiphysis. Micro-CT studies confirm the arcuate artery provides exclusive blood flow to the anterior-superior and superior-medial aspects of the humeral head. Disruption at the lateral edge of the groove instantaneously obliterates this perfusion, creating a high-risk ischemic zone overlapping with patterns of early aseptic focal AVN. Level IV [6,7,16,28]

3 Ischemia, Tendinopathy & Tendon-to-Bone Healing

3.1 The Ischemic Etiology of LHBT Tendinopathy

The pathophysiology of LHBT tendinopathy is heavily influenced by the biological and microvascular environment. Healthy tendons are composed primarily of type I collagen fibrils with contributions from type III and V collagens, proteoglycans, and decorin. However, as the LHBT descends into the constrained bicipital groove, it becomes susceptible to a unique vascular phenomenon. Level V [30]

The Hypovascular Watershed Zone
Level IV anatomical injection studies identified a distinct "hypovascular zone" located 1.2 to 3.0 cm distal to the tendon's origin at the supraglenoid tubercle. This segment corresponds to the transitional boundary where the tendon exits the glenohumeral joint and enters the proximal intertubercular groove—a watershed between proximal (suprascapular artery) and distal (ALAB) vascular territories connected by reduced-caliber choke vessels with extremely limited arterial reserve. Level IV [3,21]

When tenosynovitis develops (from repetitive overhead traction, internal impingement, or adjacent rotator cuff tears), localized synovial inflammation and cellular edema ensue. Because the bicipital sheath and transverse humeral ligament form an unyielding tunnel, swelling exponentially increases interstitial pressure, mechanically compressing the delicate choke vessels and trans-mesotenon branches. The resulting localized ischemia triggers:

  • Hypoxia-induced cellular apoptosis in tenocytes
  • Upregulated expression of matrix metalloproteinases (MMPs)
  • Aberrant extracellular matrix remodeling → collagen disorganization and fibrocartilaginous metaplasia
  • Upregulation of TRPV1 and TRPA1 channels on peripheral nociceptors, with release of Substance P and IL-6, driving chronic peripheral sensitization and neurogenic pain

This ischemic degeneration ultimately compromises tensile strength and precipitates spontaneous rupture. Level IV [1,32,33]

3.2 ALAB Vascularity and Tendon-to-Bone Healing

Surgical tenodesis success depends entirely on robust tendon-to-bone (T-to-B) healing, requiring an intricate cascade of inflammation, cellular proliferation, and matrix remodeling to regenerate a fibrocartilaginous enthesis. The regional vascularity supplied by intact ACHA branches is paramount for delivering macrophages, fibroblasts, and mesenchymal stem cells (MSCs). Level V [34,35]

Key Basic Science Finding
While early biomechanical testing favored intratunnel repairs for stiffness, long-term histological quality strongly favors surface repairs. Creating a large bone tunnel permanently destroys trabecular architecture and compromises intraosseous anastomotic networks from the ALAB, creating a relatively ischemic microenvironment. Subtle micromotion within the tunnel induces chronic inflammation, favoring weak fibrous scar tissue over mature fibrocartilage. Preserving extraosseous ACHA branches through less invasive surface repairs may optimize orthobiologic conditions for enthesis regeneration. Level III [34]

4 Subpectoral vs. Suprapectoral Tenodesis

Biceps tenodesis is highly favored over tenotomy in younger, active populations. While tenotomy effectively relieves pain, it is associated with:

  • "Popeye" deformity: up to 70%
  • Persistent muscle spasms/cramping: 15–25%
  • Measurable decrease in supination endurance

The surgical discourse has polarized around two anatomical zones: Level II [8,14]

  • All-arthroscopic suprapectoral biceps tenodesis (ASPBT): Within Taylor's Zone 1 or Zone 2, proximal to the pectoralis major tendon insertion
  • Open/mini-open subpectoral biceps tenodesis (OSPBT): In Taylor's Zone 3, distal to the bicipital groove, at the diaphyseal-metaphyseal junction below the pectoralis major

Suprapectoral morbidity mechanism: ASPBT secures the tendon high in Taylor's Zone 1/2, inadvertently leaving 2.0 – 4.5 cm of potentially pathologic, degenerated tendon retained within the distal bicipital groove. This retained segment acts as a persistent pain generator from ongoing tenosynovitis, friction, and hidden micro-tears. Level II [9,11,14]

Subpectoral morbidity mechanism: OSPBT completely extricates the LHBT from the groove, eliminating friction-related pain. However, open dissection in the anatomically dense, vascularly rich Taylor's Zone 3 carries higher risks of deep infection (hematoma as nidus for Cutibacterium acnes) and neurological injury (musculocutaneous nerve, lateral antebrachial cutaneous nerve from aggressive medial retraction). Level II [12,14,18]

4.3 Iatrogenic Vascular Complications

Intraoperative Hemorrhage and "Red Out"

During arthroscopic suprapectoral tenodesis, the surgeon must frequently decorticate the groove floor with a high-speed burr. If the ALAB courses anteriorly (22% prevalence) or the surgeon aggressively burrs the lateral wall (61.5% of mesotenon branches reside there), the ALAB is at imminent risk. Under elevated arthroscopic pump pressure, transection may not present as pulsatile bleeding. Upon withdrawal, catastrophic hemorrhage ensues, causing complete "red out" and potentially necessitating emergent conversion to open surgery. Level IV [5,6,17]

Deep Space Osteomyelitis

Unrecognized bleeding leads to massive hematomas within the deltopectoral interval. In a Level IV clinical series of 353 subpectoral tenodesis patients, hematomas served as biological nidi for Cutibacterium acnes colonization, with indolent organisms propagating along interference screw threads into the humeral diaphysis, causing destructive osteomyelitis requiring extensive debridement and prolonged IV antibiotics. Level IV [18]

Pseudoaneurysm

Arterial trauma that breaches the tunica intima and media without complete vessel severance can result in delayed pseudoaneurysm formation, typically presenting weeks postoperatively with expanding pulsatile swelling, severe pain, and potential compressive neuropathy. Diagnosis requires duplex ultrasound or MRA; management may require endovascular embolization or open aneurysmorrhaphy. Level IV [17,29]

Focal Avascular Necrosis (AVN)

⚠ Most Devastating Complication
Suprapectoral anchors placed too proximally and laterally can physically obliterate the intraosseous branches of the ALAB, disrupting the arcuate artery of Laing. This manifests months postoperatively with paradoxical severe pain, progressive ROM loss, cystic changes and subchondral sclerosis progressing to frank articular collapse of the superomedial humeral head. Treatment typically necessitates joint resurfacing or reverse total shoulder arthroplasty. Level IV [16,24]

5 Surgical Safe Zones & Protective Strategies

5.1 Subpectoral Dia-Metaphyseal Safe Zone

For open or all-arthroscopic subpectoral tenodesis, the optimal bone socket location lies at the dia-metaphyseal junction:

  • Distance inferior to articular margin: 3.0 cm
  • Position: Below the inferior border of the subscapularis, above the distal pectoralis major insertion
  • Principle: Strictly inferior to the transverse branching network of the ACHA, leaving ALAB and descending branches completely undisturbed
  • Fracture prevention: Unicortical preparation only; match interference screw diameter to tendon diameter; avoid excessive over-reaming

Restricting dissection superior to the distal border of the pectoralis major also protects the musculocutaneous and radial nerves from traction, compression, or errant drilling. Level V / Level IV [17]

5.2 Suprapectoral Arthroscopic Boundaries

The critical anatomical landmark during arthroscopy is the lateral fat pad along the lateral wall of the intertubercular groove:

  • All dissection, burring, and electrocautery must remain medial to the fat pad
  • Aggressive lateral decortication risks ALAB transection → arcuate artery compromise → AVN
  • The "distal-suprapectoral" approach using an accessory low anterolateral portal (~1.5 cm proximal to pectoralis major) balances groove pain avoidance with vascular safety
  • Anchor placement should be central or slightly medial on the groove floor, purposefully avoiding the lateral cortical margin
  • Avoid the extreme proximal aspect near the articular margin to protect the main ACHA trunk before bifurcation
Level V [38,39]

Patients present with paradoxical severe pain and mechanical ROM loss. Treatment typically requires arthroplasty. Risk magnified in variant anatomy or concomitant proximal humerus fractures.

Level IV — Anatomical injection studies Arthroscopy / Annals of Anatomy
Identified a hypovascular watershed zone 1.2–3.0 cm distal to the tendon origin at the supraglenoid tubercle, at the boundary between suprascapular artery and ALAB territories. 7 Conclusion

The intersection of the long head of the biceps tendon and the anterior circumflex humeral artery is a region of immense anatomical complexity and profound clinical consequence. The microvasculature of the bicipital groove—principally supplied by the ALAB and extending into the humeral epiphysis via the arcuate artery of Laing—is not merely an anatomical footnote, but a crucial determinant of biological tendon health.

Ischemic compromise within the watershed zone of the LHBT acts as a primary biological catalyst for degenerative tendinopathy, neurogenic pain generation, and eventual structural rupture. The integrity of these vascular networks is essential for tendon-to-bone healing, with surface repair techniques demonstrating superior long-term vascular preservation.

While high-level meta-analyses confirm that functional outcomes of suprapectoral and subpectoral tenodesis remain statistically equivalent, the complication profiles diverge sharply. Suprapectoral interventions carry heightened risk of ACHA/ALAB iatrogenic injury manifesting as hemorrhage, pseudoaneurysm, and devastating focal AVN. Subpectoral approaches protect proximal vasculature but introduce risks of osteomyelitis and neurological injury.

Future priorities must include continued mapping of vascular variations (particularly the 22% anterior-coursing ALAB), respecting the lateral fat pad boundary during suprapectoral procedures, and maintaining fixation precisely 3.0 cm inferior to the articular margin during subpectoral techniques to optimize orthobiologic healing while minimizing iatrogenic vascular morbidity.
