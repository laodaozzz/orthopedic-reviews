Femoroacetabular Impingement Syndrome — Clinical Update

Evidence-Based Review 2020–2025

1. Introduction: The Evolution of a Syndrome

From mechanical theory to globally recognized clinical entity

Femoroacetabular Impingement (FAI) syndrome has undergone a profound transformation over the past two decades, evolving from a novel mechanical theory proposed to explain "idiopathic" osteoarthritis into a globally recognized clinical entity supported by high-level evidence. Understanding FAI requires navigating a complex landscape where morphology, mechanics, and symptomatology intersect.

The historical trajectory of hip preservation surgery was fundamentally altered in the early 2000s by Ganz and colleagues, who posited that subtle deformities of the acetabulum and proximal femur could cause repetitive mechanical conflict—impingement—leading to labral tearing, chondral delamination, and the eventual onset of secondary osteoarthritis.1 This mechanistic insight shifted the paradigm from salvage arthroplasty to joint preservation, sparking rapid expansion in arthroscopic techniques and concurrent demand for rigorous clinical validation.

The last five years have been particularly pivotal. The publication of three seminal randomized controlled trials—the UK FASHIoN, Australian FASHIoN, and FAIT trials—has provided the first Level I evidence comparing arthroscopic intervention against conservative management. These studies, alongside updated consensus statements like the Warwick Agreement and the 2023/2024 AAOS Clinical Practice Guidelines, have refined understanding of whom to treat, how to treat them, and what outcomes to realistically expect.

Scope of This Review This report synthesizes contemporary literature to provide an exhaustive update for the practicing orthopedic surgeon—covering developmental etiology in elite athletes, statistical performance of diagnostic maneuvers, granular results of major clinical trials, and emerging data regarding the prevention of osteoarthritis.

2. Definition and Consensus

The Warwick Agreement and Beyond

The nomenclature and diagnostic criteria for FAI have historically been inconsistent, leading to confusion between asymptomatic morphology and symptomatic disease. The 2016 Warwick Agreement Level I — Consensus represented a watershed moment in standardizing these definitions. Convened to build an international, multidisciplinary consensus, the Agreement explicitly defined FAI syndrome as a motion-related clinical disorder of the hip with a triad of symptoms, clinical signs, and imaging findings. This definition was endorsed by 25 clinical societies.3

2.1 The Diagnostic Triad

The Warwick Agreement emphasized that morphology alone is insufficient for a diagnosis of FAI syndrome. A definitive diagnosis requires the simultaneous presence of all three elements:

🩺
Symptoms

Motion-related groin pain, often exacerbated by flexion and rotation

🔍
Clinical Signs

Positive provocative testing (e.g., FADIR) reproducing typical pain

📐
Imaging Findings

Radiographic evidence of cam or pincer morphology

This "syndrome-based" approach protects the clinician from treating radiographs rather than patients. FAI morphology is prevalent in the asymptomatic population, particularly among athletes. Therefore, the diagnosis is fundamentally clinical, with imaging serving as a confirmatory tool rather than a standalone diagnostic modality.

2.2 Recent Consensus Updates (2020–2025)

Following the Warwick Agreement, the 2024 ESSKA Consensus Level I — Consensus on hip and groin pain reinforced these definitions while recommending the term "morphology" rather than "lesion" or "abnormality" for asymptomatic radiographic findings to avoid pathologizing normal variants.5

The 2023 AAOS Clinical Practice Guideline Level I — Guideline update further codified the management pathway, emphasizing a multimodal diagnostic approach and judicious use of advanced imaging.7

⚠ Critical Distinction The presence of cam or pincer deformity in the absence of symptoms is merely "FAI morphology"—not "FAI syndrome." This distinction is critical for preventing overtreatment.

3. Etiology and Epidemiology

The Developmental Hypothesis

3.1 The "Cam-mencement": Skeletal Maturation and High-Impact Loading

The etiology of FAI morphology, particularly cam deformity, has shifted from a congenital anomaly to a developmental adaptation to mechanical loading during skeletal immaturity. Level II — Cohort

Longitudinal studies indicate that osseous cam morphology is preceded by cartilaginous hypertrophy at the femoral head-neck junction and typically forms during the period of physeal closure, roughly between ages 12 and 15 in males.8

The mechanism involves vigorous mechanical loading across the proximal femoral physis during the growth spurt. High-impact athletic activities—specifically those involving repetitive flexion, rotation, and shear forces—place stress on the anterosuperior femoral head-neck junction. This stress is hypothesized to cause subclinical alterations in the growth plate, potentially akin to a subclinical slipped capital femoral epiphysis (SCFE) or stress-induced physeal widening.8

Dose-Response Relationship Research indicates a dose-response relationship between the frequency of athletic activity and the prevalence of cam morphology. Adolescents participating in elite-level sports are significantly more likely to develop cam morphology than recreational or non-athletic counterparts—particularly in soccer, ice hockey, and basketball.8

3.2 Epidemiology and Asymptomatic Prevalence

A major challenge is the high prevalence of FAI morphology in asymptomatic individuals. Distinguishing incidental morphology from pathogenic morphology is central to clinical evaluation.

A 12-year prospective study of elite academy soccer players found that by young adulthood (mean age 28), 80% of hips exhibited cam morphology, and 94% of participants had at least one affected hip.14 This extraordinarily high prevalence suggests that in certain athletic cohorts, cam morphology may be the norm rather than the exception.

3.3 Implications for Screening and Prevention

The developmental etiology raises the possibility of primary prevention. If cam morphology is a maladaptive response to load during a specific developmental window, then modulating that load could theoretically prevent the deformity. However, the Warwick Agreement and subsequent guidelines advise against treating asymptomatic morphology.1,10

Current recommendations focus on vigilance and education: clinicians and coaches should be aware that hip pain in an adolescent athlete may signal physeal stress. Early recognition and activity modification during the critical growth phase could potentially mitigate the development of severe cam morphology, although longitudinal interventional studies confirming this "preventative" effect are still needed.10

4. Diagnosis: The Clinical Assessment

Integrating history, physical examination, and imaging

4.1 Symptomatology and Patient History

The hallmark symptom is motion-related or position-related pain in the hip or groin.

  • Location: Classic anterior groin pain, often described by the "C-sign" (patient cupping hand above the greater trochanter). Pain patterns can be heterogeneous—lateral trochanteric, buttock, or even lumbar referral.2
  • Aggravating Factors: Deep flexion, internal rotation, and adduction. Common triggers include prolonged sitting ("theater sign" or "car sign"), squatting, getting in/out of cars, and pivoting during sports.
  • Mechanical Symptoms: Clicking, catching, locking, or giving way—often indicating unstable labral tears or chondral flaps. Painful catching suggests intra-articular pathology; painless snapping suggests extra-articular sources (iliopsoas tendon or IT band).4

4.2 Physical Examination: Statistical Performance of Special Tests

Recent systematic reviews (2020–2024) have scrutinized the diagnostic accuracy of classic impingement tests.16-19

Clinical Pearl: Chaining Strategy Given the limitations of individual tests, a "chaining" strategy improves accuracy. Combining FADIR, Foot Progression Angle Walking (FPAW), and maximal squat test: if all three are negative, the negative likelihood ratio drops to 0.15. In a population with 55% FAI prevalence, three negative tests reduce the post-test probability to approximately 15%, robustly ruling out the condition.17,18

4.3 Diagnostic Imaging

Radiographic Assessment

  • AP Pelvis: Assess acetabular version (crossover sign, posterior wall sign), lateral center edge angle (LCEA), Tönnis grade, coxa profunda, or protrusio acetabuli.
  • Dunn View (45° or 90°): Gold standard for visualizing asphericity of the femoral head-neck junction (cam morphology). Profiles the anterosuperior quadrant.
  • Frog-leg Lateral: Useful for assessing femoral head sphericity in a different plane.

Advanced Imaging (MRI / MRA)

  • Chondral Status: Chondral delamination ("carpet phenomenon" or "wave sign") characteristic of cam impingement.
  • Biochemical MRI (dGEMRIC / T1rho): Measures glycosaminoglycan content as a proxy for cartilage health. Used as primary outcome in the Australian FASHIoN Trial; clinical utility remains specialized.25

5. Differential Diagnosis

Distinguishing FAI syndrome from mimickers

5.1 Extra-Articular Hip Impingement (EHI)

Recent literature has highlighted extra-articular impingement syndromes that can coexist with or mimic classic FAI.27

  • Subspine Impingement (SSI): Mechanical conflict between the distal anterior femoral neck and a hypertrophic anterior inferior iliac spine (AIIS). Often sequela of prior rectus femoris avulsion. Diagnosed by pain with straight hip flexion and CT showing prominent AIIS (Type II/III). Treated with arthroscopic decompression.
  • Ischiofemoral Impingement (IFI): Narrowing of the ischiofemoral space causing entrapment of the quadratus femoris. Presents with posterior buttock pain exacerbated by long-stride walking. MRI shows quadratus femoris edema. Conservative care is primary; surgical resection reserved for recalcitrant cases.
  • Iliopsoas Impingement: A tight or snapping iliopsoas tendon compresses the anterior labrum at the 3 o'clock position (distinct from the 1–2 o'clock tear in cam FAI). Pain with active hip flexion and palpable snapping.

5.2 Hip-Spine Syndrome

In older athletes or patients with concurrent lumbar pathology, Hip-Spine Syndrome describes the coexistence of hip pathology and lumbar spinal stenosis.29 Hip pathology typically refers pain to the groin and anterior thigh (C-sign); spinal stenosis refers pain to the buttock and posterior thigh. The intra-articular diagnostic injection is the gold standard discriminator—significant pain relief following fluoroscopically guided hip injection confirms the hip as the primary generator.30

5.3 Athletic Pubalgia (Core Muscle Injury)

Athletic pubalgia has a high co-occurrence with FAI syndrome (reported up to 94% in some series).32 Stiffness in the FAI hip increases shear forces across the pubic symphysis, leading to injury of the rectus abdominis insertion or adductor longus origin. A "layered" surgical approach—addressing both the hip and the core—is often required.

6. Treatment: Evidence from High-Impact Trials

Level I evidence guiding the choice between conservative management and surgical intervention

6.1 Conservative Management: Personalised Hip Therapy

Conservative care remains the first line of treatment. The UK FASHIoN trial utilized Personalised Hip Therapy (PHT)—a rigorous, individualized, outcome-based program focusing on core stability, abductor strengthening, and neuromuscular control, delivered by specialist hip physiotherapists. This represents "best conservative care" and serves as the benchmark against which surgery must be compared.25,33

6.2 Surgical Management: Hip Arthroscopy

  • Osteochondroplasty: Resection of cam deformity (femoral neck) and pincer deformity (acetabular rim) to restore spheric head-neck junction and adequate clearance.
  • Labral Management: Labral repair is now the standard of care over debridement, showing superior long-term outcomes. Labral reconstruction (autograft or allograft) is utilized for irreparable tissues.
  • Capsular Management: Capsular closure is critical to restore hip stability and prevent iatrogenic microinstability—now standard practice.

6.3 Landmark Randomized Controlled Trials

ℹ Note on Evidence Base: The source document identifies 3 landmark RCTs (UK FASHIoN, FAIT, Australian FASHIoN) providing Level I evidence for FAI syndrome management. Additional landmark studies with longer follow-up are derived from systematic reviews of these trials. The field is evolving, and further RCTs may be forthcoming.

UK FASHIoN Trial

Multicenter RCT · 23 NHS hospitals · 348 participants · Level I

Comparison: Hip Arthroscopy vs. Personalised Hip Therapy (PHT)

Primary Outcome (iHOT-33 @ 12 months): Surgery group demonstrated significantly greater improvement — mean difference +6.8 points (95% CI 1.7–12.0, p=0.0093), exceeding the MCID of 6.1 points.33

5-Year Follow-Up: Benefits of surgery are durable. Survivorship >85%, with continued statistically significant improvements in iHOT-12, HAGOS, EQ-5D.36

FAIT Trial (Femoroacetabular Impingement Trial)

Multicenter RCT · 222 participants · Level I

Comparison: Arthroscopy vs. Physiotherapy + Activity Modification

Primary Outcome (HOS-ADL @ 8 months): Surgery group showed +10.0 point greater improvement (p<0.001). Significantly more patients achieved MCID (51% vs 32%).38

3-Year Follow-Up: Superiority maintained (+8.9 points HOS-ADL). While plain radiographs (mJSW) showed no difference in OA progression, MRI outcomes (SHOMRI score) revealed significantly less cartilage damage progression in the surgery group—providing biological evidence of joint preservation.40

Australian FASHIoN Trial

Multicenter RCT · Mechanistic primary outcome · Level I

Comparison: Arthroscopy vs. PHT

Primary Outcome (dGEMRIC @ 12 months): No significant difference in dGEMRIC index between groups—likely due to high heterogeneity of baseline values or technical limitations.25

Secondary Outcome: Patient-reported outcomes (iHOT-33) significantly favored surgery — adjusted difference +14 units (p=0.003).

Synthesis and Clinical Recommendation The convergence of data from these three RCTs provides a robust evidence base. Surgical intervention provides superior symptomatic relief, functional improvement, and quality of life compared to the best available conservative care for symptomatic FAI syndrome. While conservative care is beneficial and should remain first-line, patients who fail therapy can be counseled with high confidence that arthroscopy offers a reliable pathway to improvement.

7. Prognosis: Osteoarthritis Prevention and Return to Sport

Long-term goals: return to activity and preventing OA

7.1 OA Prevention

The "Ganz hypothesis" posits that FAI is a precursor to OA. Evidence on disease modification is emerging and nuanced:

  • Radiographic Evidence: Short-to-medium term studies (e.g., FAIT 3-year follow-up) have not demonstrated a significant difference in joint space narrowing (mJSW) between groups. Plain radiographs may be insensitive to early changes, or observation periods may be too short.40
  • MRI Evidence: The FAIT trial's SHOMRI data is critical—surgery group had significantly less cartilage damage progression at 3 years, providing the first RCT-level biological evidence supporting the joint preservation hypothesis.40
  • Long-Term Survivorship: Systematic reviews show conversion to Total Hip Arthroplasty (THA) rate of 10–32% at 10+ years. The strongest predictor of failure is pre-existing cartilage damage. Patients with Tönnis Grade >1 or joint space <2mm have significantly poorer outcomes.42

7.2 Return to Sport (RTS)

Systematic reviews (2023–2024) report the following:44,45

  • Overall RTS Rate: 80–85% following hip arthroscopy
  • Return to Pre-Injury Level: Approximately 79–80%
  • Timing: Mean time to RTS is approximately 7–9 months
  • Professional Athletes: RTS rates >90%, likely due to access to intensive supervised rehabilitation and earlier diagnosis.45
Prognostic Counseling Point While surgery effectively relieves pain and improves function, the absolute prevention of osteoarthritis remains a likely but yet-to-be-fully-proven long-term benefit, contingent on early intervention before irreversible cartilage damage occurs.

8. Conclusion

The management of FAI syndrome has matured significantly. The contemporary approach demands:

  1. Strict adherence to the diagnostic triad (Warwick Agreement) to avoid treating asymptomatic morphology.
  2. Recognition of the developmental etiology in young athletes to guide counseling and potential future prevention.
  3. A rigorous differential diagnosis to identify extra-articular and spinal mimickers.
  4. Evidence-based treatment selection, utilizing best conservative care first, but offering arthroscopy confidently when symptoms persist—supported by Level I data.
  5. Realistic prognostic counseling, emphasizing that surgery effectively relieves pain and improves function, with emerging evidence supporting joint preservation.

As the field moves forward, the focus will shift toward personalized medicine—using advanced imaging biomarkers and patient-specific factors to identify the "perfect candidate" who will derive the maximum lifelong benefit from hip preservation surgery.
