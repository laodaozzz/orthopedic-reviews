Executive Summary

The surgical management of skeletal trauma has evolved through a dialectic process over the past century, oscillating between the mechanical rigorousness of anatomical reconstruction and the biological necessity of vascular preservation. Modern orthopedic guidelines, largely shaped by the comprehensive research of the AO Foundation and corroborated by extensive meta-analytic data, have settled on a nuanced approach often termed "biological osteosynthesis."

🔑 Core Thesis
While mechanical stability is a prerequisite for bone healing, the biological vitality of the bone fragments—specifically the integrity of the periosteal blood supply—is the ultimate determinant of union, infection resistance, and long-term remodeling.

The "extent of periosteal stripping" is no longer a binary choice but a calculated surgical decision based on the "personality" of the fracture. For articular fractures, anatomical reduction remains paramount, necessitating precise, localized stripping to ensure joint congruity. However, for the vast majority of diaphyseal and metaphyseal injuries, the evidence overwhelmingly supports bridge plating and Minimally Invasive Plate Osteosynthesis (MIPO) techniques that preserve the soft tissue envelope.

1. Physiological Foundations of Cortical Perfusion

The periosteum is not merely a passive connective tissue membrane; it is a complex, highly vascularized organ essential for cortical survival, particularly in the post-traumatic setting.

1.1 The Dual Circulation of Cortical Bone

Bone perfusion is maintained by two distinct vascular systems:

  • Centrifugal (Endosteal) System: The nutrient artery enters the diaphysis, branching within the medullary canal. High-pressure arterial blood flows from the marrow outward through Volkmann's canals and Haversian systems to supply the inner two-thirds of the cortex.
  • Centripetal (Periosteal) System: The periosteum, supplied by surrounding muscle cuff and fasciocutaneous vessels, sends arterioles inward to supply the outer one-third of the cortex.
⚡ The "Traumatic Switch"
Following a fracture, the nutrient artery is frequently disrupted, causing the high-pressure intramedullary system to fail. The periosteal system becomes the dominant, often sole, source of perfusion for the healing cortex. Laser Doppler flowmetry studies in sheep models have demonstrated that reaming destroys the endosteal supply but triggers a compensatory six-fold increase in periosteal flow.[1]

1.2 Quantification of Flow Reduction from Stripping

Research utilizing laser Doppler flowmetry has quantified the impact of surgical stripping. In sheep tibia models, periosteal stripping resulted in a statistically significant 20% decrease in cortical bone perfusion over the entire length of the tibia.[2]

Comparative studies in rat segmental fractures show that while blood flow increases substantially in unstripped fractures (the hyperemic response), this normalization is significantly delayed in fractures subjected to stripping. This perfusion deficit correlates directly with impaired mechanical recovery; fractures with preserved periosteum demonstrate significantly higher bending moments and rigidity at 12 weeks compared to stripped controls.[3]

1.3 Cellular Consequences: Osteocyte Apoptosis

🦴 Histological Evidence
Studies on murine calvaria have shown that periosteal removal induces rapid apoptosis of osteocytes in the outer cortical layers. As early as 3 to 7 days post-stripping, there is a statistically significant increase in the number of "empty lacunae"—the hallmark of bone death—restricted to the outer half of the cortex beneath the stripped area.[4]
  • Sclerostin Downregulation: Immunohistochemical analysis reveals a dramatic decrease in sclerostin distribution in the stripped cortical bone, indicating disruption in the bone's mechanotransduction and remodeling signaling pathways.[4]
  • Cortical Porosity: In the chronic phase, the body attempts to revascularize necrotic cortex through osteoclastic resorption (cutting cones), leading to a transient increase in cortical porosity—"surgical osteoporosis"—which weakens the bone-implant interface.[5]

1.4 The Timeline of Revascularization

Nascent vessel growth is measurable within 3 days post-injury but peaks between 14–21 days.[6] Periosteal stripping resets this clock. Because the periosteum serves as a reservoir for mesenchymal progenitor cells and a scaffold for angiogenesis, its removal forces vessels to migrate from the more distant muscle envelope, delaying bridging of the fracture gap.

Traditional compression plates, when pressed firmly against stripped bone, act as a barrier to revascularization. The area directly beneath the plate (the "footprint") remains ischemic longer than surrounding bone, driving the evolution from broad, flat plates to "limited contact" designs.[7]

2. Evolution of AO Principles: From Mechanics to Biology

2.1 The Era of Absolute Stability (1960s–1980s)

In its early decades, the AO advocated for "absolute stability" to achieve primary (direct) bone healing, bypassing callus formation. This required anatomical reduction and interfragmentary compression, leading to extensive exposures and circumferential stripping. While mechanically rigid, this often led to "aseptic necrosis" beneath the plate—dead bone that failed to unite or became infected years later.[8]

2.2 The Era of Biological Osteosynthesis (1990s–Present)

The Four AO Principles Today
  1. Fracture Reduction: Restore anatomical relationships (articular) or functional alignment (diaphysis).
  2. Fracture Fixation: Provide stability appropriate to the fracture "personality."
  3. Preservation of Blood Supply: Meticulous handling of soft tissues (MIPO, unreamed nailing).
  4. Early Mobilization: Allowing functional loading.[8]

3. Anatomical Recommendations & Vascular Territories

The concept of the "angiosome"—a three-dimensional block of tissue supplied by a specific source artery—is critical. Surgical incisions must be planned to respect these vascular territories.[11]

3.1 The Tibia: A Vascular Minefield

The distal tibia is the most vulnerable region due to its subcutaneous nature and lack of muscular cover.

  • Medial MIPO Approach: The "safe zone" is the epiperiosteal tunnel on the medial face. The saphenous vein and nerve must be retracted anteriorly. The plate is slid over the periosteum without stripping.[14]
  • Anterolateral Approach: Used when medial soft tissue is compromised. The plate is placed under the anterior compartment muscles. The skin, subcutaneous fat, and extensor retinaculum are raised as a single full-thickness fasciocutaneous unit.[14]
  • The "Choke Vessel" Rule: A minimum skin bridge of 7 cm is recommended between incisions to prevent skin necrosis.[11]

3.2 The Femur: The Perforator-Rich Linea Aspera

The linea aspera receives perforators from the profunda femoris artery. The lateral aspect is the standard safe zone for MIPO. The vastus lateralis muscle is lifted off the periosteum, and the plate is tunneled submuscularly.[16]

⚠️ Danger Zone
The posterior aspect of the lateral intermuscular septum contains perforating vessels. Excessive stripping posterior to the septum can devascularize the femoral shaft.[16]

3.3 The Humerus: Radial Nerve and Vascular Intervals

The anterior MIPO approach is favored to avoid the radial nerve. The plate is tunneled deep to the biceps but superficial to the brachialis, protecting the periosteum. In MIPO, the nerve is avoided by using safe zones proximally and distally, bridging the danger zone without direct exposure.[18]

3.4 The Radius and Ulna

Forearm fractures often require absolute stability to restore rotational anatomy. Compression plating is standard, but stripping is limited to the plate width ("footprint"), not circumferentially.[19]

4. Comparative Clinical Outcomes: MIPO vs. ORIF

4.1 Infection and Soft Tissue Complications

Infection is the most devastating complication of periosteal stripping, as devascularized bone cannot mount an immune response and antibiotics cannot penetrate avascular sequestra.

  • Distal Tibia: A meta-analysis revealed MIPO significantly reduces wound infection risk vs. ORIF (OR = 0.53 for superficial infection; OR = 0.33 for overall complications).[20] I
  • Distal Fibula: MIPO complication rate was 14% vs. 37% for ORIF, driven by wound healing disorders and skin necrosis in the ORIF group.[22] II
  • Humeral Shaft: ORIF was at statistically higher risk of infection vs. nailing (p = 0.007) and MIPO, due to large dissection required.[23] I

4.2 Bone Union and Healing Time

  • Humeral Shaft: A systematic review found MIPO resulted in significantly shorter union time (mean difference = 3.12 weeks) and lower incidence of non-union (OR = 0.27).[18] I
  • Tibial Shaft: MIPO demonstrates superior biological healing vs. ORIF, particularly in comminuted fractures.[24] II
  • Pediatric: MIPO and elastic nailing are gold standards, leading to rapid, robust union due to the thick, osteogenic pediatric periosteum.[10] III

4.3 The Trade-off: Radiation, Malreduction, and Learning Curve

  • Radiation Exposure: MIPO requires significantly longer fluoroscopy times (e.g., 87 seconds vs. 41 seconds in distal tibia cases).[27]
  • Malalignment: Rotational and angular deformities are more common without direct visualization. Requires meticulous attention to indirect reduction cues.[21]
  • Technical Difficulty: MIPO has a steeper learning curve; surgeons must rely on tactile feedback and 2D imaging to build a 3D mental model.[28]

5. Guidelines for Specific Fracture Personalities

The decision to strip or not to strip is dictated by the "personality" of the fracture—a composite of the bone involved, the fracture pattern, and the location.

5.1 Intra-articular Fractures (e.g., Tibial Pilon, Distal Humerus)

Hybrid Approach

Anatomical reduction of the joint surface is non-negotiable. This requires an open approach and localized stripping of articular fragments to ensure perfect congruence. Once the "articular block" is reconstructed (absolute stability), the meta-diaphyseal component should be spanned (bridged) without further stripping.[8]

Pins and screws placed near the joint should be at least 14 mm from the joint line to avoid penetrating the capsule and causing septic arthritis.[30]

5.2 Diaphyseal Fractures (e.g., Femur, Tibia Shaft)

Periosteal stripping should be strictly avoided. The gold standard is Intramedullary Nailing (IMN) or MIPO bridge plating.[10]

🚫 Comminution Rule: Do Not Touch
Loose fragments should never be stripped to check their position. They derive their only blood supply from soft tissue attachments. Stripping them turns them into necrotic sequestra. They should be left untouched and bridged by the plate.

5.3 Pediatric Fractures

The pediatric periosteum is thick, biologically aggressive, and the primary engine of growth and remodeling. Elastic Stable Intramedullary Nailing (ESIN) or submuscular plating is preferred, utilizing the thick periosteal sleeve as a reduction aid.[10]

5.4 Non-Unions and the "Induced Membrane"

Treating non-unions often requires controlled stripping (decortication) to restart the healing process. In the Masquelet Technique, the body forms an "induced membrane" around a cement spacer that mimics the periosteum, becoming vascularized and secreting growth factors (VEGF, BMP-2).[32]

6. Surgical Techniques for Biological Fixation

6.1 Indirect Reduction Maneuvers

  • Ligamentotaxis: Traction restores length and alignment by tensioning the intact soft tissue envelope.[8]
  • Percutaneous Joysticks: Schanz pins inserted into main fragments serve as handles to manipulate alignment without opening the fracture site.[34]
  • Gravity Reduction: In proximal tibia fractures, hanging the leg over the table edge uses gravity to pull the distal fragment into alignment.[35]
  • Plate-Assisted Reduction: The plate is fixed to one fragment and used as a tool to pull the other into alignment (common in femoral MIPO).[17]

6.2 Bridge Plating Mechanics

The "working length" of the plate (distance between the two innermost screws) should be optimized: a longer working length reduces strain concentration and allows micromotion necessary for callus formation. The plate length should generally be 2–3 times the fracture length in comminuted cases, or 8–10 times in simple fractures. Leaving 2–3 holes empty over the fracture site prevents overly stiff constructs that suppress callus.[36]

6.3 Tunneling and Plate Insertion

A tunnel is created using a blunt instrument (Cobb elevator or the plate itself) between the muscle and the periosteum. In the femur, plates are inserted proximal-to-distal; in the tibia, usually distal-to-proximal. Before screw insertion, verify plate centering in both AP and lateral views.[16]

6.4 Cerclage Wiring: A Re-evaluation

Modern cerclage wiring is seeing a resurgence if the wire is passed over the periosteum using a specialized passer. Studies suggest the impact on perfusion is minimal when not overtightened. It can be a valuable tool for holding reduction while a nail or plate is applied.[34]

7. Future Directions and Emerging Technologies

  • Optical Sensing Drills: Experimental drills equipped with diffuse reflectance spectroscopy can detect the optical signature of the bone-tissue interface, warning the surgeon of a periosteal breach or proximity of a vessel or nerve.[37]
  • Preoperative Angiosome Mapping: Doppler ultrasound to map perforators preoperatively is transitioning from plastic surgery to orthopedics, allowing incisions tailored to individual vascular anatomy.[11]
  • Biodegradable Membranes: Synthetic membranes mimicking the periosteum (barrier function + growth factor delivery) offer promise for single-stage reconstruction of large defects.[5]

8. Summary of Evidence-Based Guidelines

  1. Diaphyseal Fractures: MIPO or IMN is the standard. Strict avoidance of periosteal stripping. Bridge the zone of injury.
  2. Articular Fractures: Anatomical reduction is priority. Strip only articular margins necessary for visualization. Bridge the metaphysis.
  3. Comminution: Do not touch. Loose fragments are vascularized by soft tissue only.
  4. Incisions: Respect angiosomes. Plan incisions based on vascular territories. Maintain wide skin bridges (>7 cm).
  5. Fixation: Use locking plates as "internal external fixators," minimizing plate-to-bone contact.

Mastery of orthopedic trauma surgery today is defined not by how well a surgeon can dissect, but by how well they can operate without dissecting.

1. Acute Vascular Response to Intramedullary Reaming (Sheep Model)
Microsphere estimation · Ovine tibia · Level of Evidence: Animal study (not applicable to I–V scale)

Reaming destroys endosteal supply but triggers a compensatory 6-fold increase in periosteal flow, demonstrating the critical survival mechanism of the periosteal system.[1]

2. Effect of Periosteal Stripping on Cortical Bone Perfusion
Laser Doppler flowmetry · Sheep model · Level of Evidence: Animal study

Periosteal stripping resulted in a 20% decrease in cortical bone perfusion over the entire tibial length, not just at the fracture site.[2]

3. Effects of Periosteal Stripping on Healing of Segmental Fractures
Rat model · 12-week follow-up · Level of Evidence: Animal study

Unstripped fractures demonstrated significantly higher bending moments and rigidity at 12 weeks compared to stripped controls, with delayed normalization of blood flow in the stripped group.[3]

4. Periosteum Removal Effects on Osteocytes (Mouse Calvaria)
Histological & immunohistochemical analysis · Murine model · Level of Evidence: Animal study

Periosteal removal induced rapid osteocyte apoptosis (3–7 days) with increased empty lacunae and dramatic decrease in sclerostin distribution.[4]

5. MIPO vs. ORIF for Humeral Shaft Fractures – Systematic Review & Meta-Analysis
Comparative studies · Level of Evidence: I (Systematic review of comparative studies)

MIPO resulted in shorter union time (mean difference 3.12 weeks) and lower non-union rate (OR = 0.27). ORIF at higher risk of infection vs. MIPO.[18]

6. Wound Infection & Healing: MIPO vs. Intramedullary Nail for Distal Tibial Fractures
Meta-analysis · Level of Evidence: I

MIPO significantly reduces wound infection risk vs. ORIF (OR = 0.53 superficial; OR = 0.33 overall complications).[20]

7. MIPO vs. ORIF for Distal Fibular Fractures
Comparative study · Level of Evidence: II–III

MIPO complication rate of 14% vs. 37% for ORIF, driven by wound healing disorders attributable to lateral angiosome disruption.[22]

8. Currents of Plate Osteosynthesis in Osteoporotic Bone
Review · Implant evolution · Level of Evidence: Expert opinion / narrative review

Documented the evolution from DCP → LC-DCP → LCP, each reducing periosteal contact footprint and preserving epiperiosteal vascular network.[7]
