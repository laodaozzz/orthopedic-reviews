1. Introduction & Historical Context

The anterior cruciate ligament (ACL) serves as the primary restraint to anterior tibial translation and a critical secondary stabilizer against internal tibial rotation, orchestrating complex knee kinematics and proprioceptive feedback. In the United States, the annual incidence of ACL injuries approaches 68.5 cases per 100,000 persons, with athletes engaged in pivoting and cutting sports bearing disproportionate risk Ref 1.

Evolution of ACL Management

Historically, the paradigm for managing acute ACL ruptures has experienced dramatic shifts. During the 1970s and 1980s, open primary repair was the standard of care. However, it was ultimately abandoned due to exorbitant failure rates, often exceeding 50% at mid-term follow-up, leading to near-universal adoption of autograft and allograft ACL reconstruction (ACLR) Ref 3. The biological failure was attributed to the hostile intra-articular environment; specifically, synovial fluid contains plasmin, an enzyme that prematurely degrades the fragile fibrin clot required for ligamentous bridging, fibroblast migration, and angiogenesis Ref 5.

While ACLR reliably restores gross mechanical stability, it is not without limitations. The reconstruction process involves harvesting native tissue (e.g., bone-patellar tendon-bone [BPTB] or hamstring tendons), which introduces donor-site morbidity, incomplete restoration of native neurosensory proprioception, and altered joint kinematics Ref 4. Graft failure rates remain between 3% and 10%, and return-to-sport (RTS) rates at pre-injury levels are approximately 65% Ref 8. Furthermore, ACLR has not reliably demonstrated the capacity to halt post-traumatic osteoarthritis (PTOA), which affects up to 50% of patients two decades post-surgery Ref 11.

The 2023–2026 Renaissance in Primary Repair

The period between 2023 and 2026 has witnessed a profound renaissance in primary ACL repair, driven by:

  • High-resolution MRI for improved preoperative planning
  • Minimally invasive arthroscopic precision
  • Breakthroughs in orthobiologics

Modern techniques—including Bridge-Enhanced ACL Restoration (BEAR), Internal Bracing (IB) with suture tape augmentation (e.g., FiberTape), and Dynamic Intraligamentary Stabilization (DIS, e.g., Ligamys)—theoretically maintain the anatomical footprint, vascular supply, and proprioceptive mechanoreceptors Ref 13.

2. Evidence-Based Clinical Practice Guidelines

The 2024 Freddie Fu Panther Sports Medicine Symposium Consensus LOE IV

A global consortium of 59 international orthopaedic experts convened to address the controversies surrounding joint preservation. Published in KSSTA in 2025, the symposium utilized specific case-based scenarios to isolate treatment preferences based on patient age and activity level Ref 16.

🔍 Key Finding: Panther Symposium

For young, active athletes (e.g., 17-year-old soccer player with acute proximal tear), 90% consensus established ACLR as the uncompromising standard of care. Only 7% of experts advocated for primary repair, citing unacceptably high biological failure rates. BPTB autograft remains the preferred graft choice for 51% of surgeons.

For older, active patients (>50 years with high functional demands), treatment preferences were significantly more heterogeneous:

  • 61% preferred ACLR
  • 34% endorsed non-operative management with structured rehabilitation
  • 5% selected primary ACL repair

ESSKA Consensus on ACL Functional Instability LOE I

ESSKA delineates the critical difference between mechanical laxity and functional instability Ref 19:

  • Laxity: Objective, passive biomechanical measurement (e.g., anterior tibial translation side-to-side difference of >3 mm on KT-1000/2000 arthrometer)
  • Instability: Subjective, dynamic functional symptom—most commonly "giving way" during high-magnitude loads

Functional instability, rather than isolated static laxity, is the absolute primary driver for surgical intervention Ref 19.

EU-US Meniscus Rehabilitation Consensus (2024–2025) LOE I

This landmark joint initiative by ESSKA, AOSSM, and AASPT emphasizes the symbiotic relationship between meniscal preservation and ACL kinematics Ref 20. The consensus mandates:

  • Meniscal repair should be performed whenever biologically feasible, as meniscal deficiency exponentially amplifies strain on the healing ligament
  • Stable vertical meniscal repairs allow immediate full weight-bearing
  • Complex radial or root tears necessitate strict non-weight-bearing and ROM restrictions (0–90°) for 4–6 weeks

AAOS Clinical Practice Guidelines Update LOE I

The current AAOS CPG provides strong recommendations (★★★★) Ref 26:

  • Use of BPTB or hamstring autografts over allografts in young, active patients due to significantly lower revision rates
  • Moderate-strength recommendation against routine postoperative functional knee bracing
  • Moderate-strength recommendation that in select high-risk patients (high-grade rotatory laxity [pivot shift ≥2], generalized hyperlaxity [Beighton >4], young athletes returning to pivoting sports, or revision surgery), addition of ALLR or LET in conjunction with ACLR is appropriate to reduce graft failure Ref 28
📋 AAOS Position on Primary Repair

The AAOS has refrained from endorsing primary ACL repair as an equivalent alternative to ACLR. The focus of the 2024–2026 updates revolves around augmenting standard ACLR with Lateral Extra-Articular Procedures (LEAPs) rather than primary repair techniques.

3. Anatomical Prerequisites: The Sherman Classification

The renewed clinical viability of primary ACL repair is inextricably tethered to precise patient selection, governed predominantly by the anatomical morphology of the ligamentous rupture. The Sherman Classification (1991) remains the definitive anatomical framework Ref 31.

Critical Imaging Limitation

⚠ Preoperative Planning Caveat

High-resolution MRI has been shown to underestimate the ACL tibial stump length and misclassify the exact tear location in up to 49% of cases Ref 35. AOSSM consensus protocols mandate that surgeons counsel patients preoperatively that the definitive decision between primary repair and autograft reconstruction will be made intraoperatively, contingent upon direct arthroscopic visualization Ref 33.

Current Level I and II evidence dictates that primary ACL repair should be exclusively reserved for acute (<21 to 45 days post-injury) Sherman Type I and highly selected Type II tears Ref 7. Cohort studies report up to 82.2% survivorship in meticulously selected adult populations with these tear types Ref 6. Mid-substance tears (Type III) and chronic tears exhibiting significant retraction and devascularization are absolute contraindications for isolated primary repair.

4. Biological Augmentation: The BEAR Paradigm

The Bridge-Enhanced ACL Restoration (BEAR) procedure represents a revolutionary transition from purely mechanical fixation to biologically augmented tissue engineering. The technique utilizes a proprietary, highly porous extracellular matrix scaffold derived from bovine type I collagen. Surgically placed within the gap between torn ACL ends, the implant is saturated with autologous blood, creating a protective viscoelastic bridging sponge that shields the native fibrin clot from plasmin-rich synovial fluid. Over time, the scaffold promotes platelet degranulation, releasing growth factors (PDGF, TGF-β) that stimulate fibroblast migration and tenocyte proliferation Ref 5.

Level I Evidence: The BEAR II Clinical Trial LOE I

The BEAR II trial—a prospective, randomized clinical trial published in AJSM—compared BEAR to standard autograft ACLR in 100 young, active patients (median age 17 years) with complete ACL tears within 45 days of injury Ref 3.

✅ BEAR II: Two-Year Results
  • Primary non-inferiority endpoints achieved for IKDC subjective scores and AP knee laxity
  • Dramatically superior hamstring strength index in BEAR cohort vs ACLR (p<0.05) due to no autograft harvest
  • By 8 weeks, bovine implant largely resorbed and replaced by native cellular tissue, new collagen, and robust blood vessels
  • Restored ligament closely mimics native ACL orientation, size, and volume on serial MRI

The Age Paradox

⚠ Critical Limitation: Age-Dependent Failure

Across all BEAR clinical trials, the aggregate re-tear rate is approximately 14%–15%—more than double the 4%–6% failure rate of optimal BPTB or hamstring autograft ACLR Ref 3.

Multivariate logistic regression reveals a profound age-dependent paradox: the odds of ipsilateral revision surgery decrease by 32% for each 1-year increase in patient age (OR 0.684; 95% CI 0.517–0.905; p<0.05) Ref 41.

5. Mechanical Augmentation: Internal Bracing & DIS

Internal Bracing (Suture Tape Augmentation) LOE II

Internal brace ligament augmentation (IBLA) involves spanning the native repaired ACL with ultra-high-molecular-weight polyethylene (UHMWPE) and polyester suture tape (e.g., FiberTape), acting as an internal "seatbelt" sharing biomechanical load during early healing Ref 44.

📋 Key Distinction

While internal bracing is mechanically essential for protecting frail primary repairs, a 2023 KSSTA systematic review found no significant long-term clinical advantages in PROMs, knee laxity, or graft failure rates when robust autograft reconstructions are augmented with an internal brace Ref 49.

Dynamic Intraligamentary Stabilization (DIS / Ligamys) LOE II

DIS utilizes the Ligamys device—an intra-osseous spring-screw mechanism implanted in the tibia designed to maintain constant, dynamic tension on the repairing ACL throughout full range of motion Ref 17.

⚠ Clinical Drawbacks of DIS
  • Re-rupture rate: 20.8%–29% Ref 1
  • Hardware removal rate markedly elevated vs. ACLR (OR = 8.19; 95% CI 2.89–23.20; p<0.05) Ref 13
  • High-tension spring mechanism causes hardware irritation, localized osteolysis, and soft-tissue inflammation
  • Significantly worse absolute joint position sense (JPS) errors vs. healthy controls, suggesting compromised proprioceptive recovery Ref 50

6. Comparative Meta-Analyses: Failure Rates, Laxity & PROMs

Recent meta-analyses (2024–2025) published in AJSM and BMC Surgery have aggregated robust data across thousands of patients to definitively compare modern ACL primary repair (inclusive of BEAR, IB, and DIS) versus autograft ACL reconstruction Ref 7, 13.

📊 Core Trade-Off

Primary repair facilitates accelerated early subjective recovery and complete avoidance of donor-site morbidity, but at the explicit cost of a markedly elevated risk of anatomical and clinical failure (approximately two to four times higher than reconstruction).

Interpretation

The aggregated data illustrates that modern ACL repair yields earlier return to sport and highly comparable subjective knee function. However, the overall risk of experiencing graft failure or requiring revision surgery is approximately two to four times higher following ACL repair compared to reconstruction Ref 7. The GRADE assessment of this evidence base remains "low to moderate" due to observational bias, lack of blinding, and study heterogeneity Ref 13.

⚡ Confounding Insight

The significantly faster return to sport observed in repair groups may act as a direct catalyst for higher failure rates. By accelerating rehabilitation and permitting high-impact cutting maneuvers as early as 6 months, the biologically immature repairing ligament is subjected to extreme tensile loads before complete structural and cellular remodeling has occurred.

7. The Lateral Extra-Articular Shift

Leading medical journals are publishing pivotal Level I RCTs that actively reshape surgical algorithms, shifting focus away from primary repair toward reinforcing highly stable reconstructions in vulnerable populations.

The Santy Orthopedic Center Trial (STABILITY / ALLR) LOE I

Published in The Lancet Regional Health – Europe (December 2025), this landmark prospective RCT evaluated 593 young, active patients (aged 18–35) at high risk for graft failure, comparing isolated ACLR to ACLR combined with Anterolateral Ligament Reconstruction (ALLR) using hamstring autografts Ref 53.

The addition of a Lateral Extra-Articular Procedure (LEAP) effectively neutralizes severe rotatory pivot-shift forces that typically overload the intra-articular graft Ref 30. This robust Level I evidence heavily influences current AAOS and ESSKA recommendations, steering clinicians toward protecting autografts with anterolateral augmentation rather than experimenting with primary repairs in high-demand youth cohorts Ref 28.

The ACL SNNAP Trial LOE I

Funded by the NIHR and published in The Lancet portfolio, this trial compared a "rehabilitation-first" strategy (with option for delayed surgery) to immediate surgical reconstruction for non-acute ACL injuries with persistent symptomatic instability Ref 55. At 18 months, patients randomized to immediate surgical reconstruction demonstrated substantially superior functional outcomes, knee stability, and overall satisfaction compared to non-surgical management.

⚠ Implication for Primary Repair

This trial cements the absolute necessity of surgical intervention for chronic functional instability—a scenario where primary repair is universally contraindicated due to inevitable tissue retraction and devascularization Ref 36.

The Ongoing ACL STARR Trial Ongoing

The international ACL STARR (Anterior Cruciate Ligament Stratified Accelerated Repair or Reconstruction) trial is currently underway (2024–2028), supported by the NIHR and NHMRC with $2.5 million in collaborative funding. This single-blind RCT directly compares proximal ACL repair against standard ACL reconstruction Ref 57. Upon completion, it will provide the highest echelon of prospective evidence regarding precise indications, cost-effectiveness, and long-term joint health implications.

8. Rehabilitation & Return to Sport

The 2024–2025 ESSKA-AOSSM-AASPT consensus strictly mandates that rehabilitation protocols pivot from arbitrary time-based assumptions to rigid, criterion-based milestones Ref 22.

Mitigating Re-Injury Risk

Survival analyses demonstrate that younger age at primary surgery and earlier time to RTS are independent, significant risk factors for secondary injury. Crucially, for every 1-month delay in returning to sport, the risk of secondary ACL injury decreases by 13% Ref 2. Yet, systematic reviews reveal that the mean time for RTS testing remains perilously early, averaging 7.3 months post-reconstruction Ref 59.

RTS Clearance Criteria

Clearance for unrestricted sporting activity must strictly require:

  • Limb Symmetry Index (LSI) >90% on single-leg hop test batteries
  • Isokinetic quadriceps and hamstring strength symmetry
  • Absence of joint effusion Ref 9
  • Psychological readiness: Cognitive-behavioral interventions such as graded exposure (GE) therapy demonstrate superior outcomes, with athletes achieving RTS rates of 68.9% vs. 45.0% with standard care, alongside improvements in biomechanical landing symmetries Ref 61
✅ Rehabilitation Principle

Regardless of whether a patient undergoes primary repair or autograft reconstruction, success is intrinsically tied to the quality and pacing of postoperative rehabilitation. Criterion-based, not time-based, progression is mandatory.

9. Conclusions

The current evidence-based landscape reflects a highly nuanced intersection of anatomical pathology, biomechanical engineering, and precise patient demographics. While pioneering preservation techniques—most notably BEAR and Internal Suture Bracing—have successfully overcome the catastrophic limitations of historical open primary repair, they have not unseated Autograft ACL Reconstruction as the universal gold-standard intervention.

Primary ACL repair demonstrates undeniable value in preserving native neurosensory proprioception, maintaining flexor/extensor muscle ratios by eliminating harvest-site morbidity, and accelerating early subjective clinical recovery. However, its Achilles' heel remains a persistent, two-to-four-fold increase in failure rates. This is disastrously magnified in high-demand patients under 21 years of age.

📋 Current Evidence-Based Algorithm

Primary repair is indicated almost exclusively for mature adults presenting with acute, proximal (Sherman Type I) avulsions of excellent tissue quality. For the vast majority of the at-risk population—particularly high-demand young athletes—the trajectory of surgical innovation favors mechanical augmentation of traditional ACL reconstructions via Lateral Extra-Articular Procedures (LEAPs) such as ALLR or LET, which dramatically lower graft failure risk and preserve long-term meniscal and chondral health.

As ongoing landmark trials such as ACL STARR reach maturity, the precise algorithmic boundaries defining when to repair and when to reconstruct will continue to crystallize, ultimately driving highly individualized, evidence-based orthopaedic care.
