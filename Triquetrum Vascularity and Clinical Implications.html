1. Introduction to Carpal Anatomy & Triquetral Biomechanics

The human carpus comprises eight carpal bones arranged in proximal and distal rows, facilitating the multiplanar mobility and dynamic stability required for global hand function. Within this framework, the triquetrum (os triquetrum, pyramidal bone) occupies a critical position at the ulnar (medial) aspect of the proximal carpal row, lying distal to the ulnocarpal joint and beneath the pisiform bone.Ref 1

Articular & Non‑Articular Surfaces

The triquetrum is characterized by its distinct pyramidal morphology with several unique surfaces:Ref 1

  • Superior (proximal) surface: Lateral convex portion interfaces with the TFCC and articular disk; medial portion remains rough and non‑articular.
  • Inferior (distal) surface: Sinuously curved and concave, articulating with the hamate bone.
  • Lateral base: Flat quadrilateral facet for the lunotriquetral joint.
  • Palmar (volar) surface: Hosts an isolated oval facet exclusively for the pisiform bone (sesamoid within the FCU tendon).

Biomechanical Role

From a biomechanical perspective, the triquetrum functions as an indispensable stabilization node during radioulnar deviation and flexion‑extension kinematics.Ref 5 It transmits substantial forces across the ulnocarpal and midcarpal joints, undergoing complex helicoidal and rotational motions across the convex hamate surface. As a primary component of the proximal row—which acts as an intercalated segment without direct tendinous insertions—the triquetrum relies entirely on capsuloligamentous constraints for stability.Ref 6

⚠ Critical Dependency The integrity of the triquetrum's osseous and ligamentous architecture is inextricably dependent upon a reliable, redundant, and highly specialized vascular network. Disruptions—whether from acute trauma, repetitive microtrauma, or systemic microvascular disease—precipitate fracture nonunion and avascular necrosis (AVN).Ref 7

2. Ligamentous Architecture as a Vascular Conduit

Unlike diaphyseal long bones with extensive periosteal surfaces, carpal bones are overwhelmingly covered by avascular articular cartilage.Ref 11 Nutrient vessels are therefore anatomically restricted to entering exclusively through limited non‑cartilaginous, rough areas where ligaments and joint capsules attach.Ref 8

Dorsal Ligamentous Complex

On the dorsal aspect, the triquetrum is stabilized by the dorsal radiocarpal ligament (radiotriquetral ligament), extending from the dorsal rim of the radius to the dorsal triquetrum.Ref 15 This is mechanically continuous with the dorsal intercarpal ligament (triquetroscaphoid ligament), originating on the triquetrum and extending transversely across the dorsal carpus.Ref 16 These dorsal extrinsic ligaments carry a dense plexus of arterioles from the dorsal intercarpal arch directly into the triquetral cortex.Ref 4

Palmar Ligamentous Complex

On the palmar aspect, the triquetrum receives insertions from the palmar ulnocarpal complex, including the ulnotriquetral and ulnolunate ligaments arising from the volar radioulnar ligament at the ulnar fovea and styloid base.Ref 20 The palmar scaphotriquetral ligament spans the volar surface, superficial to the deep arcuate ligament complex.Ref 15

Intrinsic Lunotriquetral Ligament

The lunotriquetral (LT) interosseous ligament binds the triquetrum to the lunate, forming a critical segment of the proximal row's continuous articular surface.Ref 6 Micro‑CT studies universally detect substantial nutrient vessels entering the triquetrum precisely at this ligamentous interface, demonstrating that intrinsic stabilizing structures are also vital components of the intraosseous perfusion network.Ref 4

🔑 Key Concept — Ligaments as "Biological Umbilicals" Intrinsic intercarpal ligaments are not merely mechanical tethers; they are critical biological conduits for arterial inflow. Disruption of the LT ligament involves immediate, albeit localized, traumatic devascularization of adjacent cortices.Ref 4

3. Extraosseous Arterial Anatomy of the Carpus

The carpal vascular architecture relies on multiple, overlapping collateral pathways fed by three main descending conduits: the radial artery, ulnar artery, and anterior interosseous artery (with minor posterior interosseous contributions).Ref 7 These branch and converge at the radiocarpal and midcarpal joints to form transverse arterial arches connected by longitudinal anastomoses.

Palmar Carpal Arch System

The deep palmar arch, formed by direct anastomosis of the terminal radial artery and deep palmar branch of the ulnar artery, is the most distal and hemodynamically significant volar arcade.Ref 9 As the ulnar artery traverses Guyon's canal—lateral to the pisiform and superficial to the flexor retinaculum—it bifurcates, with the deep branch completing the deep vascular arch distal to the hook of the hamate.Ref 16

Recurrent arteries (radial and ulnar) arise from the deep palmar arch and traverse proximally to anastomose with terminal branches of the anterior interosseous artery, constituting the major volar collateral circulation.Ref 9

Dorsal Carpal Arch System

The dorsal system is supplied by the dorsal carpal branch of the radial artery, dorsal branch of the ulnar artery, and terminal interosseous branches.Ref 3 These feed two primary transverse arcades: the dorsal radiocarpal arch and the dorsal intercarpal arch (dICa).Ref 4 The dICa is generally the largest, coursing transversely across the distal proximal row and serving as the primary extraosseous source for both the lunate and triquetrum.

Tripartite Supply to the Triquetrum

★ Unique Triquetral Vascular Advantage

The triquetrum is classified as a highly vascularized bone receiving nutrient supply from a tripartite source:

  • Dorsal intercarpal arch (dICa)
  • Palmar intercarpal arch (pICa)
  • Direct arterial branches from the ulnar artery — uniquely distinguishing the triquetrum from adjacent carpal bones

This direct ulnar artery contribution, combined with diffuse arch‑based collateral inflow, makes the triquetrum's extraosseous perfusion arguably the most robust and redundant in the entire carpus.Ref 4

4. Nutrient Foramina: The Extraosseous–Intraosseous Interface

The transition from extraosseous arterial network to intraosseous medullary microcirculation occurs at the nutrient foramina. Their spatial distribution, density, and cross‑sectional area dictate the internal vascular watershed and biological resilience against ischemia.Ref 14

Topographical Distribution

Unlike long bones with a single dominant nutrient artery, the triquetrum features a multi‑focal, highly distributed vascular entry system.Ref 4

  • Total nutrient vessels: The triquetrum is penetrated by an average of ≥7 distinct nutrient vessels per bone (exact figure varies by specimen; some sources report up to 12), reflecting massive vascular redundancy.Ref 4
  • Dorsal predominance: An average of ≥5 vessels enter exclusively from the dorsal non‑articular surface, compared to only 1–2 vessels from the palmar surface.Ref 4
  • Dorsal vessel characteristics: Dorsal vessels are significantly wider in diameter and possess significantly higher total vascular volume than palmar counterparts (p < 0.05).Ref 4
  • Medial and distal aspects: Present minor foraminal penetrations, often restricted to the distal half of the rough dorsal surface.Ref 1

5. The Gelberman & Panagis Classification (1983)

The foundational understanding of intraosseous carpal microcirculation was established through pioneering injection and chemical clearing studies by Gelberman, Panagis, Taleisnik, and Baumgaertner in the early 1980s.Ref 8

Methodology

In 1983, Gelberman et al. utilized the Spalteholz clearing technique on 25 fresh human cadaveric limbs. Colored or radio‑opaque medium (latex or barium sulfate) was injected under physiological pressure into the radial and ulnar arteries. Carpal bones were harvested, decalcified, dehydrated, and submerged in Spalteholz fluid (methyl salicylate + benzyl benzoate), rendering the hydroxyapatite matrix optically transparent for direct visualization of internal vascular arborization.Ref 8 Ref 10

Classification Criteria

Categorization was based on three critical variables:Ref 8

  1. Absolute size and anatomical location of nutrient vessels
  2. Presence, absence, or density of functional intraosseous anastomoses
  3. Physiological dependence of large volumetric areas on a single intraosseous vessel

Risk Category Vascular Characteristics Carpal Bones Clinical AVN Risk
Group I Large areas dependent on a single intraosseous vessel; absence of significant anastomoses Scaphoid, Capitate, 8–20% of Lunates HIGH
Group II Vessels from ≥2 distinct extraosseous areas; notably lacks internal anastomoses Trapezoid, Hamate LOW–MODERATE
Group III Robust nutrient arteries through ≥2 non‑articular surfaces; consistent, rich intraosseous anastomoses; no area dependent on single vessel Triquetrum, Trapezium, Pisiform, 80–92% of Lunates LOW
✓ The Triquetrum: Group III — Maximal Vascular Resilience Spalteholz specimens demonstrated extensive vascular inflow from both dorsal and palmar surfaces with highly consistent, macroscopic intraosseous anastomotic networks. Interruption of any single extraosseous source does not result in total ischemia to any portion of the bone.Ref 10 Ref 4

6. Contemporary Revelations: Micro‑Computed Tomography

While the Spalteholz technique provided invaluable two‑dimensional data, it was fundamentally limited in volumetric quantification and subject to decalcification artifacts. High‑resolution micro‑CT, paired with radiopaque contrast agents, has superseded chemical clearing, offering unprecedented fidelity.Ref 5 Ref 8

7. Surgical Safe Zones: The "Lateral Axis"

In modern orthopedic surgery—ORIF, percutaneous screw placement, transosseous bone tunnel drilling for ligament reconstruction—inadvertent obliteration of primary intraosseous vascular conduits can cause iatrogenic AVN or nonunion, particularly in Group I bones.Ref 5

Volumetric Density Mapping

The lateral axis is definitively the most appropriate trajectory for compression screws, K‑wires, or tendon grafts, minimizing risk of transecting the dense dorsal and palmar vascular plexuses.Ref 5 Integration of these micro‑CT‑derived safe zones into preoperative planning is expected to further reduce complication rates.

8. Clinical Pathology I — Triquetral Fractures

Triquetral fractures account for 15–19% of all carpal fractures, making the triquetrum the second most frequently fractured carpal bone after the scaphoid.Ref 18 Importantly, 20–50% occur concomitantly with distal radius fractures, ulnar styloid fractures, scaphoid fractures, or severe ligament tears.Ref 33

Morphological Classification

Table 4 — Morphological Classification of Triquetral FracturesRef 18
Fracture Type Mechanism Frequency Vascular Implications
Dorsal cortical (chip) fractures FOOSH → severe dorsiflexion/ulnar deviation or forced palmar flexion; direct shearing from ulnar styloid or avulsion by dorsal ligaments Most common Rich dorsal vascular bed → rapid healing; excellent prognosis
Body fractures High‑energy axial loading driving ulnar styloid or hamate proximal pole into the triquetral body (sagittal, coronal, or comminuted split) Less common Overlapping dorsal/palmar territories → low nonunion risk despite severity
Volar cortical (avulsion) fractures Extreme hyperextension; avulsion of palmar LT or ulnotriquetral ligaments Rarest Potentially most problematic for stability; moderate vascular concern

Diagnostic Challenge

Standard orthogonal radiographs frequently lack sensitivity due to complex overlapping osseous shadows. Clinical studies indicate up to 18.9% of triquetral fractures are missed on initial X‑ray.Ref 18 When ulnar‑sided wrist pain persists with localized triquetral tenderness (especially palpated with radial deviation), CT imaging is strongly indicated.

Healing Potential

The prognosis for triquetral fractures is directly linked to the bone's Group III vascular classification. For the ubiquitous dorsal chip fractures:

  • Conservative management with short arm cast or rigid wrist orthosis for 4–6 weeks is the gold standard.Ref 18
  • No significant difference in PROMs, ROM, or grip strength regardless of specific immobilization duration or modality.Ref 18
  • Nonunion and post‑traumatic AVN rates are exceedingly low.Ref 4

For displaced body fractures, surgical intervention (ORIF via the lateral axis safe zone) may be required, though the biological risk of nonunion remains remarkably low.Ref 12

9. Clinical Pathology II — Avascular Necrosis of the Triquetrum

AVN (osteonecrosis) represents cellular death of bone and marrow due to critical interruption of blood supply, leading to subchondral collapse and degenerative joint disease.Ref 36 While AVN frequently afflicts the lunate (Kienböck's disease) and scaphoid (Preiser's disease)—both Group I bones—triquetral AVN is exceedingly rare, a stark testament to its Group III classification.Ref 4 Ref 36

Pathophysiological Mechanisms

  • Extraluminal factors: Severe/crush trauma tears nutrient vessels; massive intraosseous edema increases intraosseous pressure → compression of micro‑capillary networks (bone compartment syndrome).Ref 36
  • Intraluminal factors: Systemic conditions induce focal clotting, micro‑emboli, or altered lipid metabolism (SLE, sickle cell disease, leukemia, coagulopathies).Ref 47 Corticosteroids promote fat embolization, adipocyte hypertrophy, and endothelial dysfunction.Ref 45

Diagnosis & Management

MRI is the gold standard for early AVN detection (~91% sensitivity). Normal carpal marrow shows homogeneous high T1 signal; AVN reveals focal/diffuse signal reduction on T1 and altered T2/STIR signals corresponding to cellular death, edema, and marrow replacement.Ref 36 Ref 23

  • Pre‑collapse disease: Immobilization + COX‑2 NSAIDs + bone‑health optimization (Vitamin C, calcium, Vitamin D).Ref 36
  • Advanced disease with collapse: Surgical salvage — primarily Proximal Row Carpectomy (PRC), excising scaphoid, lunate, and necrotic triquetrum to allow capitate‑radius articulation.Ref 45

10. Future Directions

The future paradigm lies in dynamic, in vivo vascular mapping:Ref 7

  • Ultra‑high‑field MRI (7‑Tesla+): Dynamic contrast‑enhanced sequences to visualize real‑time intraosseous capillary perfusion in living patients, predicting AVN or nonunion weeks before structural collapse.Ref 8
  • Patient‑specific 3D‑printed drill guides: Integration of normative micro‑CT vascular data into CAD software; guides molded from standard CT scans to physically restrict drill trajectories to the lateral axis safe zone during percutaneous pinning, fracture fixation, or ligament reconstruction.Ref 5

11. Conclusion

The vascular anatomy of the carpal triquetrum represents a masterpiece of evolutionary biomechanical design. Supplied extensively by the ulnar artery and the robust dorsal and palmar intercarpal arches, the triquetrum benefits from an immensely redundant extraosseous network transitioning through a high concentration of nutrient foramina predominantly on the dorsal surface. While this dorsal porosity creates structural vulnerability predisposing to cortical avulsion fractures, it simultaneously supplies a massive localized vascular volume that virtually guarantees rapid, uncomplicated consolidation.

Internally, the triquetrum's Group III Gelberman classification—with ≥7 distinct nutrient penetrations and robust intraosseous anastomoses—completely insulates it from the devastating ischemic pathologies that ravage adjacent carpal bones. AVN remains a true clinical anomaly requiring catastrophic compounding of insults to overcome the bone's inherent biological redundancies.

Modern micro‑CT has not only validated these historical classifications but has revolutionized surgical approaches by precisely mapping the lateral axis as a safe corridor for hardware placement. An exhaustive understanding of this vascular anatomy empowers surgeons to respect vital soft‑tissue attachments, confidently navigate intraosseous safe zones, and optimize both conservative and surgical management of complex wrist pathologies.
