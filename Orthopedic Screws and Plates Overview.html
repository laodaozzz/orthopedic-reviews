1. Introduction: The Evolution of Fracture Biomechanics and AO Principles

The modern era of orthopedic trauma surgery is fundamentally anchored in the biomechanical and biological doctrines established by the Arbeitsgemeinschaft für Osteosynthesefragen (AO Foundation), a collective formed in Switzerland in 1958. Prior to the codification of these principles, the management of musculoskeletal trauma frequently relied on prolonged plaster immobilization, traction, or rudimentary, non-standardized surgical interventions. These historical methods often resulted in devastating clinical outcomes, including profound joint stiffness, severe muscle atrophy, fracture malunion, or complete failure of the bone to heal (nonunion).1

The AO introduced a revolutionary paradigm shift by advocating for surgical interventions designed to achieve four core imperatives:

  • Precise anatomical reduction of fracture fragments
  • Stable internal fixation using standardized implants
  • Meticulous preservation of periosteal and endosteal blood supply
  • Facilitation of early, active, and pain-free mobilization of the patient

To translate these theoretical biological imperatives into reliable clinical reality, a highly sophisticated armamentarium of precision-engineered metallic implants—specifically orthopedic screws and plates—was developed, standardized, and continuously refined over the subsequent decades.1

The Strain Theory of Tissue Differentiation

At the core of the AO philosophy is the intricate understanding of bone mechanobiology—specifically the complex relationship between mechanical strain and tissue differentiation at the fracture site. Strain, defined as the deformation of the fracture gap relative to its original size under load, ultimately dictates the physiological pathway of bone healing.3

Tissues possess vastly different tolerances for mechanical strain:3

  • Granulation tissue can withstand and develop under up to 100% strain
  • Fibrous tissue and cartilage can tolerate approximately 10% strain
  • Mature lamellar bone can only form and survive in environments with less than 2% strain

Absolute vs. Relative Stability

Constructs providing "absolute stability" virtually eliminate all micromotion at the fracture site, keeping strain well below the 2% threshold. This environment enables primary (direct) bone healing—characterized by immediate advancement of osteoclast-led Haversian cutting cones across the fracture plane, followed by osteoblast deposition of new bone, completely bypassing the formation of a periosteal callus.3

Constructs providing "relative stability" permit a highly controlled degree of micromotion, keeping strain between 2% and 10%. This controlled mechanical stimulus promotes secondary (indirect) bone healing via endochondral ossification, resulting in a robust, visible callus.3

Orthopedic screws function essentially as highly efficient simple machines that convert applied rotational torque into linear compression or stable structural anchorage.7 Plates function as sophisticated internal splints or tension bands that neutralize pathological forces—such as axial loading, bending, torsion, and shear—that would otherwise disrupt the delicate healing tissues at the fracture site.8

2. Anatomical and Structural Classification of Orthopedic Screws

Screws are the most ubiquitous and frequently inserted implants in orthopedic surgery, utilized either as independent fixation devices or as critical anchoring components in conjunction with plates, intramedullary nails, and external fixators.10 A surgical bone screw is a highly engineered mechanical device composed of specific anatomical zones:13

  • Head: Provides a precisely shaped recess (star-shaped, hexagonal, or cross-shaped) for screwdriver attachment; the undersurface arrests forward motion against cortical bone or plate rim.7
  • Core (shaft): Dictates overall bending strength and determines pilot drill bit size. Bending strength is exponentially related to the radius of the core.7
  • Thread: Generates frictional grip and pull-out resistance. The pitch—the linear distance traveled during one 360° rotation—governs advancement speed and bone engagement density.7
  • Tip: May be blunt (requiring a pre-tapped track) or feature fluted cutting edges (self-tapping).7

2.1 Cortical versus Cancellous Bone Screws

Cortical screws are engineered for dense diaphyseal bone. They feature a relatively large core diameter and a shallow, fine thread pitch, allowing a maximum number of threads to engage within the relatively thin but intensely hard cortical layer, maximizing pull-out resistance.7

Cancellous screws are designed for the porous metaphyseal and epiphyseal bone. They feature a thin central core relative to wide, deep threads, resulting in a substantially larger thread-to-core diameter ratio.16 The coarse pitch allows maximum volume of soft trabecular bone to be captured between each thread revolution. Cancellous screws are frequently manufactured with a partially threaded shaft, inherently facilitating interfragmentary compression when fixing metaphyseal fractures.7

2.2 Locking versus Non-Locking Screws

Non-locking screws possess a smooth undersurface on the head. When driven into bone through a standard plate hole, further torque generates immense axial tension, producing active compressive force that pulls the bone against the plate. This frictional mechanism is the sole source of stability in conventional plating.19

Limitations of Non-Locking Constructs

This forceful compressive footprint can severely compromise the periosteal vascular network, potentially leading to localized cortical ischemia, necrosis, delayed fracture healing, and increased susceptibility to deep infection. In osteoporotic bone, threads may strip before adequate plate-to-bone compression is achieved.19

Locking head screws (LHS) feature external threads on the head that mate precisely with internal threads in Locking Compression Plate (LCP) holes, physically locking rigidly at a fixed angle.19 Crucially, because the screw locks into the plate itself, it arrests before forcefully pulling bone toward the plate. The resulting construct functions as an internal fixator rather than a frictional device, offering significantly higher resistance to shear and torsional forces, preventing screw toggle, and preserving periosteal blood flow.3

3. Structural Design versus Biomechanical Function: Lag and Position Screws

A profound and frequently misunderstood concept in AO principles is the strict decoupling of a screw's physical anatomy from its biomechanical function. Structural design refers to the static, engineered properties of the implant (fully vs. partially threaded, core diameter, pitch). Biomechanical function describes the dynamic mechanical state achieved in vivo, which is entirely dictated by the surgeon's operational technique.14

3.1 The Lag Screw Technique: Achieving Interfragmentary Compression

The explicit biomechanical function of a lag screw is to generate active, intense, and static compression across a fracture plane, creating the pinnacle of absolute stability, eliminating pathological micromotion, and providing the optimal mechanical environment for direct primary bone healing.4

Non-Negotiable Requirement: The threads must purchase only in the far bone fragment. If threads engage the near fragment, compression is physically impossible.7

The standardized AO lag screw technique for cortical bone:7

  1. Anatomical Reduction Fracture fragments are perfectly aligned and held rigidly using pointed reduction forceps.
  2. Gliding Hole Preparation The near cortex is drilled using a bit matching the outer thread diameter (e.g., 4.5 mm bit for a 4.5 mm screw). Threads pass smoothly through without gripping.
  3. Pilot Hole Preparation A drill sleeve centers the trajectory; the far cortex is drilled using a smaller bit matching the core diameter (e.g., 3.2 mm for a 4.5 mm screw).
  4. Countersinking The outer rim of the gliding hole is beveled, vastly increasing contact surface area for the screw head. Failing to countersink creates severe stress risers.
  5. Measurement, Tapping, and Insertion Depth is measured, far cortex tapped (if needed), and screw inserted. Continued torque pulls the far fragment toward the near fragment, generating intense interfragmentary compression.

Key note: Partially threaded cancellous screws inherently function as lag screws—smooth proximal shaft acts as a gliding zone.7 Modern headless compression screws (e.g., Herbert screw) achieve lag function through variable pitch geometry.24

3.2 The Position Screw Technique: Neutral Stabilization

The biomechanical function of a "position screw" is to hold two bone fragments in a specific anatomical relationship without compressing them.7

Clinical applications include:

  • Syndesmotic stabilization: After severe rotational ankle fractures, compressing the tibiofibular syndesmosis would pathologically narrow the ankle mortise and lead to osteoarthritis.7
  • Highly comminuted fractures: When intermediate fragments are pulverized or missing, a lag screw would collapse the joint surface or shorten the bone.28
  • Maxillofacial surgery: Bilateral sagittal split osteotomies require rigid holding without altering mandibular geometry.27

Technique: The surgeon drills a uniform, continuous pilot hole across both cortices using only the core-diameter bit (e.g., 3.2 mm through all cortices for a 4.5 mm screw). As the screw advances, threads engage both fragments simultaneously at the same linear rate, preserving the spatial relationship.7

4. The Five Fundamental Functions of Orthopedic Plates

Regardless of the modern plate's physical shape, the AO recognizes exactly five fundamental biomechanical modes of application. These are functions, not physical descriptions—a single versatile implant (e.g., standard LC-DCP) can serve any of these functions depending on fracture morphology and surgical sequence.9

4.1 Protection (Neutralization) Plating

Deployed in conjunction with lag screw fixation. While a lag screw provides exceptional interfragmentary compression, it is vulnerable to external bending, torsional, and axial loading. The plate acts as a mechanical bypass, absorbing and neutralizing physiological loads, transmitting them safely from proximal to distal intact bone, shielding the fragile lag screw interface.5

Applications: Simple oblique, spiral, or butterfly fractures → Absolute stability → Primary (direct) healing.

4.2 Compression Plating

Used to generate absolute stability in transverse or short oblique diaphyseal fractures where steep geometry precludes independent lag screws. Achieved via two methods:8

  • Articulated external tension device that physically pulls the plate before final screws are secured
  • Dynamic Compression Principle: Plate holes with sloped, spherical shoulders force eccentrically placed screw heads to slide toward center, translating bone fragments toward the fracture site

Critical Technique — Pre-bending: The plate must be slightly pre-bent so its center stands off the bone by 1–2 mm at the fracture site. Without pre-bending, compressing the near cortex causes the far cortex to hinge and gap open.3

Applications: Transverse/short oblique diaphyseal fractures → Absolute stability → Primary healing.

4.3 Bridging Plating

A deliberate departure from absolute stability. In highly comminuted injuries, attempting to reduce every shattered fragment requires massive periosteal stripping, leading to bone necrosis. Bridge plating relies on relative stability: the plate is anchored only to proximal and distal major fragments, spanning the "zone of injury" without touching fracture fragments.5

Long plates with widely spaced screws (low screw density) maximize working leverage. The biological environment is optimized for rapid secondary bone healing through callus formation.9

Applications: Highly comminuted diaphyseal fractures → Relative stability → Secondary (callus) healing.

4.4 Buttress (Antiglide) Plating

Exclusively applied to metaphyseal/epiphyseal regions for unstable partial articular fractures (tibial plateau, distal radius, Hoffa fractures). Acts as a rigid retaining wall to block and resist the sliding trajectory of articular fragments along shear planes.5

The plate must be accurately contoured or deliberately slightly under-contoured to act as a retaining spring. Anchored to stable diaphyseal bone first, then traps the articular fragment.33

Applications: Metaphyseal/epiphyseal partial articular fractures → Absolute stability → Primary healing.

4.5 Tension Band Plating

Transforms pathological tensile forces into therapeutic compressive forces. Eccentrically loaded bones (femur, olecranon, patella) have a tension side (convex) and a compression side (concave). A plate applied to the convex tension cortex prevents distraction, forcibly redirecting forces through the bone to generate dynamic compression across the opposite cortex.31

Absolute Requirement: The cortex opposite the plate must be structurally intact to withstand the massive compressive loads. If comminuted, the construct will fail.34

Applications: Eccentrically loaded bones with intact opposite cortex → Absolute stability → Primary healing.

5. Current Clinical Epidemiology and Implant Utilization Trends

The global landscape of orthopedic trauma relies immensely on internal fixation devices, with over 7 million fracture fixation surgeries performed annually in the United States alone, fueling an orthopedic plates and screws market projected to reach $34.45 billion by 2029.37

5.1 The Dominance of Variable Angle Locking Compression Plates (VA-LCP)

The standard Locking Compression Plate (LCP) and its advanced derivative, the Variable Angle LCP (VA-LCP), have achieved absolute clinical and market dominance, largely supplanting traditional compression plates.21

The LCP system features "combi-holes" that accept both conventional cortical screws and locking head screws, allowing hybrid techniques: gross reduction with conventional screws, then permanent neutralization with locking screws.3

The VA-LCP has rapidly become the undisputed gold standard for complex peri-articular fractures (distal radius, distal femur, proximal tibia).41 VA-LCP systems allow surgeons to articulate the locking screw trajectory within a 15-degree cone before permanently locking, enabling meticulous customization of screw placement to capture tiny articular fragments while steering clear of joint penetration.41

5.2 Screw Utilization Trends

Despite 4.5 mm large-fragment cortical screws offering exponentially superior bending resistance, clinical surveys reveal that 3.5 mm small-fragment screws are the most frequently utilized globally.44 This highlights a deliberate clinical compromise: while 3.5 mm screws experience slightly higher fatigue and breakage rates, their smaller profile permits less invasive dissection, minimizes soft tissue irritation, and leaves smaller stress-riser holes for potential implant removal.11

Cannulated screws have experienced explosive growth, now representing a dominant sector of the $1.7 billion independent bone screw market.10 Their hollow central shaft allows advancement over a fluoroscopically-guided K-wire, deeply intertwined with the shift toward Minimally Invasive Plate Osteosynthesis (MIPO).50

5.3 Material Science Innovations

While standard 316L stainless steel has historically been foundational, current practice demonstrates a massive, irreversible transition toward titanium alloys (e.g., TiAl6Nb7). Titanium's Young's modulus more closely approximates human cortical bone, critically mitigating stress shielding—the localized osteopenia when an overly rigid implant deprives native bone of physiological loading.55

Perhaps the most profound shift is the rising prevalence of bioresorbable screws and pins (28% growth rate recently).37 Manufactured from polylactic acid (PLA), polyglycolic acid (PGA), or biodegradable magnesium alloys, these implants provide initial stability, then undergo controlled degradation, progressively transferring physiological loads back onto healing bone without requiring a second operation.37

Additionally, antimicrobial coatings utilizing active silver ions, iodine, and antibiotic-polymer composites are being integrated into plate systems to render metallic interfaces hostile to bacterial adhesion.57

6. Conclusion

The ongoing evolution of orthopedic plates and screws is a profound testament to the rigorous application of biomechanical engineering to human physiology. The foundational principles established by the AO have systematically standardized an inherently chaotic surgical environment by demanding an exact, scientific understanding of physical forces.

The critical distinction between structural design and dynamic biomechanical function—exemplified by identical cortical screws deployed in either compressive "lag" or neutral "position" modes—requires profound surgical intent and mastery of basic physics. Similarly, the realization that a single metal plate can neutralize forces, compress bone, bridge comminution, buttress shear, or actively band tension highlights that biological stability is derived from the intelligent application of physics, not merely the presence of metal.

Driven by rapidly shifting epidemiological trends toward an aging, osteoporotic population, modern trauma practice has pivoted aggressively toward anatomically pre-contoured VA-LCP systems. Supported by minimally invasive cannulated screw technologies, superior biocompatibility of titanium alloys, and emerging bioresorbable structures, the future of fracture management aims to achieve an ultimate clinical balance: providing the uncompromising mechanical stability demanded by original AO principles while maintaining maximum biological respect for the vital microvascular environment of the healing human skeleton.
