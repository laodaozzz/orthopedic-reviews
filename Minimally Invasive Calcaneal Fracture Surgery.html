1. Executive Summary

Displaced intra-articular calcaneal fractures (DIACFs) are profoundly disabling injuries with long-term health outcomes comparable to those of myocardial infarction or chronic renal disease. LOE I The historical gold standard — open reduction and internal fixation (ORIF) via the extensile lateral approach (ELA) — provides excellent exposure but carries wound complication rates of 5.8% to 43%. LOE II This has catalyzed a paradigm shift towards minimally invasive surgery (MIS).

Key Thesis The sinus tarsi approach (STA) and percutaneous screw fixation (PSF) are not interchangeable. The optimal technique is critically dependent on fracture pattern and complexity as defined by the Sanders classification. PSF excels for simple patterns (Sanders II, tongue-type), while STA is superior for complex comminuted fractures (Sanders III) requiring direct articular reconstruction. LOE II

Both techniques are technically demanding with significant learning curves — approximately 20–26 cases for STA proficiency LOE III — underscoring the importance of surgeon experience in achieving predictable results.

2. Introduction: The Clinical Challenge

2.1 Epidemiology & Pathoanatomy

Calcaneal fractures are the most frequently encountered tarsal bone injuries, constituting 1–2% of all fractures and approximately 60% of all tarsal bone fractures. LOE I Of these, 70–75% are displaced and involve the articular surface of the subtalar joint (DIACFs). The characteristic mechanism is high-energy axial loading (fall from height, motor vehicle accident). LOE I

The axial impact generates shear and compression forces that produce a predictable pathoanatomic pattern:

  • Primary shear fracture line — divides calcaneus into medial and lateral portions, splitting the posterior facet
  • Secondary compression fracture line — runs behind the joint, dividing bone into anterior and posterior segments
  • Resultant deformity: loss of calcaneal height, increased width (lateral wall blowout), and varus angulation of the tuberosity LOE I

The anatomical disruption centers on the subtalar joint, the primary weight-bearing articulation of the hindfoot. Even residual displacement of 2 mm can drastically alter contact pressures, predisposing to debilitating post-traumatic arthritis. LOE II Adjacent structures at risk include the peroneal tendons laterally, the flexor hallucis longus tendon medially, and the richly innervated subtalar and surrounding joints.

3. Classification Systems & Radiological Assessment

3.1 Essex-Lopresti Classification

Based on plain radiographs, this system describes two primary patterns based on the secondary fracture line orientation: LOE I

Joint Depression Type

Secondary line exits just behind the posterior facet. Articular surface is driven down into the calcaneal body.

Tongue-Type

Secondary line runs posteriorly, exiting through the tuberosity. Creates a large posterosuperior fragment with articular surface. Surgical emergency if Achilles tendon pull displaces fragment superiorly, threatening posterior heel skin viability.

3.2 Sanders CT Classification

The most widely used system for surgical planning. Based on coronal CT at the widest diameter of the posterior facet. LOE I

Type I — Nondisplaced

Displacement < 2 mm. Conservative management typically indicated.

Type II — Two-Part

Single fracture line of posterior facet. Amenable to PSF or STA. Good prognosis with appropriate fixation.

Type III — Three-Part

Typically centrally depressed fragment. STA preferred for direct visualization and elevation. Higher risk of post-traumatic arthritis.

Type IV — Comminuted

≥4 articular fragments. Consider ELA or primary subtalar arthrodesis. Worst prognosis.

Clinical Pearl The Sanders classification does not merely describe the fracture — it fundamentally dictates the surgical philosophy. Type II fractures with large, intact fragments may be amenable to indirect percutaneous reduction, while Type III/IV fractures almost necessitate direct visualization through an approach like the STA. LOE I

4. Sinus Tarsi Approach (STA)

4.1 Indications

The STA is indicated for Sanders Type II and Type III fractures, with its principal advantage in complex, comminuted fractures where direct anatomical reduction of the articular surface is essential. LOE II The defining feature is direct, targeted visualization of the posterior facet for precise elevation and reduction of depressed articular fragments.

4.2 Step-by-Step Operative Procedure

Patient Positioning

Lateral decubitus position with affected limb superior. A padded bump elevates the limb horizontally, enabling unobstructed lateral and axial fluoroscopic views without limb manipulation. LOE III

Incision & Dissection

A curvilinear incision of 3–7 cm is made over the sinus tarsi, starting ~1 cm inferior and anterior to the lateral malleolus tip, extending distally toward the fourth metatarsal base. LOE II The sural nerve and peroneal tendons are identified and protected. The sinus tarsi fat pad is excised. The extensor digitorum brevis origin may be reflected distally to enhance visualization.

Fracture Reduction

The most critical phase. Global calcaneal morphology is restored first using a Schanz pin in the posterior tuberosity as a "joystick" to restore length, height, and correct varus. LOE II An external distractor may supplement this maneuver. Then, under direct visualization through the sinus tarsi window, a periosteal elevator or bone tamp elevates depressed posterior facet fragments. The undamaged talar articular surface serves as a template. Reduction quality is confirmed via direct visualization, tactile probe feedback, and multi-planar fluoroscopy. Dry arthroscopy may be used as an adjunct for magnified verification. LOE II

Fixation & Closure

Provisional K-wire stabilization is followed by definitive fixation. For simpler patterns, 4.0 mm or 6.5 mm cancellous screws may suffice. For comminuted fractures, a low-profile pre-contoured calcaneal plate acts as a buttress on the lateral wall. A subchondral "kickstand" screw provides direct support beneath elevated articular fragments. LOE II

4.3 Screws vs. Plates Within the STA

A systematic review and meta-analysis comparing these fixation strategies within the STA found no significant difference in AOFAS scores, but plate fixation was significantly superior in: LOE I (systematic review/meta-analysis)

  • Restoring and maintaining calcaneal width
  • Preventing loss of Böhler's angle over time
  • Achieving smaller final intra-articular step-off
  • Lower incidence of fixation failure
Recommendation For complex, comminuted DIACFs (Sanders III), plate fixation via the STA is recommended over screws-only fixation for a more robust buttress construct. LOE I

5. Percutaneous Screw Fixation (PSF)

5.1 Indications

PSF is indicated for fractures amenable to closed, indirect reduction: LOE II

  • Tongue-type fractures — large, singular posterior fragment ideal for joystick manipulation
  • Sanders Type II fractures — less comminuted joint depression patterns
  • Patients with compromised soft tissues or significant comorbidities (diabetes, PVD, heavy smoking) where wound risk minimization is the overriding priority

Contraindication: Fractures >3 weeks old, as early healing makes closed reduction difficult. LOE II

5.2 Step-by-Step Procedure

Positioning & Imaging

Performed on a radiolucent table in lateral decubitus. The C-arm must be positioned for unimpeded acquisition of true lateral, Broden's (lateral oblique), and Harris (axial) views by rotating the machine alone — without moving the patient's leg — to maintain reduction during guide pin placement. LOE II

Indirect Reduction

The most challenging step. A Steinmann/Schanz pin in the tuberosity is used as a joystick for traction (length), inferior pull (height/Böhler's angle), and rotation (varus correction). LOE II For joint depression types, a blunt instrument inserted percutaneously can lever depressed fragments upward. Reduction is judged solely on fluoroscopy — requiring significant spatial reasoning. PSF is fundamentally a technique of fluoroscopically-guided reduction; screws maintain the achieved reduction.

Screw Fixation

Cannulated screws are placed through stab incisions. Common construct: posterior tuberosity to sustentaculum tali/anterior process, plus a subchondral screw from lateral to medial. 6.5 mm partially threaded cannulated screws provide the most robust fixation strength. LOE III (biomechanical)

Limitation of PSF The technique is poorly suited for multifragmentary, depressed fractures (Sanders III) because there is no reliable percutaneous maneuver to selectively engage and elevate multiple small, independent articular pieces without direct visualization. PSF suitability is a direct function of fracture morphology.

6. Comparative Clinical and Radiological Outcomes

6.1 Functional Recovery (AOFAS Scores)

When evaluating the entire cohort of DIACF patients, multiple comparative studies with 8–10 year follow-up found no statistically significant difference in overall AOFAS hindfoot scores between STA and PSF groups. LOE II

However, this "overall equivalence" masks a clinically crucial distinction when results are stratified by fracture severity:

  • For Sanders Type III fractures, STA demonstrates a significantly higher rate of good-to-excellent outcomes: 76.9% (STA) vs. 33.3% (PSF) in one study; 75% vs. 20% in another. LOE II
  • PSF groups showed better scores on sagittal/hindfoot motion subscales (possibly reflecting less soft-tissue scarring)
  • STA groups reported less postoperative pain in some studies
Critical Interpretation An uncritical reading of "overall" equivalence is misleading. The clinically actionable information lies in the subgroup analysis by Sanders classification. Direct articular visualization and robust fixation of the STA are necessary for good outcomes in complex (Type III) fractures. LOE II

Calcaneal width restoration is the most consistent radiological difference favoring STA. A persistently widened heel can cause subfibular impingement — compression of peroneal tendons between the lateral malleolus and the widened lateral wall, resulting in chronic lateral hindfoot pain and dysfunction. LOE II

6.3 Pedobarographic Insights

A novel study utilizing dynamic pedobarography challenged the reliance on traditional scoring systems. LOE II Despite the STA group having better AOFAS and radiological outcomes, this group demonstrated significant deficiencies in pressure and force distribution during walking. This paradoxical finding suggests that standard clinical scores may be insensitive to subtle but important biomechanical deficits in gait, potentially missing nuances of altered joint loading with long-term consequences.

The entire rationale for MIS techniques is a direct response to the soft-tissue failures of the ELA. PSF carries the lowest wound risk due to minimal tissue disruption (stab incisions only). LOE II

7.2 Qualitative Risk Differences

The complication profiles reveal a qualitative difference in the nature of risk:

  • PSF risk is primarily technical — potential for malreduction or hardware malposition due to the "blind" nature of the procedure
  • STA risk is primarily biological — potential for wound healing issues related to the incision

A surgeon's choice thus involves weighing the risk of a technical complication (PSF) against the risk of a healing complication (STA).

7.3 Long-Term Sequelae

Post-traumatic subtalar arthritis is the most common and feared long-term complication regardless of treatment method. LOE I Its development is strongly correlated with injury severity (Sanders classification) and quality of articular reduction. No significant difference in secondary arthrodesis rates between STA and PSF suggests that even an anatomical reduction may not fully abrogate arthritis risk predetermined by the initial high-energy impact. LOE II

8. Special Considerations

8.1 Learning Curve

Both techniques have steep learning curves that significantly influence outcomes. LOE II

  • STA learning curve: Proficiency after approximately 20–26 cases. Mean operative time decreases from 124 to 85 minutes after 24 cases. Radiation exposure and reduction quality also improve significantly. LOE III
  • PSF learning curve: Not quantified as precisely, but complete reliance on indirect reduction and complex fluoroscopic interpretation suggests an equally challenging process. LOE III
Implication The learning curve is a major confounding variable in the literature. Reported "efficacy" of a technique is inextricably linked to the surgeon's position on their learning curve. These complex fractures should ideally be managed by experienced surgeons at specialty trauma centers. LOE II

8.2 Postoperative Rehabilitation

Rehabilitation is lengthy and crucial, with similar general principles for both techniques:

  • Phase I (Weeks 0–2): Strict non-weight-bearing (NWB), posterior splint, elevation, ice, pain management. Gentle active toe ROM only.
  • Phase II (Weeks 2–8): Transition to removable CAM boot. Gentle active/passive ankle and subtalar ROM. Strict NWB maintained.
  • Phase III (Weeks 8–12): Based on radiographic healing, progressive weight-bearing initiated (touch-down → partial → full over several weeks).
  • Phase IV (>12 weeks): Formal physical therapy for gait mechanics, strength, endurance, balance, and proprioception retraining.

This 8–12 week non-weight-bearing period is a major contributor to overall morbidity independent of surgical technique, with significant socioeconomic consequences and physiological effects including disuse atrophy and joint stiffness. LOE III

9. Evidence-Based Technique Selection Algorithm

1

Initial Assessment

High-quality plain radiographs (lateral + axial) and multi-planar CT scan. Assess soft-tissue envelope for "wrinkle sign" indicating readiness for surgery. Evaluate risk factors: smoking, diabetes, PVD.

Fracture Classification

3

Technique Selection by Fracture Pattern

→ PSF for: Sanders Type II (minimal comminution), Tongue-type fractures, patients with compromised soft tissues/comorbidities.
→ STA for: Sanders Type III, comminuted Type II, joint depression not amenable to closed reduction, significant lateral wall comminution.
→ ELA / Primary Arthrodesis for: Sanders Type IV with unreconstructable comminution.

↓
4

Factor in Surgeon Experience

Early learning curve surgeons should begin with simpler patterns (Sanders II) and consider referral of complex cases (Sanders III/IV) to subspecialty-experienced surgeons.

9.2 Concluding Remarks

The evolution from the extensile lateral approach to minimally invasive techniques has been one of the most significant advances in DIACF management. The evidence clearly demonstrates that the key to optimizing outcomes is not declaring one technique universally superior, but thoughtfully applying the correct technique to the specific fracture pattern and patient. LOE I

Looking forward, high-quality prospective RCTs stratifying patients by Sanders classification are needed. Integration of advanced technologies — patient-specific 3D-printed models, intraoperative navigation systems — may flatten the learning curve. The continued use of sophisticated objective outcome measures, such as dynamic pedobarography, will deepen our understanding of the true functional consequences of these injuries.
