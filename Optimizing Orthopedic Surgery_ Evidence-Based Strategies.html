High-Value Care in Orthopedic Surgery

An evidence-based framework for patient selection, preoperative optimization, and mitigation of unnecessary procedures

1. The Challenge of Overuse & Variation

The escalating volume and cost of orthopedic surgery have brought increased scrutiny to the value and appropriateness of many common procedures. A growing body of evidence reveals significant overuse, a weak evidentiary foundation for many interventions, and wide practice variation driven by non-clinical factors. [1]

⚠ Key Finding

An unnecessary back surgery is performed on an older adult every eight minutes, costing Medicare up to $600 million annually. Over 200,000 procedures met overuse criteria over a three-year period, totaling an estimated $2 billion. [2]

Identifying Low-Value Procedures

Table 1 — Overuse Criteria for Common Spinal Procedures (Lown Institute Analysis)
Procedure Overuse Criterion Key Exclusions
Spinal Fusion / Laminectomy Low-back pain without red-flag indicators (radicular symptoms, trauma, herniated disc, discitis, spondylosis, myelopathy, radiculopathy, scoliosis) Neurological compromise; major structural deformity
Vertebroplasty Osteoporotic spinal fractures without complications Bone cancer, myeloma, hemangioma

Institutional Variation

Even among prestigious hospitals, overuse rates diverge dramatically. Between 2019–2021, UC San Diego Health had an overuse rate of just 1.2%, whereas the Hospital of the University of Pennsylvania had a rate of 32.6%. [2] This disparity indicates that institutional culture and local practice patterns, rather than patient pathology, are powerful drivers of clinical decision-making.

Single-Provider Impact

At one high-overuse hospital, a single physician was responsible for 92% of the 336 procedures meeting overuse criteria. This demonstrates how the practice patterns of one high-volume surgeon can skew an entire institution's utilization metrics. [2]

2. The Evidence Gap

When subjected to the gold standard of clinical evidence—the randomized controlled trial (RCT)—many common elective orthopedic interventions fail to demonstrate superiority over less invasive, non-operative alternatives. [3]

Table 2 — Umbrella Review of Level-1 Evidence for Common Orthopedic Procedures (BMJ) LOE: I
Procedure Finding vs. Non-Operative Care Evidence Level
Carpal Tunnel Decompression Definitively superior to non-operative treatments I (RCT)
Total Knee Replacement Definitively superior to medication/physical therapy I (RCT)
Total Hip Replacement No RCTs comparing surgery to placebo/non-surgical treatment — critical evidence gap Not available
Arthroscopic Meniscal Repair No RCTs comparing surgery to placebo/non-surgical treatment Not available
Other Common Procedures (6) No significant difference in disability or function compared to non-surgical care I (RCT)

This lack of clear evidence creates a vast "grey zone" where many treatments offer scant benefit to most patients. The balance of benefits and harms varies substantially among individuals, creating an environment ripe for "preference misdiagnosis"—where a clinician delivers a service misaligned with the patient's values and preferences. [1]

Geographic & Provider-Level Drivers

A cross-sectional population-based study in Norway demonstrated that higher rates of knee arthroscopy were directly associated with a greater number of orthopedic surgeons in a geographic area, while higher rates of spine surgery were associated with a greater number of hospitals—not higher prevalence of spinal pathology. [4] LOE: II–III

3. Evidence-Based Non-Operative Management

The most direct strategy to mitigate unnecessary surgery is to establish a robust, multi-modal, non-operative treatment pathway as the default standard of care for most degenerative orthopedic conditions. The principle of "conservative care first" ensures that patients exhaust safer, less invasive options before considering surgery. [5]

Therapeutic Modalities for Knee Osteoarthritis

Table 3 — Non-Operative Interventions for Knee OA (Evidence Summary)
Modality Description Evidence
Physical Therapy & Exercise Quadriceps strengthening, low-impact aerobic exercise (swimming, cycling), Tai Chi I
Weight Management Every pound exerts 3–7× greater force across the knee; weight loss improves pain, function, and stiffness II
Pharmacotherapy NSAIDs (oral/topical); acetaminophen; topical analgesics I
Corticosteroid Injections Short-term relief (up to 12 weeks) for acute flare-ups I
Viscosupplementation Hyaluronic acid injections to lubricate the joint II
Assistive Devices Unloader braces, canes, walkers to reduce joint load II–III
PRP Therapy Patient's own growth factors to stimulate tissue repair I (RCTs showing improvement)

Nonradicular Low Back Pain — ACP Guideline

The American College of Physicians (ACP) strongly recommends non-drug therapies as the initial line of treatment for nonradicular low back pain. [15] LOE: I

Table 4 — ACP Recommendations for Nonradicular Low Back Pain
Line Intervention Notes

Key Insight

While the evidence supporting many elective surgeries is weak, the evidence for structured, multi-modal non-operative care for conditions like knee OA and low back pain is robust and consistently recommended by leading professional bodies. [11] The common surgical prerequisite of "failed conservative care" is often poorly defined and inadequately implemented.

4. Shared Decision-Making & Patient Selection

Shared Decision-Making (SDM) is a process by which physician and patient collaborate to arrive at an evidence-based treatment that best aligns with the patient's individual goals of care. [17] LOE: II

Decision-Making Models Compared

Table 5 — Models of Clinical Decision-Making
Model Physician Role Patient Role
Paternalistic Dictates the decision Passive recipient
Informed Provides information Decides alone
Shared Decision-Making Contributes clinical expertise, risks/benefits Contributes values, preferences, life goals

Research shows that Patient Decision Aids (PDAs) can directly reduce rates of expensive elective surgeries in favor of non-operative management by correcting information asymmetry and empowering patients to choose options aligned with their goals. [19]

CO-JR: 10-Point Holistic Preoperative Checklist

The Checklist Outpatient–Joint Replacement (CO-JR) shifts the assessment from "Can this joint be replaced?" to "Does this patient possess comprehensive resources for a successful outcome?" [25] LOE: III–IV

  1. Patient Education — Understanding of procedure, recovery, and role
  2. Patient Motivation — Commitment to active rehabilitation
  3. Medical Fitness — BMI ≤32 kg/m², HbA1c ≤7%, negative cotinine
  4. Physical Capability — 30-second sit-to-stand test
  5. Computer & Internet Capability — Readiness for telemedicine
  6. In-Home Support — Designated caregiver postoperatively
  7. Transportation — Access to follow-up appointments
  8. Financial Ability — Capacity to cover expected/unexpected costs
  9. Postoperative Self-Sufficiency — Task management during recovery
  10. Cognitive Ability — Screen for impairment & delirium risk

Limitation noted in source: No single risk-stratification tool is currently comprehensive enough to capture all facets of patient risk; an integrated, multi-domain approach is necessary. [26]

Psychosocial Screening

Psychological state is as critical to surgical outcome as physiological condition. Surgeons are often unable to accurately identify high-catastrophizing patients through routine consultation, making validated screening tools mandatory. [32–37] LOE: II

Table 7 — Validated Psychosocial Screening Tools
Tool Construct Measured Clinical Threshold
PHQ-2 / PHQ-9 Depression Positive screen warrants further evaluation
Pain Catastrophizing Scale (PCS) Rumination, magnification, helplessness Score >30 = clinically significant; predicts poor pain control
Tampa Scale for Kinesiophobia (TSK) Fear of movement/re-injury Score >37 = high kinesiophobia; associated with activity avoidance

Patient-Reported Outcome Measures (PROMs)

PROMs capture a patient's health status directly from their perspective. Preoperative PROM scores are better predictors of recovery trajectory than physician-completed assessments. [44–47]

  • Generic: PROMIS Global-10 (physical, mental, social domains)
  • Condition-Specific: KOOS Jr. (knee), HOOS Jr. (hip), NDI (neck), ODI (lower back)

Advanced Imaging for Complex Cases

  • 3D-CT Modeling: Enables patient-specific virtual anatomy for precise implant planning and custom 3D-printed implant design in complex/revision THA. [52–57]
  • Weight-Bearing MRI: Images the joint under physiological load, revealing dynamic pathology (meniscal extrusion, cartilage wear) not visible on standard supine MRI. [58–61]

6. Prehabilitation: Optimizing the Surgical Candidate

Prehabilitation is a multimodal strategy to optimize a patient's physical and psychological condition before surgery. A 2024 systematic review and meta-analysis of 48 RCTs (n > 3,500) confirmed that prehabilitation significantly improved preoperative function, health-related quality of life (HRQOL), and muscle strength. [67–69] LOE: I

💡 Prehabilitation as "Behavioral Stress Test"

A patient's engagement with a prehabilitation program—attending PT, quitting smoking, improving diet—serves as a powerful real-world predictor of their motivation and self-efficacy, and thus their likely adherence to postoperative rehabilitation protocols.

The AAOS Appropriate Use Criteria determined that interventions to address mental and social health were appropriate in nearly all clinical scenarios, providing a strong mandate for routine psychosocial screening and referral. [72–73]

Value-Based Care & Bundled Payments

The transition from fee-for-service to value-based care (VBC) aligns financial incentives with patient outcomes. Under bundled payments, a single payment covers all services for an episode of care (e.g., TKA + 90 days post-acute care). [90–91]

NYU Langone Bundled Payment Results

A three-year study demonstrated: decreased length of stay, discharges to inpatient rehab fell from 44% → 28%, 90-day readmission rates nearly halved, and average episode cost to CMS dropped by ~$7,000. [92]

When a hospital is financially responsible for the entire 90-day episode, a periprosthetic joint infection (PJI) from operating on a poorly controlled diabetic patient becomes not just a poor clinical outcome but a direct financial loss—creating powerful incentive alignment with evidence-based practices. [93]

8. Key Studies from the Literature

Note: The source document is a framework review, not a primary RCT report. The studies listed below are those explicitly cited and described within the source document. The document does not provide 5 individual landmark RCTs with sample sizes and endpoints; rather, it references umbrella reviews, cross-sectional analyses, and implementation studies.
  1. 1
    Lown Institute Medicare Analysis (2019–2021)
    Retrospective analysis of Medicare data. Found 200,000+ procedures meeting overuse criteria over 3 years, costing ~$2 billion. Identified dramatic institutional variation (1.2%–32.6% overuse rates). LOE: III
  2. 2
    BMJ Umbrella Review of 10 Common Orthopedic Procedures
    Extensive umbrella review of level-1 evidence. Only carpal tunnel decompression and total knee replacement demonstrated definitive superiority over non-operative care. Two procedures (THR, meniscal repair) lacked any RCTs vs. non-surgical treatment. LOE: I
  3. 3
    Norway Cross-Sectional Population-Based Study
    Demonstrated that surgical rates were driven by supply of medical resources (number of surgeons, hospitals) rather than disease prevalence. LOE: II–III
  4. 4
    2024 Prehabilitation Meta-Analysis (48 RCTs, n > 3,500)
    Multimodal prehabilitation (exercise + nutrition + psychology) significantly improved preoperative function, HRQOL, and muscle strength, with benefits extending into the postoperative period. LOE: I
  5. 5
    Multidisciplinary Lumbar Fusion Review (Spine journal)
    Mandatory multidisciplinary conference advocated for non-operative management in 58% of patients previously recommended for lumbar fusion by an outside surgeon. LOE: II
  6. 6
    German Second Opinion Program for Knee Arthroplasty
    Second opinion confirmed need for immediate surgery in only 40% of cases; 40% recommended only if condition worsened; 20% deemed not indicated. Increased patient confidence in decision. LOE: II
  7. 7
    NYU Langone Bundled Payment Study (3-year)
    Demonstrated decreased LOS, reduced discharges to rehab (44%→28%), nearly halved 90-day readmissions, and ~$7,000 per-episode cost reduction under bundled payment model. LOE: II

9. Proposed High-Value Care Pathway

An ideal high-value orthopedic care pathway integrates evidence at every stage of the patient journey: [Source]

1

Comprehensive Non-Operative Management (Default)

Mandatory multi-modal program (3–6 months): PT, weight management, pharmacotherapy, injections, patient education.

2

Multidisciplinary Surgical Evaluation

Only upon documented failure of optimized non-operative care. Formal review by multidisciplinary team (surgeons, physiatrists, pain specialists, PTs).

3

Holistic Shared Decision-Making

Multi-domain assessment (clinical risk, psychosocial, PROMs, imaging) presented via Patient Decision Aids, aligning decision with patient values.

4

Mandatory Multimodal Prehabilitation

Optimize medical risk factors (BMI, HbA1c, albumin, Hgb, smoking, opioids), exercise training, nutrition, and psychological preparation.

5

Value-Based Payment Framework

Bundle entire care continuum (non-op through 90-day postop) under value-based model aligning financial incentives with outcomes.
