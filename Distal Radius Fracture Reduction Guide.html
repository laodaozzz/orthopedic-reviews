1. Introduction & Epidemiology

Distal radius fractures constitute one of the most ubiquitous clinical entities encountered within orthopedic surgery and emergency medicine, representing an estimated 17.5 percent—approximately one-sixth—of all adult fractures presenting to emergency departments globally. Level I First anatomically described by Abraham Colles in 1814 at the Royal College of Surgeons in Dublin, the eponymous Colles fracture and its pathomechanical variants remain a cornerstone of acute trauma management.1

The fundamental objective of treating these injuries, whether through non-operative casting or operative internal fixation, is the meticulous restoration of native radiocarpal and radioulnar anatomy. Anatomical restoration is intrinsically linked to the preservation of grip strength, wrist kinematics, and the prevention of accelerated post-traumatic osteoarthropathy.2

Despite the paradigm shift over the past two decades toward operative intervention utilizing volar locking plate osteosynthesis, closed reduction and external immobilization via plaster casting or splinting remains the fundamental and most frequently utilized modality of care. Level I2

Bimodal Epidemiological Distribution

🦴 Pediatric Population

Forearm fractures account for roughly 40% of all pediatric long bone fractures. Peak incidence occurs during the metaphyseal growth spurt: 10–12 years in females and 12–14 years in males. The pediatric distal radius physis contributes 75% of longitudinal growth of the radius, endowing pediatric fractures with immense remodeling potential.3

🧓 Adult / Geriatric Population

In females over 50, incidence surges precipitously due to osteoporotic, mechanically compromised metaphyseal bone collapsing under low-energy falls from standing height onto an outstretched hand (FOOSH).2,9

📋 Indications for Closed Management
  • Undisplaced or minimally displaced extra-articular fractures
  • Stable displaced fractures following successful anatomic reduction
  • Pediatric physeal and metaphyseal injuries
  • Highly comminuted fractures in low-demand or elderly patients
  • Temporizing measure prior to operative fixation (to protect soft tissues)

2. Clinical Anatomy & Traumatic Pathomechanics

A profound comprehension of the osseous architecture, ligamentous constraints, and musculotendinous vectors of the distal forearm is the absolute prerequisite for executing a successful closed reduction.7

Key Anatomical Structures

  • Scaphoid & Lunate Fossae: Two distinct cartilage-lined concave facets on the distal radial articular surface, separated by a subtle anteroposterior central ridge.9
  • Sigmoid Notch & DRUJ: The ulnar margin articulates with the ulnar head forming the distal radioulnar joint—the pivot point for pronation/supination, stabilized primarily by the TFCC.2
  • TFCC Tears: Occur in approximately 50% of extra-articular and up to one-third of intra-articular distal radius fractures.9
  • Metaphyseal Stress Riser: The dense cortical bone of the radial diaphysis flares outward and thins dramatically, yielding to porous cancellous bone—creating a zone of structural vulnerability.3
  • Volar-Ulnar Corner: A critical structural keystone supporting the volar lunate facet via radiolunate ligament attachments; failure to recognize injury can result in volar carpal subluxation.2

3. Morphological Fracture Classification: Colles vs Smith

The morphology, angulation, and inherent stability of a distal radius fracture are rigidly dictated by the direction of the force vector, the magnitude of the impact, and the precise degree of wrist flexion or extension at the exact millisecond of failure.10

⚠️ Important Distinction

Barton's fractures (dorsal or volar articular margin shear with carpal subluxation) and die-punch fractures (lunate-driven impacted fragment into the lunate fossa) are intra-articular, ligamentously unstable variants that are virtually impossible to maintain with cast immobilization and routinely necessitate operative plating.2,6

Radiographic Indicators of Instability

🚨 High-Risk Fracture Features

Pre-reduction radiographs are the most reliable predictor of post-reduction stability.2 The following findings predict that even an anatomically perfect closed reduction will almost certainly displace within the cast:

  • Initial dorsal angulation > 20°
  • Severe dorsal or volar metaphyseal comminution
  • Radial shortening > 5 mm
  • Displaced intra-articular extensions (> 2 mm step-off)

In these scenarios, closed reduction is performed strictly as a temporizing measure and the patient should be prepared for operative intervention (ORIF with volar plating, CRPP, or external fixator).2

Advanced Imaging: CT provides multi-planar reconstructions essential for evaluating intra-articular die-punch fragments. MRI is unparalleled for evaluating concomitant soft-tissue injuries such as TFCC tears or intrinsic carpal ligament disruptions.2

5. Patient Preparation & Procedural Analgesia

Pre-Reduction Neurovascular Documentation

It is an unyielding medicolegal and clinical requirement to document a complete neurovascular examination prior to any analgesia or manipulation.14

Required Neurovascular Assessment

  • Median Nerve: Sensory distribution (thumb, index, middle finger); motor—thenar opposition. Special attention due to proximity to volar cortex.2
  • Radial Nerve: Sensory first web space; motor—wrist/finger extension.
  • Ulnar Nerve: Sensory ring/small fingers; motor—interossei, adductor pollicis.
  • AIN/PIN: IP flexion of thumb; MCP extension respectively.14
  • Vascular: Radial/ulnar pulses; capillary refill; compartment assessment.14
🚨 Acute Carpal Tunnel Syndrome

If pre-reduction examination reveals dense median nerve sensory loss or acute thenar weakness, this indicates acute CTS. If gentle closed reduction does not rapidly resolve symptoms, the threshold for emergent operative carpal tunnel release must be dramatically lowered.2,7

Procedural Analgesia: The Hematoma Block

The intra-articular hematoma block remains a highly effective, low-risk, and resource-efficient analgesic modality widely utilized in emergency departments.14

  1. Identification: Palpate the dorsal wrist cortical step-off between the 3rd and 4th dorsal extensor compartments.14
  2. Sterilization: Aseptic prep with chlorhexidine or iodine-based solution.14
  3. Needle Introduction: 20–22 G needle from dorsum, aimed distally, walking along dorsal cortex until tip falls into fracture gap.14
  4. Confirmation: Aspiration of dark, non-pulsatile hematoma and marrow fat.14
  5. Injection: 5–10 mL of 1% or 2% lidocaine without epinephrine (± bupivacaine).14
  6. Dwell Time: Allow ≥ 10–15 minutes for periosteal infiltration before traction.14

6. Mechanical Ttraction: The Chinese Finger Trap Paradigm

Continuous, sustained longitudinal traction is arguably the single most critical component of a successful closed reduction. The objective is ligamentotaxis—utilizing the intact soft-tissue envelope to drag the distal fragment out of its impacted state.2

Physiology of Muscular Fatigue

By suspending the limb in finger traps with a steady counter-weight, the forearm musculature is subjected to continuous tension. Over 15–20 minutes, Golgi tendon organs are stimulated, sending inhibitory signals to alpha motor neurons, inducing profound muscle relaxation and fatigue. This renders the fracture highly mobile, significantly decreasing the manual force required.14

Finger Trap Application Protocol
StepDetail

💡 Improvised Traction: Gauze Roll Technique

In resource-limited settings, standard rolled gauze (Kerlex) can replicate finger trap biomechanics: fold in half, create individual digit loops via overhand knots or clove hitches, secure to index and middle fingers, wrap base of digits circumferentially, suspend from IV pole with standard counter-weights.21

Note: Patients should be counseled that dependent digits will exhibit venous pooling and discoloration during traction—a benign, anticipated response.21

7. Algorithmic Closed Reduction Maneuvers

Colles Fracture Reduction (Dorsal Displacement)

The reduction relies on a precise three-step biomechanical sequence: disimpaction → longitudinal translation → palmar restitution.5

1

Disimpaction

Paradoxically increase dorsal deformity to break bony impaction and unlock fragments.5

2

Translation

Thumbs dorsally on distal fragment, fingers volar on proximal shaft. Apply longitudinal traction.5

3

Palmar Restitution

Push distal fragment distally, then apply strong palmar-directed force with wrist flexion and slight ulnar deviation.5

Smith Fracture Reduction (Volar Displacement)

The biomechanical mirror image, demanding more aggressive manipulation and unyielding vigilance.7

1

Disimpaction

Exaggerate palmar angulation; may require temporarily releasing finger trap traction.7

2

Translation

Reverse hand placement: thumbs on volar distal fragment, fingers on dorsal proximal radius.7

3

Dorsal Restitution

Push distally to clear volar cortex, then firmly extend (dorsiflex) the distal fragment.7

⚠️ Smith Fracture: Augmentation Often Required

Smith fractures are inherently unstable due to constant flexor tendon pull. If early radiographs confirm loss of reduction, percutaneous K-wire fixation (Kapandji or crossed-wire: 2–3 stout K-wires) or joint-spanning external fixator should be applied. K-wires are typically removed at 6–8 weeks.7

Pediatric Considerations

The thick, resilient periosteal sleeve in children rarely tears circumferentially, remaining intact on the concave side. The clinician must forcefully angulate into the deformity to relax the periosteum, then use it as a hinge to "close the book." Pediatric fractures have immense remodeling potential—repeated traumatic reduction attempts are discouraged if acceptable functional alignment is achieved.3,17

8. Principles of Reproducible Immobilization

📐 Charnley's Three-Point Molding Principle

"Bent plaster, straight bone." — Sir John Charnley, 195025

Casts must act as active biomechanical splints utilizing three opposing pressure points to counteract deforming muscle forces.

Colles Fracture Mold

  • Primary fulcrum: Dorsal distal radius (pushes volar)
  • Counter 1: Volar forearm shaft
  • Counter 2: Volar proximal palm

Smith Fracture Mold

  • Primary fulcrum: Volar distal fragment (pushes dorsal)
  • Counter 1: Dorsal forearm shaft
  • Counter 2: Dorsal metacarpals

Critical Cast Boundaries

  • Proximal: Distal to antecubital fossa (preserve elbow flexion)14
  • Distal volar: Proximal to distal palmar crease (preserve MCP flexion)8,14
  • Distal dorsal: Proximal to MCP joint heads14
🚫 Critical Molding Errors to Avoid

Never use fingertips or thumbs to press into setting plaster. Focal indentations create high-pressure point loads against skin, causing ischemic breakdown, pressure necrosis, and excruciating pain.24 Always use broad, flat palm surfaces.

The Cast Index (CI)

The Cast Index is a validated radiographic measurement quantifying mold quality: the ratio of sagittal depth to coronal width at the fracture level.29

  • Optimal CI < 0.8: Indicates sufficient sagittal flattening29
  • CI ≈ 1.0: Circular cast — fails three-point fixation — highly predictive of redisplacement27

9. Evidence-Based Wrist Positioning

The Cotton-Loder Position: A Hemodynamic Fallacy

🚫 Historical Warning: The Cotton-Loder Position

Extreme palmar flexion + maximum ulnar deviation was historically used to maintain Colles reductions. Modern EBM universally condemns this position due to catastrophic soft-tissue consequences.2

Pathophysiology: Extreme wrist flexion compresses the median nerve within the rigid carpal tunnel, drastically decreasing tunnel volume and exponentially spiking interstitial pressures. This is compounded by fracture hematoma, edema, and circumferential casting—precipitating acute carpal tunnel syndrome.2,31,32

✅ Modern Evidence-Based Standard

Clinical guidelines from the AAOS, BOA, and BSSH dictate immobilization in neutral flexion (0°) or slight extension (dorsiflexion).4

Systematic reviews demonstrate that dorsiflexion/neutral positioning yields significantly better range of motion and more stable radiological parameters vs. palmar flexion, while completely bypassing the iatrogenic risks of the Cotton-Loder position.35

Standard of evidence: Multiple multi-center RCTs. Level I
