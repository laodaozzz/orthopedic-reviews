Comprehensive Review: Unilateral Biportal Endoscopy (UBE)

Current Status, Research Hotspots, and Clinical Evolution (2020–2024)

Period: 2020-2024 Sources: Web of Science Core Collection, PubMed, PMC Focus: Bibliometrics, Techniques, Outcomes, Complications

1. Introduction

The paradigm of spinal surgery has evolved toward minimization of approach-related morbidity. Unilateral Biportal Endoscopy (UBE), or Biportal Endoscopic Spinal Surgery (BESS), represents a disruptive innovation in this continuum. Unlike full-endoscopic uniportal surgery (FESS/PELD), UBE employs a “floating” dual-portal system, dissociating the optical channel from the working channel. This allows the use of standard open surgical instruments (e.g., high-speed drills, Kerrison rongeurs) under magnified, fluid-expanded visualization.

Key Technological Distinction

The biportal nature enables triangulation. By separating visualization from instrumentation, UBE allows for a greater range of motion and instrument angulation, enabling surgeons to look around corners and perform complex tasks like undercutting the contralateral facet joint—capabilities constrained in uniportal endoscopy.

The period from 2020 to 2024 represents a critical maturation phase. UBE is no longer experimental for simple discectomy; it has been validated for complex pathologies including multisegmental stenosis, high-grade spondylolisthesis requiring fusion, and thoracic ossification. This expansion has also identified unique physiological challenges, particularly related to the hydrodynamics of continuous saline irrigation, introducing risks of intracranial hypertension and fluid extravasation.

2. Bibliometric Analysis: Global Research Trends (2020–2024)

Academic output on UBE has exhibited a distinct “citation burst” and exponential growth, reflecting its transition from a niche technique to a mainstream option. Level II Evidence (Bibliometric Study)

2.1. Publication Volume and Velocity

Research volume shows a stark inflection point from 2019-2020. The modern refinement of the technique sparked a surge that peaked in 2023. By mid-2024, systematic searches identified over 231 core articles directly investigating UBE/BESS. This growth continued despite the COVID-19 pandemic, likely driven by UBE’s inherent advantages for shorter hospital stays—factors that became premium during pandemic constraints.

2.2. Geographic Hegemony: The “East Asian Axis”

A consistent finding is the dominance of East Asia, particularly South Korea and China.

  • South Korea: Contributed ~72.6% of early literature, boasting the highest H-index (16). Institutions like Wooridul Spine Hospital serve as global training centers. Level II Evidence
  • China: Rapidly accelerated output, instrumental in conducting large-sample comparative studies and meta-analyses. Level II Evidence
  • Global Diffusion: There is a discernable lag in adoption and publication volume from Western nations. The United States contributes less, often focusing on review articles or later-phase adoption.

2.3. Evolution of Research Hotspots

Keyword analysis reveals shifting priorities:

  • 2016–2019 (Validation): “lumbar disc herniation,” “discectomy.”
  • 2020–2022 (Refinement): “spinal stenosis,” “decompression,” “learning curve,” “complications.”
  • 2023–2024 (Expansion): “interbody fusion” (ULIF), “cervical spine,” “thoracic myelopathy,” “ossification of ligamentum flavum.” Level III Evidence (Keyword Trend Analysis)

3. Technical Fundamentals and the Learning Curve

3.1. Quantifying the Learning Curve

The learning curve for UBE has been a major subject of inquiry, often quantified using Cumulative Sum (CUSUM) analysis. The consensus is that UBE possesses a “flatter” and more accessible learning curve compared to uniportal PELD. Level II Evidence (Meta-analysis)

  • Case Thresholds: Proficiency is typically achieved after 28 to 35 cases.
  • Phasic Breakdown:
    • Early Phase (Cases 1–25): Longer operative times, higher fluoroscopy use, struggles with fluid turbulence.
    • Proficiency Phase (Cases >35): Operative times stabilize (~60–65 min for decompression), complication rates plateau.
  • Comparative Learning: Uniportal (PELD/PTES) often requires 35 to 40+ cases for similar proficiency. The steeper curve is attributed to the disorientation of the 2D monocular view and the “fulcrum effect.” UBE’s triangulation is more intuitive for surgeons trained in open microscopy or knee arthroscopy. Level II Evidence

4. Expansion of Indications: Lumbar Interbody Fusion (ULIF)

The most significant clinical trend is the adoption of UBE for lumbar fusion, commonly termed ULIF (Unilateral Biportal Endoscopic Lumbar Interbody Fusion). This aims to mitigate the soft tissue trauma of open TLIF and the limitations of tubular MIS-TLIF.

4.1. Comparative Efficacy: ULIF vs. MIS-TLIF

The literature is rich with comparative studies. The primary advantage of ULIF lies in the immediate perioperative period. Level II Evidence (Comparative Cohort Studies)

4.2. Complication Profile

  • Dural Tears: Occur in 2.9%–6.4% of cases. Often small and manageable. Level II Evidence
  • Symptomatic Hematoma: Low incidence (1.4%–4.3%) due to continuous washing effect. Level II Evidence
  • Deep Infection: Exceedingly rare (~1.4%). Continuous saline lavage acts as mechanical debridement. Level II Evidence

5. Expansion into Challenging Anatomy: Cervical and Thoracic Spine

From 2020 to 2024, UBE broke the “lumbar barrier.”

5.1. Cervical Spine: UBE Posterior Cervical Foraminotomy (UBE-PCF)

UBE-PCF is a motion-preserving alternative to ACDF. It preserves the posterior tension band while minimizing muscle stripping pain. Meta-analyses show it achieves significant improvements in NDI and VAS scores, comparable to open and full-endoscopic techniques. The overall complication rate is approximately 6.2%, with dural tears at 2.1%. Level II Evidence (Meta-analysis)

A sophisticated nuance is the comparison of approach angles. A 2024 study found that both ipsilateral and contralateral inclinatory approaches yield equivalent outcomes. The contralateral approach allows undercutting the spinous process and viewing the nerve root exit zone perpendicularly, advantageous for severe foraminal stenosis. Level III Evidence (Comparative Cohort)

5.2. Thoracic Spine: The Frontier of Ossification (OLF)

Thoracic Ossification of the Ligamentum Flavum (T-OLF) is formidable. Open laminectomy carries high paralysis risk. UBE has been applied with a focus on minimizing dural manipulation.

Critical Technique Evolution: Outside-In vs. Inside-Out

The current standard is the “Outside-In” or “En Bloc/Floating” technique. The surgeon drills the lamina until the OLF mass is detached from bone before entering the canal, then lifts it away from the dura. This minimizes instrument insertion into the compromised canal, a safer strategy than the older, riskier piecemeal “Inside-Out” approach. Level IV Evidence (Case Series)

Studies report significant recovery in JOA scores (e.g., from 12.6 to 15.6) without the instability often caused by wide open laminectomies.

6. Comparative Outcomes: UBE vs. Uniportal Endoscopy vs. Microscopy

6.1. UBE vs. Uniportal (PELD)

  • Operative Time: PELD is faster for simple discectomies (~99 min vs. ~116 min for UBE). Level II Evidence
  • Blood Loss: PELD is “drier” (~20 mL vs. ~45-80 mL), but clinical significance is minimal.
  • Extent of Decompression: UBE shows a statistically significant advantage in “Dural Sac Expansion”. Independent visualization allows more thorough bilateral decompression (ULBD) from a unilateral approach. Level II Evidence

6.2. UBE vs. Microscopic Surgery

  • Muscle Enzymes: UBE results in significantly lower postoperative Creatine Kinase (CK) and inflammatory markers (CRP, IL-6), quantifying the muscle-sparing claim. Level III Evidence
  • Clinical Equivalence: Long-term outcomes (VAS, ODI at 1 year) are statistically equivalent. The benefit of UBE is concentrated in the first 2-6 weeks of recovery. Level II Evidence

7. Emerging Complications: The Hydrostatic Pressure Paradigm

As UBE case volume increased, a specific class of fluid dynamics-related complications emerged, distinct from open surgery risks.

7.1. Pathophysiology of Intracranial Hypertension and Seizures

High-pressure irrigation compresses the thecal sac, displacing CSF cranially and raising Intracranial Pressure (ICP). In cases of incidental durotomy, fluid can be forced directly into the subarachnoid space, causing a “washout” of electrolytes or direct pressure effects linked to generalized seizures. Level IV Evidence (Case Reports)

7.2. Antibiotic Neurotoxicity

The use of antibiotics (e.g., Gentamicin, Cefazolin) in irrigation fluid poses a risk. If a dural tear occurs, these neurotoxic agents are driven directly into the CNS, lowering the seizure threshold. Current recommendation: avoid adding antibiotics to irrigation fluid. Level V Evidence (Expert Opinion/Case Report)

7.3. Monitoring and Prevention

A pivotal 2024 study introduced Ocular Ultrasonography to monitor the Optic Nerve Sheath Diameter (ONSD) as a proxy for ICP during UBE.

Critical Safety Threshold Identified

ONSD >5.3 mm correlates with dangerously high ICP. The study recommends maintaining irrigation pressure between 30 and 50 mmHg and strict mean arterial pressure (MAP) goals. Level II Evidence (Prospective Cohort)

8. Conclusion and Future Directions

The evidence from 2020 to 2024 confirms UBE’s transition to a standard of care for a broad spectrum of spinal pathologies.

Key Conclusions:

  1. Dominance: Led by South Korean and Chinese innovation, with massive acceleration in publication volume and quality.
  2. Versatility: UBE is superior for “hard” pathologies (fusion, ossification, stenosis) due to the ability to use standard osteotomes/drills. PELD remains efficient for soft disc herniations.
  3. Safety Profile: While musculoskeletal trauma is minimized, the technique introduces Hydrostatic Pressure as a critical safety variable. The risk of seizures and intracranial hypertension is manageable through strict low-pressure protocols (<50 mmHg) and vigilance for dural integrity.
  4. Future Outlook: Integration with navigation/robotics and standardized “fluid safety” guidelines. Long-term follow-up (5-10 years) on ULIF fusion integrity remains the final validation required.
