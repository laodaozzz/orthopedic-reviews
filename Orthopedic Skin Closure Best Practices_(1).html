The Critical Final Step in Orthopedic Surgery

Surgical wound closure in orthopedics represents far more than a perfunctory final step; it is a critical phase that profoundly influences postoperative recovery, patient morbidity, and the overall economic burden on the healthcare system. Complications arising from inadequate closure — persistent wound drainage, dehiscence, and surgical site infection (SSI) — can precipitate a cascade of adverse events.

⚠ Clinical Impact of SSI

An SSI, even when superficial, can extend hospital stay, double readmission rates, and increase annual healthcare costs by as much as 300%. In arthroplasty, superficial infection can progress to deep periprosthetic joint infection (PJI) — a catastrophic complication requiring multiple revision surgeries, prolonged antibiotics, and causing significant negative impact on quality of life.

Unique Challenges in Orthopedics

Wound healing in orthopedic surgery is complicated by a unique confluence of factors:

  • Implants — Large foreign-body devices (plates, rods, joint prostheses) create a permanent nidus for bacterial colonization and biofilm formation.
  • Tissue quality — High-energy trauma compromises local blood supply and the innate immune response.
  • High-tension environments — Closures over mobile joints are subjected to high tensile and shear forces.
  • Patient comorbidities — Older adults with fragile, atrophic skin; diabetes mellitus; immunosuppression; and smoking prevalence all impair wound healing.

The Problem of Clinical Variability

Despite the clear significance of wound closure, there is a striking lack of international consensus on the optimal method, leading to significant variability. Surveys reveal that while 85–87% of surgeons believe wound closure directly affects outcomes after joint replacement, practices diverge considerably. For example, a national survey in Spain found that over 90% of surgeons use metal staples for skin closure in joint arthroplasty — a practice contested by a substantial body of evidence.

📋 Evidence–Practice Gap

The critical task of closure is often delegated: residents close the wound in approximately 50% of cases, and only 57% of attending surgeons perform the closure themselves. This may suggest a perception of closure as a less critical part of the operation.

Section 1: Staples versus Sutures — A Critical Appraisal

The debate over metal staples versus traditional sutures is one of the most enduring and contentious topics in orthopedic surgery. The body of high-level evidence, primarily systematic reviews and meta-analyses, is fraught with conflicting conclusions.

1.1 Evidence Favoring Sutures: Higher SSI Risk with Staples

A persistent theme within the literature is the association between skin staples and an increased risk of surgical site infection.

Landmark Finding (Smith et al., 2010) Level I

A 2010 BMJ meta-analysis (683 wounds, 6 trials) demonstrated that the risk of superficial wound infection was over 3× greater with staples vs. sutures (RR 3.83, 95% CI 1.38–10.68; P=0.01). In hip surgery specifically, the risk was nearly 5× higher (RR 4.79, 95% CI 1.24–18.47; P=0.02).1

This has been corroborated by subsequent analyses:

  • van de Kuit et al. (2022) Level I — In high-quality, low risk-of-bias studies, staple closure was associated with significantly higher SSI risk (RR 2.56, 95% CI 1.20–5.44), driven primarily by hip arthroplasty (RR 2.51, 95% CI 1.15–5.50).3
  • Krishnan et al. (2019) Level I — Meta-analysis of 17 RCTs (2,446 patients): primary analysis showed higher SSI risk with staples (RR 2.05, 95% CI 1.38–3.06). However, sensitivity analysis restricted to low-bias studies yielded non-significant results (RR 1.45, 95% CI 0.31–6.79), demonstrating result "fragility."2

1.2 Countervailing Evidence: Equivalence or Staple Superiority

The persistence of the debate is fueled by evidence that finds either no difference or an advantage for staples:

  • Smith & Nace (2017) Level I — A meta-analysis focusing exclusively on TKA found that sutures were associated with a higher likelihood of both superficial and deep infections, abscess formation, and wound dehiscence. The primary drawback of staples was prolonged wound discharge.4
  • Rui et al. (2016) Level I — Found no significant difference in infection rates (cumulative RR 1.06, 95% CI 0.46–2.44), concluding the evidence base is inadequately powered.5
✅ Undisputed Advantage of Staples

Multiple studies consistently report that wound closure is significantly faster with staples, reducing overall operative time. Some economic analyses suggest the savings in OR time could make staples cost-effective in high-volume centers.

1.3 Deconstructing the "Evidence Paradox"

The existence of high-level meta-analyses with diametrically opposed conclusions points to profound weaknesses in the underlying primary research:

  • Small, underpowered cohorts incapable of reliably detecting differences in a relatively rare outcome like SSI.
  • Poor randomization and allocation concealment — introduces selection bias.
  • Lack of blinding of outcome assessors — introduces detection bias.
  • "Fragile" results — removal of a single study can flip outcomes from significant to non-significant.

This "evidence paradox" directly contributes to the well-documented disconnect between published evidence and routine clinical practice, allowing clinicians to selectively cite evidence supporting pre-existing practice patterns.

Section 2: Suture Methods — Technique Matters

Beyond the choice of closure material lies the critical importance of surgical technique. The method by which a suture is placed significantly impacts tension distribution, wound edge eversion, security, and the cosmetic and functional outcome.

2.1 Subcuticular (Intradermal) Closure: The Gold Standard for Cosmesis

The subcuticular technique, in which the suture is passed through the dermal layer parallel to the skin plane, avoids external stitch marks ("train tracks"), leading to a finer, less conspicuous scar.

Evidence Summary Level I

A 2020 Cochrane Review found moderate-certainty evidence that subcuticular sutures probably improve patient satisfaction vs. transdermal sutures. A Journal of Drugs in Dermatology review (15 studies) confirmed superiority for aesthetic outcomes on trunk and extremities.

Crucially, subcuticular sutures probably decrease the incidence of overall wound complications (RR 0.79, 95% CI 0.64–0.98) with no increase in SSI risk vs. staples (RR 0.81, 95% CI 0.64–1.01).

Limitations: More technically demanding, requires greater precision for wound edge eversion, and is more time-consuming than interrupted sutures or staples.

2.2 Continuous vs. Interrupted Suturing

The choice involves a fundamental trade-off between efficiency and security:

Continuous (Running) Suture

  • Speed of placement
  • More even tension distribution → less tissue ischemia
  • Only 2 knots → less foreign material in wound
  • EFORT 2024 Consensus Level V gives a strong recommendation for continuous barbed sutures in deep fascial layer closure in arthroplasty.3

Interrupted Suture

  • Principal advantage: security — if one suture fails, the rest remain intact
  • Preferred for potentially contaminated wounds or high-risk patients
  • Most common method for skin closure after ORIF of ankle fractures in trauma surveys
💡 Evidence-Based Layered Strategy

The optimal approach leverages advantages of each technique at different tissue layers: continuous barbed sutures for deep fascial layers (speed, watertight seal, minimize foreign body reaction) and subcuticular closure with smooth monofilament for the skin layer (optimal cosmesis, minimized infection risk).

Section 3: Suture Material Selection

Suture materials are not interchangeable; they possess distinct properties related to composition, structure, absorption profile, and tensile strength that make them suitable for different tissues and clinical scenarios.

3.1 Absorbable vs. Non-Absorbable: Challenging Old Dogma

A long-held tenet has been that non-absorbable sutures are required for skin closure. However, the most current evidence challenges this:

Key Evidence Level I

A 2023 systematic review (27 RCTs, 5,096 patients) found no significant difference between absorbable and non-absorbable sutures for skin closure in wound infection, scar formation, and dehiscence. An earlier meta-analysis found absorbable sutures were associated with a significantly lower risk of wound breakdown (OR 0.12, 95% CI 0.04–0.39).

This evidence represents a practice-changing insight. Absorbable sutures, particularly subcuticular, eliminate the need for suture removal — providing benefits in patient convenience, comfort, and cost savings.

3.2 Monofilament vs. Multifilament (Braided)

This structural distinction has profound implications for both infection risk and handling:

  • Multifilament (braided): Microscopic interstices can harbor bacteria via capillarity, allowing biofilm formation and persistent suture abscess. Better handling and knot security.
  • Monofilament: Smooth, non-porous surface — more resistant to bacterial adherence, less friction. Trade-off: more "memory," less knot security, requiring more throws.
⚠ Note: The recommendation for monofilament sutures in skin closure is based on strong theoretical rationale (reduced bacterial adherence) and supported by the EFORT consensus principles. Specific RCTs comparing monofilament vs. multifilament for orthopedic skin closure are limited in the source document.

3.3 Innovations in Suture Technology

Barbed Sutures Level V — EFORT Consensus

Monofilament sutures with small circumferential barbs that anchor into surrounding tissue, eliminating the need for knots. The EFORT 2024 consensus gives a strong recommendation with high-level evidence for their use in deep layer closure.3

  • Significantly shorter surgical duration (no knot tying)
  • Tighter, more watertight closure
  • Uniform tension distribution → less tissue ischemia and gapping
  • Added security: barbs anchor suture even if nicked

Antimicrobial-Coated Sutures Level V — EFORT Consensus

Sutures coated with triclosan that elute into surrounding tissue, creating a zone of bacterial inhibition. EFORT gives a strong recommendation based on meta-analyses from general/colorectal surgery demonstrating decreased SSI risk.3

⚠ Critical Caveat

There is currently no specific evidence demonstrating that antimicrobial-coated sutures reduce the risk of deep prosthetic joint infection (PJI) in arthroplasty. While effective for superficial wound infections, their ability to prevent the most devastating complication remains unproven.3

Section 4: Adjunctive and Complementary Therapies

Modern wound closure extends beyond sutures and staples. A growing body of evidence supports adjunctive therapies that supplement traditional methods, creating a more robust and protected healing environment.

4.1 Topical Skin Adhesives (2-Octyl Cyanoacrylate) Level V

Topical skin adhesives serve as an adjunct applied over a well-approximated subcuticular closure. They provide an additional layer of wound security and act as an impermeable microbial barrier.

  • A study in geriatric hip fractures found the suture/glue combination was associated with shorter hospital stay and fewer interventions for wound drainage.
  • EFORT 2024 notes 2-octyl cyanoacrylate has been associated with lower wound complications vs. standard silver dressings in TKA and better cosmetic/functional outcomes vs. staples.3
  • DERMABOND PRINEO system integrates liquid adhesive with self-adhering polyester mesh, providing closure strength greater than staples.
  • Risk: Potential for localized contact dermatitis — a preoperative sensitivity test may be prudent.3

4.2 Incisional Negative Pressure Wound Therapy (iNPWT)

iNPWT involves a sterile foam dressing over the closed incision, sealed with an occlusive film, connected to a vacuum pump delivering sub-atmospheric pressure. Mechanisms include exudate removal, hematoma/seroma reduction, mechanical tension reduction, and sealed contamination barrier.

✅ High-Risk Patients: Strong Evidence

The AAOS clinical practice guideline recommends iNPWT for high-risk surgical incisions.6 EFORT concludes iNPWT effectively reduces complications, readmissions, and reoperations in scenarios such as periprosthetic fractures and revision arthroplasty, making it cost-effective in this population.3

⚠ Low-Risk Patients: Not Recommended

For low-risk patients undergoing primary joint arthroplasty, EFORT states iNPWT offers no additional benefit compared to modern active dressings and substantially increases costs without improving outcomes.3

4.3 Hemostasis and Dressings

Hemostasis with Tranexamic Acid (TXA) Level I

The EFORT consensus gives a strong recommendation for TXA (IV or topical) in hip and knee arthroplasty. TXA effectively reduces perioperative blood loss, minimizes hematoma formation, and has a protective effect against PJI.3

Dressing Management Level V

  • Active/interactive dressings are superior to simple passive dressings.
  • Initial dressing should be left undisturbed for a minimum of 48 hours.
  • Change only when saturated or at manufacturer-recommended duration to minimize contamination exposure.3

Section 5: Evidence-Based, Risk-Stratified Framework

While the ideal closure method is a multifactorial decision, a framework grounded in risk stratification and the best available evidence can guide surgeons toward optimizing outcomes.

Synthesis of Key Findings

  • Staples vs. Sutures: Unresolved due to poor primary research quality. Weight of higher-quality evidence suggests caution with staples, particularly in hip arthroplasty (high-tension closures).
  • Technique: Subcuticular closure offers superior cosmetic outcomes and patient satisfaction without increasing SSI risk; may reduce minor wound complications.
  • Materials: Modern absorbable sutures provide equivalent outcomes with convenience benefits. Monofilament preferred for skin. Barbed sutures superior for deep fascial layers.
  • Adjuncts: Skin adhesives, iNPWT (high-risk), and TXA enhance the closure system.
⚠ The Guideline Gap: Major professional bodies including the AAOS have intentionally refrained from issuing prescriptive guidelines for skin closure material/technique.6 This reflects the contested, low-quality evidence surrounding the staple vs. suture debate. The onus is on the individual surgeon to critically appraise the literature.

🟢 Standard Protocol — Low-Risk Patient / Primary Arthroplasty

1
Hemostasis: Meticulous intraoperative hemostasis + routine IV or topical TXA.3
2
Deep Layer: Continuous, absorbable, antimicrobial-coated barbed suture (e.g., STRATAFIX PDS Plus) for watertight, knotless closure.3
3
Skin Closure: Continuous, absorbable monofilament suture (e.g., Monocryl) via subcuticular technique.
4
Adjunctive Barrier: Apply topical skin adhesive (e.g., 2-octyl cyanoacrylate) over the closed incision as microbial barrier.3
5
Dressing: Modern, active, absorbent dressing — left undisturbed for minimum 48–72 hours unless saturated.3

🔴 Enhanced Protocol — High-Risk Patient / Procedure

Major trauma, revision arthroplasty, uncontrolled diabetes, immunosuppression, periprosthetic fractures

+
Follow all steps of the Standard Protocol above.
!
Substitute dressing with iNPWT: Apply incisional Negative Pressure Wound Therapy over the closed incision to proactively manage exudate and reduce tension.3

This summary is based exclusively on the provided source document. Key limitations include:
