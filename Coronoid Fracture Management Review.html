1. Introduction: The Evolution of Elbow Instability Management

The ulnar coronoid process, once considered a minor bony prominence of limited clinical consequence, has been recognised as the "keystone" of elbow stability. It functions as the primary anterior buttress of the ulnohumeral joint, providing essential structural constraint against posterior subluxation and anchoring critical soft-tissue stabilisers, including the anterior capsule and the medial collateral ligament (MCL). Evidence level: Narrative review

Management has undergone a paradigm shift over the last three decades — from benign neglect to aggressive anatomical restoration — driven by deeper understanding of complex instability patterns: the "Terrible Triad" (Hotchkiss) and Varus Posteromedial Rotatory Instability (VPMRI) (O'Driscoll). Not specified in source

Historically, poor outcomes following complex elbow dislocations — stiffness, recurrent instability, and rapid post-traumatic arthritis — were accepted as inevitable. It was not until the seminal works of researchers like Hotchkiss (coining the "Terrible Triad") and O'Driscoll (elucidating anteromedial facet fracture mechanics) that the pathology was deconstructed into manageable components. Level IV: Case series & expert opinion

Key Conceptual Shift The coronoid fracture is rarely an isolated event; rather, it is a sentinel marker for significant ligamentous disruption that compromises the containment of the ulnohumeral articulation.

Clinically, these fractures typically occur in young, active patients following high-energy trauma. Fracture patterns are often subtle on plain radiography, yet the implications for joint mechanics are profound. A deficiency in the coronoid buttress transforms the elbow into a joint that is inherently unstable in extension and resistant to concentric reduction, creating a therapeutic tightrope between stabilising the joint and permitting the early motion necessary to prevent debilitating contracture.

2. Surgical Anatomy & Biomechanics

2.1 Osseous Topography

The coronoid process projects anteriorly from the proximal ulna, forming the distal and anterior floor of the greater sigmoid notch. Its concave articular surface matches the convexity of the humeral trochlea. Anatomically, the coronoid is divided into three clinically relevant regions: Anatomical fact — not graded

  • Tip: The most proximal aspect, frequently avulsed in posterior dislocations. It represents the insertion of the anterior capsule and is essential for preventing posterior translation in extension and lower degrees of flexion. Cadaveric/observational data
  • Anteromedial Facet (AMF): The medial slope of the coronoid — largely unsupported by the ulnar diaphysis, projecting medially into the varus stress plane. It articulates with the medial crista of the trochlea and acts as the primary bony restraint against varus stress. Loss creates a "cliff" off which the medial trochlea slides. Cadaveric biomechanical data
  • Base: Comprises the bulk of the bony process, continuous with the proximal ulnar metaphysis. Fractures here usually involve high-energy mechanisms and are often associated with trans-olecranon fracture-dislocations. Not specified in source

2.2 Soft-Tissue Attachments & Footprints

The osseous importance of the coronoid is magnified by its role as a point of attachment for critical soft-tissue stabilisers: Cadaveric anatomical studies

  • Anterior Capsule: Inserts approximately 6 mm distal to the tip. This means small "tip" fractures (Regan-Morrey Type I) are essentially soft-tissue avulsions of the anterior capsule rather than structural bone loss. Restoration re-establishes the "check-rein" function against hyperextension and posterior subluxation. Cadaveric data [Ref 4]
  • MCL (Anterior Bundle / AMCL): Inserts onto the sublime tubercle, located 18 mm distal to the tip. Fractures involving the AMF often extend to include the sublime tubercle, producing a "double hit": loss of the bony varus buttress plus detachment of the primary valgus stabiliser. Cadaveric data [Ref 4]
  • Brachialis Muscle: Inserts at the rough tuberosity at the coronoid base. While primarily a flexor, it provides dynamic compressive force. In fracture settings, it can act as a deforming force, distracting large fragments proximally. Not specified in source [Ref 6]

2.3 Biomechanical Constraints

The "Fortress" Concept [Ref 5] In Terrible Triad injuries, the primary constraint (coronoid) is broken, the secondary constraint (radial head) is fractured, and the lateral ligamentous constraint (LCL) is torn. This removal of all anterior and lateral barricades allows the elbow to escape posteriorly with minimal force.
  • Primary Constraints: The ulnohumeral articulation (coronoid and olecranon). The coronoid prevents posterior translation. Cadaveric biomechanical data
  • Secondary Constraints: The radial head transmits roughly 60% of the load and resists valgus stress. When the MCL is torn or the coronoid is fractured, the radial head becomes a critical secondary stabiliser. Cadaveric biomechanical data [Ref 5]
  • Instability Thresholds: Fractures involving ≥50% of coronoid height consistently lead to instability in the presence of LCL injury. Even smaller fractures (10–30%) can produce micro-instability or subluxation if the radial head is also resected or comminuted. Cadaveric biomechanical data [Ref 5]

3. Classification Systems

3.1 Regan & Morrey Classification (1989)

A descriptive system based on the height of the coronoid fragment on lateral radiograph. For decades it was the standard. Original classification system [Ref 1]

  • Type I: Avulsion of the tip. Historically viewed as "nuisance fractures."
  • Type II: Fragment involving ≤ 50% of coronoid height.
  • Type III: Fragment involving > 50% of coronoid height.
Key Limitation Reliance on 2-D lateral view fails to capture medial–lateral extent. Anteromedial facet fractures are frequently misclassified as "Type II." The historical dismissal of Type I fractures as benign has been challenged by the Terrible Triad, where a Type I fracture heralds catastrophic instability. Expert commentary [Ref 1, 6]

3.2 O'Driscoll Classification (2003) — Current Gold Standard

Based on fracture location and injury mechanism, derived from CT morphology. This is the preferred system for surgical planning. Expert consensus / classification study [Ref 1]

PLRI / Terrible Triad

Type I: Tip Fractures

Subtype 1: < 2 mm coronoid height
Subtype 2: > 2 mm (up to 50%)

Mechanism: Posterolateral rotatory instability. Avulsion of anterior capsule.

Implication: Manage via lateral column; suture lasso for capsular repair.

VPMRI

Type II: Anteromedial Facet (AMF) Fractures

Subtype 1: AMF rim only
Subtype 2: AMF rim + tip
Subtype 3: AMF rim + sublime tubercle (MCL insertion)

Mechanism: Varus posteromedial rotatory instability. Shearing of medial facet.

Implication: Requires medial approach and buttress plating.

Trans-Olecranon

Type III: Basal Fractures

Subtype 1: Coronoid body and base
Subtype 2: Trans-olecranon fracture-dislocation

Mechanism: High-energy axial loading or direct trauma.

Implication: Managed as part of proximal ulna/olecranon fixation with plates and lag screws.

4. Surgical Indications & Decision Making

The decision to operate is no longer binary based on fragment size. It is a nuanced decision based on residual stability after associated injuries are addressed — the concept of "functional stability": a joint that remains reduced through a functional arc (30–130° flexion). Expert consensus [Ref 9]

4.1 Indications for Conservative Management

  • Small Tip Fractures (OD Type I, subtype 1): If the radial head is intact (or reconstructable) and the LCL is repaired, the coronoid fragment may not require fixation. Remaining coronoid height > 90% is considered biomechanically sufficient. Cadaveric data [Ref 5]
  • Stable Arcs: If examination under anaesthesia or dynamic fluoroscopy shows stability above 30° flexion, hinged brace with extension block is acceptable — but requires vigilant radiographic follow-up. Expert consensus [Ref 9]

4.2 Indications for Surgical Fixation

Absolute Indication If the radial head is removed (radial head arthroplasty), the coronoid becomes the primary anterior stabiliser. Fixation is mandatory to prevent posterior dislocation. Expert consensus [Ref 14]

Terrible Triad Injuries

  • Fixation is mandatory for Type II or III (R-M) fractures (significant structural loss).
  • Type I Controversy: Some argue that rigid fixation of radial head and LCL makes coronoid fixation unnecessary. However, the majority consensus favours suture lasso fixation even for small fragments, as it repairs the anterior capsule and prevents "drop-back." Systematic reviews [Ref 14, 17, 22]

Anteromedial Facet Fractures (OD Type II)

  • Surgery is indicated for virtually all displaced AMF fractures. Leaving these untreated leads to rapid, devastating post-traumatic arthritis due to joint incongruity and chronic subluxation. Case series [Ref 6, 11]
  • Even small fragments involving the sublime tubercle (OD Type II, subtype 3) require fixation to restore MCL competence. Expert opinion [Ref 25]

Basal Fractures (OD Type III)

  • Invariably treated surgically as part of the reconstruction of the olecranon or ulnar shaft. Not specified in source [Ref 1]

5. Surgical Approaches

5.1 Lateral Approaches ("Window" Technique)

Indication: Terrible Triad injuries (OD Type I) where the radial head is also fractured. Expert consensus [Ref 13]

  • Intervals: Kocher (Anconeus / ECRL) or Kaplan (EDC / ECRB). Often through the traumatic LCL defect. Not specified in source [Ref 13]
  • Visualization: Through the radial head defect ("trans-radial window"). Excellent for suture lasso or A-P screws. Surgical technique description [Ref 21]
  • Advantages: Single incision for LCL, radial head, and coronoid.
  • Limitations: Poor medial visualization; difficult to plate the AMF.

5.2 Medial Approaches

Indication: AMF fractures (OD Type II) or large basal fractures requiring medial buttress plating. Expert consensus [Ref 4]

  • FCU Split (Hotchkiss/Over-the-Top): The workhorse approach. The ulnar nerve is identified and protected; FCU heads are split to expose the sublime tubercle and AMF. Surgical technique [Ref 4]
  • Ulnar Nerve Management: Debate between routine anterior transposition (higher resolution rate: 70% vs 22%) and in-situ decompression (less devascularisation). Systematic review [Ref 30–32]
  • Advantages: Direct perpendicular access to AMF for buttress plating; MCL repair possible.
  • Limitations: Risk of ulnar neuropathy (up to 33%); higher rates of heterotopic ossification. Systematic review [Ref 30, 33]

5.3 Posterior Approach

Indication: Trans-olecranon fracture-dislocations or complex global instability requiring access to both columns. Expert consensus [Ref 4]

  • Universal access; ideal for applying hinged external fixator.
  • Limitation: Extensive soft-tissue stripping increases wound problems, seroma, and stiffness.

6. Fixation Techniques: Principles & Evidence

6.1 Suture Lasso Technique — Workhorse for Type I (Tip) Fractures

Rationale: Small tip fragments are often too comminuted for a screw. The primary goal is reattachment of the anterior capsule. The suture acts as a tension band. Technique and biomechanical rationale [Ref 35, 36]

  1. Exposure: Lateral trans-radial window.
  2. Drilling: Two parallel bone tunnels from dorsal ulna (crista supinatoris) anteriorly to the coronoid fracture bed. Surgical technique [Ref 13]
  3. Passing: Heavy non-absorbable suture (No. 2 or No. 5 FiberWire) passed through anterior capsule and around the fragment via suture passer or 18-gauge needle technique. Technique description [Ref 27]
  4. Tying: Sutures tied over the dorsal cortical bridge after joint reduction. Technique description [Ref 19]

Biomechanics: Suture lasso stiffness ~119 N/mm; lower than screws (284 N/mm). However, clinical series show lower complication rates (implant failure) and equivalent stability because it avoids fragment comminution. Cadaveric biomechanical study [Ref 38]

6.2 Screw Fixation — For Large Solid Fragments

  • Posterior-to-Anterior (PA): Easier technically; weaker in resisting shear forces. Biomechanical study [Ref 39]
  • Anterior-to-Posterior (AP): Better compression against brachialis pull; technically demanding.
  • Load to failure: 405 N (vs 207 N for suture) in solid fragments. Biomechanical study [Ref 38]
  • For basal fractures, screw alone is often insufficient. Plate + screw provides highest stability (83 Nm energy to failure vs 10 Nm for screws alone). Biomechanical study [Ref 41, 42]

6.3 Buttress Plating — Gold Standard for AMF (OD Type II) Fractures

Rationale: AMF fractures result from varus shearing force. A screw provides compression but cannot resist sliding. A plate applied to the medial surface physically blocks distal displacement. Biomechanical study [Ref 6, 40]

  • Via medial (FCU split) approach.
  • Pre-contoured mini-fragment plate (T-plate or coronoid-specific plate) on the anteromedial surface.
  • Screws in "raft" configuration under the subchondral bone. Surgical technique [Ref 1]
  • Screw-only constructs in AMF models show high failure rates under cyclic varus loading compared to plated constructs. Biomechanical study [Ref 40]

7. Management of Specific Injury Patterns

7.1 The "Terrible Triad" (Dislocation + Radial Head Fx + Coronoid Fx)

The classic posterolateral instability pattern. Goal: restore anterior and lateral columns to create a stable enclosure. Expert consensus [Ref 3–5]

Sequential "Lateral-to-Medial" Surgical Algorithm

1
Lateral Exposure — Kocher or Kaplan interval.
2
Radial Head Assessment — If comminuted (>3 fragments), excise; this creates the "window" to the coronoid.
3
Coronoid Address — Through the radial head defect:
• Type I → Suture lasso passed (sutures NOT tied yet)
• Type II/III → Screw fixation if fragment is large; separate medial incision if needed.
4
Radial Head Restoration — Fix (if simple) or replace with metallic arthroplasty (tensions LCL, acts as spacer).
5
LCL Repair — Reattach to lateral epicondyle (isometric point) with suture anchors or transosseous tunnels.
6
Coronoid Suture Tying — With joint reduced and radial head spacer in place, tension and tie sutures.
7
Final Stability Check — Full ROM. If stable → leave MCL alone. If unstable → repair MCL or apply hinged external fixator.
The "To Fix or Not to Fix" Debate Systematic reviews suggest that if the radial head and LCL are rigidly restored, small Type I fractures may be left untreated. However, counter-evidence shows ignoring the coronoid leads to micro-instability and greater extension deficits. The suture lasso adds minimal morbidity while providing a "safety belt." Most trauma protocols now advocate fixation of all coronoid fractures in the Triad setting. Systematic reviews / comparative studies [Ref 14, 17, 22]

7.2 Anteromedial Facet Fractures (VPMRI)

Dubbed the "silent killer" of the elbow — often subtle on X-ray, leading to missed diagnosis and rapid joint destruction. Colloquial description, not graded

  • Pathomechanics: Varus force. LCL almost always avulsed. Medial coronoid shears off. Radial head typically intact.
  • Management Algorithm:
    1. Medial Approach: Isolate ulnar nerve; buttress plate the AMF (critical step).
    2. Lateral Approach: Repair the LCL.
  • If the AMF is not fixed, the medial trochlea grinds into the fracture defect → severe arthritis within months. With appropriate buttress plating, functional outcomes are generally excellent (MEPS > 90). Retrospective study [Ref 11, 24]

8.2 Post-Traumatic Osteoarthritis (PTOA)

  • Incidence: 20–75% at long-term follow-up (10+ years). Long-term outcome studies [Ref 50–52]
  • Predictors: Residual incongruity, persistent micro-instability, coronoid nonunion. Outcome studies [Ref 8]

8.3 Heterotopic Ossification (HO)

  • Incidence: 12–50% in Terrible Triad injuries. Retrospective cohort studies [Ref 54]
  • Risk factors: Concomitant head injury, delay to surgery, aggressive dissection, severity of initial dislocation. Retrospective studies [Ref 54]
  • Prophylaxis: Indomethacin (75 mg daily × 6 weeks) or single-dose radiation (700 cGy). Evidence for functional ROM benefit is mixed. Systematic review / RCT-level data [Ref 55]

8.4 Ulnar Neuropathy

  • Incidence: 10–20% of all elbow trauma; rises to 33% with medial approach. Systematic review [Ref 30–32]
  • Symptom resolution is significantly higher with anterior transposition (70%) vs in-situ release (22%). Systematic review [Ref 30]

9. Conclusion

The ulnar coronoid process is the linchpin of elbow stability. Management has evolved from passive observation to active, anatomically based reconstruction of joint containment. The O'Driscoll classification has established itself as the essential tool for surgical planning, correctly differentiating the Terrible Triad (lateral column / tip pathology) from VPMRI (medial column / facet pathology). Narrative synthesis

Four Technical Mandates from Current Evidence
  1. Suture Lasso Fixation is the superior choice for small tip fractures — recruits the capsule without risking comminution. [Ref 22, 35, 38]
  2. Buttress Plating is the absolute requirement for AMF fractures to resist varus shearing forces. [Ref 6, 40]
  3. Screw Fixation retains a role for large basal fragments but must often be supplemented with plating. [Ref 41, 42]
  4. Sequential Restoration of the radial head and LCL is critical in the Terrible Triad, with coronoid fixation as the "safety belt." [Ref 3, 5, 14]

While the Terrible Triad remains a formidable injury, the modern orthopaedic surgeon, armed with biomechanical principles and refined fixation techniques, can now reliably restore a stable, functional elbow — transforming a once-devastating diagnosis into a manageable surgical challenge.
