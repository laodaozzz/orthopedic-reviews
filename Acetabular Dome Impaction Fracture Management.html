1. Introduction & Pathoanatomic Considerations

Acetabular fractures involving the superior weight-bearing dome represent one of the most formidable challenges in orthopaedic traumatology. These injuries compromise the structural integrity of the hip joint at its most critical load-bearing interface, portending a high risk of long-term disability, pain, and post-traumatic osteoarthritis (PTOA). I

The Critical Role of the Acetabular Dome

The acetabulum is a complex three-dimensional structure formed by the confluence of the ilium, ischium, and pubis. It is conceptualised as an inverted "Y" supported by two primary osseous columns: the anterior (iliopubic) column and the posterior (ilioischial) column.1 These columns converge superiorly to form the acetabular roof (dome) — the principal weight-bearing surface responsible for transmitting immense physiological forces from the axial skeleton to the lower extremities.

Key Radiographic Thresholds (from source):
  • Roof arc angle <45° on all three views → inherently unstable, typically requires ORIF.1
  • CT incongruity >2 mm in the superior 10 mm of the acetabulum → strong indication for surgical fixation.1
  • Articular displacement >2 mm → most widely accepted threshold recommending ORIF.1

2. Classification & Injury Mechanisms

The Judet and Letournel classification remains the global standard, categorising acetabular fractures into ten distinct patterns: five elementary and five associated.11,12 I

Important limitation: Dome impaction is not a distinct pattern within the Judet and Letournel system but rather a critical injury feature accompanying several patterns (most commonly transverse, T-shaped, and both-column). A newer "Three-column classification" has been proposed, elevating the acetabular roof as a separate "roof column" (A3 fractures).15

Epidemiology & Mechanism

Acetabular fractures follow a bimodal distribution:1

  • Young patients — high-energy mechanisms (MVA, motorcycle, falls from height).5
  • Elderly patients — rising incidence from low-energy falls (osteoporotic bone).1

Dome impaction results from axial loading through the femur, where the femoral head is driven superiorly into the acetabular roof — particularly when the hip is in extension.5,18

3. Prognostic Significance of Dome Impaction

Articular impaction is one of the most powerful negative prognostic indicators in acetabular fracture treatment.18 II

Critical evidence from meta-analyses:
  • Dome impaction increases the odds of conversion to THA by 2.08–3.23×.22,24 II
  • The "gull sign" (dense crescent shadow on AP radiograph) is pathognomonic for superomedial dome impaction and was 100% predictive of ORIF failure in some elderly cohorts.19 III

Pathophysiological Reframing

Dome impaction should be conceptualised not as a simple fracture characteristic but as a distinct biomechanical entity signifying the failure of the hip's primary load-bearing architecture. The articular surface and subchondral bone are crushed into softer cancellous bone, creating a structural void.29 The surgical challenge is not the displaced fragment itself but the loss of underlying support. Successful treatment must therefore address both the subchondral void (e.g., bone graft) and provide robust mechanical support (e.g., subchondral raft screws).18

4. Primary Operative Management: Open Reduction & Internal Fixation (ORIF)

ORIF has been the established gold standard for displaced acetabular fractures, particularly in younger, physiologically robust patients.1 I

4.2 Dome Impaction Reduction Techniques

  1. Elevation through the fracture site — curved elevators/bone tamps passed through fracture lines after joint distraction.19
  2. Cortical window technique — a small window in the supra-acetabular ilium provides direct portal for elevation from above.18
  3. Direct intrapelvic reduction — the modified Stoppa approach allows visualisation and elevation from within the pelvis.27

The reduced femoral head serves as an intraoperative template; concentricity is confirmed with fluoroscopy.43

4.3 Fixation Strategies & Bone Grafting

Non-negotiable step — Impaction Bone Grafting (IBG):
  • Filling the subchondral void with morselised cancellous bone (autograft preferred from iliac crest / greater trochanter).18
  • Subchondral "raft" screws (3.5 mm cortical) placed parallel to the joint surface create a buttress scaffold.18
  • For medial displacement: buttress plate along the pelvic brim.36

4.4 Clinical & Radiographic Outcomes

  • Anatomical reduction (<2 mm) → good-to-excellent outcomes in 75–80%, HHS typically >80.29 IV
  • With dome impaction (elderly): even with "good" reduction (≤3 mm), radiographic OA rate of 40% vs. 7% without impaction.53 III
  • Poor reduction (>3 mm): mean HHS 68.6 (vs. 82.1); poor outcomes in 78%.53
  • Conversion to THA in elderly with dome impaction: 14–33%.27
Conceptual framework — "The Biological Race": The composite of elevated cartilage, impacted bone graft, and metal hardware is inherently less stable than intact bone. Long-term success hinges on graft incorporation before fixation fails. In the elderly, osteoporosis weakens construct stability while diminished healing capacity slows graft incorporation — biasing the race toward failure.18,44

5. Primary Total Hip Arthroplasty — The "Fix-and-Replace" Paradigm

In response to high ORIF failure rates in older patients, a paradigm shift has occurred: performing combined ORIF and acute THA in a single procedure.26 III

5.1 Conceptual Basis

The goal of "fix" is not perfect articular reduction but rather structural pelvic integrity to support the acetabular component. This allows immediate weight-bearing, mitigating immobility-related complications in geriatric patients.8,26

5.2 The "4Es" Indication Model8

  • Established post-traumatic arthritis or AVN
  • Existing hip arthritis or femoral head/neck fracture
  • Elderly patient with comminuted fracture & osteoporotic bone
  • Expected undesirable outcome (severe cartilage injury, marginal impaction, thin posterior wall)

5.4 Outcomes

  • Mean Harris Hip Scores: 70–92 across series.26
  • Earlier independent ambulation and higher patient satisfaction vs. ORIF alone.26
  • Cemented THA for protrusio: 93.1% 15-year survival (endpoint: all-cause acetabular revision).69 IV

Mobilisation

Acute THA permits immediate weight-bearing, a critical advantage in the elderly. ORIF requires 6–12 weeks of protected weight-bearing.26

Complications

Evidence is conflicting due to selection bias:

  • A Medicare database study found THA had higher 30- and 90-day readmissions, infection rates, and reoperation.71
  • Another systematic review found ORIF had a higher rate of non-fatal complications.72
  • One-year mortality rates are comparable between groups.26
Ongoing trial: The GATOR trial (Geriatric Acetabular FracTures: Open Reduction Internal Fixation Versus Replacement — NCT03713853) is a multicenter prospective RCT designed to address this controversy.73

6.2 ORIF vs. Acute THA in Middle-Aged Patients

A key case-controlled study in middle-aged patients with posterior wall fractures found that at 6.2-year follow-up, 37% of ORIF patients had required or been referred for THA, vs. only 13% revision rate in the acute THA group.68 III

6.3 Acute vs. Delayed THA

A systematic review comparing acute vs. delayed (salvage) THA found II:

  • Functional outcomes and complication rates were comparable.
  • Delayed THA had a significantly higher revision rate (17.1%) vs. acute THA (4.3%).59
  • Performing THA in a hip with prior hardware, scar, distorted anatomy, and potential bone loss may compromise long-term durability.

7.1 Post-Traumatic Osteoarthritis

PTOA is the most common and consequential long-term complication, with incidence ranging from 12% to 57%. Key predictors include non-anatomic reduction, articular impaction, femoral head cartilage damage, advanced age, and complex fracture patterns.1,8,24

7.2 Avascular Necrosis

AVN incidence: ~11.7% (range <10% to 11.7%). Average time to diagnosis: ~13 months; most cases apparent within 18–24 months.79

8. Emerging Technologies & Future Directions

8.1 Minimally Invasive Fixation (MIF)

Percutaneous cannulated screws under fluoroscopic/CT guidance for non-displaced or minimally displaced fractures. Benefits: reduced operative time, blood loss, and systemic complications.83,84

8.2 3D Printing

  • Patient-specific physical pelvic models for tactile preoperative planning.87
  • Meta-analysis: reduces operative time by 40–60 min, blood loss by 170–225 mL.87
  • Patient-specific surgical guides and custom titanium implants under investigation.89

8.3 Robotic-Assisted Surgery

Preoperative CT-based virtual planning with intraoperative optical tracking and robotic arm guidance for precise percutaneous screw placement. May expand indications for MIF.92,93

8.4 Advanced Biological & Metallic Augmentation

  • Highly porous metals (tantalum, trabecular titanium): excellent osteoconduction, strong initial press-fit, bone ingrowth.97
  • Modular porous metal augments: fill large segmental defects; fixed to host bone with screws.97
  • Scaffolds and stem cells for chondral defects — clinical evidence still nascent.100

9. Clinical Recommendations & Evidence Gaps

Recommendations

Young, active patients (<60 yr) with good bone quality: I
ORIF remains standard of care. Goals: anatomical reduction, elevation of impacted dome fragments, impaction bone grafting, subchondral raft screws, and buttress plating.
Elderly patients (>65 yr) with comminution, severe dome impaction, osteoporosis, or pre-existing OA: II
"Fix-and-replace" with primary THA + stabilising internal fixation. This provides a definitive single-stage solution with early mobilisation and potentially better long-term index procedure survival.
Middle-aged patients (~60–65 yr) or younger with high-risk patterns: III
Nuanced shared decision-making required. Weigh long-term native joint preservation (demanding ORIF) against higher index procedure survival with acute THA. Consider activity level, occupation, and expectations.

Unresolved Questions & Evidence Gaps

  • Randomised controlled trials comparing ORIF vs. THA in geriatric patients — the GATOR trial (NCT03713853) is anticipated.73
  • Standardised accelerated rehabilitation protocols — current practices highly variable and lacking strong evidence.26
  • Core outcome set (COS) for surgically treated acetabular fractures — significant gap hindering evidence synthesis.101
  • Long-term clinical impact and cost-effectiveness of 3D printing and robotics — further research required.
