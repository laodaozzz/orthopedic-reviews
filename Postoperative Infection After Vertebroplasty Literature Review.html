Postoperative Infection Following Percutaneous Vertebroplasty

A Comprehensive Interactive Educational Summary of Incidence, Diagnosis, and Management — Synthesized from a 2019–2024 Systematic Review

Epidemiology & Incidence Evidence Level I – Systematic Review

Percutaneous vertebroplasty (PVP) and balloon kyphoplasty (PBK) have revolutionized the management of osteoporotic vertebral compression fractures (OVCFs) and spinal malignancies. Despite their favorable safety profile, the introduction of polymethylmethacrylate (PMMA) bone cement carries the rare but devastating risk of implant-associated infection, including spondylitis, spondylodiscitis, and epidural abscess formation. [1]

Overall Incidence

A landmark analysis of the South Korean Health Insurance Review and Assessment (HIRA) database encompassing 23,868 patients established an overall surgical site infection (SSI) incidence of 0.20%.[8] Systematic reviews covering 2000–2025 corroborate this as "exceedingly rare," yet mortality in infected cohorts may reach 12.3%.[1] The general background rate of spinal infections in the broader population is approximately 2.2 per 100,000 per year, indicating that iatrogenic PMMA introduction significantly amplifies local vertebral osteomyelitis risk.[9]

📌 Key Epidemiological Insight The incidence clusters within high-risk subgroups—particularly oncologic cohorts with concomitant immunosuppression, malnutrition, and cytotoxic therapy—while remaining low in general osteoporotic populations.[6]

PVP vs. Kyphoplasty: Is There a Difference?

Theoretical concerns about differential infection risk between the two modalities have been largely settled. The HIRA database study found:

  • PVP: 0.21% incidence (43 cases)
  • PKP: 0.13% incidence (4 cases)
  • p = 0.3872 — no significant difference[8]

This confirms that infection risk is intrinsic to percutaneous transpedicular access and foreign-body implantation, not to the specific technique of cavity creation.[4]

Etiology & Pathogenesis Evidence Level I – Systematic Review

Mechanisms of Bacterial Entry

Post-PVP infections arise through three primary mechanisms:[10]

  • Direct Inoculation: Predominant for early-onset infections. The Jamshidi needle translocates commensal skin flora (S. epidermidis, C. acnes) into the vertebral body, particularly during multiple fluoroscopic adjustments in complex anatomy.
  • Hematogenous Seeding: The valveless Batson's venous plexus allows retrograde flow from pelvic and abdominal viscera. Bacteria from UTIs, dental abscesses, or endocarditis can seed the vertebral body, with PMMA acting as a preferential colonization site.
  • Contiguous Spread: Extension from adjacent soft tissue infections (e.g., psoas abscess), less common in PVP context.[10]

The Biofilm Problem

The pathogenesis of post-PVP infection is fundamentally an implant-associated infection. PMMA bone cement is biologically inert but acts as a substrate for bacterial colonization. The "race for the surface" concept posits that in the avascular environment of necrotic bone or within the cement-bone interface, bacteria frequently outcompete host cells for adhesion.[12]

Once adhered, organisms such as S. aureus and S. epidermidis produce a protective glycocalyx matrix, forming a biofilm. Within this biofilm, bacteria enter a sessile, metabolically reduced state that renders them phenotypically resistant to systemic antibiotics, even if genetically susceptible.[12] Additionally, the exothermic polymerization of PMMA causes thermal necrosis of surrounding bone, creating a devitalized zone (sequestrum) poorly perfused and highly susceptible to secondary infection.[2]

🔴 Clinical Implication The biofilm on retained PMMA cement explains the >93% failure rate of conservative antibiotic-only therapy.[1] Eradication frequently requires surgical removal of the cement.

Microbiological Landscape Evidence Level II–III – Cohort Studies & Case Series

PYOGENIC Staphylococcus aureus

Most frequently isolated organism — 44–59% of pyogenic vertebral osteomyelitis globally. Aggressive course with rapid abscess formation. MRSA is a growing concern in healthcare-exposed patients.[5,12]

PYOGENIC CoNS (S. epidermidis)

Increasingly recognized as true pathogens rather than contaminants. Indolent course delays diagnosis for months. Robust biofilm formation on PMMA makes eradication difficult without hardware removal.[3,12]

MYCOBACTERIAL M. tuberculosis

In some Asian series, TB accounted for up to 35.3% of isolated pathogens.[1] May represent reactivation of latent TB precipitated by fracture trauma or misdiagnosis of primary Potts disease.[16,17]

FUNGAL Candida / Aspergillus

Rare but growing. Candida associated with candidemia from central lines. Aspergillus reported even in immunocompetent patients, potentially from contaminated injections.[6,18,19]

GRAM-NEGATIVE E. coli / Pseudomonas

Less common; significantly associated with hematogenous seeding from urinary tract, especially in elderly patients with UTI history.[5]

Diagnostic Dilemmas & Solutions Evidence Level II–III

Diagnosing infection after PVP is one of the most challenging tasks in spinal surgery. The classic triad of fever, pain, and leukocytosis is frequently incomplete or absent. The "symptom-free interval" can range from days in virulent S. aureus to years in low-grade fungal or Propionibacterium infections.[20]

Clinical Presentation

  • New or worsening back pain — the hallmark symptom. Infectious pain is typically non-mechanical, present at rest, and worse at night.[21]
  • Fever — present in <50% of patients with pyogenic spondylodiscitis — an unreliable sign.[21]
  • Neurological deficits — late signs indicating epidural abscess or pathological collapse.[10]

Biopsy

When clinical and radiographic findings are suggestive but not definitive, CT-guided percutaneous biopsy is mandatory before initiating antibiotics.[22] Needle biopsy yield is typically 50–70%; if initial biopsy is negative with high clinical suspicion, repeat or open biopsy is indicated. Empirical antibiotics should be avoided prior to biopsy unless the patient is septic or has progressive neurological deficits, as they significantly reduce culture yield.[15]

Therapeutic Management Evidence Level II–III

Conservative Management

Conservative management (6–12 weeks of IV antibiotics followed by oral suppression) has a >93% failure rate in post-PVP infections due to biofilm on retained PMMA.[1] It is generally reserved for:

  • Patients medically unfit for surgery
  • Very early infections caused by highly sensitive organisms
  • Small abscesses without neurological compromise

Surgical Management

Surgery is the definitive treatment for established infections. Key principles:

  • Debridement and Cement Removal — technically demanding; PMMA interdigitates with trabecular bone ("hard like granite")[23]
  • Anterior Approach (Corpectomy) — provides best exposure for complete cement removal with direct dural visualization[14]
  • Posterior Approach (Costotransversectomy) — for patients who cannot tolerate anterior surgery; allows circumferential decompression[23]
  • Titanium Instrumentation — current consensus supports use in active infection, as titanium does not harbor biofilm as aggressively as stainless steel[9]

The Masquelet Technique (Two-Stage Reconstruction)

  • Stage 1: Aggressive debridement + placement of antibiotic-loaded PMMA spacer (delivers high local vancomycin/tobramycin concentrations) + induces bioactive membrane formation
  • Stage 2: Spacer removal after 6–8 weeks + reconstruction with bone graft inside induced membrane[24]

Management Algorithm

Step 1 — Suspect Infection

New/worsening pain + elevated CRP → Order MRI

Step 2 — MRI Confirms Spondylodiscitis?

Yes + Neurological Deficit / Instability / Epidural Abscess: → Immediate Surgery (Debridement + Stabilization)

Yes + Neurologically Intact: → CT-Guided Biopsy

Step 3 — Biopsy Results

Positive: Targeted IV antibiotics (6–12 weeks). Monitor CRP. If CRP fails to drop or symptoms worsen after 2–4 weeks → Surgery.

Negative: Repeat biopsy or open biopsy. Do NOT treat empirically without organism identification — critical to rule out TB or fungal infection.

🔴 Prognostic Factor The most significant predictor of post-treatment mobility is the preoperative neurological status (OR = 6.0, p = 0.001).[1] This underscores the critical need for early diagnosis and intervention before irreversible neurological injury.

Emerging Themes & Research Directions Evidence Level IV – Case Reports

For researchers, a review of 2019–2024 literature reveals several "novelty" themes representing the frontier of clinical reporting:[1,2,17,19,20]

1. Fungal Spondylodiscitis in Immunocompetent Hosts

Aspergillus or Candida infections in seemingly immunocompetent individuals. The "diagnostic odyssey" narrative—months of treatment for "culture-negative" osteomyelitis before fungal stains reveal the pathogen.[19]

2. Silent TB Reactivation

PVP performed for "osteoporotic" fracture that actually harbored latent Potts disease. Pain relief from PVP masks simmering infection, leading to catastrophic delayed presentation.[17]

3. Extreme Latency ("Skip Lesions")

Infections occurring years after index procedure — one notable case documented 9-year latency. Evidence for late hematogenous seeding from distant events (e.g., dental work).[20]

4. Septic Cement Embolism

Infected cement emboli causing septic pulmonary embolisms — bridging spinal surgery, infectious disease, and pulmonology.[2]
