I. The Architecture of Evidence in Orthopaedics

A guide to clinical trial registries, biomedical databases, and strategic evidence retrieval

Evidence-based practice forms the bedrock of modern orthopaedic surgery and musculoskeletal medicine. The ability to critically appraise and synthesize the vast body of clinical research requires a sophisticated understanding of the global architecture of evidence—the ecosystem of clinical trial registries where research is born and the biomedical databases where it is published and archived. Review / Expert Opinion

1.1 The Foundation of Transparency: Clinical Trial Registries & the WHO ICTRP

The Mandate for Registration

The landscape of clinical research was reshaped by a collective move toward transparency aimed at mitigating publication bias—the tendency for studies with negative or unfavorable results to remain unpublished, skewing the evidence base. Narrative Review The International Committee of Medical Journal Editors (ICMJE) mandates that for an interventional clinical study to be considered for publication, it must first be registered in a public, WHO-compliant registry before the enrollment of the first participant.2 While the ICMJE policy focuses on interventional trials, many registries also accept observational studies, further enhancing transparency across all research designs.1

ClinicalTrials.gov: The Global Epicenter

Operated by the U.S. National Library of Medicine, ClinicalTrials.gov is the largest and most widely utilized single clinical trial registry in the world.3 It serves as a global repository for both publicly and privately funded clinical studies conducted in over 200 countries. A typical study record provides structured information including purpose, recruitment status, eligibility criteria, study locations, and contact information.5

Key Insight for Orthopaedic Researchers: ClinicalTrials.gov encompasses RCTs evaluating new treatments or devices5, large-scale prospective multicenter longitudinal cohort studies on total joint arthroplasty7, and studies tracking the natural history of conditions like cerebral palsy.8 The detailed inclusion and exclusion criteria in each record are particularly valuable for assessing study relevance.

Key International Registries

While ClinicalTrials.gov is dominant, a comprehensive global search necessitates awareness of other WHO-recognized primary registries:

  • ISRCTN Registry: A primary WHO and ICMJE-recognized registry with strong UK presence, accepting all types of clinical research from around the world, including non-interventional designs.1
  • EU Clinical Trials Information System (CTIS): In 2024, CTIS was officially designated as a WHO primary registry, replacing the older EU-CTR platform and enhancing global accessibility of EU/EEA trial data.11
  • Other Notable Registries: ANZCTR (Australia/New Zealand), ChiCTR (China), CRiS (Republic of Korea), CTRI (India), among many others.10

The WHO ICTRP: The Federated Search Portal

The proliferation of primary registries created data fragmentation. The WHO addressed this by creating the International Clinical Trials Registry Platform (ICTRP)—a meta-search portal providing a single point of access to records from all WHO-recognized primary registries.12,13 As of 2020, the ICTRP contained records for nearly 700,000 trials, surpassing ClinicalTrials.gov alone.4 For any researcher aiming to conduct a systematic and unbiased search, utilizing the WHO ICTRP is a methodological necessity.

1.2 Accessing the Published Record: Major Biomedical Literature Databases

PubMed/MEDLINE: The Cornerstone

PubMed is the freely accessible search interface for the MEDLINE database, containing millions of citations. Its central importance stems from its Medical Subject Headings (MeSH), a controlled vocabulary that allows highly structured and precise searches far more powerful than keyword searches.14,15

Embase: The Comprehensive Alternative

Produced by Elsevier, Embase includes all of MEDLINE's content plus citations from over 3,000 journals not indexed in MEDLINE. Two features are particularly vital for orthopaedics:18

  • Conference Abstracts: Deep indexing of millions of conference abstracts provides access to cutting-edge findings before full-text publication.
  • Emtree Thesaurus: More granular than MeSH, with extensive indexing for medical devices, including trade names and manufacturers.20

Scopus: Bibliometric & Interdisciplinary Analysis

Scopus is the leading database for bibliometric analysis—quantitative assessment of research output. It provides citation metrics, author affiliations, and funding information to map research trends, identify prolific authors, and analyze collaboration networks.22,23

The Cochrane Library: Gold Standard for Synthesized Evidence

The Cochrane Library is the world's foremost repository of systematic reviews and meta-analyses. Before embarking on a new research project, searching the Cochrane Library is the essential first step to determine if a comprehensive synthesis already exists.25,26

II. Adhesive Capsulitis: Evidence Synthesis from Prospective Cohort Studies

Applying evidence retrieval principles to a common orthopaedic condition—frozen shoulder

Adhesive capsulitis is a clinical syndrome characterized by the spontaneous onset of shoulder pain accompanied by a progressive, debilitating loss of both active and passive glenohumeral motion.29 By synthesizing findings from key prospective cohort studies and high-quality systematic reviews, a nuanced, evidence-based understanding emerges that challenges long-held clinical dogma. Prospective Cohort Evidence

2.1 The Natural History: Re-examining the Classic Narrative

The Traditional Three-Phase Model

The classic description posits a progression through three overlapping stages, with a total duration estimated at one to three years:30

Phase 1: Freezing

Gradual onset of severe, diffuse pain (often nocturnal). Range of motion begins to decrease.

Duration: 6 weeks – 9 months

Phase 2: Frozen

Pain may subside; replaced by profound stiffness. External rotation most severely affected.

Duration: 4 – 12 months

Phase 3: Thawing

Gradual, spontaneous improvement in range of motion and resolution of symptoms.

Duration: 6 months – 2+ years

Challenging the Dogma of "Self-Limitation"

Key Evidence Update: The concept that frozen shoulder is uniformly "self-limiting" and inevitably leads to full recovery is a clinical myth. A substantial body of long-term prospective evidence demonstrates that a large minority are left with persistent symptoms and objective deficits.

Reeves (1975) — the foundational prospective study — followed 41 patients for 5–10 years and found the average total duration was 30.1 months. More importantly, over half still had some slight, objectively measured restriction of movement.34 Prospective Cohort

Binder et al. (1984) followed 40 patients for a mean of 44 months, confirming that objective restriction of shoulder range of motion frequently persisted, even when functional impairment was minimal.35 Prospective Cohort

Hand et al. (2008) — the most compelling data — followed 223 patients (269 shoulders) for a mean of 4.4 years. Findings:36 Prospective Cohort

  • 41% of patients reported some ongoing symptoms
  • 6% continued to experience severe symptoms with significant pain and functional loss

A 2017 systematic review by Wong et al. concluded that the long-standing theory of progression through distinct recovery phases leading to complete resolution is "unfounded" and, in their words, "fiction."37 Moderate-quality evidence from modern longitudinal studies shows most improvement occurs early in the disease course, not in a late "thawing" phase. Systematic Review

2.2 A Web of Causality: Risk Factors & At-Risk Populations

While primary adhesive capsulitis is often termed "idiopathic," a robust body of evidence identifies strong associations with various systemic and metabolic conditions. The condition is increasingly understood as a potential local manifestation of underlying systemic pathology. Multiple Cohort Studies

The Central Role of Metabolic Disease

Diabetes Mellitus (10–36% prevalence vs 2–5% general) Thyroid Disorders Dyslipidemia Obesity / High BMI Cardiovascular Disease Cervical Spondylosis
  • Diabetes Mellitus: The strongest and most consistent risk factor. Prevalence rises from 2–5% in the general population to 10–36% in individuals with diabetes.29 A large-scale Korean nationwide cohort study (>3.4 million subjects) confirmed significantly increased risk in both type 2 diabetes and prediabetes.41 Diabetes predicts a higher likelihood of developing frozen shoulder after surgery42, greater utilization of nonoperative treatments44, and poorer long-term outcomes.45 The pathophysiology involves accumulation of advanced glycation end products (AGEs) in capsular tissue, promoting inflammation and abnormal collagen cross-linking.29 Large Cohort
  • Thyroid Disorders: Both hypothyroidism and hyperthyroidism are associated with increased risk.40
  • Dyslipidemia & Obesity: Multiple large cohort and case-control studies have identified hyperlipidemia and high BMI as independent risk factors.40,48

Demographic & Other Systemic Factors

  • Age & Gender: Peak incidence between ages 40–70 years with a clear female predominance.29
  • Post-Surgical/Post-Traumatic States: A prospective cohort study of 505 patients undergoing elective shoulder surgeries found the incidence of postoperative frozen shoulder to be 11%.42 Prospective Cohort
  • Dupuytren's Contracture: Histological studies reveal remarkable similarities in collagen and myofibroblast changes between adhesive capsulitis and Dupuytren's tissue, suggesting a common predisposition to fibrosis.31
Clinical Implication: The clustering of diabetes, prediabetes, obesity, hyperlipidemia, and cardiovascular disease strongly suggests adhesive capsulitis is often a sentinel sign of systemic metabolic syndrome. Diagnosis of "idiopathic" frozen shoulder should prompt screening for these conditions.

2.3 Long-Term Outcomes of Therapeutic Interventions

The evidence supports a value-based, stepped-care approach, beginning with the least invasive options. However, recent high-quality evidence challenges the traditional role of some first-line therapies.

Conservative Management

  • Physical Therapy (PT): Long-standing mainstay, though evidence is mixed. A Cochrane review concluded there is little overall evidence to support PT as a standalone treatment.26,30 Cochrane Review
  • "Watchful Waiting" vs. Physical Therapy: A recent Level I RCT (Siddiq et al., 2024) randomized 61 patients to standard PT or watchful waiting. At 12 months, no significant differences were found in ASES, DASH, or pain scores. However, PT costs were ten times higher. This potentially practice-changing evidence suggests formal PT may offer no additional clinical benefit over guided self-management.54 Level I – RCT
  • Intra-articular Corticosteroid Injections: Consistent, high-quality evidence from systematic reviews shows significant short-term benefits (improvements in pain and function for up to 7 weeks), though benefits are often not sustained long-term.55,57 Systematic Review / Meta-analysis
  • Hydrodistension: May offer modest improvements in pain and disability at 3–12 weeks (mixed quality evidence).27 A 2-year prospective study of 202 patients reported excellent long-term outcomes, though patients with diabetes had higher recurrence rates.46 Prospective Cohort

Interventions for Refractory Cases

  • Manipulation Under Anesthesia (MUA): Long-term prospective studies demonstrate excellent, sustained results. One study with mean follow-up of 15 years found significant, lasting improvements in motion and function.58 A large prospective cohort of 295 patients found short-term gains maintained at 3.6 years.59 Prospective Cohort
  • Arthroscopic Capsular Release: Reserved for the most recalcitrant cases. A prospective study of 210 patients found 46% failed initial injection therapy and proceeded to release with significant improvements at 3 months.45 A long-term study (mean 7 years) confirmed improvements in motion, pain, and function were maintained and sometimes enhanced over time.60 Prospective Cohort

III. Synthesis & Future Directions

3.1 Integrated Strategy for Evidence Retrieval

An expert-level, comprehensive workflow for unbiased evidence retrieval in orthopaedics:

  1. Step 1 — Identify Ongoing/Unpublished Trials: Begin at the WHO ICTRP search portal to capture a global view of all registered trials, minimizing selection bias.4
  2. Step 2 — Search for Existing Synthesized Evidence: Search the Cochrane Library to determine if a high-quality systematic review already exists.25
  3. Step 3 — Comprehensive Primary Literature Search: Perform parallel searches in PubMed/MEDLINE (MeSH) and Embase (Emtree). This dual-database approach is the standard for high-quality systematic reviews.14,18
  4. Step 4 — Analyze the Research Landscape: Use Scopus for bibliometric analyses to identify seminal papers, map collaborations, and quantify trends.22

3.2 Gaps in Evidence & Recommendations for Future Research

  • Natural History: Need for large-scale, modern prospective studies using validated patient-reported outcome measures to define true prognosis and identify predictors of poor outcomes.
  • Risk Factors & Pathophysiology: Precise molecular mechanisms linking hyperglycemia, dyslipidemia, and inflammation to capsular fibrosis require further elucidation. Prospective studies should investigate whether early management of systemic conditions can prevent onset.
  • Treatment Outcomes: The "watchful waiting" RCT54 is potentially practice-changing but needs replication. Future research should identify specific patient subgroups who benefit from structured PT. There is a lack of head-to-head RCTs comparing modern options (hydrodistension vs. MUA vs. arthroscopic release). Emerging biologics (e.g., PRP) require larger, well-designed RCTs.61
