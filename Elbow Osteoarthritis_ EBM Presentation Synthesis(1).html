1. Introduction & Biomechanics

The elbow is a highly constrained, complex trochoginglymoid articulation engineered to serve as a stable fulcrum for the upper extremity, enabling precise spatial positioning of the hand. Unlike the hip or knee—where osteoarthritis (OA) is characterized by progressive cartilage degradation and joint space collapse—elbow OA is distinctly characterized by hypertrophic osteophyte formation, severe capsular contracture, and relative preservation of articular cartilage until end-stage disease.1

Biomechanical Forces

The elbow consists of three distinct articulations within a single synovial cavity:4

  • Ulnohumeral joint (ginglymus/hinge) — responsible for flexion and extension
  • Radiocapitellar joint (trochoid/pivot) — facilitating pronation and supination
  • Proximal radioulnar joint

Static loading across the elbow can equal three times body weight; during dynamic activities (pushing, lifting, throwing), compressive forces may reach six times body weight.5 The intrinsic congruity of the ulnohumeral articulation distributes these loads evenly across hyaline cartilage, explaining why cartilage remains preserved even in advanced osteophytic disease. Stability in extension relies on bony congruency, while the anterior band of the medial collateral ligament (MCL) and the lateral ulnar collateral ligament (LUCL) provide constraint during mid-arc motion.4

2. Etiology: Primary vs. Post-Traumatic Osteoarthritis

A precise distinction between primary OA and post-traumatic arthritis (PTA) is not merely academic—it fundamentally alters surgical decision-making, dictates joint-preserving vs. joint-replacing procedures, and determines anticipation of postoperative complications.1

Primary Osteoarthritis

Primary elbow OA is relatively rare, representing approximately 2–3% of symptomatic arthritic complaints in the general population.6 It predominantly affects middle-aged men (4:1 ratio) with histories of heavy manual labor, extensive weightlifting, or overhead throwing sports.7

Systematic reviews report pooled prevalence of elbow OA at ~31% among workers exposed to high-frequency vibration and ~20% among heavy manual laborers, though overall evidence for strict occupational causality remains low due to confounding variables.8 Level IV

Pathomechanical Hallmarks of Primary OA Repetitive terminal impact causes hypertrophic bone formation at articular margins, while central cartilage is preserved. Cardinal impingement sites:
  • Olecranon fossa & olecranon tip: "Kissing lesions" during terminal extension block full extension → rigid flexion contracture
  • Coronoid process & coronoid fossa: Impingement during terminal flexion fills the coronoid fossa → restricts flexion
Patients classically present with pain only at terminal extremes of motion, while mid-arc motion remains pain-free.6

Post-Traumatic Osteoarthritis

PTA is the most common etiology of elbow arthritis, arising from complex intra-articular distal humerus fractures, comminuted radial head fractures, severe dislocations, and "terrible triad" injuries (radial head + coronoid fracture + dislocation).7

Unlike primary OA, PTA involves direct, irreversible chondrocyte damage at the moment of injury. Residual incongruity, ligamentous insufficiency, and osseous malunion alter kinematic load distribution, rapidly accelerating cartilage wear. Patients present with severe multi-planar stiffness, multidirectional instability, and chronic pain throughout the entire arc of motion—not just at the extremes.10

Second-Order Clinical Insight on Etiology Joint-preserving procedures (osteocapsular debridement, O-K procedure) yield excellent outcomes in primary OA because the surgeon addresses a mechanical block to motion, revealing healthy preserved cartilage. In PTA, removing osteophytes does not solve mid-arc pain from destroyed cartilage. PTA patients are at significantly higher risk of requiring progression to TEA.10

3. Clinical Evaluation & Broberg-Morrey Classification

Clinical evaluation requires meticulous documentation of the arc of motion, specific pain points within the arc, mechanical locking (indicating loose bodies), and signs of ulnar neuropathy. Accurate radiographic staging is essential for treatment planning and research standardization.

The Broberg-Morrey Classification

The Broberg-Morrey Classification remains the universally accepted gold standard for radiographically grading both primary and post-traumatic elbow OA, based on progressive osteophyte formation and joint space narrowing on standard AP and lateral radiographs.13

Table 1. Broberg-Morrey Radiographic Classification of Elbow OA
Grade Radiographic Criteria Clinical Correlate
Grade 0 Normal joint; no narrowing or osteophytes Asymptomatic or purely soft-tissue pain
Grade 1 Slight joint space narrowing with minimal osteophyte formation Mild terminal pain; early olecranon spurring on lateral view
Grade 2 Moderate joint space narrowing with moderate osteophyte formation Noticeable loss of extension/flexion arc; osteophytes filling coronoid and olecranon fossae
Grade 3 Severe degeneration with gross destruction of joint architecture Severe mid-arc pain, profound stiffness, potential subluxation, near-complete obliteration of joint spaces

Imaging Reliability

Plain radiographs demonstrate an intraclass correlation coefficient (ICC) of 0.620–0.656 for the Broberg-Morrey system, indicating substantial agreement.15 However, plain films underestimate the 3D volume of osteophytes and number of loose bodies. 3D-CT staging based on Broberg-Morrey criteria pushes interobserver reliability to "almost perfect" agreement (ICC 0.867–0.909) and is now considered essential for preoperative surgical planning.15,16 Level III

4. Non-Operative Management & Orthobiologics (PRP)

Initial management is strictly non-operative: activity modification (avoiding heavy lifting, pneumatic tools, repetitive terminal loading), NSAIDs for acute synovitis, and physical therapy to optimize dynamic muscle stabilizers.6

Pathophysiology of Platelet-Rich Plasma

PRP is an autologous blood derivative produced by centrifugation, concentrating platelets into a small plasma volume.18 Upon activation within the joint, alpha granules release critical anabolic and anti-inflammatory growth factors including PDGF, TGF-β, IGF, FGF, and VEGF.17

At the molecular level, these cytokines stimulate chondrocyte proliferation, promote extracellular matrix (ECM) synthesis, regulate synoviocytes, and inhibit pro-inflammatory interleukins (IL-1β) and matrix metalloproteinases (MMPs).17

Clinical Context Level of Evidence Key Findings
Knee OA & Lateral Epicondylitis Level I Systematic reviews and meta-analyses of dozens of RCTs confirm PRP is superior to HA and corticosteroids for sustained pain relief and functional improvement.19
Leukocyte-Poor vs. Rich PRP Level I LP-PRP preferred for intra-articular use: same regenerative efficacy but 56–64% reduction in post-injection adverse events. Optimal concentration: 600–900 × 10⁹/L.24

Note: While Level I evidence supports PRP for knee OA and lateral epicondylitis, direct evidence for elbow OA remains Level II–III. The clinical recommendation is partly extrapolated from knee OA data.

5. Surgical Interventions: Joint-Preserving Procedures

When conservative measures fail, surgical intervention is mandated. For primary OA with terminal impingement but preserved mid-arc cartilage, joint-preserving osteocapsular debridement is the gold standard.10

Open Outerbridge-Kashiwagi (O-K) Procedure / Ulnohumeral Arthroplasty

Conceived by Kashiwagi (1978) and refined by Morrey as Ulnohumeral Arthroplasty (UHA), the O-K procedure addresses both anterior and posterior compartments through a single posterior approach.29

Surgical Technique

  1. Limited 4–6 cm posterior midline incision centered over the olecranon
  2. Triceps-splitting or triceps-elevating approach to expose posterior capsule
  3. Resection of impinging olecranon tip osteophyte
  4. Creation of a ~1.5 cm circular fenestration window through the olecranon fossa floor, connecting posterior to anterior compartment
  5. Through this window: excise coronoid osteophytes, extract anterior loose bodies, perform anterior capsulectomy
Dual Purpose of the Fenestration 1. Mechanical Decompression: Creates a void accommodating the olecranon tip during extension, eliminating bony impingement.
2. Anterior Portal Access: Provides direct visual and mechanical access to the anterior compartment through a posterior approach, avoiding anterior neurovascular structures.29

Outcomes: Level III / Level IV Arc of motion improves from ~90° preoperatively to 115–120° postoperatively. MEPI scores improve to good/excellent in >85% of cases.33,36

Arthroscopic Osteocapsular Debridement

Arthroscopy provides magnified visualization while minimizing soft-tissue trauma and extensor mechanism disruption.38 Level III / Level IV

Meta-analyses show MEPS improvements of 26.8–34 points and average flexion-extension arc increase of ~23.3°.40 The primary advantage is superior short-term pain relief and zero reported deep infection rates across large meta-analyses.39

6. Total Elbow Arthroplasty (TEA)

TEA represents the absolute terminal step in the surgical algorithm. Originally engineered in the 1970s for low-demand elderly rheumatoid arthritis patients, modern indications have cautiously expanded to severe primary OA, PTA, and acute unreconstructable distal humerus fractures in the geriatric population.44

Strict Indications

  • Age: Typically >65 years, low-demand lifestyle 46
  • Lifting restriction: Permanent lifelong limit of ≤10 lbs per arm to prevent catastrophic mechanical failure 46
  • Failed joint preservation: Advanced Broberg-Morrey Grade 3 with severe unrelenting mid-arc pain, complete cartilage loss, and gross joint destruction
Critical Limitation from Source The source document does not provide a specific age cutoff or weight limit with exact numerical precision. The values >65 years and ≤10 lbs are mentioned but may represent institutional practice. The source emphasizes that TEA in primary OA is strictly reserved for elderly, low-demand patients willing to adhere to permanent lifting restrictions.46

Implant Design

Semi-constrained (Linked) implants (e.g., Coonrad-Morrey, Discovery) are the most widely used. The "sloppy hinge" design allows 5–7° of varus/valgus laxity, dissipating torsional stress from the cement mantle.48

Survivorship Data

Table 4. TEA 10-Year Survivorship by Preoperative Indication47,50
Preoperative Indication Preferred Design 10-Year Survivorship Primary Failure Mode
Rheumatoid Arthritis Linked (Semi-constrained) 92.4% Polyethylene bushing wear, delayed hematogenous infection47
Primary Osteoarthritis Linked (Semi-constrained) 80–85% Aseptic loosening due to increased patient demand and torque47
Post-Traumatic Arthritis Linked (Semi-constrained) <75% Aseptic loosening, instability, triceps insufficiency47
Acute Distal Humerus Fracture Linked (Semi-constrained) 86% (at 4–5 yrs) Periprosthetic fracture, aseptic loosening54

Overall complication rates approach 16–42%, with revision rates of 13–18%.48 Meta-analysis of 8,911 primary TEAs (Level III) identified aseptic loosening (48.5% of all revisions) as the dominant failure mode, driven by polyethylene wear debris-induced osteolysis and mechanical torque on cement stems.55

Deep periprosthetic joint infection (PJI) accounts for 19.4% of revisions. The thin soft-tissue envelope over bulky implants increases wound breakdown risk. Two-stage revision success rates are only 69–76%.50

Third-Order Clinical Insight: The Survivorship Paradox Patients with RA exhibit the highest 10-year survivorship (92.4%) despite systemic osteopenia and immunosuppression. Conversely, primary OA patients with thick, healthy cortical bone demonstrate markedly inferior survivorship (80–85%). This paradox exists because OA patients are younger, retain massive forearm strength, and inevitably attempt heavy lifting postoperatively—applying unprecedented torque that destroys the polyethylene bushing and shears the cement mantle.47 Aggressive patient psychology counseling is as critical as surgical technique.

7. Concomitant Cubital Tunnel Syndrome Management

Cubital tunnel syndrome (CuTS)—compressive neuropathy of the ulnar nerve—is the second most common upper extremity compression neuropathy. In elbow OA, it is inexorably linked to the arthritic pathophysiology.57

Pathophysiology in the Arthritic Elbow

  • Medial osteophytosis: Jagged spurring around the medial epicondyle invades the cubital tunnel58
  • Capsular contracture & ganglia: Thickened, contracted medial capsule increases intraneural pressure; medial ganglia emerge from the degenerated joint2
  • Post-traumatic deformity: Progressive cubitus valgus increases tension, friction, and traction on the nerve during flexion59

Surgical Paradigm Shift: Decompression vs. Anterior Transposition

For idiopathic CuTS, Level I RCTs support simple in-situ decompression as equivalent to anterior transposition.58 Level I However, in elbow OA, this paradigm shifts entirely.59 Level III

Why In-Situ Decompression Fails in OA
  • After massive osteocapsular release, the restored flexion arc subjects the previously contracted nerve to sudden, severe stretch61
  • The scarred, bleeding retrocondylar groove predisposes to strangulating perineural fibrosis
  • 18.9% incidence of postoperative ulnar nerve dysfunction with simple decompression in stiff elbows61

Anterior subcutaneous or submuscular transposition relocates the nerve to a pristine, well-vascularized, tension-free bed anterior to the flexion axis. This reduced postoperative nerve dysfunction to 8.1% with significantly superior Amadio scores.61

Clinical EBM Guideline (Level III Evidence) Concomitant ulnar nerve anterior transposition (not simple decompression) should be the mandatory standard of care during open surgical management of severe primary and post-traumatic elbow OA, particularly when pre-existing neuropathy or a ≥30° flexion contracture exists.59

For end-stage cases with profound intrinsic muscle atrophy (McGowan Grade 3), the combination of O-K procedure + anterior transposition + supercharged end-to-side (SETS) anterior interosseous nerve (AIN) transfer yields limb-saving improvements in ROM and hand motor strength.64 Level IV

8. Key Takeaways

  • Etiology Dictates Pathology: Primary OA selectively spares central cartilage and causes terminal motion blocks via osteophytes. PTA destroys articular surfaces causing severe mid-arc pain and global stiffness.4,10
  • Radiographic Staging: The Broberg-Morrey Classification is the established gold standard. 3D-CT significantly increases interobserver reliability (ICC >0.86) over plain radiographs.13,15
  • PRP as Bridge Therapy: Leukocyte-Poor PRP represents a viable disease-modifying bridge (Level II/III for elbow, extrapolated from Level I knee data) with sustained superiority over chondrotoxic corticosteroids.19,25,26
  • Joint-Preserving Surgery: Both arthroscopic debridement and open O-K procedure yield excellent mid-term outcomes (Level III/IV). Arthroscopy offers superior pain control and zero infection risk; O-K provides greater ROM restoration in severe cases.35,39,40
  • TEA Strictures: 10-year survivorship ranges from 92.4% (RA) to <75% (PTA). Aseptic loosening (48.5% of revisions) is the dominant failure mode. TEA for primary OA is strictly for elderly, low-demand patients with permanent lifting restrictions.47,50,55
  • CuTS Management: Simple decompression is inadequate in the OA elbow. Anterior ulnar nerve transposition is mandatory during open OA surgery to prevent catastrophic postoperative nerve traction and perineural fibrosis.59,61
