1. Anatomy, Pathomechanics & the Keystone Principle

The Lisfranc Joint Complex

The Lisfranc (tarsometatarsal / TMT) joint complex is not a single articulation but a sophisticated structure comprising three functional columns and three distinct joint types: tarsometatarsal, intermetatarsal, and intertarsal.[1] It represents the critical transition between the rigid midfoot and flexible forefoot.[2]

Three-Column Anatomy

  • Medial Column: First metatarsal (M1) ↔ medial cuneiform (C1). Provides the primary axis for push-off.
  • Middle Column: M2 & M3 ↔ middle & lateral cuneiforms (C2 & C3). The most rigid column; houses the keystone.[1]
  • Lateral Column: M4 & M5 ↔ cuboid. The most mobile column, accommodating foot adaptation to uneven terrain.[1]

Ligamentous Stabilizers

Stability is conferred by a robust ligamentous system that is significantly stronger on the plantar aspect. The dorsal ligaments are comparatively weak, explaining the common dorsal/lateral displacement pattern in high-energy injuries.[1] Primary stabilizers include:

  • Plantar tarsometatarsal ligaments – strongest overall stabilizers
  • Interosseous ligaments – between adjacent cuneiforms
  • The Lisfranc ligament – the most critical single structure, spanning from plantar-lateral C1 to plantar-medial M2 base[1]
Critical Anatomical Gap: There is no direct intermetatarsal ligament between M1 and M2. The entire responsibility for stabilizing the first and second rays falls on the interosseous Lisfranc ligament.[1]

The Keystone Principle

The central biomechanical concept of the midfoot is the "keystone" configuration of the second TMT joint. The base of M2 is recessed in a stable bony mortise formed by the medial and middle cuneiforms, creating the primary source of inherent stability for the entire transverse arch.[1]

🔑
Surgical Implication: A Lisfranc injury represents a failure of this keystone, typically displaced dorsally and laterally. The entire surgical strategy—regardless of fixation choice—is fundamentally focused on reconstituting the keystone. AO principles state that restoring M2–C2 anatomy "creates the base for all further reductions."[6]

2. Classification & Pathoanatomy

Two Primary Injury Patterns

Lisfranc injuries span a wide severity spectrum from low-energy athletic mechanisms to high-energy fracture-dislocations. The critical distinction between injury types drives operative decision-making:[1,11]

The Foundational Goal: The quality of anatomic reduction is the single most important factor predicting long-term outcomes.[11] Failure to achieve anatomic reduction directly correlates with post-traumatic arthritis (PTA), chronic pain, and functional impairment.[8] The choice of fixation construct is secondary to the surgeon's ability to achieve a perfect reduction.

Critical Reduction Sequence (AO Principles)[6]

1

Restore the Keystone (M2–C2)

Re-establishing the anatomical relationship between M2 and C2 creates the base for all further reductions.[6]

2

Restore the Lisfranc Interval (C1–M2)

Pointed reduction forceps placed from medial C1 to lateral M2, compressing in the vector of the Lisfranc ligament.[6]

3

Address Remaining Joints Sequentially

1st → 3rd → 4th → 5th TMT joints, using K-wires as provisional fixation.[6,16]

Iatrogenic Deep Peroneal Nerve (DPN) Injury: The medial dorsal incision places the DPN at significant risk. A 2020 retrospective study[22] found:
  • 11% DPN injury rate from primary Lisfranc fixation
  • 23% overall DPN injury rate when hardware removal (HWR) was added
  • 71% of injuries were permanent at ≥15-month follow-up[22]

This transforms HWR from a "routine" procedure into a high-risk operation, fundamentally altering the fixation debate.[22]

4. The Central Debate: Primary Arthrodesis (PA) vs ORIF

Paradigm Evolution

Historically, ORIF was the standard for all displaced injuries; arthrodesis was reserved as a salvage procedure.[9] This paradigm shifted following Ly & Coetzee's 2006 prospective RCT[13], which demonstrated that for purely ligamentous injuries, primary arthrodesis produced superior functional outcomes compared to traditional ORIF.[13]

The Unresolved Question: Adjacent Joint Arthritis

Critical Evidence Gap: Does fusing the TMT joints transfer stress proximally, leading to adjacent joint arthritis (AJA) at the talonavicular and calcaneocuboid joints in 10–30 years?[25] A 2024 SRMA explicitly states: "The long-term effects of PA are largely unknown but may include increased adjacent joint arthritis… especially in young and active patients."[25] The same review noted: "Adjacent joint arthritis was not reported in the current literature."[25]

This unstudied risk of AJA is the only reason rigid ORIF remains in the conversation, and the primary driver behind investigation of flexible fixation options.

5. Fixation Constructs Compared

Rigid Fixation: Transarticular Screws vs Dorsal Bridge Plating

Biomechanical cadaveric studies show comparable stability between these constructs.[33,35] However, clinical data favors dorsal plating:

  • A 2020 meta-analysis[36] found significantly better AOFAS scores with dorsal plates (p=0.02).
  • A prospective comparative study[16] reported AOFAS 83.1 (plate) vs 78.5 (screw), with higher anatomic reduction rates (90.6% vs 82.1%) and lower reoperation rates.[16]

The rationale: transarticular screws violate and destroy articular cartilage, creating a guaranteed arthritic lesion. Dorsal bridge plates span the joint as an extra-articular construct, preserving cartilage.[33]

Flexible Fixation: Suture Button (Suspensory Fixation)

This paradigm is intended only for purely ligamentous injuries, stabilizing the critical C1–M2 interval.[21,38]

⚡
Transformative RTS Data (2025 SRMA): 100% return to sport, 100% return to preinjury level, and a weighted mean time to RTS of only 16.8 weeks. Pooled AOFAS score: 90.1. Secondary procedure rate: 0%.[50,41]
The Critical Advantage: Suture button fixation eliminates hardware removal, directly abolishing the 23% iatrogenic DPN injury risk from HWR.[22,50]
Caveats: Biomechanically, screw fixation is stiffer with less C1–M2 diastasis under load.[45,46] Concerns exist regarding "creep" (late loosening) and button migration, especially in osteoporotic bone.[38] All clinical data derives from case series (Level III/IV) with short-term follow-up—no RCTs yet exist.[39,47]

6. The Hardware Removal Dilemma

The Impossible Clinical Dilemma

Two high-quality 2024 studies present an intractable contradiction in the ORIF pathway:

↑ PROMIS
PF scores improved after HWR
(p=0.002)[60]
23%
DPN injury from HWR
(71% permanent)[22]

A 2024 retrospective cohort study[60] found patients who underwent HWR had statistically significant improvement in PROMIS Physical Function (p=0.002) and Pain Intensity (p=0.008) scores after removal.

Simultaneously, HWR carries a 23% risk of permanent iatrogenic DPN injury.[22] This forces a choice between suboptimal function (retained hardware) or a 1-in-4 risk of nerve damage (HWR)—the most powerful argument against rigid ORIF as a primary strategy.

Practice-Changing Implication: This flaw in the ORIF "exit strategy" is the primary engine driving Lisfranc care toward the two "no-HWR" options: Primary Arthrodesis and Suture Button Fixation.

7. Evidence-Based Treatment Algorithm

Stage 1: Diagnosis & Stability Assessment

High Index of Suspicion Required: Up to 20% of Lisfranc injuries are missed on initial evaluation.[8,11]
  • Initial non-weight-bearing (NWB) radiographs: Look for the "fleck sign" (Lisfranc ligament avulsion) or C1–M2 diastasis.[10]
  • If NWB films negative/equivocal: Weight-bearing (WB) radiographs are mandatory.[11]
  • If pain prohibits WB: CT scan for subtle fractures or subluxation.[11]

Stage 2: Treatment Decision

✓

Stable Injury (No diastasis on WB films)

Non-operative: NWB cast × 6 weeks → Walker boot with protected weight-bearing.[11]
⚡

Unstable Injury (Displaced on NWB or WB films)

Operative treatment required. Decision depends on injury pattern and patient factors:

A

Pattern A: Bony / Comminuted / Intra-Articular

Recommendation: Primary Arthrodesis (PA)
Classic, undisputed indication. Articular surfaces non-salvageable. Single definitive procedure with AOFAS 84.4, reoperation 17.6%, PTA 2.8%.[9,25]
B

Pattern B: Primarily Ligamentous – Lower Demand / Older (>40)

Recommendation: Primary Arthrodesis (PA)
Most predictable medium-term outcome. Superior pain relief (VAS 1.4), lowest reoperation (17.6%) and PTA (2.8%) rates. For "subtle" injuries, outcomes at least equivalent to ORIF.[25,30]
C

Pattern C: Primarily Ligamentous – Young (<40), High-Demand Athlete

The most complex decision requiring extensive shared decision-making. Rigid screw ORIF is increasingly obsolete for this population.

Option 1: Primary Arthrodesis
✅ Level I evidence for superior medium-term pain relief, function, return to activity (79.2%). Dramatically lower reoperation (17.6%) and PTA (2.8%).
⚠️ Unknown long-term (10–20+ year) risk of adjacent joint arthritis in young patients.[25]
Option 2: Suture Button Fixation
✅ Spares cartilage. No HWR (eliminates 23% DPN risk). 100% RTS at preinjury level in 16.8 weeks. AOFAS 90.1.
⚠️ Low-level evidence (no RCTs). Unknown long-term stability against creep. Biomechanically less stiff.[39,46]
Research Gaps Identified:
  1. Long-term (15+ year) RCTs comparing PA vs ORIF, with primary outcome of radiographic/clinical AJA.[25]
  2. Prospective RCTs comparing suture-button flexible fixation directly against PA for purely ligamentous injuries in athletes.[39]
