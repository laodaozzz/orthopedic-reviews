Two-Stage Exchange Arthroplasty for Chronic Fungal Periprosthetic Knee Infection

Evidence-Based Surgical & Pharmacological Summary — First-Stage Protocol

1. Pathobiology & Epidemiology of Chronic Fungal PJI

Fungal periprosthetic joint infection (fPJI) is among the most catastrophic complications in revision total knee arthroplasty (TKA). Although it accounts for only 1–2% of all infected arthroplasties, its clinical trajectory is notoriously recalcitrant, associated with high morbidity and economic burden. Evidence: III

The predominant pathogens are Candida species, with Candida albicans responsible for approximately 41.5–55.8% of cases, followed by C. parapsilosis (29.4%), C. glabrata (7.8%), and C. tropicalis (5.6%). These dimorphic organisms adhere to both synthetic polymers (ultra-high-molecular-weight polyethylene) and metallic substrates (porous titanium, cobalt-chromium alloys), synthesizing complex biofilms embedded in a dense extracellular polymeric substance (EPS) matrix. Evidence: III

The EPS matrix acts as a physical barrier that sequesters antifungal molecules, while the hypoxic, nutrient-deprived biofilm core induces a dormant phenotypic shift in fungal cells (persister cells), rendering them virtually impervious to cell-wall–active agents. Diagnosis is frequently delayed due to non-specific, indolent presentations.

⚠ Clinical Pearl

When fPJI is complicated by transcortical/transcutaneous sinus tracts and a history of multiple failed debridements, conservative or single-stage modalities are strictly contraindicated. DAIR yields success rates of only 0–28.7% (averaging ~12%), and single-stage exchange carries recurrence rates up to 36%. Evidence: III

The two-stage exchange arthroplasty is the definitive gold standard for chronic, complicated fPJI, with success rates of 64.4–85.0%. The first stage aims at absolute biological and mechanical eradication of the pathogen. Evidence: III

2. Preoperative Topography & Sinus Tract Pathobiology

A draining sinus tract communicating with the joint space is a pathognomonic major criterion defining periprosthetic joint infection. Pathobiologically, it is an epithelialized, fistulous conduit created by the host immune system to vent suppurative exudate and microbial byproducts from a closed, high-pressure intra-articular space. Evidence: III

In fPJI with multiple failed debridements, the sinus tract is surrounded by extensive fibrotic scarring, avascular tissue, and severely compromised dermal integrity. The entire soft tissue envelope surrounding the extensor mechanism is profoundly compromised due to repeated surgical incisions, chronic inflammation, and recurrent effusions—rendering the surrounding tissue profoundly hypoxic. Evidence: IV

⚡ Critical Data Point

fPJI with chronic sinus tracts almost universally guarantees a polymicrobial infection. Studies indicate 33–66% of fungal PJIs harbor concomitant bacterial pathogens—most notably Staphylococcus aureus, coagulase-negative staphylococci, Serratia marcescens, or Pseudomonas aeruginosa. Fungal adhesins facilitate symbiotic co-adhesion, creating an inter-kingdom synergistic polymicrobial biofilm. Evidence: III

3. Intraoperative Diagnostics: Methylene Blue–Guided Biofilm Mapping

Mature inter-kingdom biofilms and microscopic sinus tract arborizations are frequently imperceptible under standard illumination. A highly effective, evidence-based adjunct involves intra-articular injection of dilute methylene blue solution immediately prior to formal arthrotomy. Evidence: III

Protocol Steps

  1. Induction of anesthesia; proximal thigh tourniquet; sterile draping.
  2. Large-bore needle aspiration of joint fluid → secured for comprehensive microbiological culturing (prolonged fungal incubation, sonication) and histology.
  3. Preparation of 0.1% methylene blue: mix 10 mL of 0.5% methylene blue with 40 mL sterile normal saline (total 50 mL).
  4. Injection under pressure into the knee joint capsule.
  5. Passive range of motion through full flexion/extension for several minutes for homogeneous distribution.

Methylene blue possesses high biochemical affinity for the negatively charged EPS matrix and devitalized necrotic collagen. Healthy vascularized tissue repels the dye. In the presence of a chronic fistula, the dye tracks retrogradely through transmural pathways, staining the epithelialized walls and identifying occult, subfascial fistulae—effectively converting a standard anatomically guided debridement into a visually directed, tumor-like oncological resection. Evidence: III

✓ Binary Visual Field Principle

Any tissue stained blue harbors biofilm or necrotic debris and must be aggressively extirpated. Healthy tissue remains unstained.

4. Surgical Exposure & Radical Soft Tissue Excision

The overriding principle of exposure in revision arthroplasty is to achieve untethered, circumferential access to implant-bone interfaces and intramedullary canals without iatrogenic avulsion of the fragile extensor mechanism. Evidence: IV

Modified Medial Parapatellar Approach

  • Utilize previous midline vertical incision to prevent narrow ischemic skin bridges.
  • Incorporate elliptical, en bloc excision of the sinus tract cutaneous ostium and surrounding fibrotic scar tissue.
  • Sharp dissection favored over electrocautery until subfascial plane is entered.
  • Systematic resection of fibrotic tissues in the lateral gutter; division of patellofemoral ligaments.
  • Remove the polyethylene tibial insert early to decompress the joint and reduce extensor mechanism tension. Evidence: IV

Tumor-Like Resection of Sinus Tract & Synovium

Guided by methylene blue staining, the surgeon excises the entire sinus tract en bloc from its cutaneous origin through fascial layers to the joint capsule. Debridement must be aggressive, methodical, and compartmental—covering the medial gutter, lateral gutter, suprapatellar pouch, and posterior capsule. A minimum of five distinct tissue samples should be obtained from deep periprosthetic areas for independent microbiological culturing and histopathology. Evidence: III

5. Advanced Instrumentation for Bone-Preserving Implant Extraction

The extraction of well-fixed prosthetic components in the presence of severe chronic infection demands that all foreign materials—metallic implants, entire PMMA cement mantle, distal intramedullary cement restrictors, and any retained non-absorbable sutures—be completely removed. However, careless techniques can precipitate massive iatrogenic bone loss and periprosthetic fractures. Evidence: IV

Key Techniques

  • Gigli Saw for Femoral Component: The braided wire is passed behind the posterior condyles and drawn proximally/anteriorly, cleanly shearing through the acrylic cement mantle while conforming to the sagittal geometry of the prosthesis without violating subchondral host bone.
  • Osteotome Stacking: Multiple thin osteotomes inserted sequentially into the interface rather than levering on a single thick blade—allowing broad, even distribution of displacement forces via uniform axial pressure, preventing localized cortical infractions.
  • Ultrasonic Cement Removal: Devices converting electrical energy into 20–40 kHz mechanical vibrations that selectively melt and shatter PMMA through localized frictional heating and acoustic cavitation, enabling piecemeal extraction deep within intramedullary canals with minimal damage to native cortical bone. Evidence: IV
  • Anteromedial Cortical Window: For fully porous-coated sleeves or heavily osseointegrated stems, a rectangular fenestration is created in the anteromedial diaphyseal cortex, providing direct access to the implant-cement or implant-bone interface from a retrograde direction. The cortical flap is preserved and later repaired with cerclage wires. Evidence: III
  • In Vivo Disassembly of Modular Components: High-speed carbide drill bits and metal-cutting burrs disrupt adapter bolts or morse taper junctions, with wet sterile sponges packed around the joint to capture metallic debris.

6. Radical Chemical Debridement & Biochemical Biofilm Eradication

Mechanical debridement alone is fundamentally insufficient to eradicate chronic fungal PJI. Microscopic biofilm fragments, residual hyphae, and dormant persister cells remain embedded in trabecular bone interstices, Haversian canals, and soft tissue margins. An aggressive, multimodal chemical debridement protocol is mandatory. Evidence: III

📋 Sequential Irrigation Protocol

Step 1: 3% Hydrogen Peroxide → 3–10 min dwell (effervescence disrupts EPS; denatures fungal proteins via oxidative stress).
Step 2: 1.5–3.0 L normal saline washout.
Step 3: 10% Povidone-Iodine solution → 3–5 min soak (overwhelming biocidal shock via halogenation).
Step 4: 1.5–3.0 L normal saline final washout to clear cytotoxic agents.
Step 5: Full sterile team change (gowns, gloves, draping, instruments) before spacer implantation.

7. Pharmacokinetics & Antifungal Selection for PMMA

The ALBC spacer must achieve intra-articular concentrations far exceeding the minimum biofilm eradication concentrations (MBEC)—often hundreds of times higher than planktonic MICs—while avoiding nephrotoxicity/hepatotoxicity from equivalent systemic therapy. The spacer must be loaded with both antifungal and broad-spectrum antibacterial agents (e.g., vancomycin + gentamicin/tobramycin) to address the high polymicrobial co-infection rate. Evidence: III

The critical constraint: PMMA polymerization generates core temperatures of 65–100°C+. Any admixed agent must be profoundly heat-stable. Evidence: II

7a. Voriconazole: The Excipient Paradox

Voriconazole exhibits excellent broad-spectrum fungicidal activity, is heat-stable, elutes readily, and maintains activity for >30 days. However, pure voriconazole powder is not commercially available—surgeons must use the IV infusion formulation containing 93.5% inactive excipients (sulfobutyl ether beta-cyclodextrin). Evidence: II

⚠ Critical Warning — Structural Failure Risk

To achieve 600 mg pure voriconazole per 40 g PMMA, 10.5 g of commercial powder must be added. This catastrophically degrades cement strength: compressive strength falls to 68 MPa (FAILS ISO 5833 limit of 70 MPa), bending strength plummets to 37 MPa (26% below the 50 MPa minimum), and impact resistance drops by 64%. Evidence: II

7b. Amphotericin B: Deoxycholate vs. Liposomal

Amphotericin B remains the historic and contemporary standard for local delivery in fPJI. However, the specific formulation is critical:

  • Amphotericin B deoxycholate: Hydrophobic molecule forms covalent crosslinks with curing PMMA matrix → becomes trapped. Cumulative release rates as low as 0.03% of incorporated load—functionally sub-therapeutic. Evidence: II
  • Liposomal Amphotericin B (AmBisome®): Lipid nanotechnology encapsulates the active polyene in a hydrophilic phospholipid bilayer, preventing crosslinking with PMMA. Lipid vesicles elute freely through hydrophilic capillary pores. Releases 770 μg vs. 23 μg over 7 days (33× improvement), fully maintaining fungicidal activity while buffering host tissues against raw polyene cytotoxicity. Evidence: II
✓ Recommended Agent

Liposomal Amphotericin B (AmBisome®) is vastly superior to the deoxycholate formulation for local delivery via ALBC in modern fPJI management.

8. Step-by-Step ALBC Spacer Preparation Protocol

8a. Formulation Specifications

Baseline carrier: 40 g high-viscosity PMMA with 20 mL MMA monomer. Pre-impregnated cement (e.g., Copal G+C or Palacos R+G) recommended for antibacterial prophylaxis. Pure vancomycin powder (1–4 g) should be manually added for MRSA coverage. Evidence: IV

Target Active Drug Dose Total AmBisome® Powder PMMA Carrier Clinical Application
50 mg 1.33 g 40 g Prophylaxis; highest structural integrity
100 mg 2.66 g 40 g Standard dynamic articulating spacer
200 mg 5.32 g 40 g High-dose static block (severe infection)

Each vial of AmBisome® = 50 mg active AmB in 1.33 g total lyophilized lipid powder. Data from reference 53.

8b. Manual Mixing Protocol

  1. Meticulous Pulverization: All antimicrobial powders must be ground with sterile mortar and pestle to break down aggregates and maximize surface area-to-volume ratio for optimal dissolution/elution kinetics.
  2. Dry Admixture: Combine pulverized powders with 40 g dry PMMA polymer in a sterile open bowl; hand-mix ≥30–60 seconds before adding liquid monomer. Failure to dry mix creates stress risers → spacer fracture and variable elution.
  3. Monomer Introduction: Add 20 mL liquid MMA; stir gently, scraping bowl sides. A 3:1 powder:liquid ratio may reduce peak exothermic temperature (closer to 65°C vs. 75°C+), protecting heat-sensitive lipids.
  4. ⚠ NO VACUUM MIXING: Vacuum mixing eliminates air voids, creating dense, impenetrable cement that severely attenuates drug elution. ALBC spacers must be hand-mixed in an open bowl to intentionally entrap air, creating an interconnected capillary network for drug dissolution and elution. Evidence: II
  5. Molding & Implantation: During the late dough phase (dull, matte surface), hand-mold directly into the joint or inject into silicone molds.
🔑 Critical Design Principle

The air voids in hand-mixed ALBC are not defects—they are the pharmacokinetic elution engine. Capillary channels allow synovial fluid to permeate the cement, dissolve embedded drugs, and carry them into the joint space.

9. Spacer Design: Static vs. Articulating

The decision between a dynamic (articulating) and a static ALBC spacer is one of the most critical, outcome-defining junctures in the first-stage operation. While articulating spacers are favored in many revision settings for preserving quadriceps elasticity and patient mobility (~100° vs. ~90° flexion), they are absolutely contraindicated in the setting of severe soft tissue compromise. Evidence: III

Why Static Spacers Are Mandatory in Severe fPJI

  1. Elimination of Cutaneous Shear Forces: Articulation generates profound tensile/shear forces across the anterior skin envelope. In tethered, multiply operated skin, this induces microvascular trauma, marginal flap necrosis, and catastrophic wound dehiscence. A static spacer completely immobilizes soft tissues, providing the mechanical rest required for neo-angiogenesis and primary wound healing.
  2. Maximization of Antimicrobial Payload: Static spacers can be fabricated in situ with 2–3 full 40 g bags of PMMA (80–120 g), delivering exceptionally high localized antifungal/antibacterial payloads while physically obliterating the entire joint dead space to prevent hematoma formation.
  3. Structural Bridging of Massive Cavitary Bone Loss: Chronic fPJI with polymicrobial biofilm-induced osteolysis and iatrogenic bone loss from multiple revisions frequently produces AORI Type II or III defects. Static spacers with central intramedullary dowels (3-mm Steinmann pins or bent rush rods) enveloped in ALBC bridge these deficits, maintaining limb length and preventing progressive metaphyseal collapse. Evidence: III
📌 Technical Pearl — Extensor Mechanism Preservation

Strategically interpose a smooth, thin layer of hand-molded ALBC between the anterior femoral cortex and the undersurface of the extensor mechanism during curing. This creates a glide plane that prevents fibrotic obliteration of the suprapatellar pouch, facilitating the second-stage reimplantation exposure. Evidence: IV

Despite the undeniable drawbacks of static spacers—profound joint stiffness, severe arthrofibrosis, disuse osteopenia, and demanding re-exposure—they remain the only viable construct capable of salvaging the limb when the soft tissue envelope is critically endangered. The temporary sacrifice of joint kinematics is a biologically necessary trade-off for pathogen eradication, integument healing, and extremity preservation.
