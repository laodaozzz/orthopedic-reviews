Femoral Fixation & Physeal Sparing in Pediatric MPFL Reconstruction

Evidence-based educational guide for senior residents and clinicians

1. Introduction & Clinical Significance

Patellofemoral instability in skeletally immature patients presents a significant surgical challenge. Recurrent instability rates following conservative management are high (40-70%), particularly in the presence of risk factors like trochlear dysplasia, patella alta, or elevated TT-TG distances. I [1, 3]

The Medial Patellofemoral Ligament (MPFL) is the primary static restraint against lateral patellar translation, contributing approximately 60% of the restraining force during the first 30 degrees of knee flexion. II [3] While MPFL reconstruction has become the gold standard for surgical stabilization, its application in children requires meticulous attention to anatomy due to the intimate relationship with the distal femoral physis.

Critical Consideration: The distal femoral physis contributes approximately 70% of longitudinal femoral growth and 37% of total limb length. Iatrogenic physeal injury can lead to growth arrest and progressive genu valgum deformity, which may paradoxically exacerbate the original instability. III [4, 5]

2. Detailed Anatomical Considerations

2.1 MPFL Footprint Topography

The MPFL origin does not arise from a single point but from a saddle-shaped depression between the medial epicondyle and adductor tubercle. Cadaveric studies using coordinate measuring devices have quantified the center of the MPFL attachment as:

  • 14.3 mm proximal and 2.1 mm posterior to the medial epicondyle
  • 8.3 mm distal and 2.7 mm anterior to the adductor tubercle II [7, 8]

2.2 The Physis: Three-Dimensional Complexity

Contrary to common perception, the distal femoral physis is not a flat, two-dimensional disc but a complex, undulating structure with central convexities and peripheral concavities that change with age. II [9]

Surgical Pitfall: A tunnel that appears "clear" of the physis on a lateral fluoroscopic projection may transect a central physeal peak hidden from view due to this undulating topography.

2.3 Age-Dependent Migration of the Footprint

The relationship between MPFL origin and physis evolves with growth:

  • In the majority of pediatric cases, the MPFL origin is located distal to the physis (mean 6.9-10 mm) II [11]
  • In younger children (<7 years), the footprint is consistently and safely distal
  • As patients approach skeletal maturity, the insertion may migrate to or proximal to the physeal scar III [13]
  • Sex Dimorphism: Skeletally immature females have an MPFL origin 1.3-1.8 mm closer to the physis than age-matched males, reducing the safety margin. III [13]

3. Preoperative Imaging & Planning

3.1 The Schöttle Point in Pediatric Patients

The radiographic gold standard for MPFL femoral insertion is Schöttle's point. On a true lateral radiograph, it is defined by:

  1. A line extending the posterior femoral cortex
  2. A perpendicular line intersecting the posterior-most point of Blumensaat's line
  3. A perpendicular line passing through the posterior transition of the femoral condyle

The point lies 1-2 mm anterior to the posterior cortical line, between the two perpendiculars. II [7]

3.2 Advanced Imaging Modalities

Standard radiographs often fail to reveal the 3D complexity of the physis. Preoperative MRI is indispensable for measuring the specific distance from the saddle point to the nearest physeal undulation. Studies show a mean distance of ~9-10 mm, but with a range of 2-16 mm, meaning some patients have virtually no margin for error. II [12]

4. Surgical Technique: The Femoral Tunnel

4.1 The "Aim at the Scope" Technique

To reliably achieve the compound angle required for safety, surgeons can utilize this technique: With the arthroscope in the anterolateral portal viewing the intercondylar notch, the guide pin is placed at Schöttle's point and aimed directly toward the camera lens (or PCL footprint). This naturally imparts the required anterior and distal angulation. III [25]

4.2 The Geometry of Safety

Critical Angulation Parameters:
  • Distal Angulation (Sagittal Plane): 15° to 20° distally to direct hardware away from the physis
  • Anterior Angulation (Coronal/Axial Plane): 15° to 20° anteriorly to contain tunnel within condylar bone II [11, 21]

4.3 Tunnel Mechanics

In children, blind-ended sockets (20-25 mm depth) are standard to prevent lateral cortex breach and physeal tethering. Drill diameter should be minimized (typically 6-7 mm) as larger drills exponentially increase physeal damage risk. III [25, 29]

5. Graft Selection & Fixation Methods

5.1 Graft Considerations

Gracilis tendon autograft or allograft is preferred due to adequate strength and size compatibility with 6 mm tunnels. Semitendinosus may require larger tunnels (8-9 mm), increasing physeal risk. III [14, 29]

5.2 Fixation Comparison: Interference Screws vs. Suture Anchors

Biomechanical vs. Safety Trade-off

Interference Screws: Higher stiffness (~34.5 N/mm) but require larger tunnels (7 mm) that increase physeal risk. II [32]

Suture Anchors: Lower stiffness (~14.7 N/mm) but minimal physeal violation with small pilot holes (1.8-2.8 mm). Clinical outcomes show no significant difference in redislocation rates. II [34, 37]

6. Alternative Techniques & Concomitant Pathology

6.1 Non-Anatomic Procedures

The Adductor Magnus Tenodesis avoids distal femur instrumentation but is non-isometric, leading to abnormal patellofemoral contact pressures. Systematic reviews show significantly higher recurrence rates (14.8% vs. 1.3% for anatomic techniques). I [2, 40]

6.2 Genu Valgum Management

For patients with concomitant valgus deformity, simultaneous Implant-Mediated Guided Growth (IMGG) using tension band plates is supported. Correction occurs at approximately 0.7 degrees per month. Combined approaches show low recurrence (~4%) and successful mechanical axis normalization. II [44, 45]

7. Complications & Rehabilitation

7.1 Growth Arrest Mechanism

Injury to the medial distal femoral physis causes partial growth arrest (physeal bar). If the medial physis stops growing while the lateral continues, progressive genu valgum develops, paradoxically increasing lateral patellar vector force and worsening instability. III [4, 42]

7.2 Rehabilitation Protocols

Rehabilitation varies by fixation type:

  • Rigid Fixation (Screws/Anchors): Early range of motion; graft tensioned in 30-60° flexion; immediate weight-bearing in brace locked in extension. III [25]
  • Non-Anatomic/Soft Fixation: More conservative with restricted flexion for first 4-6 weeks. IV [30]

Return to sport rates for anatomic techniques reach 100% in some series, with typical timeline of 4-6 months. II [40, 48]

Conclusion

MPFL reconstruction in skeletally immature patients requires precise anatomic restoration while respecting physeal safety. The evidence supports physeal-sparing approaches using blind-ended epiphyseal sockets or suture anchor fixation. Safety is predicated on specific compound drilling trajectories (15-20° anterior and distal), verified by multi-planar fluoroscopy including the critical "notch view."

For young patients, suture anchors offer a safety net by minimizing osseous violation. For those with concomitant valgus, simultaneous implant-mediated guided growth corrects alignment while protecting the graft. Adherence to these rigorous anatomical and technical parameters enables successful stabilization while preventing long-term sequelae of recurrent instability.
