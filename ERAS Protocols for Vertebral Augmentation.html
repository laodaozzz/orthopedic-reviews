ERAS for PVP & PKP

Comprehensive Nursing Education Summary: Enhanced Recovery After Surgery Protocols for Percutaneous Vertebroplasty and Kyphoplasty

1. Introduction: The Paradigm Shift in Geriatric Spine Care

The integration of Enhanced Recovery After Surgery (ERAS) principles with minimally invasive vertebral augmentation techniques (Percutaneous Vertebroplasty (PVP) and Percutaneous Kyphoplasty (PKP)) represents a major advancement in care for the elderly with Osteoporotic Vertebral Compression Fractures (OVCF).

Clinical Burden: OVCFs affect ~25% of postmenopausal women and 40% of women aged ≥80. They trigger a "fracture cascade" of immobility, deconditioning, and increased fall risk. Level IV

🦴

PVP vs. PKP

PVP involves direct cement injection for stabilization and pain relief. PKP first uses a balloon to restore vertebral height and create a cavity, allowing for lower-pressure cement fill and potential deformity correction. Level IV

⚡

ERAS Rationale

Traditional perioperative care (prolonged fasting, high opioid use, enforced bed rest) is harmful to the elderly. ERAS minimizes physiological stress, reduces complications, shortens hospital stay, and lowers costs. Level II/III

2. Preoperative Phase: Education & Optimization

Success begins with "prehabilitation"—preparing the patient physically and psychologically.

Nursing Education Protocol: Use multimedia tools. Key elements: Explain procedure, teach the "No BLT" Rule (No Bending, Lifting, Twisting), and practice log-rolling technique preoperatively. Nursing Focus

2.1 Fasting & Nutrition

  • Solids: Fast for 6 hours prior to anesthesia. Level II
  • Clear Carbohydrate Liquids: Encourage intake up to 2 hours before surgery to maintain hydration and reduce insulin resistance. Level II

2.2 Medication Management

  • Anticoagulants: Requires careful bridging strategy (e.g., LMWH) balancing thrombotic vs. bleeding risk. Level IV
  • Pre-emptive Analgesia: Administer non-opioid analgesics (e.g., Acetaminophen, Celecoxib) before incision to block central sensitization. Level II

3. Intraoperative Care: Minimizing Stress

Anesthesia Preference: ERAS strongly favors Local Anesthesia with Sedation over General Anesthesia. This allows real-time neurological monitoring during needle/cement placement, promotes hemodynamic stability, and enables faster recovery. Level III

3.1 Critical Monitoring: Bone Cement Implantation Syndrome (BCIS)

BCIS is a rare, life-threatening complication. Triad: Hypoxia, Hypotension, Altered Consciousness. Often presents with a sudden drop in EtCO2 under GA. Level IV

Nursing Action: Immediate recognition. Surgeon must stop injection. Increase FiO2 to 100%. Prepare for fluid/vasopressor resuscitation. Nursing Focus

3.2 Positioning & Thermoregulation

Prone positioning can compromise respiratory mechanics in kyphotic patients; continuous SpO2 and EtCO2 monitoring is mandatory. Active warming is required to maintain normothermia (>36°C) and prevent coagulopathy. Level II

4. Multimodal Pain Management Protocol

ERAS utilizes multiple agents to achieve opioid-sparing analgesia, reducing side effects that delay mobilization.

5. Early Ambulation & Functional Recovery

ERAS Core Principle: Mobilize patients 2 to 4 hours post-procedure. This counters the destructive effects of bed rest (sarcopenia, atelectasis, VTE) and provides psychological reinforcement. Level II

5.1 The Log Roll Technique (Mandatory)

  1. Supine, pillow between knees. Cross arms over chest.
  2. Roll as a single unit (shoulders & hips together) onto side.
  3. From side-lying, bend knees, bring legs off bed, and use arms to push torso upright, keeping spine straight.

Nursing Focus: This must be taught and practiced before surgery.

6. Complication Monitoring & Management

6.1 Cement Leakage (Extravasation)

Incidence: Up to 80% (PVP) and 50% (PKP) are asymptomatic. Level III

Symptomatic Leakage:
• Epidural/Canal: Weakness, saddle anesthesia, bowel/bladder dysfunction → Surgical Emergency.
• Foraminal: Dermatomal radiculopathy (e.g., shooting leg pain).
• Venous: Can lead to Pulmonary Cement Embolism (PCE).

6.2 Pulmonary Cement Embolism (PCE)

Presents days to weeks post-op with dyspnea, pleuritic chest pain, tachycardia. Differentiate from intraoperative BCIS. Management involves O2 and anticoagulation (heparin to warfarin) for 3-6 months. Level IV

6.3 Adjacent Level Fractures

Up to 25% risk within 6 months due to biomechanical stress. Patients must report new pain as a "red flag." Level III

7. Discharge Planning & Long-Term Prevention

Patients often discharge same-day or next-day (<24h) if criteria are met: stable vitals, manageable pain (VAS <4/10), voiding, independent mobility with log-roll.

7.1 Home Care Instructions

  • Wound: Steri-strips/Glue. Keep dry 24h. Ice for soreness.
  • Activity: Walk immediately. No lifting >10 lbs for 4 weeks. No driving for 24h post-sedation/opioid use.
  • Red Flags: Call provider for fever >101°F, new weakness, or chest pain.

Secondary Fracture Prevention is Critical. A VCF indicates severe osteoporosis. Ensure referral for anti-osteoporotic medication (Bisphosphonates, Denosumab) and Calcium (1000-1200mg) + Vitamin D3 (800-2000 IU) supplementation. Refer to PT for fall prevention. Nursing Focus
