Fixation Constructs for Pauwels Type III Femoral Neck Fractures in Young Adults

Evidence-Based Review · Ages 18–60

1. Introduction: The Clinical Imperative

The "Unsolved Fracture" in a Distinct Demographic

The femoral neck fracture in the young adult is a distinct clinical entity, differing fundamentally in etiology, morphology, and management from fragility fractures in the elderly. Accounting for only 3–5% of all hip fractures, these injuries carry a disproportionately high burden of morbidity due to the catastrophic potential for long-term disability in a working-age population. Prevalence Data

Historically termed the "unsolved fracture" by Dickson (1953), the management of displaced femoral neck fractures in young patients continues to challenge orthopaedic surgeons with high complication rates. The dual treatment objectives — anatomical restoration of the hip joint and preservation of the femoral head blood supply — are frequently at odds with the mechanical reality of the injury.

⚠ Critical Complication Rates

Non-union: 10–30% in young adults with displaced fractures
Avascular Necrosis (AVN): Up to 86% in some series of displaced fractures Level II–III

Arthroplasty, while reliable for pain relief in the elderly, is a suboptimal solution for the young, active patient. The durability of prosthetic implants is finite; a total hip replacement in a 40-year-old introduces the certainty of future revision surgeries, activity restrictions, and periprosthetic complications. Therefore, internal fixation (osteosynthesis) remains the gold standard, with the primary goal of achieving biological union of the native bone.

The Biomechanical Battlefield: Pauwels Classification

The Pauwels classification system (1935) remains the single most important tool for preoperative planning and implant selection. It recognizes that the obliquity of the fracture line relative to the horizontal plane dictates biomechanical forces during weight-bearing. Classification System

Pauwels Classification of Femoral Neck Fractures
Type Angle Force Predominance Stability Clinical Significance
Type I <30° Compression Inherently stable Responds well to simple fixation; weight-bearing compresses fragments
Type II 30°–50° Mixed compression & shear Moderate Manageable with standard fixation if anatomical reduction achieved
Type III >50° Vertical shear Unstable Hallmark of high-energy young adult trauma; hostile to healing
🔬 Key Mechanistic Insight

In Pauwels Type III fractures, axial loading is converted almost entirely into vertical shear stress. Instead of compressing the fracture surfaces together, weight-bearing forces the femoral shaft superiorly and the femoral head inferiorly, creating a "shearing" motion. This is the primary driver of fixation failure in young adults. Level III – Biomechanical

2. Technical Overview of Fixation Constructs

Three Philosophies of Fixation

2.1 Parallel Cannulated Cancellous Screws (CCS)

For decades, three parallel cannulated screws (6.5mm or 7.3mm) inserted in an inverted triangle configuration have been the standard of care. The mechanism relies on "dynamic compression" — partially threaded screws allow the femoral head to slide back along the screws during weight-bearing, theoretically compressing the fracture site.

  • Advantages: Minimally invasive (percutaneous), preserves bone stock, protects blood supply
  • Limitation: No fixed connection between screws and lateral cortex allows toggling ("windshield wiper" effect). In vertical shear fractures, the sliding mechanism becomes a liability, permitting excessive shortening and varus collapse Level II

2.2 The Femoral Neck System (FNS)

Launched by DePuy Synthes in 2018, the FNS was designed to bridge the gap between DHS stability and minimal invasiveness. The system consists of:

  • A large central bolt bearing axial load
  • A smaller anti-rotation screw (ARS) preventing femoral head spin
  • Both elements locked together through a small lateral plate

The bolt can slide within the plate barrel (up to 20mm controlled compression), but the locking mechanism prevents angular change between implant and femoral shaft. This "fixed-angle" stability resists varus tipping even in osteoporotic or vertical fracture patterns. Level II

2.3 Crossed Fully Threaded Screws & BDSF

This category emphasizes implant geometry over hardware bulk:

  • Fully Threaded Screws (FTS): Threads along the entire length prevent sliding entirely, maintaining fracture length. This is a "length-stable" construct.
  • Biplane Double-Supported Screw Fixation (BDSF / F-technique): Developed by Filipov, places three screws in two diverging vertical planes. The distal screw rests on the dense calcar femorale and engages the posterior cortex; middle and proximal screws support the anterior cortex, creating a "truss" structure. Level II
📐 Paradigm Shift

The evolution from parallel CCS to FNS/BDSF represents a fundamental shift: from "how to compress" toward "how to stabilize against shear." The sliding mechanism designed for stable fractures becomes a liability in high-energy vertical shear patterns.

3. Comparative Biomechanics

Stiffness and Resistance to Vertical Shear

3.1 FNS vs. Parallel CCS

Biomechanical studies consistently demonstrate FNS superiority over parallel screws in unstable fracture models. Level IV – FEA & Cadaveric

  • Displacement: At 65° Pauwels angle, maximum femoral displacement was 2.877 mm for FNS vs. 3.149 mm for CCS — clinically representing the difference between stable reduction and initiation of varus collapse.
  • Stress Distribution: CCS concentrates Von Mises stress at the screw-thread/fracture junction (stress riser → breakage/cut-out). FNS distributes stress from the proximal bolt to the distal locking screw on the shaft.
  • Stiffness: Cadaveric testing shows FNS axial stiffness comparable to DHS and significantly higher than three cannulated screws (P < 0.01).

3.2 Crossed Screws/BDSF vs. Parallel CCS

  • Triangulation Effect: The "Alpha" configuration (three parallel + one crossed screw) significantly increases ultimate failure load. The crossed screw acts as a mechanical stop, converting shear forces into compression. Level IV – Biomechanical
  • BDSF Superiority: FEA shows BDSF provides up to 44% higher axial fixation strength than parallel screws. In an 80° model, maximum femoral displacement was significantly lower.
  • 7.3mm Fully Threaded Off-Axis Screw (2025): Maximum displacement was 7.15 mm vs. 10.40 mm for standard parallel screws. Peak Von Mises stress in the distal femur was nearly halved (113.86 MPa vs. 218.04 MPa). Level IV – FEA

3.3 FNS vs. BDSF: The Heavyweight Matchup

Direct comparisons reveal a nuanced landscape:

  • In extremely vertical fractures (Pauwels >70°), BDSF may outperform FNS in resisting shear. Maximum displacement: 3.46 mm (BDSF) vs. higher for other constructs.
  • Mechanism Difference: FNS relies on metal plate stiffness; BDSF relies on cortical bone structural integrity. BDSF is more sensitive to bone quality — exceptional in young dense bone, potentially risky in osteoporotic bone where FNS provides a safety margin.
Rotational Stability

Rotational instability can shear neo-vascularization attempting to bridge the fracture. Level IV – Biomechanical

  • FNS: Integrated ARS locked to the bolt provides "dual-row" resistance, significantly higher than a single sliding screw.
  • BDSF: Divergent planes create a multi-planar lock. Engaging anterior and posterior cortices widely maximizes the lever arm against rotational torque — superior rotational stability compared to clustered parallel screws.

4. Clinical Outcomes: Femoral Neck Shortening

The Failure of Dynamic Compression in Shear

Femoral neck shortening >5mm reduces the abductor lever arm, leading to gluteal insufficiency, gait abnormalities, and chronic pain. It is no longer considered an acceptable byproduct of "dynamic compression." Level II

4.1 Parallel CCS: Unacceptably High Rates

Clinical studies report alarmingly high rates of shortening >5mm: 20% to 54% in displaced fractures treated with parallel screws. Patients with significant shortening have lower Harris Hip Scores and SF-12 physical quality of life scores. Level II

4.2 FNS: Restoring Length Stability

FNS constrains collapse by preventing varus tilt, ensuring any sliding is strictly along the femoral neck axis:

  • Meta-Analysis (21 studies, >1,300 patients): FNS significantly reduced shortening vs. CCS (OR = 0.38). Weighted mean difference: −2.01 mm in favor of FNS. Level I – Meta-Analysis
  • Young Adult Cohort: Shortening was 2.40 ± 1.81 mm (FNS) vs. 4.54 ± 2.75 mm (CCS), P=0.005. Level II

4.3 Crossed Fully Threaded Screws: The Length-Stable Solution

By engaging threads in both fragments, these constructs resist all sliding:

  • Meta-Analysis (941 patients): Fully Threaded Screws had significantly lower shortening than Partially Threaded Screws (OR 0.27, P < 0.00001). Level I – Meta-Analysis
  • "Alpha" Configuration: Crossed screw group had significantly lower rates of severe shortening. The crossed screw acts as a strut, mechanically blocking proximal fragment sliding.
  • BDSF: Minimal loss of neck length (mean decrease ~1.02 cm over 12 months), significantly better functional outcomes vs. conventional screws. Level II
✅ Key Synthesis: Shortening Hierarchy

Crossed Fully Threaded Screws (Most Stable) ≥ FNS > Parallel Partially Threaded CCS (Least Stable)
Use of parallel partially threaded screws in Pauwels III fractures is a primary risk factor for acquired leg length discrepancy and abductor dysfunction.

5. Clinical Outcomes: Non-Union Rates

Mechanical Failure Leading to Biological Failure

Non-union is overwhelmingly mechanical in etiology: instability leads to excessive motion, preventing bridging callus formation. Level II–III

5.1 Parallel CCS

Non-union rates in young adults with displaced fractures: 10% to 18.3%. Parallel screws allow micromotion in the shear plane, favoring fibrous tissue over bone. Level I – Meta-Analysis

5.2 FNS

  • Meta-Analysis: FNS resulted in significantly lower non-union vs. CCS (OR = 0.53, 95% CI: 0.29–0.98, P = 0.04). Level I – Meta-Analysis
  • Healing Time: Shorter time to union with FNS (3.53 months vs. 4.14 months, P=0.050).
  • Even in delayed healing, the FNS construct is less likely to fail catastrophically than CCS.

5.3 Crossed Fully Threaded Screws / BDSF

  • BDSF: Union rate of 91.67% vs. 83.33% for conventional screws. Clinical practice series report 96.6% union — remarkable for this injury. Level II
  • "Compression Buttress Screw" (CBS): Non-union rate of 1.8% vs. 18.3% for standard screws in high-energy Pauwels III cohorts. Level II – RCT
  • Alpha Configuration: Fixation failure reduced from 21.4% (parallel) to 9.6%.

6. Clinical Outcomes: Avascular Necrosis (AVN)

Mechanical Stability Protects Biological Survival

AVN results from disruption of the medial femoral circumflex artery branches. The surgical goal is "first, do no harm." Level III

6.1 Parallel CCS

Despite its percutaneous nature, AVN rates remain high: 12–20%, driven largely by initial injury severity. Level III

6.2 FNS

✅ Counter-Intuitive Finding

Multiple meta-analyses show FNS is associated with a lower incidence of AVN than CCS (OR = 0.49, P = 0.01). Level I – Meta-Analysis
This supports the "stability hypothesis": AVN is not just an ischemic event at impact — it is also caused by repetitive post-operative instability that can kink or shear regenerating retinacular vessels. FNS permits revascularization that unstable CCS prevents.

6.3 Crossed Fully Threaded Screws

  • Meta-analysis: Lower AVN rate with fully threaded vs. partially threaded screws (OR 0.60, P = 0.04). Level I – Meta-Analysis
  • BDSF: AVN rates comparable to historical controls (~12%), no significant increase in risk. Level II
  • The "tamponade effect" concern (volume-filling screws raising intraosseous pressure) appears unfounded in clinical data.
🔑 Critical Conclusion

There is no evidence that modern stable constructs (FNS or Crossed Screws) increase AVN risk. Mechanical stability is a prerequisite for biological survival of the femoral head.

7.1 FNS "Efficiency"

While FNS requires a larger incision and results in higher blood loss (mean difference +14–20 mL vs. CCS), the fixed-angle jig streamlines workflow with single-wire placement dictating construct position. Studies show FNS can reduce intraoperative fluoroscopy shots compared to placing three independent parallel screws. Level II

7.2 BDSF "Learning Curve"

The BDSF technique is technically demanding. Placing a screw from the distal femoral cortex to tangentially touch the calcar and engage the posterior neck requires advanced 3D spatial awareness. Misplacement can lead to cortical splitting or neurovascular injury. This technical barrier is significant. Level III – Expert Opinion

7.3 Cost-Effectiveness

The high cost of FNS (often 5–10× that of cannulated screws) is a barrier in resource-constrained settings. Crossed Fully Threaded Screws deliver FNS-like biomechanical stability using generic, low-cost implants — a critical technique for global surgery applications. Economic Analysis

8. Evidence-Based Guidelines & Implant Selection

Second-Order Insight: The End of "Controlled Collapse"

A major thematic finding is the erosion of the "controlled collapse" dogma for young patients. For decades, surgeons accepted shortening as the price of union. The superior functional outcomes of FNS and Fully Threaded Screws suggest that length stability is as important as compression. In high-energy vertical fractures, "allowing sliding" is functionally equivalent to "allowing failure." Synthesis of Level I–II Evidence

📋 Evidence-Based Implant Selection Algorithm (Pauwels-Based)

Pauwels Type I (<30°):
Parallel CCS — Gold Standard
Cost-effective, minimally invasive, biomechanically sufficient for stable injuries.

Pauwels Type II (30°–50°) — "Grey Zone":
Parallel CCS — Acceptable if anatomical reduction is perfect.
Threshold for FNS should be low in comminution or obesity.

Pauwels Type III (>50°):
Parallel CCS — OBSOLETE
First Line: Femoral Neck System (FNS)
Reliable, reproducible stability; protects against varus, shortening, and AVN.
Alternative: Crossed Fully Threaded Screws / BDSF
For surgeons with specific expertise or in cost-constrained environments. Equal or superior biomechanics with higher technical risk.

9. Summary of Key Studies

Evidence Map

FNS vs. CCS Meta-Analysis (Frontiers in Surgery, 2023)

Meta-Analysis Level I

Systematic review of 21 studies (>1,300 patients). FNS significantly reduced non-union (OR 0.53), AVN (OR 0.49), and femoral neck shortening (OR 0.38) compared to CCS. FNS is superior for unstable femoral neck fractures. [Ref 1]

FNS vs. Three CCS: FEA & Clinical Observation (PMC, 2023)

FEA Clinical Level II–IV

Comparing FNS and CCS at Pauwels angles 55°/65°/75°. FNS had consistently lower vertical displacement (2.877 vs. 3.149 mm at 65°). Peak implant stress higher for FNS (612 MPa at 75°) but bone stress better managed. [Ref 4]

Biplane Double-Supported Screw Fixation (BDSF) — Filipov (PMC, 2019)

Prospective Cohort Level II

Prospective study of BDSF technique: 96.6% union rate, minimal neck length loss, superior rotational stability through geometric triangulation. The "F-technique" creates a truss engaging calcar and posterior cortex. [Refs 13, 17, 18]

Fully Threaded vs. Partially Threaded Screws Meta-Analysis (Springer, 2024)

Meta-Analysis Level I

Analysis of 941 patients. FCS had significantly lower femoral neck shortening (OR 0.27, P<0.00001) and lower AVN rate (OR 0.60, P=0.04) vs. PCS. Challenges the dogma that sliding is required for union. [Ref 31]

7.3mm Fully Threaded Off-Axis Screw Biomechanical Analysis (PMC, 2025)

FEA Level IV

Compared 7.3-FTOS to standard parallel screws (ICCS) in Pauwels III models. Max displacement: 7.15 vs. 10.40 mm. Peak Von Mises stress in distal femur nearly halved (113.86 vs. 218.04 MPa), indicating massive reduction in bone mechanical burden. [Ref 24]

Compressive Buttress Screw (CBS) RCT (PMC, 2024)

Randomized Controlled Trial Level I

Prospective RCT comparing CBS to off-axial screw fixation in young adults with vertical femoral neck fractures. CBS achieved non-union rate of only 1.8% vs. 18.3% for standard screws in Pauwels III fractures. [Ref 33]

FNS vs. CCS Young Adult Retrospective (PMC, 2021)

Retrospective Cohort Level II

Direct comparison in patients <60 years: Shortening 2.40 ± 1.81 mm (FNS) vs. 4.54 ± 2.75 mm (CCS), P=0.005. Healing time 3.53 vs. 4.14 months (P=0.050). Significantly lower non-union and reoperation rates with FNS. [Ref 10]

FNS vs. InterTan & CCS: Cadaveric Biomechanical (PubMed, 2022)

Cadaveric Level IV

FNS showed axial stiffness comparable to DHS and significantly higher than three cannulated screws (P<0.01) in unstable Pauwels III models. Approaches the rigidity of cephalomedullary nails. [Refs 20, 21]

Limitations & Evidence Gaps
  • Direct FNS vs. BDSF comparison: Limited head-to-head data; most comparisons are indirect. No large RCTs exist comparing these two constructs directly.
  • Follow-up duration: Many studies have limited follow-up (12–24 months); long-term AVN development (which can occur years post-injury) may be underreported.
  • Publication bias: Biomechanical studies often favor novel constructs; clinical translation may not always match laboratory findings.
  • Heterogeneity: Meta-analyses combine different fracture patterns, patient ages, and surgical techniques, which may limit direct applicability.
  • BDSF evidence base: Primarily Level II–III; no multi-center RCTs available in the source document.
