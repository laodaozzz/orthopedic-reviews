1. Biomechanics & Kinematics of the Elbow Joint

The elbow is a highly constrained, complex trochoginglymoid articulation that serves as a stable fulcrum for the upper extremity, enabling precise spatial positioning of the hand. Comprising three distinct articulations within a single synovial cavity—the ulnohumeral joint (hinge), the radiocapitellar joint (pivot), and the proximal radioulnar joint—the elbow is classified as a non-weight-bearing joint in ambulatory individuals, yet it is subjected to extraordinary mechanical forces.[4]

  • Static loading across the elbow can reach up to 3× body weight.
  • Dynamic loading (e.g., pushing up from a chair, lifting heavy objects, or throwing) can amplify forces to 6× body weight.[5]

The intrinsic congruity of the ulnohumeral articulation efficiently distributes these compressive loads evenly across hyaline cartilage. This biomechanical advantage explains why the articular surface of the elbow remains remarkably preserved even in patients with advanced osteophytosis. Stability in extension relies on bony congruency, while the anterior band of the medial collateral ligament (MCL) and the lateral ulnar collateral ligament (LUCL) provide primary soft-tissue constraint during mid-arc motion.[4]

Clinical Correlation When subjected to repetitive microtrauma, load-bearing stress is transferred to the margins of the joint, inciting the characteristic hypertrophic bone formation seen in primary elbow osteoarthritis. This marginal impingement pattern is the hallmark pathomechanic of the disease.

2. Etiology & Pathology of Elbow Osteoarthritis

The pathophysiology of elbow OA is fundamentally dictated by its etiology. A precise distinction between primary osteoarthritis and post-traumatic osteoarthritis provides the foundation for prognostic modeling and effective surgical planning.

2.1 Primary Osteoarthritis

Primary elbow OA is a relatively rare entity, representing approximately 2–3% of general population arthritic complaints.[6] It demonstrates a stark demographic predilection:

  • Male-to-female ratio: 4:1[7]
  • Typical patient: Middle-aged man with a history of heavy repetitive manual labor, weightlifting, or overhead throwing sports
  • Dominant extremity affected in 80–90% of cases[4]
  • Pooled prevalence of ~31% among workers exposed to high-frequency vibration; ~20% among heavy manual laborers (Level of evidence for strict occupational causality remains low)[8] Evidence quality: Low

Cardinal Pathological Features — Osteophyte Impingement

The central articular cartilage remains largely preserved in early-to-moderate disease. Instead, repetitive terminal impact results in hypertrophic bone formation at the articular margins:[4]

  • Posterior impingement: The olecranon tip forcefully abuts the olecranon fossa during terminal extension → massive osteophyte formation on the posteromedial olecranon tip → "kissing lesions" → rigid flexion contracture.[7]
  • Anterior impingement: The coronoid process engages the coronoid fossa during terminal flexion → aggressive anterior osteophytosis → restricted terminal flexion.[9]

Patients classically present with pain exclusively at the extremes of motion (terminal flexion and extension), while the mid-arc remains functionally pain-free.[6] As disease advances, osteophytes fragment, producing intra-articular loose bodies that cause mechanical locking and catching.

2.2 Post-Traumatic Osteoarthritis

Post-traumatic arthritis (PTA) is the most common etiology of elbow arthritis, arising as a sequela of high-energy injuries: complex intra-articular distal humerus fractures, comminuted radial head fractures, severe elbow dislocations, and "terrible triad" injuries (radial head fracture + coronoid fracture + dislocation).[7]

  • Characterized by direct, irreversible mechanical damage to chondrocytes and extracellular matrix at the moment of injury
  • Residual articular incongruity and ligamentous insufficiency alter kinematic load distribution, rapidly accelerating localized cartilage wear[7]
  • Encompasses the entire joint surface with diffuse, global joint-space narrowing
  • Patients present with severe, multi-planar stiffness, multidirectional instability, and chronic pain throughout the entire arc of motion[10]
  • Heterotopic ossification (HO) further obliterates joint mobility[12]
Second-Order Clinical Insight on Etiology Joint-preserving procedures (osteocapsular debridement, O-K procedure) yield highly predictable outcomes in primary OA because the surgeon addresses a mechanical block to motion. In PTA, removing an impinging osteophyte does not solve the underlying mid-arc pain caused by destroyed, incongruent articular cartilage. Patients with PTA are at significantly higher risk of requiring progression to interpositional arthroplasty or TEA.[10]

3. Clinical Evaluation & Radiographic Staging

Accurate radiographic classification is essential for establishing baseline severity, standardizing research, and guiding treatment algorithms. The Broberg-Morrey Classification remains the universally accepted gold standard for grading both primary and post-traumatic elbow OA.[13]

3.1 Broberg-Morrey Classification System

Table 2. Broberg-Morrey Classification — Radiographic Criteria & Clinical Correlates
Grade Radiographic Criteria Clinical Presentation
Grade 0 Normal joint; no narrowing or osteophyte formation Asymptomatic or purely soft-tissue related pain
Grade 1 Slight joint space narrowing with minimal osteophyte formation Mild terminal pain; early olecranon spurring on lateral radiograph
Grade 2 Moderate joint space narrowing with moderate osteophyte formation Noticeable loss of extension/flexion arc; osteophytes filling coronoid and olecranon fossae
Grade 3 Severe degenerative changes with gross destruction of joint architecture Severe mid-arc pain, profound stiffness, potential subluxation, near-complete obliteration of joint spaces

3.2 Imaging Modalities & Diagnostic Reliability

Standard AP and lateral radiographs are effective at capturing hallmark signs of elbow OA. In Grade 1, subtle spurring is best appreciated on the lateral view at the olecranon and coronoid tips. By Grade 2, AP views demonstrate widening of the distal humerus; lateral views show complete filling of the olecranon fossa. Grade 3 shows subchondral sclerosis, cyst formation, and massive loose bodies.[6]

  • Plain radiographs: ICC = 0.620–0.656 (substantial agreement)[15]
  • 3D-CT staging: ICC = 0.867–0.909 ("almost perfect" agreement) — now considered essential for preoperative surgical planning[15] Level III
Clinical Recommendation 3D-CT based on Broberg-Morrey criteria should be obtained for all patients being considered for surgical intervention. It allows precise mapping of osteophyte locations and hidden loose bodies, significantly reducing intraoperative time and morbidity.[16]

4. Non-Operative Management & Orthobiologics

Initial management is strictly non-operative, focusing on:

  • Activity modification: Avoiding heavy lifting, pneumatic tool operation, repetitive terminal loading[6]
  • NSAIDs: For acute synovitis management
  • Physical therapy: Optimizing dynamic muscle stabilizers of forearm and upper arm

4.1 Platelet-Rich Plasma (PRP): Mechanism of Action

PRP is an autologous blood derivative produced by centrifugation, concentrating platelets in a small plasma volume. Upon activation, alpha granules release critical growth factors: PDGF, TGF-β, IGF, FGF, and VEGF.[17] These cytokines:

  • Stimulate chondrocyte proliferation
  • Promote extracellular matrix (ECM) synthesis
  • Induce synoviocyte regulation
  • Inhibit pro-inflammatory interleukins (IL-1β) and matrix metalloproteinases (MMPs)[17]

4.2 Level of Evidence for PRP Efficacy

Table 3. PRP Evidence by Clinical Context
Clinical Context Level of Evidence Key Findings
Knee OA & Lateral Epicondylitis Level I PRP statistically and clinically superior to HA and corticosteroids for sustained long-term pain relief and functional improvement[19]
LP-PRP vs. LR-PRP Level I LP-PRP preferred for intra-articular injections: same efficacy, 56–64% reduction in adverse events. Optimal concentration: 600–900 × 10⁹/L[24]
Elbow Osteoarthritis (specific) Level II–III Mayo Clinic trial: 80% success rate with 2 PRP injections (VAS, MEPS, OES). Retrospective: 49% VAS improvement at 6 months sustained at 45% at 12 months[26][27]
Clinical Recommendation for PRP Intra-articular LP-PRP injections serve as a highly viable, disease-modifying bridge therapy for Broberg-Morrey Grade 1 and early Grade 2 elbow OA. Unlike corticosteroids (transient 4–6 weeks relief, chondrotoxic), PRP provides sustained biochemical modulation. Recommended protocol: 2–3 LP-PRP injections spaced 3–4 weeks apart.[25]

5. Surgical Interventions: Joint-Preserving Procedures

When orthobiologic therapy and conservative measures fail, surgical intervention is mandated. For patients with primary OA exhibiting terminal impingement but preserved mid-arc cartilage, joint-preserving osteocapsular debridement is the gold standard.[10]

5.1 Open Outerbridge-Kashiwagi (O-K) Procedure / Ulnohumeral Arthroplasty

Conceived by Kashiwagi (1978) and refined by Morrey as Ulnohumeral Arthroplasty, the O-K procedure addresses both anterior and posterior compartments through a single posterior approach.[29]

Surgical Technique

  • Limited 4–6 cm posterior midline incision centered over the olecranon
  • Triceps-splitting or triceps-elevating approach to expose posterior capsule
  • Resection of impinging olecranon tip osteophyte
  • Creation of a ~1.5 cm circular fenestration through the olecranon fossa floor using a trephine or motorized burr
  • This window serves dual purposes: (1) Mechanical decompression of the posterior compartment; (2) Direct portal access to the anterior compartment for coronoid osteophyte excision, loose body extraction, and anterior capsulectomy[29]
Biomechanical Insight Creating a large defect in the olecranon fossa translates the rotational fulcrum slightly proximally, functionally relaxing the collateral ligaments and anterior capsule. This allows the olecranon to recess further into the distal humerus during extension, instantly and permanently restoring terminal ROM.

Outcomes: Level III–IV Mean arc of motion improves from ~90° to 115–120° postoperatively. MEPI improves to good/excellent in >85% of cases.[36]

5.2 Arthroscopic Osteocapsular Debridement

Arthroscopy provides magnified visualization of radiocapitellar and ulnohumeral compartments while minimizing soft-tissue trauma.[38]

Outcomes: Level III–IV MEPS improvements of 26.8–34 points; average flexion-extension arc increase of ~23.3°. Deep infection rate: 0.0% across large meta-analyses.[39][40]

  • Open O-K procedure is biomechanically superior for severe rigid flexion contractures, massive posterior osteophytosis, or significant extra-articular HO that precludes safe arthroscopic portal placement.

6. Total Elbow Arthroplasty (TEA)

TEA represents the terminal step in the surgical algorithm for elbow arthritis. Originally engineered for low-demand, elderly RA patients, modern indications have cautiously expanded to include severe primary OA, post-traumatic arthritis, and acute unreconstructable distal humerus fractures in the geriatric population.[44]

6.1 Strict Indications

  • Age & Demand: Physiologically older (typically ≥65 years), low-demand lifestyle
  • Permanent lifting restrictions: Patient must agree to absolute limit of 10 lbs per arm lifelong[46]
  • Failed joint preservation: Advanced Broberg-Morrey Grade 3 with severe mid-arc pain, complete cartilage loss, gross joint destruction

6.2 Implant Designs

Table 5. TEA Implant Design Categories
Design Description Advantages Limitations
Unconstrained (Unlinked) Relies on native collateral ligaments Less stress to bone-cement interface High risk of instability/dislocation; rarely used for OA[48]
Semi-constrained (Linked) Physically linked via axle and poly bushing ("sloppy hinge", 5–7° varus/valgus laxity) Dissipates torsional stress; most successful design Poly wear debris, aseptic loosening risk

6.4 Complications & Revision Burden

Overall complication rates: 16–42%; revision rate: 13–18%.[48] A comprehensive meta-analysis of 8,911 primary TEAs across 46 studies identified:[55]

  • Aseptic loosening: 48.5% of all revisions — dominant failure mode, caused by particulate wear debris-induced osteolysis and torsional shear of cement mantle
  • Deep periprosthetic joint infection (PJI): 19.4% of revisions; two-stage revision success rate only 69–76%
Third-Order Clinical Insight — The TEA Survivorship Paradox RA patients exhibit the highest 10-year survivorship (92.4%) despite systemic osteopenia. Primary OA patients, despite healthy bone, show inferior survivorship (80–85%) because they are younger, stronger laborers who inevitably attempt to resume heavy lifting. Aggressive patient psychology counseling and strict adherence to weight-lifting limits are as critical as surgical technique itself.

7. Management of Concomitant Cubital Tunnel Syndrome

Cubital tunnel syndrome (CuTS)—compressive neuropathy of the ulnar nerve—is the second most common upper extremity compression neuropathy. In elbow OA, it is inexorably linked to the arthritic pathology.[57]

7.1 Pathophysiology in the Arthritic Elbow

The ulnar nerve traverses the cubital tunnel posterior to the medial epicondyle, with the MCL and capsule as its floor and Osborne's ligament as its roof.[3] In OA, the nerve faces multiple insults:

  • Medial osteophytosis: Jagged spurring directly invades the cubital tunnel[58]
  • Capsular contracture & ganglia: Thickened capsule increases intraneural pressure; medial ganglia emerge from the degenerated joint[2]
  • Cubitus valgus deformity (PTA): Increases nerve tension, friction, and traction during flexion[59]

7.2 EBM Surgical Management: Decompression vs. Transposition

For idiopathic CuTS, simple in-situ decompression is supported by Level I RCTs.[58] However, in elbow OA, this paradigm shifts entirely:

Table 7. Ulnar Nerve Management in Elbow OA Surgery
Approach Outcome Evidence
Simple In-Situ Decompression 18.9% incidence of postoperative ulnar nerve dysfunction due to sudden stretch & perineural fibrosis[61] Level III
Anterior Transposition Reduced dysfunction to 8.1%; significantly superior Amadio scores[61] Level III
Clinical EBM Guideline Concomitant ulnar nerve anterior transposition should be considered the mandatory standard of care during open surgical management of severe elbow OA, particularly when pre-existing neuropathy or a ≥30° preoperative flexion contracture exists.[59] In end-stage cases with profound intrinsic atrophy (McGowan grade 3), an O-K procedure combined with anterior transposition and supercharged end-to-side (SETS) anterior interosseous nerve transfer yields limb-saving improvements.[64]

8. Key Takeaways

  1. Etiology Dictates Pathology: Primary OA selectively spares central cartilage and causes terminal motion block via osteophytes. PTA irreversibly destroys the articular surface, causing severe mid-arc pain and global stiffness.
  2. Radiographic Staging: The Broberg-Morrey Classification is the gold standard. 3D-CT significantly increases interobserver reliability (ICC > 0.86) and is essential for surgical planning.
  3. Biologic Therapy (PRP): Leukocyte-poor PRP (LP-PRP) is a disease-modifying bridge therapy (Level II/III) for early-stage elbow OA, demonstrating sustained superiority over chondrotoxic corticosteroids. Protocol: 2–3 injections spaced 3–4 weeks apart.
  4. Joint-Preserving Surgery: Both arthroscopic and open O-K procedures yield excellent mid-term outcomes. Arthroscopy offers superior pain control and zero infection risk; open O-K provides greater ROM restoration in severe, rigid elbows.
  5. Arthroplasty Strictures: TEA provides excellent survivorship in RA (92.4%) but is plagued by revision rates up to 14.6% primarily due to aseptic loosening. TEA for primary OA is strictly reserved as salvage for elderly, low-demand patients committed to permanent 10-lb lifting restrictions.
  6. Concomitant Neuropathy: Ulnar nerve anterior transposition (not simple decompression) is mandatory during open OA surgery to prevent postoperative nerve traction and perineural fibrosis.
