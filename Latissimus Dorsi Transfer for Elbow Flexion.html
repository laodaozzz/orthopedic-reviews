I. Introduction: The Centrality of Elbow Flexion

The elbow joint serves as the primary fulcrum of the upper extremity, a critical link in a complex system that positions the hand in space.1 Active elbow flexion is arguably the most essential motion of the upper limb after hand function.2 This single motion governs the ability to perform nearly all fundamental activities of daily living (ADLs), including feeding, dressing, and personal hygiene.3

Consequently, the loss of elbow flexion—whether from traumatic nerve injury, muscle loss, or congenital absence—is a functionally devastating condition.2 A patient may possess a fully functional hand, but without the ability to bring that hand to the mouth, face, or perineum, its utility is severely limited. For this reason, the restoration of active, anti-gravity elbow flexion is a first-order priority in the reconstructive ladder for the upper extremity, often taking precedence over more distal or shoulder-level reconstructions.3

Since its modification for this purpose in 1955,3 the pedicled functional latissimus dorsi (LD) muscle transfer has emerged as a "workhorse" flap for this demanding reconstructive challenge.6 Its large size, significant excursion, robust and reliable neurovascular pedicle, and versatility make it an ideal option for a rotational transfer to restore dynamic function to the anterior arm.2

II. Patient Selection: Indications & Contraindications

Rigorous patient selection is the most critical determinant of a successful functional outcome. The procedure is technically demanding, but its success relies entirely on matching the correct operation to the correct pathology and patient.

A. Primary Indications

The primary indication is the irreversible paralysis or absence of the main elbow flexors (biceps brachii and brachialis muscles).2

Irreparable Nerve Injuries (Late Presentation)

This is the most common indication, intended for "chronic," "late," or "neglected" cases where primary nerve repair or transfer is no longer viable.2 After approximately six months of denervation, the native muscles undergo disuse atrophy, precluding their reinnervation.3

  • Brachial Plexus Injuries (BPI): Irreparable lesions of the upper trunk (C5, C6) — classic indication, including traumatic BPI11 and select obstetric BPI.2 Level IV
  • Musculocutaneous Nerve Lesions: Irreparable injury is a direct indication.4 Level V

Irreparable Muscle & Soft-Tissue Loss

  • Trauma: Traumatic necrosis, avulsion, or extensive loss of anterior arm compartment muscles — often from high-energy trauma, vascular injury, or compartment syndrome.17 Level IV
  • Oncologic Resection: En bloc resection of the anterior arm compartment for tumors (e.g., sarcoma) necessitating removal of flexor motors.2 Level V

Congenital Absence of Flexors

Conditions such as arthrogryposis, where the native elbow flexors are absent or non-functional.2 Level IV

B. Essential Prerequisites for Surgery

🔑 Non-Negotiable Prerequisites
  1. A Supple Elbow Joint: Full or functional passive range of motion must be present. A tendon transfer can motor a joint but cannot overcome an established contracture.4
  2. A Functional Donor Muscle: Ipsilateral latissimus dorsi must be innervated and fully functional with BMRC grade 5/5 strength.4 Preoperative EMG confirms integrity.15
  3. A Stable Shoulder Joint: An unstable shoulder compromises the entire limb and the transfer.4

C. Contraindications

Absolute Contraindications

  • Potential for spontaneous or postoperative nerve recovery of the biceps/brachialis9
  • Weak (< Grade 5), atrophied, or denervated ipsilateral latissimus dorsi4
  • Fixed elbow flexion contracture or unstable shoulder joint4

Relative Contraindications

  • Inability to comply with extensive postoperative physiotherapy17
  • Severe comorbidities precluding lengthy surgery

D. The "Dual-Defect" Indication

A primary driver for selecting the LD transfer — specifically the bipolar technique — is the presence of a "dual defect."22 This involves simultaneous loss of both motor function and anterior arm soft-tissue volume.17 The LD flap is uniquely suited to solve both problems in a single-stage operation.22

III. Applied Anatomy & Functional Flap Design

A successful transfer is predicated on meticulous understanding of the LD muscle's anatomy and, most importantly, its neurovascular pedicle.25

A. The Latissimus Dorsi Muscle

The LD is a large, flat, triangular muscle classified as a Type V muscle flap, indicating a dominant vascular pedicle and secondary segmental perforators.6 It originates from the thoracolumbar fascia and iliac crest and inserts into the intertubercular groove of the humerus. Its native function is to adduct, internally rotate, and extend the humerus.6

B. The Neurovascular Pedicle: The Flap's Lifeline

For a functional transfer, both the nerve and artery must be preserved and transferred intact.

Vascular Supply (Thoracodorsal Artery)

The dominant blood supply is the thoracodorsal artery,6 the terminal branch of the subscapular artery arising from the third segment of the axillary artery.25 The subscapular artery typically divides into the circumflex scapular artery (CSA) and the thoracodorsal artery; the main pedicle is found distal to this bifurcation.25 This pedicle is long (up to 15 cm) and reliable, providing a large arc of rotation to reach the anterior elbow.4

Nerve Supply (Thoracodorsal Nerve)

The thoracodorsal nerve arises from the posterior cord of the brachial plexus, with root contributions from C6, C7, and C8.25 Critically, it runs directly with the thoracodorsal vascular pedicle.25 A profound surgical advantage is that the thoracodorsal nerve only innervates the latissimus dorsi,25 simplifying dissection and re-education.

C. Flap Design Considerations

Pedicled vs. Free Transfer

While the LD can be used as a free flap (requiring microsurgical anastomosis), the pedicled rotational transfer is generally preferred when possible, as it avoids microsurgical complications.17 A free flap is typically reserved for cases where the ipsilateral LD is unavailable.2

Myocutaneous vs. Myofascial Flap

The flap can be harvested as muscle-only (myofascial)26 or with an "island of overlying soft tissue" (skin paddle).23

🔑 Dual Role of the Skin Paddle
  1. Soft-tissue coverage: Addresses the "dual defect" of volume and function.17
  2. Postoperative monitor: The visible skin paddle provides an immediate, non-invasive method for assessing flap viability. Changes in color, temperature, or capillary refill are earliest signs of vascular compromise.23

IV. Surgical Technique: Flap Harvest & Transfer

A. Patient Positioning

The patient is placed in full lateral decubitus position.5 The torso is stabilized with a bean bag, and an axillary roll protects the contralateral brachial plexus. The entire affected arm and lateral thorax are prepped and draped free.

Preoperatively, the anterior border of the LD is marked. If a skin paddle is planned, underlying perforating vessels can be mapped with handheld Doppler USG.29

B. Flap Harvest

  1. Incision along the anterior border of LD, or elliptical incision for skin paddle (e.g., 10 cm distal to axillary fold).17
  2. Suprafascial dissection to raise skin flaps.30
  3. Identify anterior border of LD and lift from serratus anterior.30
  4. Inferior-to-superior dissection separating LD from thoracic wall.31
  5. In the axilla, identify and meticulously dissect the thoracodorsal neurovascular pedicle entering the deep surface.25
  6. Ligate the branch to serratus anterior for maximal pedicle length.27
  7. Detach from origin and/or insertion depending on monopolar vs. bipolar plan.2

C. Tunneling and Flap Transfer

An anterior incision (e.g., deltopectoral approach) exposes recipient fixation points.3 A wide subcutaneous tunnel connects the axillary dissection to the anterior arm. For bipolar transfer, the flap is often routed "under the axilla"3 and "under the pectoralis major muscle."3

⚠️ Critical Technical Pearl: The "Blind Pass" The passage of the flap through the tunnel is the moment of highest risk for iatrogenic flap failure. The essential pearl is to pass the flap while simultaneously palpating the pedicle as it enters the tunnel. This continuous digital monitoring ensures the pedicle has not been rotated, kinked, or placed under excessive tension.3 Intraoperative Doppler assessment post-transfer is mandatory.29

V. Advanced Fixation Strategies: Bipolar vs. Monopolar

A. Monopolar (Unipolar) Transfer

First described by Schottstaedt,5 only the origin of the LD is detached while the native humeral insertion is left intact.9 The detached origin is tunneled ventrally and sutured to the distal biceps tendon.9 Generally indicated for restoring pure motor function when the anterior arm bulk is intact.4 Some literature suggests monopolar transfer may be "comparable or even superior to bipolar transfer in flexion power restoration."4 Level IV

B. Bipolar Transfer (Zancolli & Mitre, 1973)

The LD is detached from both its thoracolumbar origin and humeral insertion.2 The entire muscle is rotated 180° on its pedicle. The key indication is the "dual defect" — it is "favored by surgeons in cases of lost elbow flexion with soft-tissue defects" because it simultaneously restores motor power and the natural biceps contour.17 Level IV

C. Bipolar Fixation Points

Proximal Anchor

The (former) humeral insertion becomes the new origin, fixed to the coracoid process.3 Modern techniques favor suture anchors (e.g., 4.5 mm HEALIX ADVANCE BR) drilled into the coracoid for secure bony fixation.14

Distal Anchor Options

  • Option 1 — Biceps tendon: Broad fascial end sutured to distal biceps tendon remnant (less robust).3
  • Option 2 — Single bone fixation: To radial tuberosity (mimics biceps) or proximal ulnar diaphysis (mimics brachialis).3
  • Option 3 — Split-Distal Transfer (Current State-of-the-Art): The distal muscle belly is "separated into two pieces"14 — one fixed to the radial tuberosity, the other to the ulnar tuberosity using suture anchors.14 Level V
🔑 Biomechanical Superiority of the Split-Distal Transfer

The native biceps (radial insertion) is a powerful flexor and supinator, while the brachialis (ulnar insertion) is a pure flexor in all forearm positions. A transfer fixed only to the radius may result in weaker flexion in pronation.16 The "split-transfer" reconstructs both functional units, enabling "flexion by simultaneous activation of the humeroradial and humeroulnar joints."14 Documented benefit: "flexion force in the supinated position was equal to that in the pronated position."14

VI. Surgical Pearls: Optimizing Tension & Excursion

Flap survival is only the first step; success is measured by functional, anti-gravity power. This is achieved by meticulously setting the muscle's tension and ensuring free excursion.

🔑 Pearl 1: The 90-Degree Fixation Rule ("Spontaneous Hold")

Distal fixation is performed with the elbow at 90–100° flexion and full forearm supination.3 Tension is "adjusted to ensure that the elbow spontaneously remained at 90° at the end of fixation."14 This confirms that passive tension is sufficient to overcome gravity, setting the stage for BMRC Grade M3 flexion. The elbow is immobilized in this position for 4–6 weeks.14

🔑 Pearl 2: Restoring Resting Muscle Length ("5 cm Rule")

Setting the 90° hold is ineffective if the muscle is not at optimal contraction length. A novel technique: "5 cm intervals were marked on the LD muscle before dissection." After mobilization and tunneling, the muscle is "stretched to restore its length until the interval was 5 cm."14 This ensures physiologic resting length (L₀), the point on the length-tension curve where maximum contractile force is generated. This pre-tensioning is a critical prerequisite for Pearl 1.

🔑 Pearl 3: Ensure Unencumbered Glide

After fixation, the transferred tendon must be manually ranged to confirm "full unencumbered excursion."34 Any "hang-ups" must be addressed by widening the tunnel or re-routing the flap before closure.

⚠️ Pearl 4: Debulking & Fasciotomy

The anterior compartment is a tight, non-distensible space. Surgeons must "maximize room" by performing a fasciotomy of the arm and, if necrotic muscle remains, debulking the biceps muscle belly.3 This prevents compression and ensures free glide.

VII. Outcomes & Complication Management

A. Functional Outcomes

When performed correctly, results are good to excellent.

  • Strength: Goal is M3 (anti-gravity) or M4 (against resistance) on BMRC scale.9
    • Study of 13 BPI patients: 84.6% (11/13) achieved M3 or M4.8 Level IV
    • Case report of terminal nerve injury: M4 strength.15 Level V
    • Traumatic muscle loss patients generally achieve better outcomes (some M5) vs. brachial plexus palsies.22
  • Range of Motion (ROM):
    • Active ROM of 0°–140°,17 10°–145°,16 and 90°–100° (congenital cases)20 documented.
    • 86% of patients achieved >80° of active flexion in one series.11 Level IV

B. Complications: Donor Site

The donor site is the source of the vast majority of postoperative complications.

⚠️ Seroma: The #1 Complication

Reported incidence ranges from 47%6 to 95% (19/20 patients) in one series.36 Level IV

Cause: large "dead space" from muscle removal and "shearing effects" of skin flap against chest wall.37

Evidence-Based Seroma Prevention

  1. Scalpel Dissection (not electrocautery): Comparative study showed electrocautery → 80% seroma rate; scalpel + fixation sutures → 9.1%.37 Level III
  2. Quilting Sutures: Sutures fix skin flap to underlying layers (serratus/intercostal fascia), obliterating dead space. Systematic review (2011) confirmed quilting significantly reduces seroma incidence, average volume, and total drained volume.38 Level II
  3. Fibrin Glue Adjuvant: Further enhances effectiveness of quilting.17 Level IV

Other donor-site complications: loss of adduction/extension strength (often well-tolerated),6 chronic back pain, and hypertrophic scarring.36

C. Complications: Recipient Site

  • Flap failure (fibrofatty degeneration)2
  • Infection or hematoma2
  • Skin paddle necrosis2
  • Dehiscence or failure of distal insertion (may require revision)2
  • Rare neurologic injury (e.g., radial nerve palsy)39

VIII. Comparative Analysis: Alternative Reconstructive Options

The decision to use the LD transfer is made in the context of other available procedures.

A. Pectoralis Major Transfer

A bipolar transfer of the entire pectoralis major, fixed to coracoid and biceps tendon.40 One study of 29 transfers reported 93% M3/M4 strength and 100° mean active flexion arc.40 Level IV

Key disadvantage: Results "deteriorate with time due to the development of a recalcitrant flexion deformity" — an iatrogenic elbow contracture.41 Level IV

B. Steindler Flexorplasty

A time-tested procedure (>100 years old) involving proximal transfer of the common flexor-pronator mass from the medial epicondyle to anterior humerus.17 Reliably restores M3 flexion.43 Level IV

Key disadvantage: The "Steindler Effect" — creates a forearm pronation contracture and significant loss of full elbow extension (average 27° loss).45 Also weakens the flexor-pronator mass needed for hand function.

C. Free Functioning Muscle Transfer (FFMT)

Typically uses the gracilis muscle, harvested from the thigh, requiring complex microsurgical nerve coaptation (e.g., to intercostal nerves) and vascular anastomosis.2 Reserved for salvage when local transfers are unavailable (e.g., pan-plexus injury).2

Key disadvantages: Higher technical complexity, longer operative time, and risk of total flap failure from microsurgical complications.17

IX. Conclusion & Future Directions

The functional latissimus dorsi transfer is a reliable, versatile, and biomechanically sound solution for the devastating loss of elbow flexion.17 Its status as a "workhorse" flap6 is justified by its large size, robust thoracodorsal pedicle,25 and unique ability to solve the "dual defect" when the bipolar technique is employed.17

Critical Technical Advances Synthesized

  1. Donor Site: "Scalpel and quilting" method with fibrin glue is the evidence-based standard for seroma prevention.3738
  2. Recipient Site: The "spontaneous 90-degree hold"14 must be preceded by the "5 cm rule" to restore physiologic resting length.14
  3. Fixation Evolution: The "split-distal transfer" — fixation to both radius and ulna14 — represents the current state-of-the-art, restoring consistent flexion in all forearm positions.
