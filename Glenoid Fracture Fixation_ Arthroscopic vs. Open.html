I. Executive Summary

Glenoid fractures, though relatively uncommon, represent a significant clinical challenge due to their intra-articular nature and profound implications for shoulder stability and long-term function. The management of these injuries has evolved considerably, moving from a primary reliance on Open Reduction and Internal Fixation (ORIF) to the increasing adoption of Arthroscopic Reduction and Internal Fixation (ARIF) for select fracture patterns.

🔍 Key Finding
While ORIF remains the undisputed standard of care for complex, highly comminuted glenoid fractures and those with significant extension into the scapular body and neck, ARIF has emerged as a superior or equivalent alternative for a substantial subset of injuries — particularly Ideberg type I rim fractures and select type III patterns. Level II

The indications for each procedure are largely dictated by fracture morphology. ARIF is now the preferred technique for most displaced Ideberg type I (glenoid rim) fractures and is increasingly utilized for select, less complex glenoid fossa fractures such as Ideberg type III. Conversely, ORIF is mandated for more complex patterns including Ideberg types II, IV, V, and VI, and any fracture associated with a double disruption of the superior shoulder suspensory complex (SSSC).

A critical review of clinical outcomes reveals that both techniques achieve excellent and comparable rates of fracture union (100% in direct comparisons). Functional outcomes, as measured by range of motion and standardized scoring systems (Constant, DASH), are often similar at mid-term follow-up. However, the evidence consistently demonstrates that ARIF provides significantly better early pain control, reflected in lower Visual Analog Scale (VAS) scores (mean 1.5 vs. 2.7; p=0.001). Level II

The complication profiles differ fundamentally: ORIF carries risks related to extensive surgical exposure (infection, neurovascular injury, stiffness), while ARIF's risks relate to its steep technical learning curve and percutaneous fixation without direct neurovascular visualization. The overall complication rate is lower for ARIF (6% vs. 24.6% reported in analogous coronoid fracture literature). Level III

II. Anatomy, Pathophysiology & Clinical Significance

Glenohumeral Joint Biomechanics

The glenohumeral joint is the most mobile joint in the human body, a characteristic that comes at the cost of inherent instability. The glenoid, the lateral articular extension of the scapula, serves as the shallow socket for the much larger humeral head. This significant mismatch in articular surface area means that the glenoid provides minimal intrinsic bony stability. Expert Opinion

Primary static stabilizers include:

  • Glenoid labrum — deepens the socket by approximately 50%, increasing surface area and providing a "bumper" effect against translation. Level IV
  • Capsuloligamentous complex — particularly the inferior glenohumeral ligament, acts as primary restraint against dislocation.
  • Rotator cuff muscles — provide dynamic stability through compressive centering forces during active motion.
⚠️ Clinical Significance
Even small glenoid fractures can have major implications for shoulder stability and function, leading to recurrent instability, chronic pain, and post-traumatic glenohumeral osteoarthritis (PTOA). This is because fractures disrupt both the articular surface and critical attachment points for the labrum and capsuloligamentous structures.

Diagnostic Imperatives

Computed Tomography (CT) with 3D reconstructions is the gold standard and is essential for the evaluation of any suspected glenoid fracture. It allows precise visualization of fracture pattern, fragment size, articular step-off, comminution extent, and humeral head position — indispensable for both classification and surgical planning. Level IV

III. Classification Systems

The classification of glenoid fractures is a critical component of clinical decision-making, providing a shared language and a foundational framework for the treatment algorithm. The Ideberg classification (modified by Goss) remains the most widely used and clinically relevant system. Level IV

Application in Surgical Decision-Making

The Ideberg classification serves as a practical surgical roadmap. Fracture type, as determined by CT imaging, is the first and most critical branch point in the treatment algorithm:

  • Ideberg Type I → quintessential indication for ARIF
  • Ideberg Type III → ARIF feasible; superior exit point provides safe percutaneous corridor
  • Ideberg Types II, IV, V, VI → generally necessitate ORIF

IV. Open Reduction and Internal Fixation (ORIF)

For decades, ORIF has been the standard of care for displaced, intra-articular glenoid fractures. It provides direct access for anatomical reduction and allows for robust plate-and-screw fixation constructs. Level IV

Indications for ORIF

  • Fracture-Specific: Complex glenoid fossa fractures extending into scapular body/neck — Ideberg types II, IV, V, VI Level IV
  • SSSC Double Disruption: "Floating shoulder" injuries (e.g., glenoid neck fracture + clavicle fracture) requiring open stabilization of multiple components Level IV
  • General Criteria:
    • Articular step-off > 3–5 mm
    • Fracture gap > 5 mm
    • Fragment involving > 20–25% of articular surface
    • Glenohumeral instability or subluxation present
    Level IV

Surgical Approaches

Anterior (Deltopectoral) Approach

Indications: Large anterior glenoid rim fractures (Ideberg Ia), fossa fractures with anterior/superior extension (Ideberg III, some V).
Technique: Interval between deltoid and pectoralis major; management of subscapularis (tenotomy vs. muscle-splitting). Reduction under direct vision, provisional K-wire fixation, followed by lag screws or contoured reconstruction plates. Level IV

Posterior (Judet and Modified) Approaches

Indications: Posterior rim fractures (Ideberg Ib), and nearly all fractures involving posterior glenoid, glenoid neck, and scapular body (Ideberg II, IV, V). Estimated use in ~80% of all open glenoid fixations.
Technique: Interval between infraspinatus and teres minor. Modified posterior approaches with smaller incisions and muscle-sparing intervals minimize soft-tissue damage while maintaining adequate access. Level IV

V. Arthroscopic Reduction and Internal Fixation (ARIF)

ARIF has transitioned from a novel concept to a well-established technique for specific fracture patterns, offering a less invasive alternative with unique advantages in intra-articular visualization and soft-tissue injury management. Level II

Indications for ARIF

  • Primary: Displaced anterior or posterior glenoid rim fractures (Ideberg type I) — strongest evidence base Level II
  • Expanding: Select Ideberg type III fractures (superior exit point for percutaneous corridor); some less complex transverse patterns Level IV
  • Contraindications: Significant scapular body extension requiring plate fixation; severe comminution (Type VI); inability to achieve/maintain anatomical reduction arthroscopically Level IV
🚨 Guiding Principle
The quality of the reduction must never be sacrificed for the sake of a minimally invasive approach. An expertly performed ORIF is always superior to a poorly executed ARIF.

Surgical Techniques

Visualization and Reduction

  1. Debridement & Mobilization: Arthroscopic debridement of fracture hematoma and interposed soft tissue for clear visualization.
  2. Reduction Maneuvers:
    • Blunt probe or elevator for direct manipulation
    • Percutaneous K-wire as "joystick" for fragment control
    • Indirect reduction via ligamentotaxis — sutures through intact labrum attached to the fragment; tensioning pulls fragment into anatomical position
  3. Confirmation: Direct arthroscopic visualization (no articular step-off) + intraoperative fluoroscopy (AP and axillary views)

Fixation Options

Management of Concomitant Intra-articular Pathology

✅ Major Advantage of ARIF
ARIF enables comprehensive diagnostic arthroscopy and concurrent treatment of associated injuries. Retrospective studies show associated injuries are identified and treated in a majority of cases:
  • Articular cartilage depressions: 80%
  • Labral tears: 30%
  • SLAP tears: 20%
This holistic approach restores total joint stability rather than merely fixing the fracture. Level III

VI. Comparative Outcomes

📊 Context Note
These procedures are often performed for different fracture patterns. ARIF is primarily indicated for Type I rim fractures; ORIF for complex Types II–VI. Direct comparisons (Table 3) typically involve overlapping, more complex subsets.

Fracture Union and Radiographic Outcomes

Both ARIF and ORIF demonstrate exceptionally high rates of union. A direct retrospective comparison for Ideberg type II–VI fractures found bony union in 100% of patients in both groups. Long-term follow-up of ORIF for scapular fractures confirms 100% union rates. Level III

Range of Motion and Strength

At mid-term follow-up (12–22 months), active ROM values for forward elevation, lateral elevation, external rotation, and internal rotation were comparable between groups. Long-term ORIF studies note a small but statistically significant decrease in external rotation vs. the contralateral side. Long-term ARIF ROM data is still emerging. Level III

✅ Key Outcome Difference
Postoperative pain (VAS) is the most consistent and statistically significant difference between techniques. ARIF's mean VAS of 1.5 vs. ORIF's 2.7 at 12 months (p=0.001) is attributable to the preservation of the soft-tissue envelope. Level II

Postoperative Recovery Trajectory

ARIF is associated with significantly less soft-tissue trauma, directly contributing to lower postoperative pain. Rehabilitation after ARIF may be accelerated with fewer early restrictions on ROM, facilitating earlier return to activities of daily living and potentially to sport or work. Level III

Long-Term Sequelae: Post-Traumatic Osteoarthritis (PTOA)

PTOA is the most significant long-term concern regardless of fixation method. Development is multifactorial: initial impact injury to cartilage, quality of surgical reduction, and restoration of stability. While definitive long-term comparative studies are lacking, ARIF's magnified, panoramic articular visualization may allow more precise anatomical reduction and the ability to treat concurrent chondral lesions — a compelling but unproven hypothesis for improved long-term joint preservation. Expert Opinion

VIII. Treatment Algorithm & Expert Recommendations

1
Comprehensive Imaging & Classification
Mandatory CT with 3D reconstructions for every suspected glenoid fracture. Classify using the Ideberg system.
↓
2
Assess Surgical Indications
Non-operative management for minimally displaced, stable fractures. Operative if: step-off >3–5 mm, gap >5 mm, humeral head subluxation, or fragment >20–25% of articular surface.
↓
3
Procedural Selection Based on Morphology
  • → ARIF: Ideberg Ia, Ib (primary); select Type III
  • → ORIF: Ideberg II, IV, V, VI; SSSC double disruption
↓
4
Integrate Patient Factors & Surgeon Expertise
Age, activity level, bone quality, comorbidities. Critical variable: surgeon expertise. An expertly performed ORIF will always be superior to a poorly executed ARIF. Low threshold to convert to open if anatomical reduction cannot be achieved arthroscopically.

The Critical Role of Surgeon Expertise

The current evidence represents a snapshot on a continuum of surgical evolution. Historical parallels with rotator cuff repair and instability surgery suggest that as arthroscopic techniques, instrumentation, and surgeon training continue to improve, the indications for ARIF will likely expand further. The apparent equivalence in some functional outcomes may be temporary as the field matures. Expert Opinion

Future Directions

  • High-quality prospective RCTs with >10-year follow-up comparing ARIF vs. ORIF, especially for PTOA incidence
  • Patient-specific instrumentation (PSI), computer-assisted navigation, and novel fixation implants
  • Navigation-enhanced percutaneous screw placement for ARIF
  • 3D-printed custom plates for complex ORIF reconstructions
