Percutaneous Fixation of Pelvic Ring Fractures

A Comprehensive Interactive Clinical & Technical Review — Evidence-Based Educational Summary

Introduction

Pelvic ring fractures represent a broad spectrum of injuries, from low-energy fragility fractures in the aging population to devastating, life-threatening disruptions from high-energy trauma. Accounting for approximately 3% of all skeletal fractures, these injuries pose significant challenges due to complex three-dimensional anatomy, critical load-bearing function, and intimate relationships with neurovascular and visceral structures. Level IV

Historically, management involved prolonged bed rest and traction, leading to thromboembolism, pulmonary compromise, pressure sores, and poor functional outcomes. Open reduction and internal fixation (ORIF) allowed anatomical reconstruction but introduced substantial morbidity: massive hemorrhage, deep infection rates up to 25%, iatrogenic neurovascular injury, and significant wound complications—many attributable to the surgical approach itself. Level IV

This reality has driven the evolution toward percutaneous fixation: cannulated screws placed through small stab incisions under fluoroscopic guidance. The advantages are profound: significantly reduced blood loss, shorter operative times, minimal soft tissue disruption, and markedly lower infection rates (~2%). By preserving the soft tissue envelope and avoiding disruption of the pelvic hematoma, percutaneous techniques are especially valuable in polytrauma and elderly fragility fractures. Level III

1. Foundational Principles & Patient Selection

1.1 Biomechanics of the Pelvic Ring

The pelvis functions as a closed, load-bearing ring transmitting forces from the axial skeleton to the lower extremities. Its stability derives from a formidable posterior ligamentous complex (anterior/posterior sacroiliac, sacrospinous, and sacrotuberous ligaments), which is far stronger than the anterior symphyseal connection. Level IV

🔑 Key Principle: Ring Structure Concept As a ring structure, a significant disruption in one location is almost invariably associated with a second injury elsewhere in the ring. This mandates comprehensive evaluation of the entire ring, even when an anterior injury appears isolated.

1.3 Indications for Percutaneous Fixation

  • Unstable Pelvic Ring Injuries: Tile B & C; specific APC, LC, and VS patterns where adequate closed reduction is achievable. Level IV
  • Fragility Fractures of the Pelvis (FFP): Low-energy fractures in older adults; reduces pain, facilitates early mobilization, and may lower one-year mortality. Level III
  • Polytrauma / Damage Control Orthopedics (DCO): Rapid stabilization with minimal physiological insult; helps control hemorrhage. Level IV
  • Pathologic & Impending Fractures: Metastatic disease or irradiated bone; improves mobility and quality of life. Level IV

2. Preoperative Planning

2.1 The Indispensable Role of CT

High-quality CT with multiplanar reformation (MPR) and 3D reconstruction is mandatory for any candidate for pelvic fixation. CT fundamentally alters the operative plan in a significant number of cases, often revealing occult posterior injuries missed on plain radiographs. Level III

✅ Planning = Prevention Preoperative planning is not merely preparatory—it is a preventative surgical act. Identifying aberrant vessels or unusual nerve paths on CT allows trajectory modification before the first incision. Most intraoperative "technical errors" are the culmination of planning failures.

2.2 Mapping Safe Osseous Corridors for Iliosacral (IS) Screws

A systematic, multi-step CT planning approach defines patient-specific safe corridors: Level IV

1
Obtain True Radiographic Views: Reformat axial CT into true sagittal, inlet, and outlet views of the sacrum.
2
Define Starting Point & Trajectory: On the lateral view, identify entry point inferior/posterior to the iliac cortical density (ICD). Use coronal MPR to ensure the corridor remains within the S1 sacral ala.
3
Assess Transverse Trajectory ("Bullseye"): Evaluate the planned path in the axial plane. The ideal trajectory is centrally located within the S1 body, maximizing margin for error.
4
Assess Oblique Trajectories & Measure Implant: Plan for alternative oblique corridors (critical in sacral dysmorphism). Measure corridor to determine maximum safe screw diameter and length.

2.3 Anterior Column / Pubic Ramus Screw Planning

A custom "obturator outlet oblique" view is generated by adjusting CT reformats to align with the superior pubic ramus long axis. This displays the entire corridor in a single plane. Level IV

💡 Contralateral Template Technique In displaced fractures, the uninjured contralateral hemipelvis can serve as an individualized anatomical template, allowing the surgeon to virtually reconstruct the fractured side—personalized trauma surgery at its finest.

2.4 Sacral Dysmorphism

Sacral dysmorphism (acute alar slope, residual upper sacral disks, comma-shaped foramina, prominent mammillary bodies) alters the S1 corridor fundamentally—making it smaller, more oblique, and more anteriorly/cranially located. CT is the only reliable method to identify this anatomy. Level IV

⚠ Danger: Standard Trajectory in Dysmorphism Attempting a "standard" trajectory in a dysmorphic sacrum will almost certainly result in iatrogenic injury to the L5 nerve root or perforation into the spinal canal. The corridor may be too narrow for S1 fixation, necessitating S2 screws or posterior tension band plating.

4. Operative Techniques

4.1 Patient Positioning & Reduction

The vast majority of procedures use the supine position on a fully radiolucent table, providing unobstructed C-arm access and simultaneous anterior/posterior pelvic access. Level IV

  • Longitudinal Traction: For VS / Tile C injuries to restore vertical alignment
  • Manual Manipulation: Direct pressure or limb rotation for specific patterns
  • Percutaneous "Joysticks": Schanz screws in iliac crests act as levers for multiplanar control Level IV
  • Temporary External Fixation: Both a reduction aid and temporary stabilization

4.2 IS Screw Fixation: Step-by-Step

1
Final Reduction Check: Confirm alignment on AP, inlet, and outlet views.
2
Entry Point Identification: True lateral view; entry must be inferior and posterior to the ICD to protect L5 nerve root.
3
Guidewire Insertion: Through protective soft-tissue sleeve, tapped 2–3 mm into bone; verify on lateral.
4
Biplanar Guidewire Advancement: Most critical phase. Outlet view for craniocaudal (protect S1 foramen); Inlet view for anteroposterior (protect vessels anteriorly and canal posteriorly). Pause before crossing midline to re-confirm trajectory.
5
Length Measurement & Screw Insertion: Measure depth; insert partially-threaded cannulated screw with washer. Tighten for compression.
6
Additional Fixation: Second S1 or S2 screw for enhanced stability in comminuted or bilateral injuries.

💡 Technical Pearl: Reverse Guidewire Technique Bend the guidewire tip 20–30°. If trajectory deviates, withdraw slightly and rotate—the bent tip acts as a rudder for steering within the medullary canal, minimizing false pathways in osteoporotic bone. Level IV

4.4 Post-Fixation Assessment

  1. Final Imaging: Complete set of AP, inlet, outlet, and oblique views confirming intraosseous position and reduction quality.
  2. Intraoperative Stress Examination: Manual rotational and vertical forces under live fluoroscopy—no significant motion at fracture sites confirms a stable construct. Level IV

6. Emerging Technologies & Future Directions

6.1 Computer-Assisted Navigation (CAN)

CAN creates a virtual 3D model (from preoperative CT or intraoperative 3D fluoroscopic spin) with tracked instruments displayed in real-time on the model. Studies demonstrate screw malposition reduction from 29.5% to as low as 0.1% with navigation. Level III

  • Advantages: Dramatically improved accuracy; reduced radiation exposure; confidence in narrow corridors and dysmorphism
  • Risks: Steep learning curve; registration errors or "navigational drift" can cause catastrophically misplaced screws

6.2 Robotic-Assisted Fixation

Systems use a "scan-plan-execute" workflow: intraoperative 3D CT → software trajectory planning → robotic arm rigidly aligns a guide tube. Early studies show significantly fewer cortical violations. Robotic systems are immune to poor fluoroscopic visualization (bowel gas, contrast), potentially expanding indications. Level IV

6.3 Patient-Specific Instrumentation (3D-Printed Guides)

Custom guides designed from CT-based 3D models fit uniquely onto the patient's bone and incorporate drill sleeves aligned with pre-planned trajectories. Established in spine surgery, this technology holds great promise for complex pelvic deformities and dysmorphism. Level IV

🔄 Paradigm Shift in Surgical Skill Traditional percutaneous fixation is an "artisan" craft reliant on haptic feedback and spatial reasoning. Navigation and robotics shift the skillset toward that of an "engineer"—mastery of data acquisition, software planning, and system oversight becomes paramount. Training curricula must evolve accordingly.
