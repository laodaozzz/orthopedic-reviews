Evidence-Based Management Protocol & Surgical Strategy for Severe Volar Forearm Crush Injuries

Interactive educational summary synthesising contemporary orthoplastic trauma evidence for the management of mangled upper extremity injuries with compartment syndrome, neurovascular compromise, and composite soft-tissue defects.

1. Introduction & Pathophysiological Framework

Severe high-energy crush injuries to the volar (anterior) forearm represent one of the most formidable challenges in orthoplastic reconstructive trauma surgery. These injuries are characterised by a complex amalgamation of soft-tissue avulsion, comminuted osseous disruption, and profound neurovascular compromise.[1] When a patient presents with a deep soft-tissue defect (e.g. 5 × 3 cm), concomitant cortical bone exposure, and distal neurological symptoms such as numbness, the limb is classified as a "mangled upper extremity."[1]

🔬 Key Pathophysiology — "Contagious" Injury Propagation

The defining hallmark of a severe forearm crush injury is the centrifugal progression of tissue necrosis, oedema, and fibrosis. Kinetic energy radiates outward from the impact epicentre, establishing a wide, evolving zone of injury. Capillary endothelial compromise leads to increased vascular permeability, third-spacing of protein-rich exudates, and profound interstitial oedema.[6,9]

This unmitigated inflammatory response within the closed fascial spaces of the volar forearm precipitates a vicious cycle: elevated intracompartmental pressure → venous outflow obstruction → arteriolar collapse — the classic ischaemia-reperfusion cascade culminating in acute compartment syndrome (ACS).[10] If inadequately managed, the "contagious" fibrotic effect leads to dense tendinous adhesions, severe joint contractures, and the devastating "frozen hand" sequela, with associated disuse osteoporosis and complex regional pain syndrome (CRPS).[6]

Four Foundational Pillars of Mangled Extremity Management

  1. Radical excisional debridement and complete fascial compartment decompression.[4]
  2. Rigid skeletal stabilisation.[4]
  3. Meticulous neurovascular exploration and restoration.[4]
  4. Definitive, well-vascularised soft-tissue coverage.[4]

2. Neurovascular Exploration & Compartment Syndrome

Numbness in the digits following a volar forearm crush typically arises from three distinct mechanisms: (1) direct nerve contusion/laceration/stretch (axonotmesis or neurotmesis); (2) secondary ischaemic neuropathy due to evolving ACS; or (3) anatomical entrapment within a displaced fracture fragment.[9]

2.1 Diagnosis of Acute Compartment Syndrome

The volar compartment is the most frequently affected space in upper-extremity ACS due to the dense, unyielding antebrachial fascia and high metabolic demands of deep flexor musculature (FDP, FPL).[15,16]

The pathognomonic sign is severe, crescendo pain out of proportion to the skeletal injury, exacerbated by passive digit extension.[11] However, in patients with pre-existing numbness, the ischaemic insult may mask pain. Sensory deficits (loss of two-point discrimination, paraesthesia in median/ulnar distributions) often precede motor deficits and serve as early warning signs.[11]

⚠️ Critical — Palpable Pulses Do NOT Exclude Compartment Syndrome

Capillary perfusion ceases long before systolic arterial pressure is overcome by rising tissue pressure. Palpable distal pulses may be present even with established ACS.[11]

Manometric criteria for fasciotomy:[16]

  • Absolute compartmental pressure ≥ 30 mm Hg
  • Delta pressure (diastolic BP − compartment pressure) < 30 mm Hg

Indications for prophylactic fasciotomy (military trauma registries & ACS guidelines):[19]

  • Prolonged warm ischaemia time > 12 hours
  • Severe, high-kinetic-energy crush mechanisms
  • Massive crystalloid fluid resuscitation
  • Anticipated ischaemia-reperfusion oedema following vascular repair
⏰ Timing is Everything

Delaying fasciotomy until irreversible myoneural necrosis guarantees Volkmann's ischaemic contracture — a permanent, devastating functional loss.[10]

2.2 Surgical Approaches for Volar Exploration

Extensile Volar Approach of Henry

The workhorse incision for anterior forearm exploration: incision from the lateral biceps tendon at the elbow flexion crease, extending distally to the radial styloid. The superficial internervous plane lies between brachioradialis (radial nerve) and pronator teres (median nerve) proximally, and between brachioradialis and FCR distally.[22,23]

Deep dissection requires careful mobilisation of the radial artery with sequential ligation of recurrent muscular branches. In the modified distal approach (through the FCR sheath), the palmar cutaneous branch of the median nerve must be protected.[25,26]

Median Nerve Decompression — Predictable Choke Points

The median nerve must be systematically decompressed at:[14]

  • Ligament of Struthers (if present)
  • Lacertus fibrosus (bicipital aponeurosis)
  • Two heads of pronator teres
  • Proximal fibrous arch of FDS
  • Transverse carpal ligament (carpal tunnel)

Ulnar Nerve Decompression

Via the extensile ulnar approach centred over the FCU axis. Decompress proximally through the cubital tunnel and between FCU heads; distally, release Guyon's canal completely by dividing the volar carpal ligament and separating the hypothenar tendinous arch at the hook of the hamate.[29,30]

2.4 Principles of Nerve Repair in Crush Trauma

If a nerve is contused but in continuity (neuropraxia/axonotmesis), neurolysis is performed and the nerve observed.[9] If the nerve is irreparably crushed (neurotmesis), primary end-to-end neurorrhaphy is contraindicated in contaminated crush wounds — attempting anastomosis under tension leads to intraneural fibrosis and neuroma formation.[32]

The evidence-based protocol mandates: clean resection to healthy fascicles, tagging with non-absorbable sutures, and deferral of definitive reconstruction (autologous nerve grafting, processed nerve allografts, or nerve transfers) until the soft-tissue envelope is sterilised, stabilised, and covered.[32,34]

3. Surgical Debridement & Evolving Necrosis

The success of all subsequent fixation, nerve repair, and coverage is entirely contingent upon the rigour of initial debridement. Devitalised muscle, necrotic fat, and avascular bone fragments act as potent culture media for bacterial proliferation and trigger hyper-inflammatory fibrosis that destroys tendon gliding planes and joint capsules.[6]

3.1 The "4 C's" — Traditional Paradigm vs. Modern Histopathological Evidence

Traditional intraoperative assessment relies on the Larrey-Artz "4 C's": Colour, Consistency, Contractility, and Capacity to bleed.[36]

📊 Critical Evidence Gap

Modern prospective histopathological studies demonstrate that surgeons misdiagnose muscle viability in up to 72% of borderline specimens when relying solely on the 4 C's. Muscle deemed "dead" frequently shows normal histology or only mild reversible inflammation.[39]

3.2 The Staged "Second-Look" Debridement Protocol (DIME Acronym)

The DIME protocol — Devitalised tissue removal, Infection control, Moisture balance, and Edge preparation — mandates a staged approach:[41]

  1. Index debridement (0–24 h): Emergent radical excision of unequivocally necrotic tissue, foreign bodies, and gross contamination. "Borderline" tissue preserved.[40]
  2. Broad-spectrum IV antibiotics (e.g. first-generation cephalosporin + aminoglycoside for severe contamination).[40]
  3. Second-look debridement (48–72 h): The true zone of injury has demarcated. Revascularised tissue declares itself viable; necrotic tissue is now safely excised.[40]

3.3 Management of Exposed Bone

Cortical bone fragments completely stripped of periosteal attachments and devoid of soft-tissue pedicles must be considered avascular sequestra in high-energy crush injuries. They must be radically removed to prevent deep osteomyelitis, even if this creates a segmental defect.[6] Small defects can later be reconstructed with vascularised bone grafts (e.g. medial femoral condyle flaps) or non-vascularised cancellous grafts once the soft-tissue envelope is pristine.[12]

4. Skeletal Stabilisation

Complex soft-tissue reconstruction cannot proceed over a mechanically unstable skeleton. A mobile, comminuted fracture continuously traumatises adjacent structures, exacerbates oedema, and tears newly formed microvascular anastomoses.[4]

✅ External Fixation — Primary Modality in Crush Injuries

In highly contaminated crush injuries with a 5 × 3 cm defect and significant periosteal stripping, primary internal fixation is contraindicated due to high hardware infection risk. External fixation provides rapid, rigid stability, restores length, and protects neurovascular structures.[4,12] Pin placement must be planned outside the intended zone of subsequent flap coverage.

Conversion to definitive internal plating may be considered in delayed fashion once the wound bed is sterilised and covered with healthy tissue.[4]

5. Soft-Tissue Coverage Strategies (5 × 3 cm Defect with Bone Exposure)

Wounds with exposed bone stripped of periosteum, bare tendons without paratenon, or exposed neurovascular bundles are classified as "bradytrophic" structures — incapable of generating a granulation bed autonomously. Skin grafts are strictly contraindicated as they will fail over avascular cortex.[46,47]

5.1 Negative Pressure Wound Therapy (NPWT)

NPWT applies subatmospheric pressure (−75 to −125 mmHg) via sealed polyurethane foam, exerting effects through:[47]

  • Macrostrain: Draws wound edges together, evacuates proteolytic exudates (MMPs).
  • Microstrain: Induces cellular micro-deformation, upregulates VEGF, stimulates angiogenesis and granulation tissue formation.
⚠️ NPWT Limitation on Denuded Cortical Bone

If periosteum is entirely stripped, the dense cortex has virtually no capillary network. Continuous NPWT will desiccate the exposed cortex, potentially worsening ischaemic osteonecrosis.[51] NPWT serves as an indispensable bridging modality, not a definitive alternative to vascularised flap surgery.[54]

5.2 Locoregional Pedicled Flaps

Radial Forearm Free/Pedicled Flap (RFFF)

Provides thin, pliable, hairless fasciocutaneous skin. However, elevation sacrifices the radial artery. In crush injuries where the ulnar artery may be contused or vasospastic, this risks catastrophic hand devascularisation. The donor site frequently suffers poor graft take and hypertrophic scarring.[50,52,54,58]

Relatively contraindicated unless Allen's test confirms intact ulnar circulation and complete superficial palmar arch.

Ulnar Artery Perforator Flap

Based on a discrete perforator 5–8 cm proximal to the pisiform. A 5 × 3 cm paddle of thin skin and fascia can be rotated up to 180°. Paramount advantage: absolute preservation of main ulnar and radial arterial trunks. Donor site for 3 cm width can generally be closed primarily.[61,63]

Ideal locoregional choice if local fascia is uninjured.

Posterior Interosseous Artery (PIA) Flap

Dorsal fasciocutaneous flap supplied by septocutaneous perforators from the PIA. Spares volar arteries entirely but has a high rate of venous congestion and technically demanding dissection. Vulnerable to deep interosseous crush.[8,54,65]

5.3 Microvascular Free Tissue Transfer

If local hemodynamics are devastated, healthy tissue must be imported from outside the "zone of injury" via free tissue transfer.[6]

Anterolateral Thigh (ALT) Flap

Based on the descending branch of the lateral circumflex femoral artery. Provides massive tissue territory, can be radically thinned to the subdermal plexus for ultra-thin coverage. Operates entirely outside the upper-extremity zone of injury. Simultaneous two-team approach possible; typically primary-closed donor site.[66,68]

Free Gracilis / Lateral Arm Flaps

Gracilis muscle is adept at filling cavitary defects, bringing a highly vascularised muscle belly onto denuded bone before skin-grafting. Lateral arm flap (posterior radial collateral artery) offers a lower-morbidity alternative for smaller defects.[6,54]

For a 5 × 3 cm volar defect with exposed bone in a crush setting: Ulnar Artery Perforator Flap is the ideal locoregional choice if local fascia is uninjured. If the crush zone is wide, a microvascular ALT free flap thinned to the subdermal plexus, or a Free Gracilis muscle flap, represents the gold standard for definitive coverage and maximal limb salvage.[Source document §5]

6. Timing of Soft-Tissue Coverage

Godina (1986) demonstrated that early microsurgical reconstruction within 72 hours dramatically decreased infection rates, minimised flap failures, and reduced hospital stays.[71] The BOAST-4 protocol codifies:[42]

  • Initial debridement + antibiotics: within 12–24 hours
  • Definitive soft-tissue closure/flap: within 72 hours of injury

Recent large-cohort analyses introduce nuance: delaying flap coverage beyond 72 h — even up to 7 days post-injury — does not independently incur a significant increase in deep osteomyelitis, flap failure, or non-union, provided that initial and second-look debridements were meticulous and the wound was continuously maintained with NPWT.[71]

8. Conclusion

Management of severe volar forearm crush injuries demands an aggressive, orchestrated, multidisciplinary orthoplastic approach. The initial presentation of distal numbness must immediately prompt rigorous clinical — and if necessary, manometric — evaluation for ACS. Emergent extensile volar fasciotomy arrests the ischaemia-reperfusion cascade. Damaged nerves are assessed microscopically; primary repair is avoided in crush zones in favour of delayed grafting or transfer.

Meticulous staged debridement (initial radical excision + planned second-look at 48–72 h) forms the bedrock of limb salvage, overcoming the dangerous limitations of the traditional "4 C's" criteria. Rigid external fixation protects the soft-tissue envelope while NPWT serves as an indispensable temporal bridge.

For a 5 × 3 cm defect, local perforator-based flaps (ulnar or radial artery perforator) offer ideal coverage while preserving major vascular axes. If local hemodynamics are compromised, a microvascular free flap (thinned ALT or free Gracilis) is mandated. By adhering to BOAST-4 protocols and the foundational tenets of Green's Operative Hand Surgery and Campbell's Operative Orthopaedics, surgeons can halt the progression to the "frozen hand" and maximise biological and functional salvage.[4,6,42,81,84]
