Executive Summary

The management of diabetes mellitus in patients undergoing major orthopedic surgery presents a significant clinical challenge, with profound implications for postoperative outcomes. Dysglycemia—encompassing hyperglycemia, hypoglycemia, and glycemic variability—is unequivocally linked to an increased risk of surgical site infections (SSIs), implant failure, prolonged hospital stays, and mortality. Level II

This guideline synthesizes current evidence and recommendations from leading professional societies. The central paradigm has shifted from an aggressive pursuit of "tight" glycemic control to a more nuanced, safety-focused strategy. The primary objectives are:

Core Glycemic Objectives
  • Avoid marked hyperglycemia: Blood glucose >180 mg/dL Level I
  • Prevent iatrogenic hypoglycemia: Blood glucose <70 mg/dL Level I
  • Minimize glycemic variability: Wide fluctuations independently predict adverse events Level II

Key recommendations include preoperative optimization targeting HbA1c <8.0%, meticulous management of home antidiabetic medications with specific protocols for newer agents, intraoperative control using validated variable-rate intravenous insulin infusion (VRIII) protocols to a target of 140–180 mg/dL, and a highly structured transition to subcutaneous basal-bolus insulin postoperatively. Level I

Pathophysiological Rationale

A thorough understanding of the metabolic derangements induced by surgery is fundamental to appreciating the necessity of rigorous perioperative glycemic management.

The Surgical Stress Response & Acute Insulin Resistance

Major surgery and anesthesia provoke a potent neuroendocrine stress response characterized by a surge in counterregulatory hormones (cortisol, catecholamines, glucagon, growth hormone). Level II

This hormonal milieu has profound metabolic consequences:

  • Cortisol and catecholamines stimulate hepatic glucose production (glycogenolysis, gluconeogenesis) while inhibiting insulin release from pancreatic β-cells.
  • Stress hormones and inflammatory cytokines (TNF-α, IL-6) induce systemic insulin resistance in skeletal muscle.
  • This acute, stress-induced insulin resistance is most pronounced on postoperative day 1 but can persist for up to 21 days after major surgery. Level II
  • In patients with pre-existing diabetes, this response is superimposed upon an already compromised system, dramatically amplifying hyperglycemia risk.

Hyperglycemia's Impact on Immune Function & Wound Healing

Elevated blood glucose directly sabotages the body's defense against infection and tissue repair:

  • Impaired neutrophil function: Chemotaxis, phagocytosis, and bactericidal activity are compromised. Level II
  • Reactive oxygen species overproduction: Causes cellular damage and endothelial dysfunction.
  • Disrupted wound healing cascade: Direct mechanistic link between elevated glucose and SSIs.

Epidemiological Evidence: Glycemic Metrics & Orthopedic Outcomes

Chronic Hyperglycemia (HbA1c)

Preoperative HbA1c reflects average glycemia over 2–3 months and predicts capacity to handle surgical stress. Level II

  • SSI: HbA1c >7.0% associated with SSI rates of 35.3% vs. 0.0% in spinal instrumentation surgery. Level II
  • Mortality: HbA1c >7.0% associated with increased mortality (HR 1.3) in primary joint arthroplasty. Level II
  • Shoulder arthroplasty: Preoperative HbA1c of 8.0% identified as critical threshold for deep infection risk. Level II

Acute Perioperative Hyperglycemia

Even in patients without known diabetes, stress-induced hyperglycemia (>180 mg/dL) is an independent risk factor for impaired wound healing, hardware failure, SSI, and pseudarthrosis. Level II

Critical Finding

A morning postoperative blood glucose >140 mg/dL is associated with a threefold increased risk of periprosthetic joint infection. Level II

Meta-Analytic Evidence

A meta-analysis of 15 randomized controlled trials demonstrated that intensive perioperative glucose control targeting <150 mg/dL significantly reduces SSI risk (OR 0.43) compared to conventional control. Level I

Preoperative Optimization

The preoperative period offers a critical window of opportunity to identify high-risk patients, mitigate glycemic lability, and establish a foundation for a safe surgical course.

Comprehensive Preoperative Assessment

A coordinated multidisciplinary effort involving the orthopedic surgeon, anesthesiologist, primary care provider, and endocrinologist. Level II

  • Surgical scheduling: Diabetes patients prioritized as first cases of the day to minimize fasting. Level II
  • Universal screening: All surgical admissions assessed for diabetes history; laboratory glucose on admission if no known history. Level II
  • A1c testing: Measured in all patients with known diabetes or admission hyperglycemia (>140 mg/dL) if not performed in the preceding 2–3 months. Level II

Long-Term Glycemic Assessment & Targets

HbA1c Target for Elective Surgery Level I

The 2022 Endocrine Society Clinical Practice Guideline recommends targeting a preoperative HbA1c of <8.0% (63.9 mmol/mol) for patients undergoing elective procedures. An HbA1c ≥8.0% should trigger mandatory delay and referral for intensive glycemic optimization.

High Insulin Dose Patients

For patients on >80 units/day or at high hypoglycemia risk, a more substantial basal dose reduction of 50–75% may be necessary. Level II

Immediate Pre-Induction Management

  • Glycemic target (1–4 hours before surgery): 100–180 mg/dL (5.6–10 mmol/L). Level I
  • Carbohydrate loading: NOT recommended for patients with diabetes due to hyperglycemia risk. Level II

Monitoring Protocols

  • Baseline POC-BG: Mandatory in preoperative holding area before anesthesia induction. Level II
  • Frequency: At least hourly throughout major orthopedic surgeries (procedures >1–2 hours). Level II

Variable-Rate Intravenous Insulin Infusion (VRIII)

The VRIII is the management modality of choice for major orthopedic surgery, leveraging the rapid onset/offset of intravenous regular insulin. Level II

  • Preparation: Regular human insulin 50 units in 50 mL NS (1 unit/mL) via dedicated pump.
  • Concurrent dextrose: Always run with 5% dextrose at 100–125 mL/hr to prevent ketosis and hypoglycemia.
  • Validated titration algorithm: Nurse-driven, targeting 140–180 mg/dL.
  • Hypoglycemia protocol: Non-negotiable safety component (see Table 4).

Postoperative Glycemic Control

The postoperative period is dynamic: acute surgical stress begins to subside, but glycemic instability risk remains high. Primary goals are stable control, safe IV-to-subcutaneous transition, and thoughtful medication restart.

Immediate Postoperative Care

Continue VRIII without interruption upon arrival to PACU. Maintain the 140–180 mg/dL target with monitoring every 1–2 hours. Continue until the patient is hemodynamically stable, tolerating oral diet, and a safe subcutaneous transition can be executed. Level II

The Critical IV-to-Subcutaneous Transition

⚠ Insulin Gap Alert

The half-life of IV regular insulin is mere minutes. If the infusion is stopped when the first subcutaneous dose is given, a 2–4 hour window with virtually no effective insulin coverage is created, allowing severe rebound hyperglycemia. Level II

Restarting Home Medications

  • Oral agents: Restart once eating regularly, hemodynamically stable, no contraindications (AKI, hepatic dysfunction). Level II
  • Metformin: After confirmed stable renal function.
  • Sulfonylureas: Restart cautiously due to anorexia/nausea-induced hypoglycemia risk.
  • SGLT-2 inhibitors: Only when fully recovered, euvolemic, and eating normally.
  • Medication reconciliation: Essential at discharge for safe transition to outpatient regimen.

Special Considerations & Emerging Frontiers

Glucocorticoid-Induced Hyperglycemia

Glucocorticoids are potent causes of hyperglycemia by increasing insulin resistance and stimulating gluconeogenesis. Level II

  • Oral agents are often insufficient; scheduled insulin is the treatment of choice.
  • NPH insulin can be timed to coincide with steroid peak effect.
  • For high-dose or continuous IV steroids, VRIII is the safest method. Level II

Diabetes Technology in Hospital

  • CGM: May be used for high-risk, non-critically ill patients per the 2022 Endocrine Society guideline. Must be adjunct to, not replacement for, POC-BG testing. All insulin dosing based on confirmatory POC measurement. Level I
  • Insulin pumps (CSII): Continuation considered only in institutions with established expertise. For major surgery, standard practice is to discontinue the pump preoperatively and manage with VRIII, with structured plan to transition back postoperatively. Level II

ERAS Pathway Modifications

⚠ Carbohydrate Loading Contraindicated

Standard ERAS carbohydrate loading is contraindicated in patients with diabetes due to the risk of precipitating severe preoperative hyperglycemia. Focus on minimizing fasting times via morning scheduling while adhering to rigorous glycemic protocols. Level I

Synthesized Institutional Protocol

Consolidated actionable checklists for standardized care implementation.

1 Preoperative Checklist (Pre-Assessment Clinic)
  • Patient identified with diabetes or risk factors for hyperglycemia
  • HbA1c measured if not done in the last 3 months
  • If HbA1c ≥8.0%: Elective surgery postponed; referral to endocrinology/PCP for optimization
  • Comprehensive review of diabetes complications (cardiac, renal, neurologic) completed
  • Patient scheduled for a morning surgical slot
  • Written medication management instructions provided (Tables 1 & 2)
  • Patient instructed on standard fasting guidelines
  • Patient explicitly instructed not to consume preoperative carbohydrate drinks
2 Day of Surgery Protocol (Pre-Op Holding)
  • Confirm adherence to medication instructions
  • POC-BG measured on arrival — Target: 100–180 mg/dL
  • If BG >180 mg/dL: administer SC correction insulin per scale, recheck 1 hr
  • If on basal insulin: confirm reduced morning dose was taken
  • Anesthesiology team notified of diabetic status and admission BG
3 Intraoperative Algorithm (OR)
  • For major surgery or procedures >2 hours: initiate VRIII
  • Target BG: 140–180 mg/dL
  • Monitor POC-BG at least hourly
  • Titrate VRIII per validated institutional protocol (Table 4)
  • Manage hypoglycemia (BG <70) immediately: stop insulin, give IV dextrose
4 Postoperative Protocol (PACU & Ward)
  • Continue VRIII and hourly monitoring in PACU
  • Maintain BG target of 140–180 mg/dL
  • Continue VRIII on surgical ward until transition readiness
  • After SC transition: monitor POC-BG before meals and at bedtime
5 IV-to-Subcutaneous Transition
  • Use standardized worksheet (Table 5)
  • All readiness criteria met before initiating transition
  • TDD calculated at 80% of stable 24-hr IV insulin requirement
  • 50% basal / 50% bolus regimen designed
  • CRITICAL: First SC basal dose administered 2–4 hours BEFORE stopping VRIII
6 Discharge Planning
  • Stable SC insulin regimen or safely resumed home oral medications
  • Patient/caregiver educated on "sick day" rules and hypoglycemia recognition/treatment
  • Thorough medication reconciliation performed
  • Clear written discharge instructions with diabetes medication plan
  • Follow-up appointment with PCP or endocrinologist within 1 month

Key Studies & Evidence Summary

The following key studies and analyses from the source document provide the evidence base for these recommendations.

1. NICE-SUGAR Trial (2009)

Design: Large-scale multicenter RCT in critically ill patients. Finding: Overly aggressive glycemic targets (80–110 mg/dL) provided no consistent mortality benefit and significantly increased severe hypoglycemia risk. This trial fundamentally shifted the paradigm away from tight control toward moderate targets (140–180 mg/dL). Level I

2. Meta-Analysis of 15 RCTs — Intensive Perioperative Glucose Control

Design: Meta-analysis of 15 RCTs evaluating intensive glucose control targeting <150 mg/dL (8.3 mmol/L). Finding: Intensive control significantly reduced SSI risk (OR 0.43) compared to conventional control. Provides definitive support for active management of acute perioperative hyperglycemia as an infection prevention strategy. Level I

3. 2022 Endocrine Society Clinical Practice Guideline

Design: Comprehensive evidence-based guideline for inpatient hyperglycemia management. Finding: Recommends HbA1c target of <8.0% for elective surgery; supports moderate glycemic targets (140–180 mg/dL); suggests CGM may be used for high-risk non-critically ill patients as adjunct to POC testing. Level I

4. Akiboye et al. — Management of Hyperglycemia in Orthopedic Surgery

Design: Comprehensive review. Key Findings: HbA1c >7.0% associated with SSI rates of 35.3% vs. 0.0% in spinal instrumentation; HbA1c >7.0% associated with increased mortality (HR 1.3) in joint arthroplasty; morning postoperative BG >140 mg/dL associated with 3× risk of periprosthetic joint infection. Level II

5. ASA Multi-Society GLP-1 Receptor Agonist Guidance (2024)

Design: Multi-society consensus guidance. Finding: Most patients may continue GLP-1 RAs perioperatively; those at highest risk for GI problems should follow a 24-hour liquid diet pre-procedure. Reflects the broader shift toward individualized risk assessment. Level II

Note: The source document primarily references reviews, guidelines, and meta-analyses rather than individual prospective RCTs focused specifically on orthopedic glycemic management. Evidence is synthesized from multiple sources of varying design and level. Key limitations include the predominantly observational nature of orthopedic-specific data and the extrapolation of findings from general surgical and ICU populations.
