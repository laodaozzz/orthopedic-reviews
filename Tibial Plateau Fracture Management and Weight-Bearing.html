1. Pathoanatomy and Classification

1.1 The Tibial Plateau as a Weight-Bearing Structure

A tibial plateau fracture represents a significant intra-articular injury to the proximal tibia, where it forms the lower surface of the knee joint. [1] The tibial plateau consists of two distinct articular surfaces—the medial and lateral tibial condyles—covered with hyaline cartilage that facilitates the knee's hinge-like motion. [2, 3]

The medial condyle is the larger, stronger, concave surface bearing ~60% of the knee's axial load. The lateral condyle is smaller, convex, and composed of weaker, less dense bone—rendering it more susceptible to fracture from valgus and axial loading forces. [1, 2] Disruption of this articular surface leads to joint incongruity, instability, and a significantly increased risk of post-traumatic osteoarthritis (PTOA). [3]

1.2 Mechanisms of Injury

Tibial plateau fractures exhibit a bimodal age and energy distribution:

  • Younger individuals: High-energy trauma (MVC, falls from height, sports)—complex patterns with displacement and soft-tissue damage. [7]
  • Older individuals: Low-energy mechanisms (simple falls) in the setting of osteoporosis—compression and depression-type fractures. [6]

The most common mechanism involves axial loading combined with valgus force, driving the lateral femoral condyle into the weaker lateral tibial plateau. [2]

1.3 The Schatzker Classification

The Schatzker classification (1979) is the most widely used system, categorizing fractures into six types based on pattern and location on AP radiographs. The progression from Type I to Type VI reflects increasing injury severity, higher energy, greater complexity, and worse prognosis. [9, 11, 12]

Table 1: The Schatzker Classification of Tibial Plateau Fractures
Type Description Mechanism Associated Injuries
I Pure split (cleavage) of lateral plateau, <4 mm displacement Valgus + axial load (young, strong bone) Lateral meniscal tears
II Split-depression of lateral plateau (most common type) Valgus + axial load Lateral meniscal tears (especially >10 mm depression or >6 mm widening); possible MCL injury [4,12]
III Pure depression of lateral plateau Pure axial load (often osteoporotic bone) Meniscal injuries possible
IV Fracture of medial plateau High-energy varus + axial load Neurovascular injury (popliteal artery, peroneal nerve), ACL/PCL tears, LCL injury; worse prognosis [4,6,9,12]
V Bicondylar (both plateaus) High-energy complex force Meniscal detachment, ACL avulsions [6]
VI Metaphyseal-diaphyseal dissociation High-energy with shearing component Significant soft-tissue injury, high risk of open fracture, compartment syndrome (up to 18%) [4,9]
Clinical Significance: The Schatzker type allows clinicians to conceptualize the injury not as an isolated bone break but as a complex disruption of the entire knee joint. Higher types (IV–VI) carry substantially greater risks of neurovascular injury, compartment syndrome, and long-term morbidity. [4, 6, 9]

1.4 Supplementary Classification Systems

The AO/OTA classification provides more comprehensive categorization based on articular involvement and metaphyseal extension (Schatzker VI ≈ AO Type C). [11] The "Three-Column Theory", based on axial CT imaging, divides the plateau into medial, lateral, and posterior columns—particularly useful for identifying posterior column fractures that may require specific surgical approaches. [2, 9]

2. Operative vs. Non-Operative Decision Criteria

The primary goals of treatment are to restore a stable, congruent, and well-aligned articular surface to allow for early knee motion and minimize the risk of PTOA. The decision hinges on radiological findings, clinical evaluation of stability and soft-tissue integrity, and patient-specific factors. [7]

2.1 Radiological Assessment

CT scans are now considered the standard of care for comprehensive evaluation and surgical planning. [4] Two key parameters guide the decision:

  • Articular Depression/Step-Off: The most critical factor. Non-operative management is generally reserved for fractures with step-off ≤2–4 mm. Step-off >4 mm at union is a powerful predictor for future TKA (hazard ratio 16.2). [4, 16]
  • Condylar Widening: Widening >5 mm is generally an indication for operative fixation. [4]
The 2–4 mm "Grey Zone": A landmark 2024 study challenged the traditional 2 mm threshold, finding no significant difference in patient-reported outcomes for fractures with up to 4 mm of displacement between operative and non-operative groups. This suggests the standard of care may be evolving toward a more conservative approach for borderline injuries. Level II [20]

2.2 Clinical Evaluation

  • Ligamentous Stability: >10° of varus/valgus laxity compared to contralateral knee = unstable → requires surgery. [2]
  • Soft-Tissue Envelope: Significant swelling, blisters, or open wounds indicate need for staged protocol (temporary external fixator, then ORIF at 7–14 days). [4]
  • Neurovascular Status: Vascular compromise warrants urgent investigation; compartment syndrome requires immediate fasciotomy. [1, 2]

3. Non-Operative Rehabilitation Protocol

The non-operative protocol navigates a fundamental conflict: an intra-articular fracture requires unloading for healing, yet the knee joint is notoriously susceptible to arthrofibrosis when immobilized. The solution is a phased protocol using a hinged knee brace for stability while permitting immediate, controlled ROM in an unloaded state. [2, 6]

Phase I: ProtectiveWeeks 0–8 (NWB)
Phase II: InitiationWeeks 8–12 (PWB)
Phase III: ProgressionWeeks 12+ (FWB)

Phase I: Protective Phase (Weeks 0–8) — Strictly Non-Weight-Bearing

  • NWB Weight-bearing: Strict non-weight-bearing with walker or crutches. [2, 6]
  • Brace: Hinged knee brace; initially locked in extension, unlocked for ROM sessions. [2, 6]
  • ROM Goal: ≥90° knee flexion by week 4. [6]
  • Exercises: Quadriceps setting, ankle pumps, straight leg raises. [26]
  • Monitoring: Weekly radiographs for first 3 weeks to detect secondary displacement. [2]

Phase II: Initiation of Load (Weeks 8–12) — Progressive Weight-Bearing

  • TTWB → PWB Starting with toe-touch, then 25% → 50% → 75% body weight, increasing ~25% every 1–2 weeks. [29]
  • Exercises: Normalize gait, quadriceps control, closed-chain exercises (mini-squats, weight shifts), balance/proprioceptive training. [26]

Phase III: Full Weight-Bearing (Weeks 12+)

  • FWB Full weight-bearing as tolerated, contingent upon radiographic confirmation of complete union. [2]
  • Exercises: Advanced strengthening (single-leg closed-chain, leg press, lunges), cardiovascular conditioning (cycling, aquatic therapy), sport-specific training. [26, 27]
  • Timeline: Full functional recovery often 16–20+ weeks; return to high-impact sports delayed until ≥90% strength of uninjured side. [2]

4. Operative (Post-ORIF) Rehabilitation Protocol

4.1 Surgical Goals

ORIF aims to achieve anatomical reduction of the articular surface and stable internal fixation to create an environment permitting immediate rehabilitation. [4]

4.2 Traditional vs. Emerging Protocols

Paradigm Shift: Historical dogma of prolonged NWB after ORIF is being challenged by evidence supporting immediate full weight-bearing as tolerated (FWBAT) following stable fixation with modern locking plates. Level I–II [39, 40]
  • 2018 retrospective study (n=90): Immediate FWBAT after plate fixation showed no failure of fixation or loss of reduction vs. traditional NWB group. Level II [39]
  • 2025 RCT (Schatzker I–IV): Immediate FWBAT demonstrated significantly better clinical outcomes (Rasmussen score) at 6 months with similar radiological outcomes and low rates of intra-articular collapse. Level I [40]

4.4 Stratification for Early Weight-Bearing

The evidence supports a stratified approach rather than a one-size-fits-all protocol:

  • Best candidates for early FWBAT: Schatzker I–III fractures, robust fixation, good bone quality. [40]
  • Consider traditional NWB: Highly comminuted Schatzker V/VI, osteoporotic bone, uncertain fixation stability. [4]

5.2 Displaced Fractures

For significantly displaced or unstable fractures, operative treatment is superior, yielding 87% satisfactory results vs. 66% non-operatively. [14] However, even optimally treated patients score on average 14% lower on pain/daily activities and 36% lower for sports/QoL compared to age-matched peers. [44]

5.3 Predictors of Poor Outcomes

  • Radiological: Residual depression >2 mm, varus deformity >5°, persistent instability [15, 47]
  • Patient-related: Advanced age, tobacco use, high comorbidity index, worker's compensation [15]
  • Injury/treatment: Schatzker V/VI, meniscectomy at surgery [15]

6. Complications and Long-Term Sequelae

6.1 Non-Operative Complications

  • Loss of Reduction: Secondary displacement requiring conversion to surgery. Low risk in appropriately selected patients. [6, 48]
  • Malunion: Healing in non-anatomical position.
  • Joint Stiffness: Can occur with inadequate compliance. [6]
  • VTE: Up to 10% DVT rate during immobility period. [6]

6.2 Operative Complications

  • Infection: ~21% overall; 2–8% deep infections requiring further surgery. Risk factors: open fractures, smoking, diabetes, prolonged OR time. [4, 8]
  • Wound Complications: Dehiscence, skin necrosis, especially with extensile approaches. [23]
  • Arthrofibrosis: Up to 20% of patients; may require manipulation or lysis of adhesions. [18, 19]
  • Symptomatic Hardware: Leading cause of reoperation. [20]
  • Neurovascular Injury: Iatrogenic peroneal nerve or popliteal vessel injury, especially with medial/bicondylar fractures. [4]

6.3 Post-Traumatic Osteoarthritis

The Overarching Long-Term Concern: An intra-articular fracture increases the risk of arthritis by >20-fold. PTOA has a dual mechano-biological pathogenesis: (1) chronic abnormal loading from residual incongruity, and (2) acute biological insult—chondrocyte death and inflammatory cascade initiated at the moment of impact. Even perfect surgical reduction cannot fully prevent PTOA due to this biological component. [46, 51, 52]
  • Radiographic PTOA: 25–45% at long-term follow-up. [46]
  • Conversion to TKA: 3–9% within 10 years. [16]
  • Post-traumatic TKA is technically more challenging with higher complication rates than primary TKA. [46]

7. Synthesis and Clinical Recommendations

Decision Algorithm

  1. Initial Assessment: History, physical exam (including neurovascular), radiographs. Immobilize.
  2. Advanced Imaging: CT scan for all but clearly undisplaced fractures. Classify using Schatzker system.
  3. Treatment Criteria:
    • Absolute operative indications: open fracture, compartment syndrome, vascular injury, unstable knee (>10° laxity), Schatzker IV/V/VI.
    • For Schatzker I–III: step-off >4 mm or widening >5 mm → ORIF; step-off ≤4 mm + stable → non-operative.
    • Consider patient factors (frailty, comorbidities, functional demands).
  4. Rehabilitation Protocol:
    • Non-operative: NWB 6–8 weeks, brace + early ROM, progressive WB after 8 weeks, FWB by 12 weeks.
    • ORIF with stable fixation (Schatzker I–IV, good bone): Consider immediate FWBAT.
    • ORIF with complex fractures/poor fixation: Traditional NWB 6 weeks → progressive WB.

Patient Counseling Points

  • This is a severe, intra-articular injury with long-term consequences—not a simple fracture.
  • Recovery: typically 6–12+ months to maximal improvement.
  • Complete return to pre-injury function is not guaranteed, especially after surgery for severe fractures. [43]
  • Lifelong risk of PTOA; may eventually require TKA. [2]

Future Directions

  • Large-scale RCTs to definitively establish safety and indications for immediate WB after ORIF.
  • Orthobiologic interventions targeting the acute biological cascade to prevent PTOA. [51]
