Executive Summary

The application of antimicrobial agents is a cornerstone of modern orthopedic surgery, pivotal in both preventing and treating a spectrum of challenging infections. This interactive review synthesizes current evidence-based guidelines on the use of common antibiotic classes for critical orthopedic conditions: surgical prophylaxis, open fractures, osteomyelitis, and prosthetic joint infections (PJI). The standard of care for surgical prophylaxis is anchored by timely administration of cefazolin within 60 minutes of incision, with prophylaxis rarely extending beyond 24 hours. For open fractures, immediate antibiotic administration—ideally within one hour of injury—is the most critical determinant of outcome, with duration now limited to 24–72 hours based on robust meta-analytic data. The OVIVA trial has revolutionized osteomyelitis management by demonstrating non-inferiority of highly bioavailable oral antibiotics versus prolonged parenteral therapy. For PJI, success requires integrated surgical-medical strategies targeting microbial biofilm. Across all domains, antimicrobial stewardship remains paramount.

1. Principles of Antimicrobial Prophylaxis in Orthopedic Surgery

Peri-operative antibiotic prophylaxis is a fundamental and highly effective measure for preventing surgical site infections (SSIs) in orthopedic surgery, capable of achieving a reduction of up to 81% in the relative risk of infection.Level I The rational, safe, and effective use of these agents is governed by strict adherence to guidelines on timing, agent selection, and duration, as outlined by the ASHP, IDSA, SIS, and SHEA.Level I

1.1 Foundational Pillars: Timing, Agent Selection, and Duration

Timing is Critical

The optimal window for administering preoperative antibiotics is within 60 minutes prior to surgical incision.Level I Evidence suggests that the lowest SSI rates are achieved when the antibiotic is administered within a 30-minute pre-incision window.Level II For vancomycin and fluoroquinolones (which require longer infusion), administration should begin within 120 minutes before incision.Level I

Duration of Prophylaxis

Current guidelines strongly recommend that prophylaxis be discontinued within 24 hours of the surgical procedure.Level I In many clean orthopedic procedures, a single preoperative dose is sufficient. This practice marks a significant departure from older protocols extending antibiotics for 48–72 hours, now known to provide no additional benefit while increasing adverse events, resistance, and cost.Level I

Intraoperative Redosing

Redosing is necessary when the procedure duration exceeds two half-lives of the administered antibiotic or in instances of excessive blood loss (>1500 mL).Level I For cefazolin (half-life ~1.8 hours), redosing is typically recommended every 4 hours.

⚠ Weight-Based Dosing – Critical for Efficacy Sub-therapeutic dosing is a recognized risk factor for prophylactic failure, especially in obese patients. Standard recommendation: 2 g for patients 60–120 kg; 3 g for patients >120 kg.Level I

1.2 The Central Role of Cefazolin as Standard of Care

First-generation cephalosporins, with cefazolin as the archetypal agent, are the most widely recommended antibiotics for surgical prophylaxis across orthopedic procedures, including closed fracture fixation and total joint arthroplasty.Level I Its spectrum is ideally targeted against the most common SSI organisms—Staphylococcus aureus and Streptococcus species—while having limited activity against gut flora, minimizing collateral damage.Level II

1.3 Navigating Penicillin Allergies

Clindamycin (900 mg IV) is the primary alternative for patients with severe, IgE-mediated beta-lactam allergy.Level II Vancomycin (15 mg/kg IV) is a secondary alternative, particularly with MRSA concerns, though its routine use is discouraged due to slow infusion requirements and resistance concerns.Level I

✅ Key Insight: Cefazolin Safety in Non-Severe Penicillin Allergy The historical fear of cross-reactivity between penicillins and first-generation cephalosporins is largely overstated. For patients with a non-anaphylactic allergy (e.g., delayed maculopapular rash), cefazolin is considered safe and is associated with lower SSI rates compared to clindamycin or vancomycin.Level II

1.4 Contemporary Debates: Dual Prophylaxis & Decolonization

Dual-Antibiotic Prophylaxis for MRSA

In institutions with high MRSA prevalence or for patients with known MRSA colonization, dual-antibiotic prophylaxis combining a cephalosporin with a glycopeptide may be indicated. Vancomycin + cephalosporin is effective when institutional MRSA infection rate is ≥0.25%.Level I Teicoplanin has emerged as a favorable alternative to vancomycin, with less nephrotoxicity and faster administration.Level I

Targeted vs. Universal Decolonization

Targeted strategy: Preoperative screening for S. aureus carriage, followed by 5-day intranasal mupirocin + chlorhexidine body washes for carriers.Level I Universal strategy: Decolonization for all patients undergoing high-risk procedures regardless of carrier status—more feasible but raises concerns about mupirocin resistance selection.Level I

2. Antibiotic Management of Open Fractures

The management of open fractures represents a surgical emergency where timely and appropriate antibiotic administration is a critical intervention to prevent infection, including osteomyelitis and fracture-related infection (FRI).

2.1 The Golden Hour: Critical Importance of Timely Administration

Multiple clinical practice guidelines emphasize that IV antibiotics should be administered as soon as possible, with an ideal target of within one hour of injury.Level II

🚨 Quantified Risk: Every Minute Matters One study reported a six-fold decrease in infection rates when antibiotics were given within 3 hours of injury.Level II A retrospective analysis demonstrated a 0% infection rate when antibiotics were administered within 66 minutes of injury, rising to 17% when delayed beyond this time.Level II

2.2 Stratified Approach: Gustilo-Anderson Classification

Antibiotic selection is stratified based on the Gustilo-Anderson classification, which categorizes open fractures by mechanism, wound size, and degree of soft tissue damage.Level II

Type I & II Fractures

Low-energy injuries with wounds <10 cm and minimal contamination. Cefazolin 2 g IV q8h provides targeted gram-positive coverage.Level II

Type III (A, B, C) Fractures

High-energy injuries with extensive soft tissue damage, contamination, and often vascular injury. Standard is to add an aminoglycoside (gentamicin 5–7 mg/kg IV q24h) to cefazolin for gram-negative coverage.Level II Piperacillin-tazobactam is an alternative single agent providing broad-spectrum coverage.Level II

2.3 High-Risk Contamination Scenarios

🌾 Farmyard/Fecal Contamination Add high-dose penicillin G (2–4 million units IV q4h) to standard regimen for anaerobic coverage (Clostridium spp.). Update tetanus prophylaxis.Level II
🌊 Freshwater Contamination Add fluoroquinolone (ciprofloxacin or levofloxacin) for Pseudomonas aeruginosa coverage.Level II Saltwater: Add doxycycline for Vibrio species.Level II

2.4 Paradigm Shift: Shorter Durations of Therapy

A landmark systematic review and meta-analysis found no demonstrable benefit to prolonging antibiotic treatment beyond 72 hours, regardless of fracture severity.Level I Subgroup analysis indicated that even 24–48 hour regimens were equivalent to prolonged courses.Level I

✅ Current Recommendation Antibiotics should be continued for a minimum of 24 hours after initial surgical procedure and should not exceed 72 hours, even if definitive soft tissue closure is delayed.Level II

2.5 Local Antibiotic Delivery

For high-risk open fractures (especially Gustilo Type III), local delivery systems allow extremely high local antibiotic concentrations unachievable systemically while minimizing toxicity.Level II Modalities include antibiotic-impregnated PMMA beads (tobramycin, vancomycin), gentamicin-coated intramedullary nails, and direct application of vancomycin powder.Level II

3. Therapeutic Management of Osteomyelitis

Osteomyelitis, an infection of the bone, requires a meticulous diagnostic approach and often prolonged antimicrobial therapy. A central theme in modern treatment is the imperative of obtaining a microbiological diagnosis to guide therapy, coupled with a paradigm shift toward highly bioavailable oral antibiotics.

3.1 Diagnosis as the Cornerstone of Therapy

🔬 Withhold Empiric Antibiotics in Stable Patients In clinically stable patients with suspected osteomyelitis and negative blood cultures, IDSA guidelines strongly recommend withholding empiric antibiotics until a definitive microbiological diagnosis is established via bone biopsy.Level I Premature antibiotics can sterilize bone cultures, forcing a prolonged, potentially ineffective empiric course.

An image-guided percutaneous needle biopsy or open surgical bone biopsy is the gold standard for diagnosis.Level I Samples should be sent for histopathology and culture (aerobic, anaerobic, fungal, and mycobacterial as indicated).Level I

Exceptions: Empiric antibiotics should be initiated immediately in patients who are hemodynamically unstable, septic, or exhibiting severe/progressive neurologic compromise (e.g., spinal cord compression in vertebral osteomyelitis).Level I

3.2 Acute vs. Chronic Osteomyelitis

Acute osteomyelitis (symptoms within ~2 weeks) is characterized by active inflammation without significant necrosis, and may be cured with antibiotics alone.Level II Chronic osteomyelitis (≥6 weeks) is defined by the presence of necrotic, avascular bone (sequestrum) and reactive new bone (involucrum). A draining sinus tract is pathognomonic.Level II

🚨 Chronic Osteomyelitis = Surgical Disease The avascular sequestrum is impenetrable to both host defenses and systemic antibiotics. Antibiotic therapy alone in chronic osteomyelitis is destined to fail. Successful treatment requires aggressive surgical debridement of all non-viable bone followed by prolonged antibiotics.Level II

3.3 Pathogen-Directed Antibiotic Regimens

Empiric Therapy (When Required)

Vancomycin is the common first-line empiric choice pending culture data for MRSA coverage.Level I For suspected polymicrobial infections (e.g., diabetic foot), broader coverage with piperacillin-tazobactam or vancomycin + third-generation cephalosporin is necessary.Level I

Definitive Therapy

  • MSSA: Nafcillin, oxacillin, or cefazolin (drugs of choice)Level I
  • MRSA: Vancomycin (standard); daptomycin and linezolid as alternativesLevel II
  • Gram-negative bacilli: Guided by susceptibilities; fluoroquinolones excellent for Enterobacterales; carbapenems reserved for MDR organisms (e.g., ESBL-producing)Level I

3.4 The Paradigm Shift to Oral Antibiotics: The OVIVA Trial

🏆 Landmark Evidence: The OVIVA Trial The Oral Versus Intravenous Antibiotics for Bone and Joint Infection (OVIVA) trial—a large RCT—demonstrated that switching to oral antibiotic therapy after a short initial IV course (typically ≤7 days) was non-inferior to continuing parenteral therapy for the entire duration.Level I This allows earlier discharge, reduced costs, and avoids risks of long-term venous catheters (bloodstream infections, VTE).Level I

Suitable oral agents require high bioavailability: fluoroquinolones (ciprofloxacin, levofloxacin), linezolid, trimethoprim-sulfamethoxazole, clindamycin, doxycycline, and rifampin (in combination for staphylococcal infections).Level II

3.5 Duration of Therapy

Standard: Following adequate surgical debridement, a 4- to 6-week course is recommended for chronic osteomyelitis.Level I

  • Native vertebral osteomyelitis: 6 weeks total (parenteral or high-bioavailability oral)Level I
  • Diabetic foot osteomyelitis: Variable; 6 weeks or longer if bone not resected; 2–5 days if infected bone completely resectedLevel I

4. Integrated Management of Prosthetic Joint Infections (PJI)

PJI is one of the most devastating complications in orthopedic surgery, associated with substantial morbidity, multiple surgeries, prolonged antibiotic courses, and immense healthcare costs.Level I Management is exceptionally complex due to microbial biofilm on the implant surface, which shields organisms from host defenses and systemic antibiotics.Level II

4.1 The Symbiosis of Surgical and Medical Treatment

Surgery is paramount. The primary goal is to physically disrupt and remove the biofilm and reduce bacterial burden to levels controllable by host defenses and adjunctive antibiotics.Level II Optimal outcomes require a multidisciplinary team of orthopedic surgeons, infectious disease specialists, and plastic surgeons for complex reconstruction.Level I

🛡 Conceptual Framework: The Battle Against Biofilm Each surgical and medical intervention is a specific tactic: Two-stage exchange = "scorched earth" (complete implant removal). Antibiotic-loaded cement spacers = localized "chemical warfare." DAIR procedure = "counter-insurgency" relying on anti-biofilm agents like rifampin.

4.2 Surgical Strategies and Tailored Antibiotic Protocols

DAIR (Debridement, Antibiotics, and Implant Retention)

Indications: Early postoperative PJI (within 3–4 weeks of index arthroplasty) or acute hematogenous PJI with stable implant, healthy soft tissues, and short symptom duration (<3 weeks).Level II Causative organism must be known and susceptible to an oral anti-biofilm agent.Level II

Antibiotic strategy: 2–6 weeks pathogen-specific IV therapy → long course of oral combination therapy. Total duration: 3–6 months for staphylococcal infections.Level II Rifampin is mandatory for staphylococcal PJI (must be combined with companion agent to prevent resistance).Level I

Two-Stage Exchange Arthroplasty (Gold Standard)

Indications: Chronic PJI, resistant organisms (MRSA, P. aeruginosa), significant bone/soft-tissue defects, or draining sinus tract.Level II

Procedure: Stage 1: explantation, debridement, antibiotic-loaded PMMA spacer → 4–6 weeks pathogen-directed IV therapy → Stage 2: reimplantation.Level II Recent RCT evidence suggests a 4-week IV course may be as effective as 6 weeks.Level I

One-Stage Exchange (Emerging Alternative)

Indications: Healthier patients with known, highly susceptible pathogen, minimal bone loss, healthy closable soft tissues. Contraindicated with unknown or MDR organisms.Level II Antibiotic regimen similar to DAIR (prolonged postoperative course with rifampin for staphylococcal infections).Level II

✅ One-Stage vs. Two-Stage: A Potential Paradigm Shift Growing evidence, including interim results from large RCTs, suggests one-stage exchange may offer similar success rates in appropriately selected patients.Level I Broader adoption could dramatically reduce surgeries, hospital stays, and costs—though requiring changes in training, protocols, and reimbursement.

4.3 Pathogen-Specific Management

  • Staphylococcal PJI (most common): Success of implant retention strategies hinges on rifampin—unique ability to penetrate biofilm and kill slow-growing, metabolically inactive staphylococci.Level I Always paired with companion drug (fluoroquinolone, fusidic acid, linezolid, or TMP/SMX).Level II
  • Gram-negative PJI: Fluoroquinolones are the most important class for implant-retention scenarios due to bone penetration and biofilm activity.Level II Two-stage exchange frequently necessary.
  • Other pathogens: Enterococci, Streptococci, Cutibacterium acnes—management follows same principles (aggressive debridement + pathogen-directed prolonged antibiotics).Level II

5. Key Antibiotic Classes in Orthopedics

5.1 Fluoroquinolones: Efficacy vs. Safety Concerns

Advantages

  • High oral bioavailability (~100%), enabling effective oral step-down therapyLevel II
  • Excellent bone penetrationLevel II
  • Anti-biofilm activity—cornerstone for implant-associated infectionsLevel II
  • Preferred oral agents for P. aeruginosa PJI (often only oral option)Level II
  • Crucial companion drug to rifampin for staphylococcal PJILevel II

Safety Profile (Significant Concerns)

⚠ Regulatory Warnings (FDA, Health Canada, UK MHRA)
  • Musculoskeletal: Tendinitis and tendon rupture (esp. Achilles), especially in elderly, renal impairment, concurrent corticosteroidsLevel II
  • Neurological: CNS effects (confusion, anxiety, hallucinations); peripheral neuropathy (pain, burning, numbness)Level II
  • Aortic: Increased risk of aortic aneurysm and dissectionLevel II
  • Discontinuation rate: Significantly higher than non-fluoroquinolone regimensLevel II

Clinical consensus: For severe, complex orthopedic infections (PJI, Pseudomonas osteomyelitis), benefits frequently outweigh risks, but use mandates careful patient selection, counseling, and monitoring.Level II

5.2 Carbapenems: Last-Resort Agents

Carbapenems (meropenem, imipenem, ertapenem) possess an exceptionally broad spectrum including ESBL-producing Enterobacterales.Level II Their use is strictly reserved for severe, limb- or life-threatening infections caused by confirmed MDR gram-negative pathogens.Level II

🛡 Stewardship Imperative Carbapenem-resistant Enterobacterales (CRE) represent a critical public health threat. Resistance genes on mobile plasmids facilitate rapid spread. Use requires pre-authorization from infectious disease specialists to preserve efficacy as last-resort agents.Level II

5.3 Fusidic Acid: Specialized Anti-Staphylococcal Agent

Fusidic acid has a narrow spectrum highly targeted against staphylococci (MSSA and MRSA).Level II Its primary role is as a component of long-term, all-oral combination regimens for difficult-to-treat staphylococcal infections, frequently paired with rifampin for PJI (DAIR).Level II

⚠ Must Use in Combination Rapid resistance development with monotherapy (single-step mutation). Always pair with another active anti-staphylococcal agent (e.g., rifampin).Level II Generally well-tolerated; GI upset with oral formulation; reversible hepatotoxicity possible with high-dose IV; CYP450 drug interactions.Level II

Key Evidence Sources Identified in the Document

  1. OVIVA Trial (Oral Versus Intravenous Antibiotics for Bone and Joint Infection) – Large RCT demonstrating non-inferiority of oral vs. IV antibiotics after initial short IV course for bone/joint infections. Level I
  2. Meta-analysis on antibiotic duration for open fractures (Messner et al./systematic review) – Found no benefit to antibiotic courses >72 hours for any Gustilo type, including Type III. Level I
  3. ASHP/SIS/SHEA/IDSA Guidelines for Antimicrobial Prophylaxis in Surgery – Defining timing (within 60 min), duration (<24 h), dosing, and redosing recommendations. Level I
  4. IDSA Clinical Practice Guidelines for Vertebral Osteomyelitis (2015) – Establishing biopsy-first paradigm and 6-week treatment durations. Level I
  5. IDSA Guidelines for Prosthetic Joint Infection – Comprehensive framework for PJI management including DAIR, one-stage, and two-stage protocols. Level I
  6. IWGDF/IDSA Guidelines on Diabetic Foot Infections – Guiding osteomyelitis treatment in the diabetic foot with variable duration based on surgical approach. Level I

Conclusion and Future Horizons

The evidence-based application of antibiotics in orthopedics has evolved into a highly sophisticated field, balancing infection prevention/treatment with the global crisis of antimicrobial resistance. Core principles: meticulous prophylactic protocols (precise timing, short duration, cefazolin-based); "front-loaded" strategy for open fractures (immediate, risk-stratified antibiotics, short courses); microbiological diagnosis before therapy in stable osteomyelitis patients; validated oral antibiotics for long-term treatment; and integrated surgical-medical approach for PJI targeting biofilm.

Antimicrobial stewardship permeates every recommendation—from a single prophylactic dose to a six-month PJI regimen. The profiles of key classes underscore this: fluoroquinolones limited by toxicity, carbapenems constrained by resistance ecology.

Future frontiers:

  • Novel anti-biofilm agents: Antimicrobial peptides (AMPs), compounds targeting biofilm signaling/structural integrityLevel II
  • Non-antibiotic therapies: Bacteriophage therapy, photodynamic therapy (light-activated reactive oxygen species)Level II
  • Advanced diagnostics: Next-generation sequencing of microbial DNA for earlier, more precise pathogen identificationLevel II

The continued efficacy of modern orthopedic surgery hinges on innovation, evidence-based practice, and responsible stewardship of antimicrobial resources.
