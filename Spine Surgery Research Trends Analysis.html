1. Introduction

The Transition from Novelty to Evidence-Based Precision

Spinal surgery research between 2024 and 2026 is defined by a rigorous transition from the adoption of novel technologies to their evidence-based validation and refinement. The initial enthusiasm of the early 2020s—centered on robotics, endoscopic platforms, and artificial intelligence—has matured into a phase of critical scrutiny regarding clinical utility, cost-effectiveness, and long-term safety. This report synthesizes data from over 150 primary sources, bibliometric analyses, and clinical trial results to identify the highest-impact areas for future systematic reviews and meta-analyses.

📊 Bibliometric Shift
Terms such as "instrumentation," which dominated citation bursts in the mid-2010s, have been superseded by "risk factors," "indirect decompression," and "machine learning" in the 2024–2026 window.Ref 1 The hardware problem is largely solved; the current challenge lies in patient selection, biological optimization, and minimization of approach-related morbidity.

Four Pillars of Research

  • AI / Machine Learning — Prediction of Proximal Junctional Kyphosis (PJK) and Minimal Clinically Important Difference (MCID), with a critical "transportability gap" regarding external validation.
  • Muscle-Bone-Alignment Axis — Spine-specific sarcopenia (paraspinal muscle fatty infiltration) as a more potent predictor of mechanical failure than systemic sarcopenia or bone mineral density alone.
  • Unilateral Biportal Endoscopy (UBE) — Unique complication profile with intracranial hydrostatic repercussions demanding distinct management protocols.
  • Novel Biomaterials — 3D-printed titanium and porous PEEK architectures creating new controversies regarding radiographic assessment and long-term subsidence.

2. AI & Machine Learning in Prognostication

From Regression to Deep Learning

Publication volume on AI/ML in spine surgery has grown exponentially between 2024 and 2026. The field is pivoting from simple descriptive analytics to complex predictive modeling intended to guide surgical decision-making. However, a significant dichotomy exists between the proliferation of predictive algorithms and their actual clinical deployment—the "transportability gap."

2.1 Prediction of Proximal Junctional Kyphosis (PJK)

PJK and its severe counterpart, Proximal Junctional Failure (PJF), remain the most vexing mechanical complications in Adult Spinal Deformity (ASD) surgery, with incidence rates of 20–40%.Ref 2 ML algorithms integrating granular radiographic parameters (GAP score), Hounsfield Units (HU), and paraspinal muscle quality demonstrate AUC values of 0.72 to near 1.0 in internal validation cohorts.Ref 4

🔑 Key Finding
MRI-based soft tissue metrics (paraspinal muscle fatty infiltration) are the strongest drivers of PJK prediction accuracy, often outperforming traditional alignment parameters like PI-LL mismatch.Ref 6 This suggests mechanical failure is as much a failure of dynamic stabilizers (muscles) as static stabilizers (bone/ligament).

Despite high internal performance, a critical limitation is reliance on single-center datasets. When tested on external populations, accuracy frequently degrades ("calibration drift").Ref 8 Current ML models may overfit to local surgical techniques or patient demographics rather than learning universal biological truths.

2.2 MCID Prediction & Patient-Reported Outcomes

In 2025, prospective multicenter studies validated deep learning algorithms predicting MCID achievement for ODI and VAS scores using preoperative variables.Ref 9 The SuMO (Surgery Medical Outcomes) mobile application demonstrated accurate classification of MCID vs. non-MCID achievers at 8 months postoperative (mean ODI: 0.12 vs. 0.26).Ref 9

⚠️ The "Outcome Gap"
The literature notes a scarcity of studies demonstrating improved clinical outcomes as a result of using AI tools. While prediction is accurate, it remains unproven whether access to this prediction alters surgeon behavior or patient satisfaction in a statistically significant manner.Ref 8

2.3 The "Black Box" Problem & Explainability

A pervasive theme is the trade-off between model performance and interpretability. Explainable AI (XAI) techniques, such as SHAP (SHapley Additive exPlanations), are now used to visualize feature importance for individual predictions.Ref 6 This allows clinicians to see, for example, that a specific patient's high PJK risk is driven primarily by low UIV bone density. Clinician trust remains a major barrier; surgeons are hesitant to rely on opaque algorithms for high-stakes decisions like extending a fusion level.Ref 8

3. Sarcopenia, Spinal Alignment & Mechanical Failure

3.1 Spine-Specific vs. Generalized Sarcopenia

A major insight from 2024–2026 literature is the dissociation between systemic muscle health and local paraspinal muscle integrity. Patients may have normal appendicular muscle mass yet exhibit profound fatty infiltration (FI) of the multifidus and erector spinae muscles.Ref 12

Paraspinal sarcopenia—specifically at the Upper Instrumented Vertebra (UIV)—is a significantly stronger predictor of mechanical complications than generalized metrics like the psoas index or appendicular skeletal muscle mass.Ref 2 These muscles act as a "posterior tension band," supporting the spine in extension. Their degeneration compromises this support system, increasing mechanical load on instrumentation.Ref 14

3.2 Fatty Infiltration: Quality vs. Quantity

The assessment has evolved from measuring Cross-Sectional Area (CSA) to evaluating "Muscle Quality" via fatty infiltration degree. This addresses the "Fatty Infiltration Paradox"—where muscle volume may appear preserved due to replacement of contractile tissue with adipose tissue (pseudo-hypertrophy), masking functional deficits.Ref 15

High grades of fatty infiltration (Goutallier Grade 3–4) are consistently associated with poor outcomes. Meta-analyses indicate FI is more sensitive than CSA in predicting PJK and surgical site infection (SSI).Ref 16 One study found 66.7% of PJK patients were severely sarcopenic vs. 7.4% in the non-PJK group.Ref 19

🔬 Measurement Challenge
Standardization remains a critical gap. While Goutallier grading is common, it is subjective. Newer studies utilize MRI-based pixel intensity analysis and HU mapping on CT scans for objective quantification.Ref 19 Establishing a universal threshold for "severe" spine-specific sarcopenia is a critical next step.

3.3 The GAP Score Controversy

The Global Alignment and Proportion (GAP) score was introduced to predict mechanical failure based on sagittal alignment parameters. However, 2024–2026 research has exposed significant limitations: the GAP score fails to predict mechanical failure in up to 22% of "proportioned" patients.Ref 7

The emerging consensus: in patients with severe paraspinal sarcopenia, the spine cannot support even a "perfectly aligned" construct. Failure is not a failure of alignment, but a failure of dynamic support.Ref 20 This has led to calls for a "Modified GAP Score" integrating paraspinal muscle quality metrics.

4. Unilateral Biportal Endoscopy (UBE)

4.1 Comparative Outcomes

UBE has seen a meteoric rise in adoption. Meta-analyses indicate UBE offers similar long-term clinical efficacy to uniportal endoscopy and microdiscectomy for VAS and ODI scores,Ref 24 with specific advantages in decompression efficiency (shorter operative durations, greater dural sac expansion). The learning curve is steep but stabilizes after 30–50 cases.Ref 26

4.2 Unique Complication Profile: Hydrostatic Repercussions

UBE is a "hydro-surgery" where the interplay of water pressure and neural anatomy creates distinct risks not seen in "air-based" surgery.

Postoperative Headache & Intracranial Pressure

Postoperative headache is significantly more common in UBE than open or uniportal surgery.Ref 26 The mechanism involves the Monro-Kellie doctrine: high irrigation pressure (>30 mmHg) can force fluid into the epidural space, potentially leading to:

  • Intracranial hypotension — "siphon effect" after cessation of irrigation
  • Subdural hygromas — fluid tracking cranially causing headaches, nausea, dizzinessRef 29
  • Seizures — rare; excessive pressure + dural compromise → pneumocephalusRef 30
  • Neck pain — irrigation fluid tracking up fascial planes into cervical regionRef 32
🚨 Safety Threshold
Management strategies include strict control of irrigation pressure (<30 mmHg), limiting operative time to reduce fluid absorption, and aggressive management of dural compromise.Ref 26

Cervical & Thoracic Expansion

UBE for thoracic Ossification of Ligamentum Flavum (OLF) carries elevated cord injury risk due to limited canal space and irrigation pressure concerns.Ref 33 UBE posterior cervical foraminotomy (UBE-PCF) demonstrates NDI/VAS improvement comparable to full-endoscopic procedures with a complication rate of 6.2%.Ref 34

5. Novel Biomaterials for Interbody Fusion

5.1 3D-Printed Porous Titanium (3DPT)

3DPT cages support bony ingrowth (osseointegration) directly into the implant surface. The porous Voronoi lattice design increases the coefficient of friction and reduces the modulus of elasticity to resemble vertebral bone, theoretically reducing stress shielding and subsidence.Ref 37

Meta-analyses show 3DPT cages have significantly lower subsidence rates vs. traditional PEEK (1.06% vs. 4.76% severe subsidence).Ref 38 In atlantoaxial fusions, 3DPT showed a 100% fusion rate vs. 80.6% for PEEK.Ref 40

🔍 Imaging Artifact Controversy
3DPT is radiopaque and produces significant CT/MRI artifacts (the "blooming effect"), potentially mimicking trabecular bone and leading to false-positive fusion assessments.Ref 5 Are the higher reported fusion rates real, or an artifact of imaging limitations?

5.2 Novel Porous PEEK & Surface Modifications

Fused Strand Deposition (FSD) allows PEEK to be 3D-printed with porous architecture similar to 3DPT, retaining radiolucency while providing a scaffold for bone ingrowth.Ref 42

Cutting-edge research shows porous PEEK surfaces can modulate macrophage polarization, promoting the M2 anti-inflammatory phenotype (tissue repair) while suppressing M1 (fibrous encapsulation).Ref 42 Nano-hydroxyapatite (nHA) coatings on PEEK promote VEGF expression and osteoblast differentiation, though durability concerns remain.Ref 37

6. Emerging Trends & Systemic Factors

6.1 GLP-1 Receptor Agonists in Spine Surgery

An unexpected but rapidly emerging trend is the investigation of GLP-1 Receptor Agonists (e.g., semaglutide, tirzepatide) in spine surgery. Preliminary systematic reviews suggest perioperative use does not increase adverse events and may potentially reduce pseudarthrosis and infection risk in obese/diabetic populations.Ref 45

💊 Metabolic-Surgical Intersection
The hypothesis is that improved glycemic control and weight loss optimize the host environment for fusion and healing. However, anesthetic implications regarding delayed gastric emptying remain controversial. Recent meta-analyses suggest the delay may be clinically manageable without prolonged drug cessation.Ref 49

6.2 Bibliometric Signals

  • "Risk factors" — significant citation burst 2020–2022, signaling the field's shift toward predictive analytics.Ref 1
  • "Indirect decompression" — surge reflecting dominance of lateral/oblique interbody fusion relying on ligamentotaxis.
  • "Follow-up" — burst strength of 4.59, indicating that journals increasingly demand longer-term data beyond the "2-year minimum."

8. Conclusions

The period from 2024 to 2026 marks a pivotal era of "Precision and Verification" in spine surgery. Five key themes emerge:

  1. AI is Maturing but Fragile — The single biggest barrier is the lack of external validation. Future research must bridge the "transportability gap."
  2. Muscle Matters — Spine-specific sarcopenia assessment (via FI grading) is poised to become a standard of care for preoperative stratification in ASD, potentially modifying the GAP score.
  3. Endoscopy Requires Respect — Understanding and managing hydrostatic pressure is the new safety frontier for UBE adoption.
  4. Material Science is Hybridizing — 3D-printed porous PEEK represents a promising synthesis of radiolucency and osteointegration, but imaging artifacts of titanium must be rigorously accounted for.
  5. Metabolic Intersection — GLP-1 agonists represent a new frontier in optimizing the host for surgery.
✅ Recommendation for High-Impact Research
Researchers should prioritize multicenter validation studies for AI models, standardized imaging protocols for sarcopenia, and comparative safety trials for endoscopic fluid management. The era of describing "novel techniques" is ending; the era of proving their value has begun.
