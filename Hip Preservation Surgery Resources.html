1. Introduction & Evolution of Hip Preservation

Hip preservation surgery has undergone a profound evolution over the past two decades, transitioning from a highly specialized, niche orthopaedic pursuit into a foundational pillar of comprehensive joint management. Historically, the presentation of non-arthritic hip pain in the adolescent and young adult was met with significant diagnostic ambiguity. Patients frequently endured years of chronic pain, consulting multiple healthcare providers before receiving a precise structural diagnosis—often only after irreversible chondral damage had occurred.

The modern era of hip preservation represents a paradigm shift driven by pioneering innovations in three-dimensional imaging, enhanced biomechanical modeling, and refinement of both open and arthroscopic surgical techniques. The primary objective is to proactively identify and correct underlying pathomorphological anomalies, thereby restoring normal joint kinematics, alleviating pain, and theoretically increasing the longevity of the native hip joint.

Two Core Pathomorphological Entities

  • Femoroacetabular Impingement (FAI): Characterized by abnormal bony morphology that induces dynamic collision and shearing forces during terminal ranges of motion.
  • Developmental Dysplasia of the Hip (DDH): Fundamentally defined by a lack of osseous coverage, leading to static overload, microinstability, and elevated articular contact pressures.
⚠ The Borderline DDH Challenge

The most significant diagnostic and therapeutic challenges arise in the elusive "transition zone" of borderline DDH (LCEA 20°–25°). Determining whether the hip functions primarily as an unstable dysplastic joint or as a stable joint suffering from impingement requires sophisticated, algorithmic decision-making.

3. International Clinical Guidelines & Consensus Statements

3.1 The Warwick Agreement on FAI Syndrome (2016) Level I — International Consensus

The Warwick Agreement represents a landmark international, multidisciplinary consensus. The expert panel shifted terminology from "FAI" to "FAI Syndrome" (FAIS) to emphasize the central role of symptoms.

Diagnostic Triad for FAIS

1
Appropriate Symptoms: Motion-related or position-related hip/groin pain, frequently with mechanical symptoms (catching, locking, clicking)
2
Positive Clinical Signs: Impingement test (FADIR) reproduces the patient's familiar pain
3
Corroborating Imaging: Radiographic or advanced imaging confirming cam or pincer morphology
🚫 Critical Consensus Point

Asymptomatic individuals with cam or pincer morphologies should NOT be diagnosed with FAIS and should NOT undergo prophylactic surgery. The natural history of who will eventually develop symptoms remains unknown.

3.2 AAOS & Hip Society Guidelines Level II–III

  • AAOS recommends oral NSAIDs and intra-articular corticosteroids for short-term symptomatic relief in osteoarthritis.
  • Tranexamic acid (TXA) is recommended to reduce blood loss during THA.
  • For older adults undergoing THA, low-quality evidence suggests cemented femoral stems may lower periprosthetic fracture risk.
  • Specific Appropriate Use Criteria (AUC) exist for management of DDH in infants.

3.3 IHDI Standards Level II–III

  • The IHDI classification (position of proximal femoral metaphysis / H-point relative to pelvis) guides treatment severity assessment.
  • Advocates the term "surveillance" over "screening" for infant DDH—emphasizing continuous periodic clinical exams until walking age.
  • Identifies tight swaddling as a major risk factor for DDH; promotes hip-healthy swaddling techniques.
  • Ultrasound recommended between 6 weeks–6 months; radiography at 4–6 months.
  • For adults with mild/borderline dysplasia: conservative lifestyle modifications as first-line defense (weight management, avoidance of impact sports, targeted PT).

3.4 ISHA / The Hip Preservation Society Level III–IV

Recent ISHA annual meetings highlight a shift toward multidisciplinary teamwork. Best outcomes in complex cases (borderline dysplasia, hip-spine syndrome, severe FAI) require collaboration among surgeons, sports physicians, radiologists, and specialized physiotherapists. International Delphi consensus projects establish best practice guidelines for arthroscopic interventions.

4. Biomechanics & Pathomorphology

A comprehensive understanding of pelvic orientation, femoral morphology, and intra-articular load distribution is the absolute prerequisite for patient-specific preservation strategies. Expert opinion – not specified in source

4.1 FAI Biomechanics

FAI represents a dynamic mechanism of joint damage where structural morphological abnormalities lead to premature, pathologic contact between the proximal femur and the acetabular rim during physiological ranges of motion. This collision occurs most frequently in positions of terminal hip flexion and internal rotation, generating immense shearing forces that chronically compromise the labrum and articular cartilage.

Table 3. Comparison of FAI Morphological Types
Parameter Cam Morphology Pincer Morphology
Mechanism Inclusion type — aspherical femoral head driven into acetabulum Impaction type — femoral neck impacts overhanging acetabular rim
Location of deformity Femoral head-neck junction (decreased offset) Acetabulum (retroversion, coxa profunda, protrusio)
Primary injury pattern Outside-in shearing: cartilage delamination from subchondral bone Sequential labral crushing + reactive "contre-coup" posteroinferior lesion
Labral involvement Labral detachment at chondrolabral junction Circumferential labral degeneration
Common associations SCFE, prior pediatric trauma Acetabular retroversion, coxa profunda

4.2 DDH Biomechanics

In stark contrast to FAI, DDH is fundamentally a condition of static overload, diminished contact area, and microinstability. The dysplastic socket is typically shallow, excessively anteverted, and possesses increased acetabular roof obliquity. The proximal femur frequently exhibits compensatory high antetorsion and coxa valga.

⚠ Critical Biomechanical Risk

For every 1° decrease in lateral coverage below an LCEA of 28°, there is a 13% increase in the risk of developing clinical osteoarthritis. Level III — Epidemiological/biomechanical study

Because osseous stability is compromised, the hip becomes pathologically reliant on soft-tissue stabilizers. The labrum undergoes extreme hypertrophy to augment the shallow socket, while the iliofemoral ligament sustains chronic tension.

4.3 Borderline DDH Biomechanics: The Transition Zone

Borderline DDH (LCEA 20°–25°) represents a complex "transition zone." Relying exclusively on LCEA is a documented clinical pitfall—it is a 2D measure that fails to account for anterior/posterior wall coverage, acetabular index, pelvic tilt, or femoral antetorsion.

Table 4. Biomechanical Profiles in Borderline DDH
Profile Morphological Features Functional Behavior Clinical Marker
Unstable Borderline Anterior deficiency + high femoral antetorsion Chronic instability, anterior subluxation Iliocapsularis hypertrophy (compensatory stabilizer)
Stable Borderline Isolated lateral insufficiency OR robust cam morphology "filling" the joint FAI-like mechanics, impingement symptoms No soft-tissue compensatory changes
ℹ Sex-Based Morphological Clustering

Males with borderline LCEA often show clusters of global/focal impingement (high alpha angles + superior acetabular retroversion). Females more commonly present with true anterolateral acetabular deficiency and instability.

5.3 Diagnostic Imaging Algorithm

  • Radiography (First-line): Standing AP pelvis radiograph for global acetabular coverage (LCEA, Tönnis angle, acetabular index). Dedicated hip views (Dunn, cross-table lateral, false-profile) for alpha angle and anterior coverage. Level II–III
  • MRI / MR Arthrography: Gold standard for chondrolabral lesions, ligamentous integrity, and soft-tissue evaluation. Radial sequences at 2 o'clock position are highly sensitive for cam deformities and labral pathology. Level II–III
  • 3D CT: Invaluable for advanced preoperative planning—comprehensive 360° assessment of complex morphologies, acetabular version, and femoral torsion. Level IV

6. Treatment Algorithms

6.1 FAI Syndrome Treatment Pathway

FAIS Treatment Algorithm

1
Conservative Management (First-line): Patient education, activity modification (avoid repetitive deep flexion/internal rotation), supervised PT (core stability + abductor strengthening), diagnostic/therapeutic IA corticosteroid injections. Level II
↓ (Failed after adequate trial)
2
Hip Arthroscopy (Standard of care): Femoral osteoplasty, acetabular rim trimming, complete capsular closure, anatomical labral repair/reconstruction. Superior short-term iHOT-33 scores vs. non-operative care. Return to sport: ~94% (elite), ~89% (recreational) at 6–7 months. Level II — Umbrella review
↓ (Severe/complex deformities)
3
Open Surgical Dislocation (Gold standard for severe cases): For severe global retroversion, extensive cam from SCFE, post-traumatic deformities. Modified Dunn procedure for subcapital realignment. 360° access for massive osteoplasty. Level IV
⚠ Important Timing Note

Preoperative intra-articular injections administered within 3 months prior to hip arthroscopy are associated with significantly higher postoperative infection risks. Level II

6.2 Symptomatic DDH Treatment Pathway

Because non-surgical methods cannot physically correct a shallow, anteverted socket, conservative therapy rarely provides a lasting solution for adult DDH.

Periacetabular Osteotomy (PAO)

The Bernese PAO (Ganz) is the gold-standard joint-preserving procedure for symptomatic dysplastic hips prior to advanced osteoarthritis. It reorients the acetabulum to increase lateral and anterior coverage while maintaining posterior column integrity for early mobilization.

Modern Modifications:

  • Supine Position PAO: Specialized tilted table; mean LCEA improvement from 15.5° to 35.2°. Level IV
  • Double Approach PAO (Dienst et al., 2018): Lateral decubitus with direct vision during dangerous ischial osteotomy step; avoids rectus femoris tenotomy. Level IV
  • Minimally Invasive PAO: Modified Smith-Petersen approach with curved osteotomes to minimize soft-tissue trauma. Level IV
🚫 Absolute Contraindication

Isolated hip arthroscopy is heavily contraindicated in frank DDH. Addressing secondary soft-tissue stabilizers without correcting primary osseous instability invariably fails and actively accelerates joint destruction.

7. The Borderline DDH Conundrum: Advanced Metrics & Algorithms

The management of the painful borderline dysplastic hip (LCEA 20°–25°) represents the most fiercely debated frontier in hip preservation. Misinterpreting a borderline hip's true stability profile carries profound consequences.

7.1 Catastrophic Risks of Misclassification

7.2 The FEAR Index Level III — Clinical Validation

The Femoro-Epiphyseal Acetabular Roof (FEAR) Index (Wyatt et al., 2017) is a critical radiographic tool designed to distinguish stable from unstable hips within the borderline cohort. It is grounded in the Heuter-Volkmann principle.

FEAR Index Interpretation

+
Positive FEAR Index: Laterally diverging angle → historically outward load vectors → high likelihood of instability and true dysplasia
−
Negative FEAR Index: Medially converging angle → stable, inward load transmission → more consistent with FAI or normal morphology

At an optimized threshold of > 3°, the FEAR index predicted appropriate surgical treatment (PAO vs. arthroscopy) with 80% sensitivity and 81% specificity. Strong positive correlations exist between high femoral antetorsion and elevated FEAR index values in dysplastic settings.

7.3 The PATH Score Algorithm Level III — Regression Analysis

The PATH (PAO vs. Arthroscopic Treatment of the Hip) Score synthesizes multiple morphological parameters into a decision tool. Four variables drive the decision:

Table 9. PATH Score Criteria
Criterion Instability Marker Points
ACEA < 25° Anterior un-coverage / deficient anterior wall 1
FEAR Index > −3° Adverse, laterally diverging load transmission vectors 1
Femoral Version ≥ 15° Destabilizing excess femoral antetorsion 1
Maximum Score 3

Gender-Specific Treatment Pathways

M
Males: Predominantly treated with isolated hip arthroscopy (iHA) unless PATH = 3 (severe multi-planar deficiencies) → PAO indicated
F
Females: PATH ≥ 2 → universal PAO; PATH ≤ 1 → ambiguous zone, variably distributed between PAO and iHA

For borderline hips definitively deemed stable, hip arthroscopy with meticulous capsular plication and anatomical labral preservation yields outcomes equivalent to normal hips. For those deemed unstable, the PAO remains necessary to establish congruent, load-bearing architecture.
