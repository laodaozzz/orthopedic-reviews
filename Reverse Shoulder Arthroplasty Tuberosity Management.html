1. The Central Role of Tuberosities in Modern RSA for Fracture

The surgical management of acute, comminuted 3- and 4-part proximal humerus fractures in the elderly population presents a formidable challenge, characterized by poor bone quality, complex fracture patterns, and a tenuous vascular supply. While hemiarthroplasty (HA) was once a common treatment, its outcomes have proven unpredictable, with success being critically dependent on the healing of the greater and lesser tuberosities—an often elusive goal in this patient demographic. Level I[1]

The advent and subsequent refinement of reverse shoulder arthroplasty (RSA) have marked a paradigm shift in the treatment of these injuries. Initially conceived to function in the absence of a competent rotator cuff, the philosophy surrounding RSA for fracture has evolved dramatically. It is now unequivocally established that while RSA can provide reliable pain relief and forward elevation even with tuberosity failure, the anatomical reconstruction and biological healing of the tuberosities are paramount for achieving superior functional outcomes, restoring rotational capacity, and ensuring long-term implant stability and survival. Level I[3]

Key Paradigm Shift: The evolution from "tuberosities are optional" to "tuberosities are integral" represents a fundamental transition in surgical philosophy, moving RSA from a salvage procedure to a truly reconstructive one, where the goal is to restore the entire proximal humeral anatomy around a biomechanically advantaged prosthesis.

2. Biomechanical Rationale: From Rotator Cuff Levers to Stabilizing Buttresses

2.1 Native Anatomy & Function

The greater and lesser tuberosities are critical anatomical landmarks that serve as insertion sites for the four rotator cuff muscles:

  • Greater Tuberosity (GT): Three facets receive the supraspinatus, infraspinatus, and teres minor—collectively acting as primary external rotators and dynamic superior/posterior stabilizers.[6]
  • Lesser Tuberosity (LT): Receives the subscapularis—the principal internal rotator and anterior stabilizer.[8]

These muscle-tendon-bone units function as force couples to produce rotation and compress/center the humeral head within the glenoid fossa during arm elevation.[7]

2.2 The Grammont Revolution and Its Limitations

The original Grammont-style RSA was revolutionary in its circumvention of the native biomechanical system. By medializing the glenohumeral center of rotation and distalizing the humerus, the RSA increases the lever arm of the deltoid muscle, transforming it into the primary elevator of the arm.[10]

This biomechanical ingenuity led to an early belief that the rotator cuff—and by extension the tuberosities—were obsolete in the setting of an RSA. Initial reports on RSA for fracture often overlooked tuberosity repair or even advocated for their excision.[14]

Critical Limitation Identified: While the deltoid can effectively power elevation and abduction, it cannot restore active rotation. Restoration of active external rotation is critically dependent on the healing of the GT with its attached infraspinatus/teres minor, and active internal rotation relies on the healing of the LT with its attached subscapularis.[1] Without these tendinous levers, patients are left with a functional pseudoparalysis in rotation, severely compromising activities of daily living.

2.3 Tuberosities as Dynamic Stabilizers & Buttresses

Beyond their role as rotational levers, healed tuberosities serve crucial secondary biomechanical functions:

  • Deltoid Wrapping: Anatomic reconstruction re-establishes the normal width and contour of the proximal humerus, improving deltoid wrapping, increasing resting tension, and improving efficiency throughout the arc of motion.[3]
  • Anteroposterior Stability: The healed GT (posteriorly) and LT (anteriorly) act as physical barriers that enhance rotational stability of the semi-constrained reverse articulation. This buttressing effect is a key factor in reducing postoperative dislocation risk.[18]
  • Implant Loading: Tuberosity non-union leads to a significant reduction in joint reaction forces, which may alter implant loading patterns and potentially contribute to long-term glenoid component loosening.[4]

3. Clinical Outcomes: Quantifying the Impact of Tuberosity Union vs. Non-Union

The biomechanical importance of tuberosity healing is substantiated by a large and growing body of clinical evidence. While RSA provides a reliable baseline of pain relief and function irrespective of tuberosity status, the functional ceiling is substantially higher when the tuberosities heal. Level I[1]

3.1 Range of Motion (ROM)

The most dramatic and consistent benefit of tuberosity healing is seen in the restoration of active range of motion, particularly rotation:

  • External Rotation (ER): Patients with healed tuberosities achieve a mean active ER of approximately 28°–31°, whereas non-union patients are left with severe deficits (8°–10°). The mean difference is consistently 15°–20°.[1,21] Level I
  • Forward Flexion: Healed groups demonstrate mean values exceeding 135° vs. ~112° in non-healed groups (mean difference 22°–24°).[20] Level I
  • Abduction: Healed: ~115° vs. Non-healed: ~95°.[20]
  • Internal Rotation: Also significantly better with tuberosity union, measured by vertebral level reachable.[2]

3.2 Functional Scores & Strength

  • Constant-Murley Score: Healed groups: 64–79 vs. Non-healed: 55–57. Mean difference can reach 24 points, far exceeding the minimal clinically important difference.[2,21] Level I
  • ASES Score: Healed: ~90 vs. Non-healed: ~69 (difference: +21.3).[1]
  • DASH Score: Healed: 22.1 vs. Non-healed: 37.6 (lower is better; difference: –15.5).[1]

3.3 Patient-Reported Outcome Measures (PROMs): An Important Nuance

Several large systematic reviews have found that while objective measures (ROM, Constant score) are clearly superior with tuberosity healing, patient-reported scores for pain (VAS) and overall function (ASES, SSV) are often not statistically different between healed and non-healed groups.[21] This underscores that RSA remains a valuable procedure with a high likelihood of patient satisfaction regardless of tuberosity healing status—a critical point for preoperative counseling. 3.5 Complications

Failure of tuberosity repair is not merely a functional issue but is associated with an increased rate of serious complications including instability/dislocation, infection, and component loosening.[1,11]

4. Preoperative Planning & Patient Considerations

4.1 Advanced Imaging for Fracture Characterization

Standard Radiographs

A trauma series (Grashey AP, scapular-Y, axillary lateral) is the foundational study for identifying fracture pattern and ruling out dislocation.[28] However, plain radiographs are notoriously unreliable for quantifying tuberosity displacement, particularly posterior GT displacement.[7] Level III

Computed Tomography (CT) with 3D Reconstructions

For any complex, displaced proximal humerus fracture being considered for surgical intervention, a CT scan with 3D reconstructions is now considered standard of care.[7] Key information provided:

  • Precise fracture morphology, comminution degree, tuberosity fragment size/shape/orientation
  • Articular integrity assessment (head-splitting patterns)
  • Bone stock quality and quantity evaluation
  • True multiplanar displacement measurement of tuberosities

Computer-Assisted Virtual Surgical Technology (CAVST) & 3D Printing

These technologies have transformed preoperative planning from passive assessment into active rehearsal:[29,30]

  • CAVST: Creates interactive 3D renderings allowing surgeons to simulate reduction, trial implant sizes/positions, and develop precise step-by-step plans.
  • 3D Printing: Produces 1:1 physical replicas providing haptic feedback and spatial understanding. Studies demonstrate significantly reduced operative time, blood loss, and fluoroscopy exposure.[29]
  • Patient-Specific Instrumentation (PSI): Custom drill guides for glenoid placement improve implantation accuracy.[34]
Paradigm Shift in Planning: The progression from 2D shadows → 3D maps → 4D rehearsals (3D + time/process) front-loads the most complex cognitive and technical challenges into the preoperative phase, converting a potentially chaotic procedure into a well-orchestrated execution of a pre-validated plan.

4.3 Surgical Timing

While some evidence suggests mid-term outcomes may be similar between acute and delayed RSA,[44] performing surgery within the first 3–4 weeks is generally preferred. Beyond this timeframe, hematoma organization, callus formation, and soft tissue contraction make fragment mobilization more challenging and can compromise the tenuous blood supply.[2,45]

5. Core Surgical Principles & Technical Execution

5.1 Surgical Approach & Exposure

The deltopectoral approach is the most commonly utilized, using the internervous plane between deltoid (axillary nerve) and pectoralis major (medial/lateral pectoral nerves).[10] Some surgeons advocate a superolateral deltoid-splitting approach for more direct GT access, though it carries higher axillary nerve injury risk.[45] Level III

5.2 Fragment Identification & Atraumatic Handling

Key principles for preserving fragment vascularity:[38,45,47]

  • Biceps Tendon Landmark: The long head of biceps runs in the intertubercular sulcus, separating GT and LT. It is typically tenodesed or tenotomized.[2]
  • Early Stay Sutures: Heavy, non-absorbable "tagging" sutures (#2 or #5 FiberWire/Ethibond) placed through the tendon-bone junction early in the procedure allow fragment manipulation without direct bone grasping.[38]
  • Tendon-Based Purchase: Pass sutures through the robust tendon substance at the insertion site rather than through osteoporotic bone, which can cause iatrogenic comminution.[38]
  • Limited Soft-Tissue Dissection: Mobilize fragments just enough for anatomic reduction without skeletonizing them—preserve blood supply from the ascending branch of anterior humeral circumflex artery and posterior humeral circumflex artery.[47]

5.4 Strategies for Stable Fixation in Osteoporotic Bone

  • Suture-Based Fixation: Heavy, non-absorbable braided sutures or suture tapes distribute forces over larger area, reducing "cut-out" risk. Purchase in rotator cuff tendon at insertion provides far stronger anchor than bone.[38]
  • Tensionable Constructs: Modern suture tape systems with tensioning devices apply measured, consistent tension before locking. Biomechanical studies confirm significantly higher initial fixation stability vs. manually knotted cerclage.[53] Level II
  • Biological Augmentation (Bone Graft): Cancellous bone from resected humeral head is osteoinductive and osteoconductive. Identified as a significant protective factor against non-union.[54] Level I
  • Cement Technique: Use distal cementation to prevent thermal necrosis at the repair site. The "black and tan" technique creates a buffer of impacted bone graft between cement and tuberosities.[1,3]

6. A Compendium of Tuberosity Fixation Techniques

6.1 Fundamental Suture Components

  • Horizontal Cerclage Sutures: Passed circumferentially around the prosthesis neck, weaving through tendon insertions. Act as a "hoop" compressing tuberosities medially against stem and against each other. Multiple sutures at different levels ensure broad compression.[17]
  • Vertical Tension-Band Sutures: Passed through drill holes in the humeral shaft distal to fracture, brought superiorly over tuberosities through tendon substance. Create a tension-band effect converting tensile forces into compressive forces, preventing proximal escape.[2]

6.3 Advanced Knotting Techniques

Biomechanical research demonstrates that double suture loop cerclage techniques are significantly stronger than single-stranded knots. The "Cow Hitch" and "Nice Knot" configurations have been shown to be the stiffest and strongest, providing superior resistance to loosening and displacement.[4]

6.4 Biological Augmentation: In-Depth

The "Horseshoe Graft" Technique

The resected humeral head is fashioned into a "U" shape, creating a trough that fits snugly around the lateral/posterior aspect of the prosthesis. This provides a large, congruent vital bony surface against which the GT can compress and heal—a bone-on-bone interface that is biologically superior to bone-on-metal. It also acts as a lateral buttress, preventing medial GT collapse.[47]

The "Black and Tan" Technique

This hybrid technique combines cementation and impaction grafting:[3,57,65]

  • Distal humeral canal is cemented for rigid axial/rotational stability
  • Proximal metaphysis is packed with morselized cancellous autograft from humeral head
  • Stem is impacted into the graft bed
  • Creates a "biologic transition zone" shielding tuberosities from cement exothermic heat
  • Reported union rate: 88%[65]

7.2 Neck-Shaft Angle (NSA) & Prosthesis Lateralization

  • NSA: The original Grammont 155° valgus design is associated with higher scapular notching risk and challenging tuberosity healing mechanics. A 135° varus NSA is associated with significantly higher tuberosity healing rates, creating a more stable platform with reduced shear stresses.[22,71] Level I
  • Onlay vs. Inlay: Onlay designs (socket on top of humeral neck cut) preserve metaphyseal bone stock and provide a better anatomic platform for tuberosity apposition. Inlay designs (recessed within metaphysis) require more bone removal.[4]
  • Global Lateralization: Increasing offset on humeral (onlay stem, lower NSA) and/or glenoid (lateralized baseplate, eccentric glenosphere) sides restores a more anatomic center of rotation, improves external rotation, reduces notching, and may enhance tuberosity healing.[4,73]

8.1 Modes of Tuberosity Failure

Pivotal Clinical Insight: A stable, fibrous non-union in anatomic position can yield functional outcomes not significantly different from solid bony union. In contrast, complete superior migration or resorption causes dramatically worse outcomes. Preventing displacement is paramount.[58]

8.2 Risk Factor Summary & Prevention Strategies

Modifiable (Surgeon-Controlled) Prevention Strategies:

  1. Biological Augmentation: Liberal use of autologous bone graft—most powerful preventative measure.[54]
  2. Implant Selection: Fracture-specific stem with 135° NSA.[19]
  3. Atraumatic Technique: Meticulous soft-tissue preservation for vascularity.
  4. Stable Fixation: Robust, multi-planar suture construct with tensionable systems.
  5. Cement Avoidance at Repair Site: Distal cementation or "black and tan" technique.[57]
  6. Supraspinatus Excision: Some advocate resecting supraspinatus from GT to reduce superior deforming forces without compromising outcomes.[11]

8.3 Salvage & Revision Strategies

  • Asymptomatic Non-Union/Malunion: Observation. Not all radiographic failures require re-intervention.[41]
  • Symptomatic Malunion with Impingement: Arthroscopic tuberoplasty or open osteotomy with revision fixation.[43]
  • Symptomatic Non-Union/Migration/Resorption: Revision fixation with bone grafting (autograft preferred, structural allograft if needed). End-stage cases may require allograft-prosthetic composite (APC) or proximal humeral replacement.[41,79]

9. Post-Operative Management & Rehabilitation

The rehabilitation protocol following RSA for fracture with tuberosity reconstruction is fundamentally different from elective RSA. The primary objective is strict protection of the bone-tendon-suture construct to allow undisturbed healing. An overly aggressive program is a common cause of fixation failure.[81]

Table 7: Evidence-Based Rehabilitation Protocol Phases
PhaseTimelineGoalsKey RestrictionsPermitted Activities
Phase 1: Strict Protection Weeks 0–6 Protect surgical repair; prevent stiffness NO active shoulder motion. Sling with abduction pillow at all times. PROM: elevation ≤90°, ER 0°–30° in scapular plane. Dislocation precautions (avoid extension beyond neutral, adduction+extension+IR). Hand/wrist/elbow AROM; pendulum (Codman's) exercises
Phase 2: Active-Assisted Motion Weeks 6–12 Gradual motion restoration No strengthening; no lifting >coffee cup. Gradual sling weaning. AAROM (pulley, dowel); gentle supine AROM progressing to upright; no resistance
Phase 3: Strengthening Weeks 12+ (radiographic healing confirmed) Restore strength and function Heavy lifting may be lifelong restriction (>20–25 lbs). Premature strengthening contraindicated. Isometrics → light resistance bands (high rep, low resistance); functional/work-specific training
Critical Warning: Strengthening exercises must not begin until there is clear radiographic evidence of tuberosity healing (typically ≥12 weeks). Premature strengthening is a primary cause of late tuberosity displacement.[81,82]

Expected Excellent Outcomes: Approximately 120° forward elevation and 20° external rotation. Full recovery may take 12+ months. Patients should be counseled that final ROM will likely be less than a native shoulder.[82]

10. Future Horizons & Emerging Technologies

10.1 3D Printing, PSI & Virtual Surgical Planning

  • Wider Adoption: As costs decrease and software improves, these tools will transition from complex/revision cases to routine preoperative workflow.[29]
  • AI Integration: Future CAVST systems will incorporate AI/ML to automatically segment fracture fragments, propose optimal reduction strategies, and recommend implant choices based on large outcome databases.[29]
  • Custom Implants: 3D-printed custom glenoid and humeral components (already used in tumor/revision surgery) could offer tailored solutions for severe bone loss not amenable to off-the-shelf implants.[95,96,97]

10.2 Biologics & Advanced Materials

  • Advanced Biological Augmentation: Future research may investigate concentrated growth factors (BMPs, PRP, mesenchymal stem cells) delivered via scaffold or injectable carrier to accelerate healing in high-risk patients.[41]
  • Bio-inductive Sutures: Sutures or tapes coated with factors promoting tendon-to-bone healing.
  • Bio-absorbable Polymers: Providing strong initial fixation before gradually being replaced by host tissue.

11. Landmark Studies Summary

Note: The source document synthesizes evidence from multiple systematic reviews and meta-analyses rather than presenting a discrete set of individual RCTs. The key evidence-generating studies are summarized below.

Systematic Review & Meta-Analysis: Tuberosity Healing Outcomes in RSA for Fracture

Multiple SRs/MAs (refs [1], [2], [20], [21]) • Level I

Consistently demonstrate statistically significant and clinically meaningful improvements in external rotation (+15°–20°), forward flexion (+22°–24°), Constant score (+10–24 points), and ASES score (+21 points) in healed vs. non-healed tuberosity cohorts. Patient-reported pain scores (VAS) often not significantly different, highlighting RSA's baseline efficacy for pain relief.

Meta-Analysis: Fracture-Dedicated Prostheses & GT Healing Rates

Frontiers in Surgery (ref [19]) • Level I

Confirmed that fracture-specific humeral stems with design features for tuberosity fixation are associated with significantly higher greater tuberosity healing rates compared to standard, non-fracture stems.

Systematic Review: Tuberosity Healing According to Humeral Inclination

OHSU Digital Collections (ref [22]) • Level I

Demonstrated that a 135° neck-shaft angle is associated with significantly higher rates of tuberosity healing compared to 155° designs, supporting the trend toward more anatomic varus prosthetic geometry.

Risk Factors for GT Non-Union: Systematic Review & Meta-Analysis

AOAO / Scholars @ UT Health San Antonio (refs [54,55,56]) • Level I

Identified autologous bone graft use as a significant protective factor against non-union. Four-part fracture pattern, diabetes, and nicotine use were identified as significant risk factors.

"7 Sutures and 8 Knots" Technique Clinical Series

PMC (refs [59,60]) • Level IV

Reported a tuberosity union rate of 87.9% using a simplified suture configuration of three sutures around the implant and five bridging sutures, demonstrating reliability of modern fixation techniques.

"Black and Tan" Technique Outcomes

ResearchGate / PubMed (refs [3,57,65]) • Level IV

Hybrid cement/impaction grafting technique demonstrated 88% tuberosity healing rate by shielding tuberosities from cement exothermic heat while providing the stability of cement distally with biology of impaction grafting proximally.

Biomechanical Study: Suture Tensioning Devices

J Shoulder Elbow Surg (ref [53]) • Level II

Confirmed that tensionable suture tape systems achieve significantly higher initial fixation stability and reduced rotational movement of tuberosities compared to manually knotted cerclage sutures.

Conclusions

The surgical treatment of acute, comminuted proximal humerus fractures in the elderly via RSA has evolved from a salvage procedure to a sophisticated reconstructive operation. Central to this evolution is the indisputable recognition of the critical importance of anatomic reduction and stable fixation of the tuberosities.

The evidence is clear: patients with healed tuberosities demonstrate dramatic improvements in external and internal rotation, enhanced forward flexion, and higher Constant scores. Furthermore, tuberosity healing enhances joint stability and is associated with lower rates of dislocation and implant loosening.

The Four Pillars of Success:
  1. Preoperative Planning is Paramount — CT with 3D reconstructions is mandatory; CAVST and 3D printing are powerful de-risking tools.
  2. Surgical Technique Must Be Atraumatic and Precise — Meticulous soft-tissue handling, tendon-based stay sutures, precise implant height/version.
  3. Fixation Must Create a Stable Bio-Mechanical Construct — Multi-planar suture constructs with tendon purchase, augmented with autologous bone graft, optimized by fracture-specific implants.
  4. Postoperative Rehabilitation Must Be Protective — 4–6 weeks strict immobilization; strengthening delayed until radiographic healing (≥12 weeks).

Tuberosity management is no longer an afterthought in fracture RSA—it is the central technical challenge and the primary determinant of superior functional outcome.
