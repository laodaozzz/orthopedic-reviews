Posterolateral Approach for Combined Posterior & Lateral Malleolar Fractures

Surgical Anatomy, Operative Strategy, and Precautions — Evidence-Based Interactive Summary

1. Executive Summary & Operative Rationale

The management of ankle fractures involving the posterior malleolus (PM) has undergone a paradigm shift — moving away from size-based indications toward a strategy focused on anatomical reduction and syndesmotic stability. The posterolateral (PL) approach is the cornerstone of this modern strategy.

1.1 Inadequacy of Traditional Fixation

Historically, PM fixation was indicated for fragments involving >25–35% of the distal tibial articular surface, typically managed with indirect reduction via ligamentotaxis and anterior-to-posterior (A-P) lag screws. Not specified

This approach is unreliable: ligamentotaxis often fails in subacute cases due to organized hematoma or early callus, and A-P screws placed without direct visualization frequently fail to achieve anatomical congruity or adequate compression. Level III

1.2 Rationale for Direct Posterior Visualization

The PL approach provides a direct, uncompromised view of the posterior fragment (most often posterolateral in morphology), allowing removal of intra-articular loose fragments, meticulous articular restoration, and stable fixation. Level III

Comparative studies demonstrate superior early functional outcomes (AOFAS scores) and fewer complications (e.g., post-traumatic arthritis) with the PL approach versus posteromedial. Level III [Ref 4]

🔑 Core Insight: Re-conceptualizing the "Trimalleolar" Fracture

The PM's significance extends beyond articular surface percentage — it is the bony attachment for the posterior syndesmosis (PITFL). A displaced PM fracture is, by definition, a syndesmotic injury. The PL approach simultaneously reconstructs the tibial plafond and restores posterior syndesmotic stability through a single incision. Level III [Refs 7, 10, 11]

2. Surgical Anatomy: Layer-by-Layer Dissection

2.1 Patient Positioning

Prone position — non-negotiable. Allows gravity retraction of posterior soft tissues and perpendicular access to the posterior tibia and fibula. Technique [Ref 11]

Landmarks: Lateral margin of Achilles tendon and posterior border of the fibula — reliably palpable even with significant swelling.

2.2 Incision

10–15 cm longitudinal incision. The AO describes it as "midway between fibula and Achilles tendon"; other descriptions place it more laterally along the posterior fibular border. Technique [Refs 8, 11]

2.3 The Sural Nerve: "Myth of the Safe Zone"

The sural nerve (providing sensation to the lateral foot) is the primary structure at risk. The short saphenous vein (SSV) runs in close proximity and guides identification. Level V [Ref 13]

⚠️ Critical Finding: Contradictory anatomical studies demonstrate that the sural nerve directly crosses the standard "midway" operative field in the majority of specimens. The concept of a "safe zone" based on external landmarks is obsolete. Level V

2.4 Deep Dissection: The Internervous Plane

The deep dissection exploits a true internervous plane: Technique [Ref 8]

  • Lateral: Peroneus longus & brevis (Superficial Peroneal Nerve)
  • Medial: Flexor Hallucis Longus (Tibial Nerve)

Steps to expose the PM (Volkmann fragment):

  1. Bluntly split areolar tissue, identify FHL belly on posterior tibia
  2. Elevate FHL subperiosteally from distal posterior tibia
  3. Retract FHL medially — acts as a "sling" protecting the posterior tibial neurovascular bundle
  4. Retract peroneal muscles laterally
  5. Exposes: posterolateral tibia (Volkmann fragment), fracture line, posterior syndesmosis

Lateral malleolus exposure: After PM fixation, reverse retraction — retract peroneals medially to expose the posterior fibular surface for plate application. Technique [Ref 11]

3. Operative Technique & Fixation Strategy

3.1 "Posterior-First" Sequence

The PM must be reduced and fixed before the lateral malleolus. Technique [Ref 17]

Rationale: The PM fragment restores articular congruity. Fixing the fibula first physically blocks direct PM reduction and obstructs fluoroscopic visualization of the articular surface.

Key Precaution: Preserve the PITFL attachment — this ligamentous "hinge" is vital for syndesmotic stability. Level III [Ref 8]

3.2 Biomechanical Fixation Constructs: "Dual Buttress"

  • Posterior Malleolus: Lag screws and/or a buttress plate (T-plate or 1/3 tubular plate) — resists proximal/posterior displacement of this shear fragment. Technique [Ref 17]
  • Lateral Malleolus: Posterior antiglide plate (1/3 tubular plate placed posterolaterally) — directly resists posterior/proximal displacement of the distal fibular fragment. Technique [Ref 18]

This "dual buttress" construct is more biomechanically robust than a standard lateral neutralization plate with A-P lag screws.

3.3 Syndesmotic Assessment

After fixing both PM and fibula, stress the syndesmosis under fluoroscopy. Because the PL approach directly repairs the PITFL's bony anchor, the syndesmosis is often rendered stable, potentially obviating trans-syndesmotic screws. Stress examination remains mandatory. Level III [Ref 10]

4. Critical Technique Choice: Single- vs. Two-Window Dissection

4.1 Definitions

  • Single-Window: All fixation (PM + fibula) through the single FHL/peroneal interval. Peroneals retracted laterally for PM, then medially for fibula. [Ref 11]
  • Two-Window: Two separate deep intervals — Window 1 (medial to peroneals) for PM, Window 2 (lateral to peroneals) for fibula. [Ref 12]

4.2 Comparative Outcomes

A 2023 retrospective study of 164 patients directly compared these techniques. Level III [Ref 12]

Vascular Mechanism: The two-window approach dissects on both sides of the peroneal tendons, isolating the skin flap and tendons on a tissue "bridge" with severed medial and lateral blood supply from peroneal artery perforators. This iatrogenic devascularization explains the 3× increase in wound necrosis. Anatomical rationale [Ref 20]

The single-window approach is not merely an alternative — it is the demonstrably superior and recommended technique to minimize soft-tissue morbidity.

5. Risks & Strategic Precautions

5.1 The Sural Nerve Paradox: 0% vs. 48%

The literature presents a stark contradiction in iatrogenic sural nerve injury rates:

Low-risk series:
0%
Simske et al. 2024:
48%

Level III [Refs 2, 24]

This discrepancy reflects a critical difference in surgical execution. The 48% rate is the default risk of blind dissection without formal nerve identification. The 0% rate is achieved by surgeons who mandate formal subcutaneous identification and protection.

⚡ Operative Assumption: Treat the 48% injury rate as your default. The only valid precaution is meticulous, formal identification of the sural nerve and SSV before incising the deep fascia. Should injury occur, 62% of patients may recover within 6 months. Level III [Ref 24]

5.2 Wound Dehiscence & Soft Tissue Necrosis

Rates vary widely: 1.5% to 44%, depending on technique and patient factors. Level III [Refs 21, 22, 23]

Independent risk factors (multivariate analysis): Level III [Ref 21]

  • Increased BMI (p = 0.028) Modifiable
  • Insulin-dependent Diabetes Mellitus (p = 0.003)
  • Staged fixation (p = 0.043)
  • Peripheral vascular disease, arterial hypertension, alcohol abuse

Surgical factor: Two-window technique increases wound complications to 32%. Level III [Ref 12]

6. Summary of Recommendations & Operative Pearls

  1. Re-Think the Injury: The PM is the bony anchor of the PITFL. Displaced PM = syndesmotic injury. The PL approach anatomically restores both the articular surface and posterior syndesmosis. [Ref 7]
  2. The Sural Nerve Is in the Incision: "Safe zones" are unreliable. The 48% iatrogenic injury rate is the penalty for blind dissection. Formal, subcutaneous nerve identification is mandatory. [Refs 13, 24]
  3. Embrace the Single-Window Dissection: The two-window technique carries a 3× higher wound complication rate (32% vs. 12%) and worse ROM. All fixation through the single FHL-peroneal interval. [Ref 12]
  4. Fix Posterior First: Reduce and fix the PM before the fibula. This ensures articular congruity and prevents the fibular plate from obstructing reduction or fluoroscopy. [Ref 17]
  5. Utilize "Dual Buttress" Constructs: Posterior buttress plate on tibia + posterior antiglide plate on fibula = most biomechanically stable fixation. [Refs 11, 17]
  6. Respect the FHL and Achilles: The 30% hallux flexion deficit and 11% Achilles shortening are iatrogenic results of aggressive retraction. Gentle handling and early mobilization are essential. [Refs 21, 22]
