1. Introduction: The Evolution of the Gold Standard

The management of end-stage ankle arthritis lies at the intersection of biomechanics, biology, and surgical precision. While total ankle arthroplasty (TAA) has experienced a resurgence with third- and fourth-generation implants, tibiotalar arthrodesis (ankle fusion) remains the definitive "gold standard" for a substantial demographic of patients. LOE II [1]

This designation reflects the procedure's enduring reliability in producing a pain-free, stable, and plantigrade foot capable of withstanding high-impact loading that would compromise prosthetic interfaces. The historical trajectory has moved from Charnley's external fixation era of the 1950s to the modern era of rigid internal fixation, driven by deeper understanding of bone-healing mechanics and the imperative to minimize soft-tissue morbidity. [2]

🔑 Clinical Decision Point

The contemporary surgeon must choose between the minimally invasive arthroscopic approach and traditional open approaches (anterior and transfibular). This decision is governed by three factors: severity of deformity, quality of the soft-tissue envelope, and specific patient demands. LOE II [2]

Arthroscopic fusion offers superior fusion rates, reduced hospital stays, and lower complication profiles compared to open techniques, primarily by preserving the periosteal blood supply essential for osteosynthesis. LOE II [4] The open approach remains indispensable for correcting rigid malalignment exceeding 15°, addressing massive bone loss, and utilizing autologous grafts (e.g., the fibula). LOE III [2]

2. Preoperative Planning & Pathomechanics

2.1 Clinical & Vascular Assessment

The primary indication is intractable pain localized to the tibiotalar joint, unresponsive to non-operative modalities (AFO bracing, NSAIDs, intra-articular injections). LOE II [1] A critical diagnostic step is differentiating true ankle joint pain from subtalar or talonavicular pathology — selective diagnostic injections are invaluable; fusing the ankle in a patient with primary subtalar arthritis results in surgical failure. [8]

The vascular status of the limb dictates the surgical approach. The anterior incision relies on angiosomes supplied by the anterior tibial artery; the transfibular approach depends on peroneal and perforating branches. In patients with compromised perfusion (diabetes, PVD), the soft-tissue preservation of the arthroscopic approach is often the deciding factor. LOE III [2]

2.2 Radiographic Analysis & Deformity Mapping

Standard weight-bearing radiographs (AP, lateral, mortise) are the baseline. The Saltzman view is particularly high-yield for determining the relationship of the calcaneus to the tibial axis, guiding coronal-plane correction. LOE III [11]

Advanced imaging (CT) is routine for:

  • Bone stock quality: Evaluating subchondral cyst depth (may require grafting) [1]
  • Deformity magnitude: Arthroscopic fusion generally contraindicated for deformities >15° varus/valgus LOE III [3]
  • Adjacent joint arthritis: If present, a TTC or pantalar fusion may be indicated instead [7]

3. The Biologic Basis of Union: Joint Preparation Principles

Surgical induction of a non-union (a joint) into a union requires fundamental alteration of the biologic environment. The widely accepted principle is "biology over mechanics" — even the most rigid fixation will fail if the bone surfaces are not biologically primed. [13]

3.1 Cartilage Removal

Total removal of hyaline cartilage is the minimum requirement. Successful fusion depends on breaching the calcified cartilage layer, which acts as a barrier to vascular ingress. Techniques include curettes and osteotomes ("cold" preparation) and high-speed burrs (4.0 mm or 5.5 mm), the latter carrying risk of thermal necrosis. Constant irrigation is mandatory when using power instruments. LOE IV [13]

3.2 "Shingling" vs. Drilling

Once subchondral bone is exposed, surface area must be maximized to recruit marrow-derived mesenchymal stem cells (MSCs):

  • Fenestration (Drilling): 2.0–2.5 mm drill bit creates multiple perforations, channeling the medullary canal to allow egress of growth factors and progenitor cells. [8,9]
  • Shingling (Fish-Scaling): A small curved osteotome creates cortical flaps ("scales"), producing a 3D topography that resists shear forces. Biomechanically increases the coefficient of friction more effectively than drilling alone. LOE IV [15]

3.3 Grafting Strategies

  • Autograft: Gold standard — distal tibia, resected fibula, or iliac crest.
  • GraftNet / Suction Trap: Inline suction filter captures bone dust and paste during arthroscopic burring; paste is rich in viable osteoblasts and can be re-injected, eliminating donor-site morbidity. LOE IV [9]
  • Fibular Strut (Transfibular): Resected fibula can be cleaned, split, and used as an onlay graft for both biological induction and structural stability. [17]

4. Arthroscopic Ankle Arthrodesis (AAA): Technique & Nuance

4.1 Setup & Portal Placement

Patient supine with thigh tourniquet. A non-invasive distractor is preferred over invasive pin distraction to avoid pin-tract infections. LOE III [3]

  • Anteromedial (AM) Portal: Primary working portal, medial to tibialis anterior tendon. "Nick and spread" technique with hemostat protects saphenous nerve/vein. [14]
  • Anterolateral (AL) Portal: Lateral to peroneus tertius. Superficial peroneal nerve at risk — transillumination recommended. [14]
⚠️ Pearl — Dorsiflexion Safety

The ankle should be held in dorsiflexion during portal creation and instrument introduction. This tensions the capsule and moves the anterior neurovascular bundle away from the joint line, minimizing iatrogenic injury. [18]

4.2 Step-by-Step Joint Preparation

  1. Soft-Tissue Resection: 4.5 mm shaver to clear anterior synovitis. Visualize medial and lateral gutters. [14]
  2. Osteophyte Clearance: Large anterior tibial osteophytes resected first (they block dorsiflexion to neutral). [14]
  3. Cartilage Denudation: Systematic removal from tibia and talus, maintaining the curvature of the talar dome (concentric fusion). [14]
  4. Posterior Reach: Common pitfall — inadequate preparation of the posterior 25%. Use curved curettes and swap portals (viewing lateral while working medial). [14]
  5. Microfracture: Subchondral plate fenestrated with awl or drill to ensure bleeding bone. [9]

4.3 Fixation Strategy

Fixation is typically percutaneous with guide pins under fluoroscopic control. Configuration: usually two or three 6.5/7.3 mm cannulated screws. Distraction must be released before tightening to allow bone surfaces to contact. LOE III [3]

5. Open Anterior Approach

The anterior approach is the workhorse for moderate deformities or when anterior hardware must be removed. [10]

5.1 Surgical Interval

A 10–15 cm longitudinal incision centered over the ankle, using the interval between Tibialis Anterior (TA) and Extensor Hallucis Longus (EHL). The neurovascular bundle lies deep to the EHL — identify and retract laterally. The TA sheath should be preserved as a vascular buffer. LOE IV [10]

5.2 Deformity Correction & Fixation

Unlike arthroscopy, the open approach allows an oscillating saw to create flat, broad cancellous surfaces — particularly useful for correcting varus/valgus deformities with asymmetric wedge cuts. LOE III [2]

Anterior plating systems (e.g., Zimmer Ankle Fix Plus, Paragon28 Silverback) act as a tension band against plantarflexion forces and allow multiple fixation points. Superior in osteoporotic bone where screw heads may subside. [11,20]

6. Open Transfibular Approach

The procedure of choice for severe valgus deformities, significant lateral bone loss, or compromised anterior soft-tissue envelope. [17]

7. Advanced Fixation Mechanics

7.1 The "Home Run" Screw

The posterior-to-anterior screw is the most biomechanically important screw in the construct, engaging the dense bone of the talar neck and directly counteracting the anterior subluxation force of the Achilles tendon. LOE IV [22]

🚨 Safety Data — Cadaveric Study [22]

Quantified risks of percutaneous "home run" screw placement:

  • Achilles tendon: 5.35 mm average clearance from guide pin
  • Peroneal tendons: 9.65 mm clearance
  • Sural nerve: Contact with pin in 45.5% of specimens; impaled in 27.3%

Implication: Blind percutaneous placement is dangerous. A mini-open incision to visualize the starting point and protect the sural nerve is strongly recommended.

7.2 Screw Configuration

  • Crossed Screws: Standard (one medial, one lateral) — provides torsion resistance.
  • Parallel Screws: Better compression, less rotational stability.
  • Triangular Configuration (3 screws): Biomechanically superior — medial, lateral, and posterior "home run." LOE IV [9]

7.3 Intramedullary Fixation

For poor bone stock or revision cases. Curved nail systems (e.g., Shotel nail) allow insertion through a medial talar entry point, curving into the tibia — avoiding the plantar incision required for traditional retrograde (TTC) nails, preserving the subtalar joint and plantar fat pad. [12]

📌 Mechanism of Arthroscopic Superiority

Open surgery requires extensive periosteal stripping, devascularizing bone ends. Arthroscopy leaves the periosteal blood supply intact, maintaining osteogenic potential at the fusion site. LOE II [26]

⚠️ Selection Bias Caveat: Open techniques are often reserved for "worse" ankles with massive deformity (>20°). Therefore, lower open fusion rates may partly reflect pathology complexity rather than technique inferiority. However, for ankles with <15° deformity, arthroscopy is unequivocally the safer and more effective choice. [4]

10.2 Long-Term Implications

Successful fusion creates a rigid lever arm. While pain is reliably relieved, mechanical stress transfers to the subtalar and talonavicular joints.

  • Adjacent Segment Disease: Radiographic subtalar arthritis is nearly universal at 10–20 years, though clinical symptoms vary. [7]
  • Position Matters: Optimal alignment (neutral dorsiflexion, slight valgus) minimizes stress transfer. Ankle varus is particularly destructive to the lateral column and subtalar joint. LOE III [7]

11. Conclusion

The modern landscape of ankle arthrodesis is defined by a trend toward biological preservation. The arthroscopic technique has proven its superiority for the majority of primary osteoarthritis cases, offering higher fusion rates and fewer complications by respecting the soft-tissue envelope. It is the procedure of choice for patients with minimal to moderate deformity.

However, open approaches are not obsolete — they are specialized tools for complex reconstruction. The anterior approach serves for hardware removal and plate fixation; the transfibular approach remains the salvage solution for severe valgus deformity and bone loss.

🎯 Fundamental Principles of Arthrodesis

Success relies less on the incision and more on adherence to:

  • Meticulous removal of calcified cartilage
  • Maximization of bone contact (shingling / drilling)
  • Rigid compression
  • Restoration of the mechanical axis
