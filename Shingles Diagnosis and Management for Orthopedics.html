Clinical Practice Guidelines for Herpes Zoster Diagnosis and Management in Orthopedic Surgery (2024-2025)

An Evidence-Based Educational Summary for Orthopedic Surgeons

Level I-V Evidence Synthesis Source: 2024-2025 CPGs & Literature Target Audience: Senior Residents & Clinicians

Executive Summary

Herpes Zoster (HZ), caused by reactivation of the Varicella-Zoster Virus (VZV), presents significant diagnostic and management challenges within orthopedic surgery. The demographic overlap between HZ-susceptible adults (≥50 years with immunosenescence) and those requiring orthopedic intervention for degenerative conditions is substantial. Surgical stress acts as a potent trigger for viral reactivation, creating unique perioperative considerations.

Key Orthopedic Implications: HZ mimics common pathologies like radiculopathy and rotator cuff tears. Elective arthroplasty during active viremia is contraindicated due to infection risks. Corticosteroid injections may trigger reactivation. Vaccination (Shingrix) represents a critical preventive strategy.

1. Epidemiology & Burden of Disease

The intersection of VZV and orthopedics is defined by converging at-risk populations. Immunosenescence increases HZ risk after age 50, coinciding with peak incidence of osteoarthritis and spinal stenosis. (Level III Evidence)Based on cohort studies and meta-analyses of epidemiological data (References 1, 4, 9).

1.1 Risk Factors in Orthopedic Populations

  • Chronic Inflammatory States: Patients with Rheumatoid Arthritis (RA), Psoriatic Arthritis (PsA), and SLE have elevated baseline HZ risk due to autoimmune pathology and immunosuppressive therapies (Level II Evidence)Systematic reviews and meta-analyses (References 4, 5, 6).
  • Surgical Stress: Major orthopedic procedures (TKA, THA) induce systemic immunosuppression via HPA axis activation. TKA carries a hazard ratio of 1.223 for HZ development, with highest risk in females aged 50-64 (HR=1.543) (Level II Evidence)Multicenter propensity-score-matched cohort study (Reference 9).
Clinical Pearl: Postoperative pain that is disproportionate, neuropathic in quality, or anatomically distinct from the surgical site may represent HZ reactivation rather than surgical failure or CRPS.

1.2 Economic and Functional Impact

Post-Herpetic Neuralgia (PHN)—pain persisting >3 months post-rash—can catastrophically compromise surgical outcomes and rehabilitation potential, particularly following TKA. In value-based care models, preventing HZ is integral to maximizing PROMs success.

2. Pathophysiology: Latency and Reactivation

VZV is neurotropic, establishing latency in dorsal root ganglia (DRG) following primary infection (chickenpox). Reactivation occurs when cell-mediated immunity falls below a critical threshold, often triggered by surgical stress ("Second Hit" hypothesis).

2.1 Orthopedic Implications of Pathophysiology

  1. Prodromal Phase: Viral replication and ganglionitis cause dermatomal pain before rash appearance (1-5 day window), leading to highest diagnostic error in orthopedics (Level III Evidence)Case reports and clinical observations (References 3, 14).
  2. Anatomical Mimicry: Because latent virus resides in DRG—the same structure implicated in radiculopathy—reactivation produces symptoms mimicking nerve root compression
  3. Second Hit Hypothesis: Surgical stress (anesthesia, trauma, blood loss) induces transient immune paralysis, allowing VZV to escape latency (Level IV Evidence)Pathophysiological rationale and case series (References 7, 8, 9).

3. Comprehensive Differential Diagnosis

The cardinal feature of HZ is dermatomal pain, which frequently mimics orthopedic pathology due to overlapping segmental innervation patterns.

3.1 Zoster Sine Herpete vs. Spinal Radiculopathy

Zoster sine herpete (ZSH) describes VZV reactivation with neuralgia but without rash, creating "ghost" pathology diagnostic confusion.

3.2 Cervical Zoster vs. Rotator Cuff Pathology

Reactivation in C5/C6 dermatome presents as acute shoulder pain mimicking rotator cuff tears. Key differentiators include history of prodromal malaise, preserved passive ROM (unless severe guarding), and skin examination for vesicles in axilla/upper back.

3.3 Thoracic Zoster vs. Costochondritis

Thoracic zoster (most common presentation) frequently mimics costochondritis. Differentiation involves the "Crowing Rooster" maneuver (negative in HZ) and distribution pattern (hemigirdle pain respecting midline in HZ vs. pinpoint tenderness at costochondral junctions).

3.4 Lumbar Zoster vs. Hip/Knee Pathology

L2-L4 zoster radiates to anterior thigh/knee, mimicking hip arthritis or intra-articular knee pathology. Normal hip/knee exams with burning anterior thigh/knee pain should prompt evaluation for HZ.

4. Diagnostic Evaluation

While primarily clinical, diagnostic adjuncts are crucial for atypical presentations like ZSH.

4.1 Gold Standard: Polymerase Chain Reaction (PCR)

  • Lesion Swab: Definitive test with high sensitivity/specificity for VZV DNA
  • Zoster Sine Herpete: Saliva PCR (emerging evidence) or CSF analysis (lymphocytic pleocytosis, elevated protein, positive VZV DNA/IgG) (Level III-IV Evidence)Case reports and diagnostic studies (References 14, 17, 27).

4.2 Limited Utility of Serology

IgG seropositivity (most adults >50) indicates past exposure only. IgM has low sensitivity/specificity and lags symptom onset. Do not rely on serology to rule out HZ.

4.3 Imaging: Rule-Out Role

MRI Spine expected unremarkable in HZ/ZSH. "Mismatch" between severe agony and benign MRI is strong heuristic for neuropathic/viral pathology.

5. Pharmacological Management (2024-2025)

Management pillars: halting viral replication to limit neural damage and aggressive neuropathic pain management to prevent central sensitization.

5.1 Antiviral Therapy

The 72-Hour Rule: Standard of care is to initiate antivirals within 72 hours of rash onset. Treatment still indicated after 72 hours if: 1) New lesions appear, 2) Complications present (ophthalmic/motor zoster), 3) Patient is immunocompromised. (Level I Evidence)Based on clinical practice guidelines (References 1, 30, 32).

5.2 Pain Management Algorithms

Acute Phase (Days 0-30)

  1. Antivirals: Most effective "analgesic" in acute phase by limiting neural destruction
  2. Gabapentinoids: Gabapentin 300mg daily titrated to 900-1800mg/day; Pregabalin 75-150mg BID
  3. Tricyclic Antidepressants: Nortriptyline/Amitriptyline 10-25mg at bedtime (use cautiously in elderly)
  4. Analgesics: Acetaminophen/NSAIDs for inflammatory component; opioids reserved for severe breakthrough

Post-Herpetic Neuralgia (PHN) Management

  • Topical Therapy: Lidocaine 5% patch (safe, minimal systemic absorption); Capsaicin 8% patch (Qutenza)
  • Systemic Therapy: Optimize Gabapentinoids/TCAs; consider SNRI (Duloxetine) if TCAs contraindicated
  • Interventional: Pulsed Radiofrequency (PRF) of dorsal root ganglion for refractory PHN (Level II Evidence)Systematic reviews of interventional treatments (References 12, 41).

6. Perioperative Management Protocols

6.1 Preoperative Screening

  • Ask patients ≥50 about HZ history and vaccination status
  • Recent HZ (within 6 months): Evaluate for PHN to establish neurological baseline
  • Rheumatologic patients on JAK inhibitors/biologics: Collaborate with rheumatology; monitor for prodromal symptoms

6.2 Cancellation and Delay Guidelines

Contraindication: Performing elective arthroplasty during active HZ is contraindicated due to:
1. Infectious risk (VZV shedding)
2. Bacterial superinfection risk (SSI/PJI)
3. Increased cardiovascular risk (Level II-III Evidence)Based on CDC guidelines, case reports, and risk analyses (References 8, 45, 58).

Surgical Delay Protocol

1
Minimum Delay: Postpone until all lesions have crusted over (typically 2-4 weeks)
2
Optimal Delay: Wait until acute pain phase resolves (typically 4-6 weeks) or even 2 months for full immune reconstitution and rehabilitation tolerance
3
Post-operative "Surprise" Zoster: Isolate (contact precautions), treat with Valacyclovir immediately, differentiate from allergic dermatitis/bacterial cellulitis

7. The Corticosteroid Conundrum

7.1 Corticosteroids as a Trigger

Systemic or local steroids suppress T-cell function, potentially triggering VZV reactivation. Epidural Steroid Injections (ESI) show low absolute risk (0.07%) but temporal relationship (median onset 15-18 days post-injection) supports causal link. Intra-articular injections also report cases of reactivation.

Recommendation: Counsel patients ≥50 about this small risk. Avoid elective injections in acutely ill, highly stressed patients, or those with prodromal symptoms.

7.2 Systemic Steroids for Acute Zoster

Combined with acyclovir, systemic steroids can moderately reduce acute pain intensity but do not prevent PHN development. Consider taper only for immunocompetent elderly with severe acute pain; avoid in osteoporosis, diabetes, or hypertension.

8. Vaccination Guidelines

Recombinant Zoster Vaccine (RZV, Shingrix) is >97% effective in adults 50-69; >91% in adults >70. Recommended for all immunocompetent adults ≥50 and immunocompromised adults ≥19.

8.1 Perioperative Timing

  • Pre-operative: Ideal to boost immunity prior to surgical immunosuppression. Schedule two doses, 2-6 months apart. First dose at least 2 weeks prior to surgery likely safe.
  • Post-operative: Resume/initiate once recovered from acute surgical phase (typically 4-6 weeks post-op)
  • History of Shingles: Still vaccinate to prevent recurrence; wait 2 months post-infection (Level I-II Evidence)CDC guidelines and rheumatology recommendations (References 4, 29, 46).

8.2 Special Populations: Rheumatic Diseases

RZV is safe (non-live vaccine) in rheumatic patients. Ideally vaccinate before starting biologic therapies. Safe to give while on low-dose methotrexate or prednisone (<20mg).

9. Complications and Long-Term Sequelae

9.1 Post-Herpetic Neuralgia (PHN)

Primary cause of chronic morbidity involving peripheral sensitization and central structural changes. Chronic neuropathic pain complicates surgical outcome assessment, potentially yielding poor PROMs despite mechanically perfect TKA.

9.2 Motor Zoster (Segmental Zoster Paresis)

Incidence 3-5% of HZ cases. Causes lower motor neuron weakness in myotome corresponding to dermatome. Prognosis good: 75% experience significant recovery within 12-18 months. Recognizing this prevents unnecessary decompression surgery.

9.3 Herpes Zoster Ophthalmicus (HZO)

Involvement of V1 trigeminal branch. Hutchinson's Sign (vesicles on nose tip) predicts ocular involvement. Requires immediate ophthalmology referral to prevent corneal scarring/blindness.

Conclusion

Herpes Zoster represents a formidable mimic and potent complication in orthopedic surgery. The 2024-2025 evidence base reinforces diagnostic vigilance and early pharmacological intervention. Orthopedic surgeons must:

  1. Delay: Postpone elective arthroplasty during active HZ
  2. Vaccinate: Ensure patients ≥50 receive Shingrix during preoperative optimization
  3. Treat: Initiate antivirals immediately upon suspicion, even without confirmed rash if clinical picture compelling
  4. Protect: Recognize corticosteroid risks and use judiciously in at-risk populations
