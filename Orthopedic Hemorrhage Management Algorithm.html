1. Introduction: The Pathophysiological Landscape of Orthopedic Hemorrhage

The management of intraoperative hemorrhage in orthopedic surgery has undergone a paradigm shift over the last decade, transitioning from a reactive model of volume replacement to a proactive, multimodal discipline known as Patient Blood Management (PBM). Uncontrolled bleeding remains one of the most significant modifiable risk factors in orthopedic surgery, directly influencing perioperative morbidity, mortality, infection rates, and functional outcomes. I

The unique challenge in orthopedics lies in the dual nature of the surgical field, which encompasses both soft tissues with collapsible vessels and osseous structures where bleeding occurs from rigid, non-collapsible Haversian canals and the trabecular meshwork. Orthopedic procedures, ranging from elective arthroplasty to high-energy trauma stabilization, inherently carry a high risk of blood loss.

In major orthopedic trauma, hemorrhage is a primary driver of the "lethal triad"—hypothermia, acidosis, and coagulopathy—which perpetuates a vicious cycle of metabolic failure. II In elective settings (spine deformity correction, revision arthroplasty), excessive bleeding obscures the surgical field, extends operative time, and necessitates allogeneic blood transfusions, which are associated with immunomodulation (TRIM), increased surgical site infections (SSI), and longer hospital stays. III

🎯 Core Principle This report presents a comprehensive, stepwise management algorithm structured around a strict Hierarchy of Hemostasis, prioritizing interventions based on invasiveness, mechanism of action, and cost-effectiveness — from systemic optimization and mechanical containment to advanced topical hemostats and damage control interventions.

1.1 The Distinct Physiology of Osseous versus Soft Tissue Bleeding

Understanding the origin of hemorrhage is the prerequisite for effective management. Soft tissue bleeding typically originates from capillaries, veins, and arteries within a collagenous matrix that allows for vessel retraction and vasoconstriction—the body's natural primary hemostatic response. In contrast, osseous bleeding presents a fundamentally different challenge. II

Osseous bleeding is driven by the intramedullary pressure and the rich vascularity of cancellous bone. Because the vessels cannot retract, natural hemostasis is delayed. Furthermore, the exposed marrow elements contain tissue factor and fat, which can enter the systemic circulation, contributing to fat embolism syndrome—a unique complication of orthopedic hemorrhage. II

2. Preoperative Optimization and Systemic Risk Stratification

The first step in the hemostatic algorithm occurs well before the incision. Preoperative optimization serves as the foundation of the hemostasis hierarchy. The goal is to maximize the patient's physiological reserve (red cell mass) and minimize their bleeding diathesis.

2.1 Anemia Management and Erythropoiesis

Preoperative anemia is a robust predictor of transfusion requirements and postoperative complications. Guidelines recommend screening for anemia 3–4 weeks prior to elective surgery. Patients with hemoglobin levels <13 g/dL should be evaluated for iron deficiency, vitamin B12/folate deficiency, or chronic disease anemia. Intravenous (IV) iron supplementation and, in select cases, erythropoietin-stimulating agents (ESA) can significantly boost red cell mass, providing a buffer against surgical loss. II

2.2 Management of Anticoagulation and Antiplatelet Therapy

The increasing prevalence of cardiovascular comorbidities in the orthopedic population necessitates a nuanced approach to antithrombotic management. The surgeon must balance the risk of thromboembolism (stent thrombosis, stroke) against the risk of catastrophic surgical site bleeding (e.g., spinal epidural hematoma). III

Bridging is reserved only for patients at the highest thrombotic risk: mechanical mitral valves, atrial fibrillation with CHADS₂-VASc score >5, or VTE within the last 3 months. III II

2.3 Pharmacological Hemostasis: The Role of Tranexamic Acid (TXA)

Tranexamic acid has become the standard of care in major orthopedic surgery. It is a synthetic derivative of the amino acid lysine that exerts its antifibrinolytic effect by blocking the lysine-binding sites on plasminogen. This prevents plasminogen from binding to fibrin and being converted to plasmin, thereby stabilizing the clot and inhibiting fibrinolysis. I

Efficacy and Safety

Multiple meta-analyses and high-quality RCTs confirm that TXA reduces total blood loss and transfusion requirements by approximately 30–50% in total joint arthroplasty (TJA), spine surgery, and hip fracture surgery. Crucially, despite its antifibrinolytic mechanism, TXA does not increase the risk of venous thromboembolism (DVT/PE), myocardial infarction, or stroke in routine orthopedic populations. I

3. Level I: Mechanical and Physical Hemostasis Techniques

The intraoperative management algorithm begins with mechanical control. These techniques rely on physical forces to occlude vessels or reduce blood flow to the surgical site.

3.1 Tourniquets: Physiology and Protocols

Tourniquets are widely used in extremity surgery to create a bloodless operative field. However, their use is a double-edged sword; while they reduce intraoperative bleeding, they induce ischemia-reperfusion injury and may increase postoperative pain and swelling. III

OPTIMIZED TOURNIQUET PROTOCOL
1 Cuff Application: Use a wide, contoured cuff. Pad the limb with stockinette or cast padding to prevent skin shear and chemical burns from prep solutions wicking under the cuff.
2 Exsanguination: Use an Esmarch bandage or elevation for 5 minutes prior to inflation. This autotransfuses blood into the central circulation and minimizes venous engorgement.
3 Inflation Pressure:
• Upper Extremity: 50 mmHg above SBP
• Lower Extremity: 100 mmHg above SBP, or preferably set based on Limb Occlusion Pressure (LOP) measurement
4 Time Limits: Safe continuous inflation limit is 90–120 minutes. If surgery exceeds 2 hours, a reperfusion interval of 10–15 minutes is recommended every subsequent hour to allow washout of acidic metabolites.
5 Deflation Protocol: In TKA, some surgeons release prior to closure for identification of major bleeders. Evidence suggests no significant difference in total calculated blood loss between early and late release strategies. In bilateral procedures, stagger deflations by 30–45 minutes to prevent precipitous hypotension.

Sources: Ref. [20, 22, 23, 24]

3.2 Patient Positioning

Gravity is a simple yet effective hemostatic tool. N/S

  • Spine Surgery: The use of a Jackson table or similar frame allows the abdomen to hang freely. This reduces intra-abdominal pressure → lowers pressure in the inferior vena cava and the valveless Batson's epidural venous plexus → significantly reduces operative bleeding.
  • Limb Surgery: Elevation of the operative site above heart level reduces arterial hydrostatic pressure and promotes venous drainage.

3.3 Bone Wax and Mechanical Barriers for Osseous Bleeding

For cancellous bone bleeding that cannot be ligated, mechanical obstruction of the Haversian canals is the gold standard. III

🚫 Critical Warning: Bone Wax Bone wax is strictly contraindicated in fracture gaps or fusion beds (e.g., spinal fusion, non-union repair). It is non-absorbable and inhibits osteogenesis, acting as a physical barrier to bone healing. It can also incite a foreign body granulomatous reaction and serves as a nidus for infection. II

4. Level II: Thermal and Energy-Based Hemostasis

When mechanical measures are insufficient for soft tissue bleeding, thermal coagulation is the next tier in the hierarchy.

4.1 Electrocautery (Monopolar and Bipolar)

  • Monopolar Cautery: Standard for dissection and hemostasis of vessels <2 mm. Current flows through the patient to a grounding pad. Generates temperatures >400°C with significant lateral thermal spread — risky near nerves. III
  • Bipolar Cautery: Current confined between the two tips of the forceps. Minimizes thermal spread; eliminates grounding pad requirement. Modality of choice in spine surgery (near dura/cord) and hand surgery (near digital nerves). Saline irrigation helps prevent sticking and charring. III

4.2 Advanced Bipolar Sealers (e.g., Aquamantys)

This technology combines radiofrequency (RF) energy with simultaneous saline irrigation. II

  • Mechanism: The saline conducts RF energy and cools the tissue, maintaining temperature at approximately 100°C. This prevents charring and smoke while shrinking collagen in vessel walls to create a seal.
  • Indications: Highly effective for diffuse, low-pressure soft tissue oozing (e.g., epidural venous plexus in spine surgery, extensive soft tissue releases in TKA and THA).
  • Evidence: Studies have shown reduced blood loss and transfusion rates with use in arthroplasty. II

5. Level III: The Hierarchy of Topical Hemostatic Agents

When mechanical and thermal methods fail, or when the bleeding source is inaccessible or diffuse (e.g., epidural bleeding, large cancellous surfaces), topical hemostatic agents are indicated. These are classified into passive, active, and flowable agents. III

5.1 Passive Hemostats

Gelatin Sponges (Gelfoam)

Porous, pliable sponges made from purified pork skin gelatin. They absorb up to 45 times their weight in blood and provide a scaffold for clot formation. They are often soaked in thrombin for enhanced effect. III

⚠️ Warning — Gelfoam Swelling Gelfoam swells significantly and must be removed from laminectomy sites to prevent spinal cord compression.

Oxidized Regenerated Cellulose (Surgicel)

Derived from plant cellulose. Lowers local pH, causing red cell lysis and formation of an acidic hematin pseudoclot. Bacteriostatic due to low pH. III

🚫 Crucial Note The acidity of Surgicel inactivates thrombin; therefore, Surgicel should not be used in conjunction with thrombin or Floseal. It also swells and has been associated with neurotoxicity if left directly on nerves; removal after hemostasis is recommended.

5.2 Flowable Hemostats: The Orthopedic Workhorse

Flowable hemostats (e.g., Floseal, Surgiflo) represent a significant advancement for orthopedic applications, particularly for bleeding from irregular cancellous bone surfaces or the epidural space. II

  • Composition: A matrix of cross-linked bovine gelatin granules mixed with human or recombinant thrombin.
  • Mechanism: Gelatin granules swell upon contact with blood, creating a mechanical tamponade (the "sandbag" effect), while thrombin actively converts the patient's fibrinogen to fibrin at the granule surface.
FLOWABLE HEMOSTAT APPLICATION TECHNIQUE
1 Identify the source of bleeding.
2 Apply the matrix directly to the source using the applicator tip. Do not inject into the vessel.
3 Compromise: Hold a wet pattie or sponge over the matrix for 2 minutes to ensure contact and prevent washout.
4 Irrigate: Once hemostasis is achieved, gently irrigate excess matrix away. The clot is integrated into tissue; excess granules are not.
⚠️ Floseal Swelling — Use with Caution Floseal swells by 10–20% within 10 minutes. While less expansive than pure Gelfoam, it should be used with caution in tight bony confines (e.g., neuroforamina). Contraindicated in cell salvage systems (unless specialized filters are used) as gelatin particles can clog filters or pass into the reinfusion line. II

5.3 Emerging Bio-inspired and Nanotechnologies

As we move into 2025 and beyond, hemostats are evolving to overcome the limitations of animal-derived products. IV

  • Self-Assembling Peptides (SAPs): Synthetic peptides (e.g., RADA16, SPG-178) that self-assemble into nanofiber networks upon contact with physiological salts in blood. They form a transparent hydrogel barrier. Non-immunogenic, transparent, and degrade into amino acids.
  • Microporous Polysaccharide Hemospheres (Arista AH): Plant-based starch spheres acting as molecular sieves. Rapidly absorb water from blood, concentrating platelets and clotting factors on their surface. Fully absorbed within 24–48 hours. Zero viral transmission risk.

6. Specific Surgical Management Protocols

6.1 Orthopedic Trauma: Damage Control for Pelvic Hemorrhage

Pelvic ring disruptions with hemodynamic instability are among the most lethal injuries in orthopedics. The bleeding is predominantly venous (presacral plexus, 80–90%) or from cancellous bone surfaces. II

STEPWISE PELVIC HEMORRHAGE CONTROL PROTOCOL
1 Mechanical Stabilization (Pelvic Binder): Immediately apply centered over the greater trochanters. Reduces pelvic volume, realigns fracture surfaces, and creates tamponade on venous bleeding.
⬇
2 Assess Stability: If hemodynamically unstable (SBP < 90 mmHg, lactate > 4 mmol/L) despite resuscitation ("Non-responder"), proceed immediately to OR for Damage Control Surgery.
⬇
3 Pre-peritoneal Pelvic Packing (PPP):
• Midline vertical incision (8 cm) from pubic symphysis upwards
• Dissect through linea alba — do NOT open peritoneum
• Bluntly dissect into Space of Retzius, move laterally toward SI joints
• Place 3 large laparotomy pads deep into true pelvis bilaterally
• Compresses presacral venous plexus directly
⬇
4 Angiography: If instability persists after packing, arterial injury is suspected (e.g., superior gluteal artery). Transfer to angio suite for embolization (coils/foam).

Sources: Ref. [46, 47, 48]

6.2 Extremity Trauma: Temporary Vascular Shunting

In mangled extremities (Gustilo IIIC fractures) with vascular injury, prolonged ischemia leads to limb loss. If the patient is unstable or orthopedic fixation will take time, a Temporary Vascular Shunt (TVS) is indicated. III

  • Indication: Damage control for patients in extremis, need for perfusion prior to lengthy fixation, or transport to a higher level of care.
  • Technique:
    1. Gain proximal and distal control of the vessel.
    2. Perform thrombectomy with a Fogarty catheter.
    3. Insert a carotid or Argyle shunt (secured with 2-0 silk ties or vessel loops).
    4. Verify distal flow (Doppler).
    5. Proceed with orthopedic stabilization (external fixator).
    6. Definitive vascular repair once bone is stable and patient resuscitated (shunt can remain 24–48 hours).

6.3 Total Joint Arthroplasty: Managing Catastrophic Vascular Injury

Injury to the popliteal artery (incidence 0.03–0.5%) during TKA is a surgical emergency. IV

🚨 Popliteal Artery Injury Algorithm
  • Recognition: Excessive bright red bleeding, expanding hematoma, or "white foot" upon tourniquet release.
  • Immediate Action: DEFLATE the tourniquet — prolonged ischemia worsens outcome.
  • Control: Apply direct pressure. Do NOT apply blind clamps into popliteal fossa (risk of tibial nerve injury or conversion to complex defect).
  • Consult: Call Vascular Surgery immediately.
  • Repair: Primary repair or vein patch (saphenous vein). Ligation is NOT an option — high amputation risk. Prophylactic fasciotomy should be considered.

6.4 Spine Surgery: Epidural and Vertebral Bleeding

  • Epidural Venous Plexus: Valveless veins engorge if intra-abdominal pressure is high. Bipolar cautery is safe; monopolar is contraindicated near dura. Flowable hemostats (Floseal) are the gold standard — they can flow around the thecal sac to tamponade bleeding in lateral gutters. II
  • Bone Bleeding (Laminectomy):
    • Use a high-speed diamond burr without irrigation for the final layer — heat and bone dust create a paste that seals Haversian canals.
    • Bone wax: use sparingly.
    • Gelfoam: can be left in place but must not be packed tightly against the thecal sac.

6.5 Presacral Bleeding Management

During sacral tumor resection or spine trauma, the presacral veins can retract into the sacral foramina, making ligation impossible. This is a potential table-death scenario. IV

  • Thumbtacks: Sterile titanium thumbtacks are hammered directly into the sacral foramen to mechanically occlude the vein against the bone. This is the "Gold Standard" rescue technique.
  • Muscle Fragment Welding: A harvested piece of muscle (rectus abdominis) is held against the bleeder with forceps. Electrocautery current is applied through the forceps and muscle, creating a biological "weld" of muscle protein to the sacral periosteum/vein, sealing the defect.

8. Conclusion

Effective management of intraoperative hemorrhage in orthopedic surgery requires a disciplined, algorithmic approach that respects the distinct pathophysiology of the tissues involved. The hierarchy of hemostasis begins with:

  1. Preoperative optimization (anemia management, appropriate antithrombotic cessation) and the universal use of Tranexamic Acid.
  2. Intraoperatively, ascend the ladder from mechanical methods (tourniquets, packing) to thermal energy (bipolar sealers) and finally to topical agents.
  3. Reserve high-cost flowable matrices for complex, irregular bleeding sites where cheaper methods fail.
  4. In life-threatening trauma, shift to Damage Control Orthopedics — utilizing pre-peritoneal packing, pelvic binding, and temporary vascular shunts — to prioritize physiological restoration over anatomical perfection.

As the field advances, the integration of nanotechnology and bio-mimetic adhesives promises to further refine our ability to achieve rapid, biocompatible hemostasis, ultimately improving patient safety and surgical outcomes.
