Clinical Applications & Outcomes of Vascularized Bone Grafts in Scaphoid Nonunion & Kienböck's Disease

An exhaustive, evidence‑based interactive analysis — with emphasis on the trapezium as a novel donor site

1 Introduction

The management of recalcitrant carpal pathologies—specifically scaphoid nonunion complicated by avascular necrosis (AVN) and Kienböck's disease—remains one of the most formidable challenges in reconstructive upper‑extremity surgery. The proximal carpal row acts as an intercalated segment devoid of direct tendinous insertions, relying entirely on ligamentous constraints and structural integrity to mediate force transmission between the hand and the forearm.[1]

Scaphoid fractures represent 60–70 % of all carpal injuries, predominantly affecting young, active adults following a fall on an outstretched hand.[2] Nonunion rates range from 5–25 %, escalating with proximal pole involvement, delayed diagnosis, and tobacco use.[1]

Kienböck's disease (aseptic lunatomalacia) involves insidious osteonecrosis of the lunate, often exacerbated by repetitive microtrauma and anatomical variants such as negative ulnar variance.[5,6] Both conditions are fundamentally driven by localized ischemia that stalls the osteogenic response.

Clinical Pearl Non‑vascularized bone grafts (NVBGs) rely on creeping substitution from the recipient bed; when that bed is ischemic (AVN), NVBG union rates plummet to ≈47 %.[9] Vascularized bone grafts (VBGs) bypass this bottleneck by transferring viable osteocytes with their own arterial supply.[10]

2 Carpal Vascular Anatomy

2.1 Scaphoid Vascularity

Approximately 80 % of the scaphoid surface is articular cartilage, severely restricting vascular ingress. The dorsal carpal branch of the radial artery supplies 70–80 % of the bone (including the entire proximal pole) via retrograde intraosseous vessels. A minor volar supply from the superficial palmar arch enters the distal tubercle and serves only the distal 20–30 %.[1]

Fracture across the waist physically severs the retrograde network, rendering the proximal pole entirely ischemic.[7]

2.2 Lunate Vascularity & Kienböck's Pathogenesis

The lunate exhibits significant anatomical variation: most possess dual dorsal‑volar supply forming a Y‑, I‑, or X‑shaped anastomotic network; a subset has a singular (predominantly volar) supply.[7] Kienböck's disease occurs in vulnerable single‑vessel patterns under increased mechanical stress. Negative ulnar variance disproportionately shifts axial loading onto the radiolunate articulation, inducing venous hypertension, subchondral microfractures, and eventual arterial occlusion.[5,6]

2.3 Surgical Pedicles for Vascularized Bone Grafts

  • 1,2‑Intercompartmental Supraretinacular Artery (1,2‑ICSRA) — A consistent branch of the radial artery coursing between the 1st and 2nd extensor compartments. Mean diameter ≈0.3 mm; susceptible to kinking.[1,12,15]
  • 1st Dorsal Metacarpal Artery (1st DMCA) — Arises from the radial artery; supplies the first web space and can support osseous flaps from the 2nd metacarpal base or trapezium with antegrade flow.[18,19,21]
  • Volar Carpal Artery — Branch of the radial artery distal to pronator quadratus; forms a transverse palmar arch; supports volar corticocancellous grafts for humpback correction.[9]
  • Dorsal Carpal Branches — Provide a rich plexus over the distal carpal row (trapezium, trapezoid, capitate, hamate).[25]

5 Comparative Procedural Analysis

5.1 Pedicled Dorsal Distal Radius (1,2‑ICSRA) Level I

The workhorse for scaphoid nonunion. A corticocancellous block from the dorsal distal radius is elevated with the 1,2‑ICSRA pedicle and rotated distally.[1,10,12]

Limitations: Thin dorsal cortex; poor structural wedge capacity for severe humpback deformity; small pedicle caliber (≈0.3 mm) prone to kinking.[11,15]

5.2 Volar Distal Radius Grafts Level III

Pyramidal corticocancellous graft from the volar metaphysis, pedicled on the volar carpal artery. Biomechanically superior for correcting humpback deformity, but requires dissection through palmar radiocarpal ligaments with risk of iatrogenic instability.[8,9]

5.3 Free Medial Femoral Condyle (MFC) Flap Level I

Provides massive, dense corticocancellous bone with independent microvascular supply. Addresses all biomechanical limitations of pedicled radius grafts. The osteocartilaginous variant (medial femoral trochlea, MFT) can replace the entire proximal pole with hyaline cartilage.[11,17,37]

Donor‑Site Morbidity 12.5 % risk of saphenous nerve paresthesia; anterior knee pain; prolonged operative time; requires microsurgical expertise.[17]

5.4 The Trapezium as Donor Site RESEARCH GAP

The trapezium (greater multangular) offers dense, structurally robust bone from the distal carpal row, pedicled on the 1st DMCA or direct dorsal carpal branches.[13,18,19,21]

Advantages (theoretical):

  • Higher compressive modulus than dorsal radius cortex — resists late graft collapse[19]
  • Antegrade arterial inflow via 1st DMCA; wide arc of rotation to scaphoid & lunate[21]
  • Single‑field operation (hand/wrist) — no forearm dissection[19]

Knowledge Gap: No robust clinical trials or prospective cohorts exist evaluating trapezial harvest for VBGs. The literature references the trapezium predominantly as a recipient site or as an obstacle during volar approaches.[3,40,56]

6.1 Kienböck's Disease: VBG Outcomes Level I

Meta‑analyses show VBG patients achieved 88.2 % contralateral grip strength — superior to radial shortening osteotomy (75.2 %) and capitate shortening (68.0 %).[30] MRI at 16–20 months demonstrates restored lunate signal intensity and trabecular reconstitution.[9] Pain relief is satisfactory in >92 % of patients at 3 months postoperatively.[9]

7 Knowledge Gaps in Contemporary Literature

7.1 The Underexplored Trapezium Donor Site MAJOR GAP

Absence of robust clinical trials evaluating the trapezium as a primary VBG donor. Research remains skewed toward distal radius and MFC grafts.[3,56]

7.2 Diagnostic Ambiguity: MRI vs. Intraoperative Perfusion DEBATE

Non‑contrast MRI is highly sensitive but possesses questionable specificity for true biological AVN. The precise threshold for "critical ischemia" mandating VBG vs. "reversible ischemia" amenable to NVBG remains poorly quantified.[7,9,27]

7.3 Biomechanical Consequences of Carpal Donor‑Site Morbidity UNKNOWN

Harvesting structural bone from tightly articulated carpal rows poses entirely unknown long‑term biomechanical risks — including potential iatrogenic trapeziometacarpal or scaphotrapezial osteoarthritis.[56,59]

7.4 Diversity in Orthopaedic RCTs

Systemic underrepresentation of diverse populations compromises the generalizability of union rates and functional outcomes across demographics and occupational demands.[2,61]

8 High‑Impact Future Research Questions

Q1 — Microvascular Mapping of the Trapezium

Cadaveric 3D micro‑angiography to quantify pedicle length, arterial caliber, and arc of rotation for trapezium‑based VBGs.[13,18,19]

Q2 — Biomechanical Efficacy: Trapezium vs. Distal Radius Wedge

Cadaveric biomechanical study comparing compressive load‑bearing and LISA restoration under dynamic wrist loading.[11,15]

Q3 — Donor‑Site Morbidity Following Partial Trapezial Harvest

Finite Element Analysis + clinical cohort to model stress distribution changes across STT and TMC joints.[56]

Q4 — RCT: Pedicled Trapezium VBG vs. Free MFC Flap

Multi‑center prospective RCT comparing union rates, operative time, and patient‑reported outcomes.[9,11,17]

Q5 — HR‑pQCT to Predict AVN & Optimize Graft Selection

Assessment of ultra‑high‑resolution imaging to definitively predict the need for structural VBG prior to surgical exploration.[27]
