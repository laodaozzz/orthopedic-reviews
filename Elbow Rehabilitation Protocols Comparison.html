Comparative Analysis of Postoperative Rehabilitation Modalities in Terrible Triad Injuries of the Elbow

Evidence-Based Review: Early Active vs. Passive Motion & Safety of the Overhead Protocol

1. Introduction

The management of complex elbow instability represents one of the most sophisticated challenges in modern orthopaedic trauma surgery. Among these injuries, the "terrible triad" of the elbow—characterized by a posterior dislocation, a fracture of the radial head, and a fracture of the coronoid process of the ulna—has historically been associated with devastating functional outcomes, including chronic instability, debilitating stiffness, and post-traumatic arthrosis. 1.1 Pathoanatomy and the Imperative for Rehabilitation

The injury typically results from a high-energy fall on an extended, supinated arm, subjecting the elbow to a combination of valgus, axial, and posterolateral rotatory forces. Case Series

  • Coronoid process: Primary anterior buttress against posterior subluxation, particularly beyond 30° of flexion.
  • Radial head: Secondary valgus stabilizer preventing proximal migration of the radius.
  • LCL complex (LUCL): Primary restraint against posterolateral rotatory instability (PLRI). Biomechanical
🔑 Key Concept — The Rehabilitation Paradox The necessity of early mobilization to prevent capsular fibrosis and stiffness versus the imperative to restrict motion to protect healing ligamentous and osseous repairs from recurrent subluxation. This tension defines the debate between active and passive mobilization strategies.

2. Biomechanical Foundations of Postoperative Motion

The clinical safety of AROM versus PROM is rooted in the biomechanical behavior of the elbow's stabilizers under different loading conditions. The distinction between "active" and "passive" is not merely one of volition but of joint reaction forces.

2.1 Dynamic Stabilization via Active Motion

Active motion relies on the neuromuscular control of the patient. The primary flexors (biceps brachii, brachialis) and extensors (triceps brachii) generate a joint reaction force that compresses the ulna into the trochlea of the humerus. This "dynamic compression" enhances ulnohumeral congruency, theoretically reducing the strain on the static ligamentous repairs. Biomechanical

Conversely, passive motion lacks this protective compressive vector. If the axis of rotation of the external force does not perfectly align with the elbow's instant center of rotation, or if gravity acts unfavorably, passive movement can generate shear forces or distraction at the ulnohumeral joint. Biomechanical

2.2 The Influence of Gravitational Forces

In the standard upright position (dependent arm), the weight of the forearm creates a distraction vector at the ulnohumeral joint. In an elbow with LCL insufficiency, this can lead to the "drop sign"—a radiographic widening of the ulnohumeral joint distance greater than 4 mm. Radiographic

📐 Biomechanical Principle Positioning the patient supine with the arm overhead converts the gravitational vector from a distracting force into a stabilizing compressive force. This biomechanical principle underpins the safety profile of the overhead protocol. Cadaveric

2.3 The "Safe Arc" Concept

Rehabilitation protocols are designed around a "safe arc" of motion, typically determined intraoperatively by the surgeon. Common restrictions include avoiding terminal extension (limiting motion to a 30°–130° arc) and performing exercises in pronation to tension the lateral structures and approximate the radial head to the capitellum. Expert Consensus

3. Early AROM vs. PROM: Comparative Clinical Outcomes

3.1 Systematic Review Evidence on Initiation Timing

A landmark systematic review and meta-analysis by Larwa et al. (2024) sought to determine whether the initiation of passive ROM exercises within the first week postoperative (≤7 days) conferred any functional benefit over delayed initiation (>7 days). Systematic Review & Meta-Analysis

Larwa et al. (2024) — Systematic Review & Meta-Analysis
36 articles · 1,123 elbows · Mean follow-up 27 months
Key Finding: Early passive ROM initiation (≤7 days) was not associated with improved final elbow ROM or Mayo Elbow Performance Scores (MEPS). This challenges the dogma that "earlier is always better" for passive mobilization.

Timing Patterns Identified:

  • Passive ROM: Most commonly initiated at 7 days postoperatively (reported in 12% of studies).
  • Active ROM: Most commonly initiated at 21 days postoperatively (reported in 15% of studies).
  • Immobilization: Cast/splint removal typically occurred at 7 days.

3.2 The Efficacy of Active Motion (AROM)

A scoping review by Siegel et al. (2024) examined manual therapeutic approaches for Heterotopic Ossification (HO) and stiffness. Scoping Review

📊 Key Finding — Siegel et al. (2024) Studies utilizing active range of motion only reported that 50% of patients did not require secondary surgery for stiffness, whereas two studies emphasized that passive range of motion was effective in less than 50% of their subjects.

The efficacy of AROM is attributed to several factors:

  1. Edema Reduction: The "muscle pump" effect facilitates venous and lymphatic return, reducing periarticular edema. Physiological
  2. Pain Feedback: AROM is inherently self-limiting—patients avoid ranges causing significant pain, preventing micro-trauma. PROM lacks this intrinsic safety loop. Observational
  3. Neuromuscular Re-education: Early active recruitment of biceps and triceps helps re-establish control and prevents disuse atrophy. Physiological

3.3 Functional Outcomes: ROM and MEPS

The ultimate goal is a functional elbow defined as a flexion-extension arc of 30°–130° and a pronation-supination arc of 50°/50°. Expert Consensus

  • Range of Motion: Final flexion-extension arc averaging between 110° and 115°. The Larwa meta-analysis confirms this outcome is achievable regardless of whether PROM starts at day 3 or day 10.
  • MEPS: Scores typically range from good to excellent (mean ~78–92 points). Initiation of PROM at ≤7 days did not produce statistically higher MEPS.

3.4 The Role of "Gentle" vs. "Forceful" Motion

⚠️ Critical Distinction "Gentle" PROM (motion within the pain-free range) is often permissible. However, "forceful" passive manipulation is widely contraindicated in the acute phase. Forceful stretching is associated with capsular tearing, hematoma formation, and potentiation of Heterotopic Ossification. Observational & Expert Consensus

4. Continuous Passive Motion (CPM): Efficacy and Controversy

4.1 Theoretical Rationale

Proponents argue that CPM prevents hemarthrosis accumulation, reduces edema, and maintains the viscoelastic properties of the capsule without requiring patient effort. Theoretical

4.2 Lack of Efficacy in Clinical Trials

High-quality evidence supporting CPM for terrible triad injuries is sparse and contradictory.

Lindenhovius et al. — CPM Review
Systematic review of CPM use in elbow trauma
Key Finding: The use of CPM did not produce any benefit in final range of motion compared to standard physical therapy exercises alone. Use was often based on anecdotal reports rather than scientific rigor. Systematic Review

4.3 Safety Concerns

  • Distraction Mechanism: CPM machines move the forearm via a lever arm. If the axis of rotation does not align with the trochlea, the device can exert a distracting force on the ulna, stressing the LCL repair. Biomechanical
  • Myofibroblast Activation: Early, continuous passive strain may stimulate myofibroblast expression and extracellular matrix deposition—paradoxically contributing to stiffness or fibrosis. Histological
  • Practical Limitations: CPM does not engage the neuromuscular system, failing to address triceps/biceps inhibition critical for long-term stability.
⚠️ Recommendation on CPM Given the lack of proven benefit and the potential risks of distraction and stiffness potentiation, CPM should not be the standard of care for terrible triad injuries. It may be reserved for specific indications such as patients with concomitant neurogenic HO or those unable to participate in active therapy. Systematic Review

5.2 The "Passive Motion" Hypothesis

Forceful passive stretching of a stiff or inflamed elbow causes micro-tearing of the capsule and brachialis muscle. This trauma exacerbates the inflammatory response, increases local blood flow, and promotes differentiation of mesenchymal stem cells into osteoblasts. Observational & Expert Consensus

✅ AROM as HO Prophylaxis Because AROM is self-limited by pain, it is less likely to induce the micro-trauma associated with HO. AROM is considered the safer modality for minimizing HO risk in the acute post-traumatic phase. Observational

5.3 Clinical Impact of HO

Interestingly, the mere presence of HO does not always correlate with lower functional scores. One study found no significant difference in DASH or SF-36 scores between patients who developed HO and those who did not, provided the HO was not severe (Class III/IV). The goal of rehabilitation is not necessarily to prevent the radiographic appearance of HO, but to prevent it from becoming symptomatic or ankylosing. Retrospective Cohort

6. The "Overhead Motion Protocol": Safety and Efficacy

6.1 Protocol Description

  1. Patient Position: Supine on a plinth or bed.
  2. Shoulder Position: Flexed to 90° (humerus vertical, arm pointing toward ceiling). Adducted to neutral.
  3. Forearm Position: Pronated—increases tension on lateral soft tissues, helps approximate radial head to capitellum.
  4. Action: Patient actively flexes and extends the elbow. Triceps works against gravity for extension; gravity assists flexion. Biomechanical

6.2 Biomechanical Mechanism of Safety

🛡️ Gravity Conversion Principle In the upright position, gravity pulls the forearm away from the humerus (distraction). In the overhead position, gravity pushes the olecranon into the trochlear notch (compression). This creates a "gravity-reduced" environment. Cadaveric Study
  • Triceps "Strap" Effect: During active extension overhead, triceps contraction creates a posteriorly directed vector that compresses the ulnohumeral joint, acting as a dynamic stabilizer against anterior subluxation.
  • Centering: This position aligns the instant center of rotation and minimizes varus/valgus torques. Biomechanical

6.3 Clinical Evidence and Safety Profile

Schreiber et al. — Overhead Motion Protocol Cohort
Patients with simple dislocations (mechanistically similar to terrible triad)
Results: Excellent final range of motion (mean arc 6°–137°) and zero cases of recurrent instability. Prospective Cohort

Cadaveric studies using 3D motion capture confirmed that the overhead position significantly reduces ulnohumeral distraction compared to the dependent (upright) position in LCL-sectioned elbows. Cadaveric

🌟 Clinical Significance The overhead protocol allows for earlier initiation of AROM (often within 3–7 days) because it mitigates the risk of subluxation that typically necessitates immobilization in the upright position. By allowing earlier motion, it directly addresses the risk of stiffness without compromising the repair.

8. Conclusion & Clinical Recommendations

Based on a rigorous review of the current literature, including systematic reviews, meta-analyses, and biomechanical trials:

  1. Primacy of Active Motion: Early AROM is the superior modality for initial rehabilitation. It leverages dynamic stabilizing effects of elbow musculature to compress the joint, reduce edema, and prevent stiffness while adhering to physiologic pain limits. Meta-Analysis
  2. Caution with Passive Motion: There is no high-level evidence to support aggressive or immediate PROM/CPM initiation within the first week. Meta-analyses indicate that delaying PROM does not compromise final ROM or functional scores. Meta-Analysis
  3. Safety of the Overhead Protocol: The Overhead Motion Protocol represents the safest method for initiating early AROM. By utilizing gravity to compress the ulnohumeral joint, it allows immediate mobilization while protecting the LCL repair. Cohort + Cadaveric
  4. Management of HO Risk: The high HO incidence (up to 58%) mandates a "do no harm" approach. Forceful passive manipulation is a known aggravator of HO and should be strictly avoided. Observational
🏆 Bottom Line Optimal rehabilitation for terrible triad injuries prioritizes early, gravity-assisted active motion (overhead protocol) over passive modalities. This approach respects the biology of the "terrible" injury while aggressively combating the inevitable tendency toward stiffness.
