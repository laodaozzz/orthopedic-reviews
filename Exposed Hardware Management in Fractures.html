1. Introduction: Clinical & Economic Burden

Exposure of internal fixation devices—plates, screws, and intramedullary nails—remains a prevalent complication, affecting approximately 5% to 16% of patients undergoing open reduction and internal fixation (ORIF) for high-energy fractures.[1] In craniomaxillofacial (CMF) trauma, incidence is estimated at 5%–6.8%, though management paradigms differ due to superior vascularity.[3]

The economic burden is substantial: the average cost of managing an infected fracture exceeds $15,000 per case, often escalating with multiple surgeries, prolonged hospitalizations, and complex soft tissue reconstructions.[5]

Historical Dogma → Modern Paradigm: Traditionally, any exposed metal was considered inevitably colonized by bacteria, mandating immediate removal. Premature removal in an unhealed fracture, however, creates a vicious cycle of instability → osteolysis → nonunion → amputation.[6] Modern management has shifted to a dual-modality strategy: maximizing fracture union through stability (retention) while simultaneously managing soft tissue defects and bioburden.

The emergence of the "orthoplastic" team approach—where orthopedic and plastic surgeons collaborate from the onset—has been pivotal in shifting outcomes from amputation to salvage.

2. Pathophysiology of the Exposed Implant

2.1. Biofilm Formation & the "Race for the Surface"

Upon implantation, orthopedic hardware (typically Titanium-6Al-4V, Stainless Steel 316L, or CoCrMo) is instantly coated with host proteins (fibronectin, fibrinogen, vitronectin), creating a conditioning film.[5] The "race for the surface" concept posits that host cells (fibroblasts, osteoblasts) and bacteria compete to colonize this conditioned surface.

If bacteria—most commonly Staphylococcus aureus or S. epidermidis—reach the surface first, they adhere via surface proteins (MSCRAMMs), proliferate, and secrete an exopolysaccharide glycocalyx, encasing themselves in a biofilm.[2] This biofilm renders bacteria:

  1. Resistant to Host Defenses: The slime layer prevents phagocytosis by neutrophils and macrophages.
  2. Resistant to Antibiotics: The Minimum Biofilm Eradication Concentration (MBEC) is often 100–1000× higher than the Minimum Inhibitory Concentration (MIC).[5]
  3. Resistant to Culture: Standard swabs often fail to detect sessile organisms, causing false-negative diagnostics.

2.2. Fracture Stability as an Antimicrobial Factor

Instability at a fracture site causes micromotion that disrupts neovascularization, damages granulation tissue, and creates a hypoxic, acidic environment favoring bacterial proliferation.[7] Conversely, absolute stability (provided by intact plating) minimizes inflammatory micromotion, preserving local vascular supply and enhancing delivery of immune cells and antibiotics. A stable implant can thus paradoxically aid infection control—a principle forming the cornerstone of retention guidelines.

3. Diagnostic Frameworks for Fracture-Related Infection (FRI)

The FRI Consensus Definition (2018–2020) provides a standardized diagnostic framework.[9]

3.1. Confirmatory Criteria Level I – Consensus

Presence of any one is diagnostic of FRI:

  • Fistula, sinus, or wound breakdown communicating with bone or implant.
  • Purulent drainage from the wound.
  • Phenotypically indistinguishable pathogens in at least two separate deep tissue specimens taken during surgery.[9]
  • Histopathological evidence of microorganisms or specific inflammatory infiltrates (PMNs) in deep tissue biopsy.[9]

3.2. Suggestive Criteria

  • Clinical signs: Erythema, local warmth, swelling, induration, or fever (≥ 38.3 °C).
  • Radiological signs: Periosteal reaction, osteolysis around screws ("halo sign"), or implant loosening.
  • Biomarkers: Elevated CRP or ESR. Note: WBC count is often normal in chronic FRI and is a poor discriminator.[2]

Diagnostic Pitfall: Superficial wound swabs are notoriously unreliable—they frequently culture skin flora rather than deep pathogens. The consensus guidelines strongly recommend against relying on swabs and advocate for deep intraoperative sampling (minimum 3–5 samples).[2]

3.3. Advanced Diagnostics

Sonication: For patients undergoing hardware removal, sonication of explanted hardware shows superior sensitivity versus tissue culture alone by disrupting biofilm and dislodging sessile bacteria. Particularly valuable for detecting low-virulence organisms like Cutibacterium acnes.[5]

Imaging: MRI is gold standard for soft tissue infection (sensitivity 98%), but ferromagnetic hardware creates artifact. Nuclear medicine scans (WBC-labeled) or CT for osteolysis assessment are preferred in these settings.[2]

4. Evidence-Based Guidelines: Hardware Retention vs. Removal

4.1. Prognostic Parameters

Hardware Stability Level I – Systematic Reviews

  • Stable hardware: Retention is viable. The implant facilitates fracture healing; once union is achieved, hardware can be electively removed.[11]
  • Loose hardware: Functionally useless and biologically detrimental—it acts as a sequestrum. Removal is mandatory, often requiring conversion to external fixation or exchange nailing.[13]

Duration of Exposure: The "Two-Week" Rule Level III

  • Acute (< 2 weeks): Highest salvage rate. Immature biofilm susceptible to mechanical disruption. Immediate soft tissue coverage strongly recommended.[8]
  • Sub-acute (2–6 weeks): "Grey zone." Retention possible but higher failure rate. Requires aggressive debridement, prolonged suppressive antibiotics, and often NPWTi-d.
  • Chronic (> 6 weeks): Typically indicates established osteomyelitis and mature biofilm. Standard of care shifts to hardware removal, necrotic bone debridement, and staged reconstruction (e.g., Masquelet technique).[7]

4.2. Anatomical-Specific Guidelines

Craniomaxillofacial (CMF) Trauma Level I – Meta-analysis (n = 7,503)

The CMF region is a "privileged site" due to rich vascular collateralization. A comprehensive meta-analysis of 7,503 patients revealed no significant difference in outcomes between hardware removal and retention groups regarding infection resolution or union rates.[3] In CMF trauma, a conservative approach is justified—hardware can often be retained even with minor purulence, provided the fracture is stable and the patient is compliant with antibiotics.[16]

Spine Level IV – Case Series

Morbidity of removing spinal instrumentation (instability, loss of correction, neurological collapse) is devastating. The consensus favors retention with aggressive debridement and prolonged suppression unless there is frank loosening or sepsis unresponsive to source control.[13]

Forearm: Caution Against Elective Removal Level I – Systematic Review & Meta-analysis (2024)

A 2024 systematic review found that plate retention is significantly associated with a lower rate of refracture compared to removal (Risk Difference 0.06 favoring retention). Overall refracture incidence after removal is approximately 7%, with some series reporting up to 18.9%.[18] Refractures predominantly occur through empty screw holes (stress risers). Elective removal of asymptomatic forearm hardware is discouraged.

Total Knee Arthroplasty (TKA) Conversion Level I – Meta-analysis (284 studies)

A meta-analysis demonstrated that staged removal (removal → healing period → TKA) is associated with significantly lower risk of Periprosthetic Joint Infection (PJI) compared to concurrent removal and TKA.[20]

4.3. Success Rates of Retention Protocols

When strict criteria are applied (stable hardware, acute exposure, aggressive debridement), retention outcomes are favorable. A seminal study of 121 patients with acute FRI treated with retention and suppressive antibiotics reported a 71% success rate for fracture union.[7] Level III

Smoking Cessation is Non-Negotiable: Smokers demonstrated a 3.7× greater likelihood of failure per month compared to non-smokers.[6]

5. Advanced Wound Therapy: NPWTi-d

Negative Pressure Wound Therapy with Instillation and Dwell time (NPWTi-d) has revolutionized the "temporizing" phase of hardware salvage by introducing a dynamic fluid cycle that actively combats biofilm and bioburden.[1,23]

5.1. Mechanisms of Action

  1. Automated Solubilization: Instillation of fluid followed by dwell time solubilizes viscous exudate, necrotic debris, and planktonic bacteria. When vacuum re-engages, material is evacuated—a form of "automated debridement."[1]
  2. Biofilm Disruption: Fluid dynamics disrupt the exopolysaccharide matrix, rendering sessile bacteria more susceptible to removal.
  3. Dilution of Inflammatory Cytokines: Cyclic lavage dilutes proteases (MMPs) and toxins, resetting wound biochemistry.[23]
  4. Macro/Micro-Strain: Negative pressure induces micro-deformations in cell cytoskeletons, stimulating angiogenesis and granulation tissue formation over exposed metal.[17]

5.2. Evidence-Based Protocols Level I – International Consensus (2020)

  • Dwell Time: 10–20 minutes recommended. Balances fluid interaction with risk of tissue maceration.[25]
  • Negative Pressure: Continuous −125 mmHg for 2–4 hours (typically 3 hours) following dwell phase.[24]
  • Dressing Selection: Reticulated Open Cell Foam with Through Holes (ROCF-CC) preferred for wounds with thick exudate or exposed hardware.[27]

5.3. Fluid Selection & Corrosion Risks

The interaction between antimicrobial solutions and metallic implants can lead to galvanic or crevice corrosion, potentially releasing toxic ions or compromising the implant.

Critical Corrosion Warning: Hypochlorous acid can polarize CoCrMo surfaces above their transpassive potential, causing rapid metal ion release and implant failure.[28] PHMB + Betaine (e.g., Prontosan) has low cytotoxicity and does not induce corrosion in orthopedic alloys, making it the safer choice.[24]

5.4. Clinical Efficacy Level I – Systematic Review & Meta-analysis

A systematic review and meta-analysis found that the NPWTi-d group had significantly fewer surgical debridements, a 4.4× greater odds of bacterial reduction, and was 2.39× more likely to achieve wound closure versus standard NPWT.[32] In a series of 28 patients with exposed hardware, an 89% salvage rate was achieved.[17]

6. Orthoplastic Approach to Soft Tissue Reconstruction

6.1. Muscle vs. Fasciocutaneous Flaps Level I – Meta-analysis (2020–2025)

Historically, muscle flaps were considered superior for covering exposed hardware due to superior vascularity and dead-space obliteration. A comprehensive meta-analysis has challenged this dogma:

  • Infection control: No significant difference in deep infection recurrence (19.4% MF vs. 14.7% FCF) or non-union rates (18.9% MF vs. 14.8% FCF).[35]
  • Morbidity: Muscle flaps had significantly higher donor site morbidity and partial flap necrosis.
  • Durability for re-operation: FCFs tolerated secondary elevation with significantly lower flap loss (2.08%) compared to muscle flaps (18.52%).[36]

Conclusion: Fasciocutaneous flaps are now often the preferred choice due to lower morbidity, better aesthetics, and superior durability for future surgical access.

6.2. Propeller Perforator Flaps Level I – Systematic Review (n = 619)

In the distal tibia and ankle, propeller perforator flaps—fasciocutaneous flaps isolated on a single perforating vessel and rotated up to 180°—have emerged as a powerful local solution. A systematic review reported a 97% overall survival rate.[37] Primary complication is venous congestion; complication rates rise to 35.1% in acute trauma and with rotations > 150°.

7. Synthetic Dermal Matrices: BTM vs. Integra

7.1. Material Science

  • Integra™ (Biologic): Cross-linked bovine collagen and chondroitin-6-sulfate. Effective in clean wounds but vulnerable in infected fields—bacteria produce collagenases that enzymatically digest the matrix.[39]
  • NovoSorb® BTM (Synthetic): Biodegradable polyurethane foam. As a fully synthetic polymer, it contains no biologic "food source" for bacteria and is resistant to enzymatic degradation, allowing it to persist even in the presence of active infection.[39]

8. Complications & Long-Term Outcomes

8.1. Refracture & Non-Union

As detailed in Section 4.2.4, removal of forearm hardware carries a 7% risk of refracture.[18] Retaining hardware with inadequately suppressed infection risks non-union; motion at the fracture site mandates immediate hardware removal.[13]

8.2. Recurrent Osteomyelitis

The most significant long-term risk of retention is chronic osteomyelitis. If biofilm is not adequately suppressed, infection invades cortical bone leading to sequestrum formation. Smoking increases failure rate by 3.7×.[6] Recurrence typically mandates: hardware removal → resection of dead bone → reconstruction via Masquelet technique or bone transport.[46]

8.3. Patient-Reported Outcomes

Patients with retained hardware often report lower pain versus those who undergo removal and suffer refracture/instability. In forearm fractures, ~12% report continued discomfort even after successful union and removal.[18] Functional outcomes are generally superior in salvage groups compared to amputation or chronic non-union groups.

9. Future Horizons

Smart Hardware

Prototypes with embedded sensors are emerging that could monitor pH, temperature, or strain to detect early biofilm formation or loosening before clinical signs appear.[47]

Next-Generation Biologics

Research is exploring mesenchymal stem cell-loaded matrices and novel orthobiologics to accelerate bone healing in the presence of infection, potentially closing the gap between retention and union.[48]

10. Key Clinical Takeaways

  • 1. Retention is the Priority in Acute CasesFor stable hardware exposed < 2 weeks, retention is preferred to maintain fracture stability. The 71% union rate supports this strategy.[7]
  • 2. NPWTi-d is EssentialInstillation therapy (10–20 min dwell, −125 mmHg for 3 hours) effectively reduces bioburden. PHMB + Betaine is safe for all alloys; Hypochlorous Acid requires caution with CoCrMo implants due to corrosion risk.[24,28]
  • 3. Synthetic Matrices (BTM) over Biologic in Contaminated FieldsBTM offers superior infection resistance (90.6% graft take vs. 77.2%), and dramatic cost savings ($7,276 vs. $13,222 total).[43–45]
  • 4. Fasciocutaneous Flaps are the New Gold StandardEquivalent infection control to muscle flaps with lower morbidity and better durability for secondary surgeries.[35,36]
  • 5. Caution with Elective RemovalRoutine removal of forearm hardware should be avoided—7% refracture risk, up to 18.9% in some series.[18]
