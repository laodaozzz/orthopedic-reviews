1. Introduction: The Evolving Landscape of Revision Arthroplasty

The field of adult reconstruction is undergoing a period of profound transformation, driven by a demographic shift reshaping the epidemiology of joint replacement. As the volume of primary total hip arthroplasty (THA) procedures rises globally, a parallel and disproportionate increase in revision burdens is emerging.

Projection Alert Demand for revision total hip arthroplasty (rTHA) is projected to double within the next decade, presenting surgeons with patients of increasing medical complexity, diminishing bone stock, and higher functional expectations.

Unlike primary arthroplasty, where surgical goals are standardized and anatomy predictable, revision surgery is a heterogeneous discipline defined by variability. The spectrum of pathology ranges from isolated polyethylene wear in a well-fixed shell to catastrophic pelvic discontinuity and massive femoral bone loss.

Successful execution of rTHA depends on a tripartite strategy:

  1. Adequate surgical exposure that preserves the soft tissue envelope
  2. Safe and bone-conserving removal of failed implants
  3. Skeletal reconstruction to restore biomechanical function and long-term stability

The last five years have witnessed the maturation of key technologies — ultrasonic osteotomes, porous metal augments, and additive manufacturing (3D printing) — which have collectively redefined the limits of reconstructability. This report synthesizes evidence from major orthopedic journals to construct a comprehensive guide covering all three domains.

2. Surgical Exposure: Strategic Approaches and the ETO

In the revision setting, exposure is not merely a preliminary step — it is a decisive factor that dictates the capacity for implant removal and the accuracy of reconstruction. Scarring of anatomical planes, heterotopic ossification, and altered landmarks demand an approach that is both extensile and respectful of neurovascular structures.

When performed by experienced surgeons, the DAA does not result in higher rates of infection or intraoperative fracture.

2.2 The Extended Trochanteric Osteotomy (ETO)

The ETO is arguably the most critical exposure technique in the revision surgeon's repertoire, providing controlled access to the femoral canal for removal of well-fixed implants and correction of proximal deformities. Level III (Case Series, Systematic Review)

Indications

  • Removal of well-fixed femoral stems, particularly those with extensive porous coating or distal fixation Level III
  • Correction of varus remodeling of the proximal femur (common with loose cemented stems)
  • Adjusting version of the reconstruction
  • Improvement of abductor mechanics by advancing the osteotomized fragment

Surgical Technique: Step-by-Step

  1. Exposure & Landmarks: Performed through a posterolateral approach. The vastus lateralis is elevated from the intermuscular septum posteriorly but left attached anteriorly to maintain vascular supply from perforating arteries.
  2. Determining Osteotomy Length: Typically extends 12–15 cm from the tip of the greater trochanter to the cylindrical diaphysis. Must expose the entire porous-coated section while preserving sufficient intact diaphysis for distal fixation.
  3. Distal Transverse Cut: The most critical step — a high-speed pencil-tip burr is used to round the corners of the transverse cut. Square corners act as stress risers that can propagate spiral fractures during levering or impaction.
  4. Posterior & Anterior Cortical Cuts: An oscillating saw cuts the posterior cortex from the greater trochanter tip distally, staying just anterior to the linea aspera. The anterior cut parallels the posterior.
  5. Controlled Fracture & Elevation: Completion is achieved by using broad osteotomes to create a "controlled fracture" of the anterior bridge — not by sawing through the medullary canal, which would damage the vascular leash. This preserves the anterior periosteal hinge.
  6. Dislocation & Removal: With the fragment elevated anteriorly, the bone-implant interface is disrupted under direct vision, significantly reducing the risk of shaft perforation.

Fixation and Healing

Table 2. ETO Fixation Constructs and Outcomes
Fixation Method Description Indication Union Rate

Technical Tip A prophylactic cable distal to the osteotomy is recommended to prevent propagation of the split during stem impaction. Nonunion is rare but presents as trochanteric migration and abductor weakness.

3. Component Removal: The Role of Ultrasonic Technology

Removal of well-fixed components, particularly porous-coated stems and cups, poses significant risk of bone loss. Traditional mechanical tools rely on force and cutting action, which can inadvertently deviate into host bone. Ultrasonic technology has revolutionized this phase, shifting the paradigm from "cutting" to "separating." Level III (Matched Case-Control)

3.1 Physics and Mechanism

Ultrasonic devices convert electrical energy into high-frequency mechanical vibrations (20–30 kHz) at the instrument tip. The mechanism involves:

  • Micromotion & friction at the point of contact
  • Heat generation that liquefies thermoplastic materials (PMMA) or disrupts the fibrous/osseous interface
  • Selectivity — cortical bone has different acoustic impedance, reducing the risk of cortical wall penetration compared to rotary burrs

3.2 Clinical Applications

Cemented Stems

The ultrasonic tool liquefies the cement mantle, allowing it to be peeled away from bone or the stem. This "in-cement" revision technique avoids cortical windows or ETOs in many cases and is particularly valuable when the mantle is intact. Level IV (NICE Guidance/Case Series)

Well-Fixed Cementless Stems

The ultrasonic blade disrupts bony ongrowth/ingrowth along the implant surface. The "hot knife through butter" effect requires patience. Level III

  • Irrigation is mandatory — continuous copious saline irrigation dissipates heat and prevents thermal necrosis of host bone
  • "Pot-bound" stems (well-fixed distally): Long, thin tips reach distal fixation zones inaccessible to standard osteotomes, potentially avoiding an ETO

Acetabular Cup Removal

Specialized curved blades follow the spherical contour of the shell, separating the cup from acetabular bone with minimal host stock removal. This preserves columns and walls critical for reconstruction.

3.3 Comparative Outcomes

Key Evidence Studies demonstrate that ultrasonic removal is associated with reduced operative time, lower blood loss, and importantly, a reduction in iatrogenic fractures and cortical perforations compared to traditional techniques. While initial equipment cost is a consideration, the reduction in complications presents a compelling value proposition. Level III (Matched Case-Control)

4. Acetabular Reconstruction: Managing Paprosky Type II & III Defects

Acetabular reconstruction is often the most challenging aspect of rTHA. The goal is stable, long-term fixation in a biologically compromised environment. The Paprosky classification provides the framework for decision-making. Level III (Expert Consensus/Case Series)

4.2 The Jumbo Cup

For Paprosky Type II and selected Type III defects, the jumbo cup (>62 mm in females, >66 mm in males) bypasses intracavitary defects and achieves rim fixation on remaining viable bone. Level III (Case Series)

Table 4. Jumbo Cup Technique Parameters
Parameter Technique Notes
Reaming Sequential reaming to true hemisphere; prioritize anterior & posterior columns Line-to-line or 1–2 mm under-reaming depending on system
Positioning Anatomical if possible; high hip center (2–3 cm) tolerated if not lateralized Lateralization increases lever arm → abductor fatigue & loosening
Fixation Multiple screws (≥5) in safe zones (posterosuperior & posteroinferior) Augments the press-fit
Survivorship >90% at 20 years in Type II defects Less effective in Type IIIB with pelvic discontinuity Level III

4.3 Trabecular Metal (TM) Augments

When host bone coverage for a jumbo cup is <50% (Paprosky IIIA/B), porous metal augments (Tantalum or Titanium) fill the defect and provide structural buttress. Level III

The "Flying Buttress" Technique

For severe superior defects, the augment is fixed to the ilium above the defect, acting as a shelf or "flying buttress" supporting the superior aspect of the cup.

Unitization Protocol

  1. The augment is screw-fixed to the host bone
  2. A layer of PMMA cement is applied to the augment-cup interface
  3. The cup is impacted, compressing the cement to create a single stable mechanical unit
Outcome Data TM augments show excellent osseointegration and survivorship, avoiding the resorption and collapse often seen with structural allografts. Level III

4.4 Cup-Cage Constructs

For Paprosky Type IIIB defects with pelvic discontinuity, a porous cup alone cannot achieve initial stability. A reconstruction cage is placed over the cup, with flanges fixed to the ilium and ischium, effectively plating the pelvic fracture and bridging the discontinuity. Level III

  • The polyethylene liner is typically cemented into the construct, allowing adjustment of version and inclination independent of cage position
  • Survivorship: ~80–90% at mid-term follow-up Level III

4.5 Custom 3D-Printed Triflange Implants

For massive defects where off-the-shelf solutions cannot achieve fixation, patient-specific custom implants represent the definitive solution. Level III (Case Series)

  • Designed from high-resolution CT; engineered to perfectly match the patient's defect
  • Incorporate specific screw trajectories targeting remaining viable bone (sciatic buttress, pubis)
  • Lead time of several weeks limits use in urgent cases
  • One study reported 100% survivorship and significant functional improvement in 19 consecutive Paprosky 3B cases Level III

5.2 Modular Tapered Fluted Stems

These are the current "workhorse" for Paprosky Type III and IV defects. Level III (Multicenter Retrospective)

  • Design: Distal tapered, fluted geometry (Wagner principle) engages the diaphysis. Flutes provide rotational stability; the taper provides axial stability. Proximal modularity allows independent adjustment of leg length, offset, and version.
  • Indications: Type IIIA, IIIB, and IV where distal fixation is achievable. Preferred in elderly patients where immediate weight-bearing is desired.
  • Survivorship: 95–98% at 10 years Level II (Prospective Cohort)
  • Complications: Subsidence typically stabilizes; junctional fracture (breakage at modular interface) is rare in modern designs Level III

5.3 Impaction Bone Grafting (IBG)

A biological reconstruction technique aimed at restoring bone stock. Level III (Case Series, Systematic Review)

  • Technique: Canal packed with morselized fresh-frozen allograft using specialized tamps; polished, tapered cemented stem (e.g., Exeter) cemented into the impacted graft
  • Mechanism: Under load, the polished stem subsides ("taper-slip"), keeping graft under compression, stimulating host bone to grow into and replace the allograft (creep substitution)
  • Indications: Younger patients (<60 years) with large ectatic canals (Type III/IV) where bone stock restoration is critical
  • Contraindications: Active infection
  • Survivorship: ~90% at 11 years Level III (Systematic Review)
Technical Caution IBG is time-consuming and technically unforgiving — poor impaction leads to massive subsidence and failure.

6. Instability Management: The Imperative of Dual Mobility

Instability is the most common cause of failure in rTHA. The revision environment — poor soft tissues, abductor deficiency, altered anatomy — is inherently prone to dislocation.

6.1 Dual Mobility (DM) Bearings: Mechanism

Dual mobility constructs utilize a large polyethylene liner within the metal shell, with a smaller femoral head articulating within the liner, creating two motion interfaces. The large effective head size (often matching shell diameter) dramatically increases the "jump distance" required for dislocation and the impingement-free range of motion. Level I (Systematic Review, Bibliometric Analysis)

Table 7. Dual Mobility vs. Fixed Bearings in Revision THA
Outcome Dual Mobility Fixed Bearing Evidence
Dislocation Rate 1–3% 7–11% Level I (Meta-analysis)
Mid-Term Survivorship 94–96% Variable Level II

6.2 Cemented DM Liners: A Versatile Technique

A particularly versatile approach involves cementing a DM liner into a well-fixed metal shell or cup-cage construct. This allows retention of a stable shell while upgrading the bearing to a high-stability option, avoiding the morbidity of shell removal. Level III (Retrospective, Case Series)

Clinical Recommendation Given the dramatic reduction in dislocation risk (1–3% vs. 7–11%), dual mobility bearings should be considered the standard of care in high-risk rTHA patients, including those with abductor deficiency, neurologic conditions, or recurrent instability.

7. Conclusion

Revision total hip arthroplasty has evolved from a salvage endeavor into a sophisticated reconstructive discipline. Key advances include:

  • Ultrasonic removal tools have enhanced explantation safety, preserving vital bone stock
  • Refined acetabular algorithms — jumbo cups, TM augments, cup-cage constructs, and custom 3D-printed implants — have rendered virtually no defect unreconstructable
  • Femoral reconstruction offers tailored solutions: modular tapered stems for efficiency and immediate stability; impaction grafting for bone stock restoration in the young
  • Dual mobility bearings have directly addressed instability, the leading cause of re-revision, with dramatic improvements in survivorship

As these technologies mature, the focus of rTHA will increasingly shift toward minimizing morbidity and optimizing functional restoration in this challenging patient population.
