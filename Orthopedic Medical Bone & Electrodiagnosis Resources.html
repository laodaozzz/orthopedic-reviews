Foundational Principles of Bone Metabolism and Pathophysiology

The Bone Remodeling Cycle

Bone is a dynamic, living tissue maintained through a continuous process of bone remodeling — a coupled sequence of resorption and formation carried out by discrete cellular packets known as bone multicellular units (BMUs).[1] The process proceeds through distinct phases:

  1. Activation: Osteoclast precursors differentiate into mature, multinucleated osteoclasts.
  2. Resorption: Osteoclasts adhere to the bone surface and secrete acid and proteolytic enzymes, creating a resorption cavity.[2]
  3. Reversal: Mononuclear cells prepare the surface for new bone formation.
  4. Formation: Osteoblasts synthesize and deposit new osteoid, which is subsequently mineralized.
Clinical Implication by Age In young adults, the process is tightly coupled and balanced, maintaining skeletal mass.[3] Peak bone mass is typically achieved in the late twenties.[4] After age 30–40, resorption begins to exceed formation, leading to progressive net bone loss.[3] For adolescents: maximize peak bone mass accrual through calcium, vitamin D, and high-impact exercise. For adults: attenuate age-related bone loss and maintain muscle strength and balance.[3,4]

Primary Osteoporosis

Postmenopausal Osteoporosis

Characterized by accelerated bone loss beginning with menopause. The decline in circulating estrogen is the principal driver — estrogen restrains osteoclast activity; its withdrawal increases osteoclast number, activity, and lifespan, resulting in high-turnover bone loss.[3] Trabecular bone is particularly affected, predisposing to vertebral body and distal radius fractures. Rising FSH levels may also contribute.[7]

Senile (Age-Related) Osteoporosis

A more gradual process affecting both sexes, characterized by a decline in osteoblast function and bone formation. Contributing factors include age-related hormonal decline (estrogen, testosterone), reduced IGF-1, decreased physical activity, and impaired calcium absorption leading to secondary hyperparathyroidism.[7] Affects both cortical and trabecular bone, contributing to rising hip fracture incidence in the very elderly.[3]

Secondary Causes of Osteoporosis

A substantial portion of osteoporosis cases, particularly in men, are secondary.[8] Identifying these causes is critical for preoperative planning, risk assessment for surgical complications, and effective postoperative management.

Table 1: Key Secondary Causes of Osteoporosis in Orthopedic Practice (Source: Sections 1.3)
Cause Mechanism Orthopedic Relevance
Glucocorticoid-Induced (GIOP) Direct inhibition of osteoblast function, increased osteoclast activity, impaired calcium absorption (≥5 mg/day prednisone >3 months)[10] Patients with RA, IBD, post-transplant requiring surgery. ACR guidelines for GIOP management apply.[13]
Immobilization/Disuse Absence of mechanical strain → profound increase in osteoclastic resorption[9] Vicious cycle: fracture → immobilization → worsened bone density → increased subsequent fracture risk
Hyperparathyroidism Increased bone turnover, cortical bone loss[16] Altered bone quality affecting implant fixation
Diabetes Mellitus Type 1: low BMD. Type 2: normal/high BMD but impaired bone quality and increased fall risk[9] Increased fracture risk despite apparently adequate density in T2DM
Gastrointestinal/Malabsorption Ca/Vit D deficiency → secondary hyperparathyroidism, osteomalacia[9] Celiac disease, IBD, post-gastric bypass patients
Hyperthyroidism Accelerated bone remodeling with net bone mass loss[9] Preoperative thyroid status assessment

Systematic Diagnosis and Fracture Risk Assessment

1
Clinical Evaluation Thorough history and physical examination to identify risk factors. Key risk factors include: advanced age, female sex, Caucasian/Asian ethnicity, personal/parental history of hip fracture, low BMI, smoking, excessive alcohol (≥3 units/day), long-term glucocorticoid use, adult-age low-trauma fracture.[11]
2
Height Monitoring & Vertebral Assessment Annual height measurement with stadiometer in older adults. Height loss ≥1.5 inches from peak, or ≥0.8 inches from a prior measurement, warrants vertebral imaging.[11] Assess for thoracic kyphosis, gait, balance, and muscle strength.[18]
3
Laboratory Screening Baseline: CBC, comprehensive metabolic panel (calcium, albumin, phosphate, creatinine, liver enzymes, alkaline phosphatase), serum 25-hydroxyvitamin D.[18] Additional: TSH, serum protein electrophoresis as clinically indicated.[23]
4
DXA Bone Mineral Density Gold standard for BMD measurement.[3] Indicated for all women ≥65 and men ≥70; younger postmenopausal women and men 50–69 with risk factors; any adult ≥50 with low-trauma fracture.[9,11]
5
FRAX Absolute Fracture Risk Integrates BMD with clinical risk factors to calculate 10-year probability of major osteoporotic fracture and hip fracture.[18] Primary utility: guiding treatment in osteopenic patients.

Interpreting DXA: WHO Diagnostic Criteria

The WHO criteria are based on the lowest T-score at the lumbar spine, total hip, or femoral neck:[3]

Table 2: WHO Diagnostic Categories for BMD
Category T-Score Clinical Action
Normal ≥ −1.0 Continue preventive measures; rescreen per guidelines
Low Bone Mass (Osteopenia) −1.0 to −2.5 Apply FRAX to determine if treatment threshold met
Osteoporosis ≤ −2.5 Pharmacologic treatment indicated regardless of FRAX
Severe Osteoporosis ≤ −2.5 + fragility fracture Immediate treatment; classify as "very high risk"

A Z-score of −2.0 or lower ("below expected range for age") should prompt a workup for secondary causes, especially in premenopausal women or men <50.[19]

FRAX Application and Limitations

In the US, BHOF recommends treatment consideration for osteopenic patients with a 10-year hip fracture probability ≥3% or 10-year major osteoporotic fracture probability ≥20%.[22]

FRAX Limitations (as noted in source) FRAX treats prior fracture as dichotomous (yes/no) — does not account for number, severity, or recency. A recent fracture (within 1–2 years) confers "imminent risk" not fully captured by 10-year probability.[20] FRAX does not incorporate fall history or dose-dependent glucocorticoid effects.[19] Clinical judgment remains paramount.

Vertebral Imaging Indications

Vertebral compression fractures are the most common osteoporotic fracture; up to two-thirds are asymptomatic. Their presence is a powerful predictor of future fractures and an independent treatment indication.[9]

Population T-Score Threshold
Women ≥70, Men ≥80T-score ≤ −1.0
Women 65–69, Men 70–79T-score ≤ −1.5

Non-Pharmacologic Management: The Cornerstone of Skeletal Health

Non-pharmacologic strategies form the essential foundation of all osteoporosis prevention and treatment plans and should be reinforced at every clinical encounter.[26]

Calcium and Vitamin D Supplementation

Table 4: Calcium and Vitamin D Recommendations (Source: Section 3.1)
Nutrient Population Recommended Intake Key Considerations
Calcium Men 50–70 1000 mg/day Dietary sources preferred. Supplements in divided doses (≤500–600 mg/dose).[19]
Calcium Women ≥51, Men ≥71 1200 mg/day Calcium carbonate requires food; calcium citrate independent of acid (better for PPI users).[19]
Vitamin D Adults ≥50 800–1000 IU/day (up to 2000 IU/day) Goal: serum 25(OH)D ≥30 ng/mL.[9]
Evidence Note Meta-analyses show a modest reduction in fracture risk with combined calcium and vitamin D supplementation, particularly in institutionalized elderly. Vitamin D supplementation alone has not been consistently shown to prevent fractures.[27] Level I

Exercise Prescription

An ideal program is multifaceted:[10]

Table 5: Exercise Prescription for Bone Health (Source: Section 3.2)
Type Examples Frequency Mechanism
Weight-Bearing Walking, hiking, jogging, stair climbing, dancing, racket sports ≥30 min on most days[4] Mechanical loading stimulates bone density maintenance
Resistance/Strength Free weights, machines, resistance bands, bodyweight exercises ≥2×/week, major muscle groups[4] Builds muscle mass; improves function; reduces fall risk
Balance/Postural Tai Chi, yoga, back extensor strengthening Regular incorporation Tai Chi shown to decrease falls by up to 47%[4]
Severe Osteoporosis Precautions High-impact exercises and activities involving excessive spinal bending/twisting (certain yoga poses, sit-ups) should be avoided due to increased vertebral fracture risk.[4]

Multifactorial Fall Prevention

  • Medication review: Reduce/eliminate psychotropic and sedating drugs when possible[9]
  • Medical conditions: Identify and correct orthostatic hypotension, visual impairment[9]
  • Home safety: Remove loose rugs and cords; ensure adequate lighting; install handrails/grab bars; use non-skid mats[10]
  • Footwear: Sturdy, low-heeled shoes with non-slip soles[10]

Lifestyle Modifications

  • Smoking cessation: Nicotine is directly toxic to osteoblasts; smoking affects estrogen metabolism, potentially leading to earlier menopause and accelerated bone loss.[3]
  • Alcohol limitation: >2 drinks/day (women) or >3 drinks/day (men) decreases bone formation and increases fall risk.[3,10]

Risk-Stratified Approach to Pharmacologic Intervention

Who to Treat

Therapy is strongly recommended for postmenopausal women and men ≥50 meeting any of:[22]

  1. Hip or vertebral fracture history — clinical diagnosis of severe osteoporosis
  2. T-score ≤ −2.5 at lumbar spine, total hip, or femoral neck
  3. Low bone mass (osteopenia) plus high fracture risk — FRAX: 10-year hip fracture probability ≥3% or major osteoporotic fracture probability ≥20%[22]

Risk Stratification: High vs. Very High Risk

AACE guidelines further stratify risk to guide initial therapy choice:[32]

Table 6: Risk Categories for Treatment Selection (Source: Section 4.2)
Category Criteria Preferred Initial Therapy
High Risk Meets standard treatment criteria without "very high risk" features Antiresorptive (oral bisphosphonate preferred)[18]
Very High Risk Recent fracture (within 12 months), multiple fractures, fractures on therapy, very low T-score (≤ −3.0), or very high FRAX probability[28] Anabolic agent first (teriparatide, abaloparatide, romosozumab)[28]

Pharmacologic Agents — Summary Table

Table 1 (from source): Summary of Key Pharmacologic Agents for Osteoporosis
Agent Mechanism Dosing Fracture Efficacy (V/NV/H) Key Considerations
Alendronate Bisphosphonate — inhibits osteoclast resorption 70 mg PO weekly Yes / Yes / Yes First-line. Strict dosing rules (empty stomach, upright 30 min). Holiday after 3–5 yr in lower risk.[23]
Risedronate Bisphosphonate 35 mg PO weekly or 150 mg monthly Yes / Yes / Yes First-line. Similar considerations to alendronate.[23]
Zoledronic Acid IV Bisphosphonate 5 mg IV yearly Yes / Yes / Yes For GI intolerance or adherence concerns. Spine fracture reduction ~70%, hip ~41% over 3 yr.[10,23]
Denosumab Anti-RANKL monoclonal antibody 60 mg SC every 6 months Yes / Yes / Yes Renal impairment friendly. NO drug holidays. Discontinuation requires transition to bisphosphonate to prevent rebound bone loss and vertebral fractures.[23,28]
Teriparatide / Abaloparatide PTH/PTHrP analog — stimulates osteoblasts Daily SC self-injection Yes / Yes / No (TP) or Yes (ABA) Very high risk. Limited to 18–24 months. Must be followed by antiresorptive.[21]
Romosozumab Anti-sclerostin — dual: ↑ formation, ↓ resorption 210 mg SC monthly × 12 months Yes / Yes / Yes Very high risk, postmenopausal women. Must follow with antiresorptive. CV warning: avoid if MI/stroke within 1 year.[28]
Raloxifene SERM — estrogen agonist in bone 60 mg PO daily Yes / No / No Option for younger postmenopausal women with spinal osteoporosis, especially with breast cancer risk. Increases VTE risk.[23]
Critical Warning: Denosumab Discontinuation Unlike bisphosphonates, denosumab effects are rapidly reversible. Missing or delaying a dose causes rapid rebound bone turnover, swift BMD loss, and an increased risk of multiple spontaneous vertebral fractures.[28] A drug holiday is NEVER appropriate with denosumab. If discontinued, transition to a bisphosphonate.

Treatment Duration and Sequential Therapy

The Bisphosphonate Holiday

After 5 years of oral bisphosphonate or 3 years of IV zoledronic acid, a drug holiday of up to 5 years may be considered in patients no longer at high risk (improved T-score, no new fractures).[18] Reassess fracture risk every 2–4 years; reinitiate if BMD declines, fracture occurs, or risk factors increase.[33] High-risk patients may continue up to 10 years (oral) or 6 years (IV zoledronic acid).[23]

Sequential Therapy Strategy

The most effective paradigm for very-high-risk patients: "build bone first" with an anabolic agent, then "lock in gains" with a potent antiresorptive. For example, 12 months of romosozumab followed by alendronate is superior to alendronate alone.[23] Similarly, teriparatide or abaloparatide courses must be followed by bisphosphonate or denosumab.[21]

The source document acknowledges that evidence levels vary by agent and indication. Specific Level I evidence from landmark RCTs is referenced for bisphosphonates (e.g., alendronate fracture reduction ~50%),[10] zoledronic acid,[10] denosumab,[21] and romosozumab.[23,28] The source does not provide a formal ranked evidence table for each agent — evidence levels are inferred from study designs described.

Electrodiagnosis: Principles and Clinical Application

Purpose of EDX Studies

An EDX study is a dynamic consultation — not a standalone screening test — performed by a neuromuscular specialist, tailored in real-time based on history, exam, and initial findings.[39,41] Primary goals for the referring orthopedic surgeon:[42]

  • Confirm & characterize suspected neuropathy (e.g., carpal tunnel syndrome)
  • Localize the lesion (e.g., C6 radiculopathy vs. median neuropathy vs. brachial plexopathy)
  • Determine severity and differentiate demyelinating from axonal pathology
  • Identify concomitant or alternative pathology
  • Prognosticate outcomes for non-operative vs. operative management

Nerve Conduction Studies (NCS): Assessing the "Highway"

NCS involves stimulating a peripheral nerve and recording the response, directly assessing large, myelinated nerve fiber function.[41]

Table 7: Key NCS Parameters (Source: Section 5.2)
Parameter Unit Abnormality Significance
Latency ms Prolonged Hallmark of demyelination — signal delayed[42]
Conduction Velocity m/s Slowed (focal) Classic sign of focal demyelination (compressive neuropathy)[42]
Amplitude mV (motor) / µV (sensory) Reduced Primary sign of axonal loss — fewer functioning fibers[42]

Needle EMG: Interrogating the "Target"

Needle EMG assesses the motor unit (anterior horn cell → axon → neuromuscular junction → muscle fibers).[44]

At Rest

Healthy muscle is electrically silent. Fibrillation potentials and positive sharp waves = spontaneous discharges from denervated muscle fibers — the definitive sign of active axonal injury. Appear 2–3 weeks post-injury.[44]

During Contraction

  • Neurogenic changes: Large, long-duration, polyphasic MUAPs → chronic injury with reinnervation[44]
  • Myopathic changes: Small, short-duration, polyphasic MUAPs → primary muscle disease[44]

Demyelinating vs. Axonal Injury — Critical Distinction

Table 8: Demyelinating vs. Axonal Injury (Source: Section 5.4)
Feature Demyelinating (Neurapraxia) Axonal (Axonotmesis)
Pathology Myelin sheath damage; axon intact Axon damage
Common cause Mild-moderate nerve compression Severe/prolonged compression or trauma
NCS signature Focal slowing of CV; prolonged latencies; conduction block at lesion; normal below[42] Markedly reduced SNAP/CMAP amplitudes; CV/latencies normal or mildly slowed in surviving axons[46]
EMG signature Normal or reduced recruitment only; no denervation[42] Fibrillation potentials & positive sharp waves confirming active denervation[46]
Prognosis Excellent — rapid, complete recovery after decompression (remyelination) Guarded & slow — axonal regeneration ~1 mm/day; may be incomplete
Clinical Example (from source) A patient with wrist drop after humeral shaft fracture: if EDX shows only demyelinating conduction block → excellent prognosis for spontaneous recovery; non-operative management. If EDX shows absent responses and widespread fibrillations → severe axonal injury; consider early surgical exploration.[42,46]

Key Differentiator: Sensory NCS in Radiculopathy

The dorsal root ganglion (DRG) lies within the neuroforamen. In radiculopathy, compression is proximal to the DRG — the peripheral sensory axon remains connected to its healthy cell body, so the SNAP is normal. In plexopathy or peripheral nerve lesion, injury is distal to the DRG, causing Wallerian degeneration — the SNAP is abnormal.[44]

When to Order EDX

Maximize utility by ordering with a specific clinical question (e.g., "Evaluate for left ulnar neuropathy at elbow vs. C8 radiculopathy").[40] Most valuable when diagnosis is uncertain, localization needed, or severity assessment required. Less useful as a low-yield screening tool for non-specific symptoms.[42]

Interpreting the EDX Report

Key components of a quality report:[50]

  • Clear clinical context with referral question
  • Tabulated NCS and EMG data with normal reference values
  • Documentation of limb temperature
  • Narrative summary synthesizing findings into a coherent impression that directly answers the clinical question
Limitations of EDX A "normal" study does not rule out pathology. Mild CTS has false-negative rates of 10–20%.[42] Pure sensory radiculopathy may show no EMG abnormalities. Mild abnormalities may exist in asymptomatic individuals. Always integrate with clinical findings, history, and imaging.
