Surgical Management of Patellar Instability in Skeletally Immature Patients with Elevated TT-TG Distance

Comparative Analysis of Isolated versus Combined Physeal-Sparing Reconstruction on Patellar Kinematics

1. Pathomechanical Basis of Patellar Instability in the Growing Knee

Recurrent patellar instability in the skeletally immature patient is a complex clinical challenge arising from a confluence of static, dynamic, and developmental factors. The decision to intervene surgically—and the choice of procedure—must be predicated on a profound understanding of the underlying pathomechanics. In patients with an elevated tibial tubercle-trochlear groove (TT-TG) distance, the biomechanical derangements are particularly pronounced, often necessitating a more comprehensive surgical strategy than what might be employed in patients with isolated soft-tissue deficiencies.

1.1 Static and Dynamic Stabilizers

Normal patellar tracking results from a finely tuned equilibrium among three components:

  • Medial Patellofemoral Ligament (MPFL): The primary static stabilizer against lateral patellar displacement, providing approximately 50–60% of the medial restraining force, particularly within the initial 20° to 30° of knee flexion—the range where the patella is most vulnerable before engaging the trochlea. Level I–II [1, 3]
  • Vastus Medialis Oblique (VMO): The principal dynamic stabilizer, exerting a critical medializing pull that opposes the lateral vector from the vastus lateralis. VMO atrophy or dysfunction following initial injury allows lateralizing forces to dominate. Level III [6]
  • Trochlear Geometry: Beyond 30° flexion, a well-formed, deep trochlea acts as a bony buttress containing the patella and preventing lateral excursion during deeper flexion. Level I–II [1]
Essential Lesion of Patellar Instability The MPFL is ruptured in >90% of acute dislocations and nearly 100% of recurrent dislocations, rendering MPFL insufficiency the cornerstone pathology. Its reconstruction is the foundation of modern surgical treatment. [3]

1.2 Congenital and Developmental Risk Factors

Trochlear Dysplasia Level II [8]: Characterized by a shallow, flattened, or even convex trochlear groove, this is one of the most significant predictors of both recurrent instability and surgical failure. A dysplastic trochlea forces the patella to rely almost exclusively on soft-tissue restraints. In children under age 10, evidence suggests the trochlea can undergo some degree of remodeling following early stabilization. [10]

Patella Alta Level II [3]: Defined by a Caton-Deschamps Index (CDI) >1.2–1.3, a high-riding patella delays engagement within the trochlear groove, extending the vulnerable arc where the MPFL is the sole restraint and placing it under increased chronic strain.

Generalized Ligamentous Laxity Level III [4]: Assessed using the Beighton score, systemic hyperlaxity compromises native MPFL integrity and may affect long-term stability of a reconstructed ligament through graft attenuation.

Coronal and Rotational Malalignment Level III [9]: Genu valgum or excessive femoral anteversion alter the mechanical axis and increase the lateral force vector on the patella, associated with inferior clinical outcomes if unaddressed.

1.3 The Critical Influence of the TT-TG Distance

The TT-TG distance, typically obtained from axial CT or MRI, quantifies the lateral offset of the tibial tubercle relative to the deepest point of the femoral trochlear groove. It serves as a proxy for the quadriceps angle (Q-angle), representing the magnitude of the laterally directed force vector on the patella. Level I–II [1, 4, 15]

Key Biomechanical Principle: Unlike trochlear dysplasia (which affects stability mainly in deeper flexion), a lateralized tibial tubercle creates a constant, abnormal lateralizing force throughout the entire range of motion. This chronic lateral pull places immense and continuous strain on the MPFL, predisposing it to failure. A patient with mild trochlear dysplasia and normal TT-TG may remain stable, but the same degree of dysplasia with TT-TG of 22 mm becomes a profound clinical problem.

1.4 The Unique Challenge of the Open Physis

Surgical management in children and adolescents is fundamentally constrained by open physes. The distal femoral physis has a complex, three-dimensional, undulating topography. The anatomic MPFL insertion (Schöttle's point) is in very close proximity to this physis, and its relationship changes with age—moving closer to or even proximal to the physeal line in older adolescents. Level IV [10]

Risk of Iatrogenic Physeal Injury Standard adult techniques involving transverse femoral bone tunnels risk premature growth arrest, limb length discrepancy, or angular deformities (genu valgum, recurvatum). Similarly, traditional tibial tubercle osteotomy (TTO) is contraindicated due to the open tibial apophysis. [11, 19]
The "Moving Target" Problem Recent evidence demonstrates that in adolescents with recurrent patellar instability, the TT-TG distance can either increase or decrease during the final growth spurt—challenging the assumption of linear, predictable growth. A 13-year-old with a borderline TT-TG of 18 mm presents a clinical dilemma: will it increase to a pathological 23 mm, or remodel to a normal 14 mm? This temporal uncertainty represents a key challenge for surgical planning. Level III [25]

2. Radiographic Quantification of Patellofemoral Kinematics

2.1 Patellar Tilt Angle (PTA)

Patellar tilt describes the coronal plane orientation of the patella relative to the femur, measured on axial MRI or CT at the level of maximum patellar width. The angle is formed between a line tangential to the posterior femoral condyles and a line along the maximal axis of the patella. Level III [26]

  • Normal PTA: < 15°
  • Pathological PTA: > 20° — strong indicator of lateral malalignment

An increased PTA leads to increased lateral patellofemoral contact pressures (Excessive Lateral Pressure Syndrome), anterior knee pain, chondral damage risk, and reduced medial facet engagement with the trochlea. [26]

Static vs. Dynamic Instability Standard static MRI is performed with the knee in extension and quadriceps relaxed. Studies show that in patients with instability, patellar tilt and lateral displacement can become pathologically abnormal only during active quadriceps contraction. Static imaging may therefore underestimate the true extent of maltracking. [28]

2.2 Patellar Rotation

Patellar rotation describes movement around the longitudinal (anteroposterior) axis and is technically demanding to measure in vivo, typically requiring dual orthogonal fluoroscopy combined with 3D computer modeling. In healthy subjects, the mean total excursion of patellar rotation through a full flexion arc is approximately 8°—establishing a very narrow window for normal biomechanics. Level IV [29]

⚠ Evidence Gap: The role of patellar rotation in instability and its response to surgical correction is poorly understood due to the difficulty of routine clinical measurement. This parameter is likely underappreciated in current practice.

2.3 TT-TG Distance: Methodological Considerations and Threshold Controversies

Critical: Measurement Modality Mismatch TT-TG values measured on CT are consistently and significantly higher than those on MRI. The classic pathological threshold of >20 mm (established by Dejour et al.) was based on CT measurements. Applying a CT-derived cutoff to an MRI measurement is fundamentally flawed and risks inappropriate surgical planning. [17]

The literature presents a wide and conflicting range of thresholds:

  • MRI-based >13 mm: Suggested to rule in patellofemoral instability (twice as likely in instability vs. controls). [32]
  • 15–20 mm range: Frequently cited as "borderline" abnormal. [33]
  • >20 mm (CT): Widely considered definitively abnormal and a strong indication for tibial tubercle transfer. [1]
  • >25 mm: Some studies suggest isolated MPFL reconstruction can be safe even at this level if other risk factors are absent. [35]

Research comparing symptomatic and asymptomatic knees within the same individual found no statistically significant difference in TT-TG measurements, suggesting the value alone may not be a decisive element and should be interpreted with caution. Level III [31]

3. Physeal-Sparing Surgical Interventions

3.1 Isolated Physeal-Sparing MPFL Reconstruction (MPFL-R)

The biomechanical goal is to restore the primary static soft-tissue checkrein against lateral patellar translation, providing stability particularly in the vulnerable early range of knee flexion. [36]

Key Technical Principles:

  • Graft Material: Autograft (semitendinosus/gracilis) or allograft; evidence shows no significant difference in outcomes or failure rates. Level II [23]
  • Patellar Fixation: Small-diameter transpatellar tunnels or, more commonly, suture anchors to minimize fracture risk. [21]
  • Femoral Fixation (Critical): Anatomic placement at Schöttle's point under fluoroscopy. Trajectory directed distally and anteriorly to avoid the physis. Hand reamers preferred over power drills to reduce thermal damage risk. [4]
  • Graft Tensioning: Performed in 30°–70° flexion; over-tensioning risks excessive medial constraint, increased medial patellofemoral contact pressures, pain, and accelerated osteoarthritis. [4]

Evidence generally supports anatomic reconstructions over non-anatomic techniques (e.g., adductor tendon sling), with more favorable patient-reported outcomes and lower redislocation rates. Level II [2]

3.2 The Roux-Goldthwait (R-G) Procedure

A soft-tissue distal realignment technique designed to medialize the effective insertion of the patellar tendon, decreasing the Q-angle and reducing the lateralizing force vector. Level IV [1]

Technique: The patellar tendon is split vertically. The lateral half is detached from the tibial tubercle, transferred medially (deep or superficial to the intact medial half), and sutured to the periosteum on the medial proximal tibia or pes anserinus tendons. This is entirely a soft-tissue procedure, preserving the tibial growth plate. [44]

Primary Indication: Recurrent patellar instability in a skeletally immature patient with a pathologically elevated TT-TG distance, where traditional tibial tubercle osteotomy is contraindicated due to the open tibial apophysis. [19]

3.3 The Combined Procedure

The rationale for pairing a physeal-sparing MPFL reconstruction with a Roux-Goldthwait distal realignment is to provide a comprehensive solution for patients with multifactorial instability—addressing both the deficient MPFL (proximal pathology) and the significant lateralizing force vector from an elevated TT-TG (distal pathology). [1]

Surgical Workflow: Typically the R-G is performed first to establish the new medialized alignment, followed by MPFL reconstruction with graft tensioning relative to this corrected alignment, theoretically reducing the risk of graft attenuation or failure. [1]

4.1 Efficacy in Correcting Patellar Tilt

Level III Evidence from a retrospective comparative study [37] demonstrates that while both procedures reduce postoperative tilt, the combined procedure provides biomechanically superior correction (6.6° vs. 3.9° improvement). A study evaluating combined MPFL reconstruction with semi-patellar ligament displacement in adolescents reported a mean postoperative tilt of 9.89°, well within the normal range. [19]

Clinical Trade-off: The combined procedure offers more robust correction of patellar tilt (linked to abnormal cartilage loading and future arthritis risk), but this must be weighed against increased surgical complexity, operative time, and potential for higher morbidity.

4.2 Impact on Patellar Rotation

Critical Knowledge Gap: "Rotation Question Mark" None of the reviewed studies provided direct comparative analysis of postoperative patellar rotation. The R-G procedure, by medially transferring only the lateral half of the patellar tendon, introduces a potential rotational moment and risk of iatrogenic internal malrotation. An isolated MPFL reconstruction cannot effectively resist this induced rotational force. The combined procedure may solve lateral translation/tilt while simultaneously creating a new, unmeasured problem of abnormal rotation. [42]

4.3 Rates of Recurrent Instability

It is crucial to distinguish between catastrophic failure (redislocation) and residual instability (subluxations, apprehension, lack of confidence).

Isolated MPFL-R: Redislocation rates range from 0% to 24.1%. Skeletally immature patients experience higher rates of postoperative recurrent instability compared to mature counterparts. Level II [22, 50]

Combined Procedure: Limited data. A systematic review of various "global" soft tissue procedures found a recurrent dislocation rate of 8.6%, but a concerning rate of recurrent residual instability of 40.2%. Level II [7]

4.4 Functional Outcomes and Return to Sport

Both procedures lead to significant improvements in patient-reported outcome measures (Kujala, IKDC, BPII 2.0). Currently available comparative data does not show clear superiority of one procedure over the other with respect to final functional scores. Level III [19]

  • Isolated MPFL-R: Return-to-sport rates of ≥87.5%. [47]
  • Combined Procedure: Comparable return-to-sport rate of ~80%. [20]

4.5 Complication Profiles

Isolated MPFL-R: Recurrent instability, stiffness, wound complications, rare iatrogenic patellar fracture or physeal disturbance. [55]

Combined Procedure: All risks of isolated MPFL-R plus risks specific to R-G: potential over-distalization increasing patellofemoral contact pressures (theoretical OA risk), concern for inducing patellar malrotation. Systematic reviews indicate combined surgeries are associated with higher overall complication rates. Level II [7, 19]

5. Clinical Decision-Making Framework

5.1 When Is Isolated MPFL-R Sufficient?

Indicated when recurrent instability is primarily driven by MPFL insufficiency without significant bony malalignment. Ideal candidates have:

  • TT-TG <15–17 mm (CT) or correspondingly lower on MRI
  • No high-grade trochlear dysplasia (Dejour C/D) or severe patella alta
  • Confidence that the reconstructed MPFL can withstand native biomechanical forces [35]

5.2 Indication for Combined Procedure

Primary indication: pathologically elevated TT-TG distance, signifying significant lateralization of the extensor mechanism pull. Additional supporting factors include:

  • Significant preoperative patellar tilt (>20°)
  • Positive J-sign on dynamic examination
  • History of failure of prior soft-tissue stabilization
  • High-demand pivoting sports participation [1]

5.3 Tiered Approach Based on TT-TG Distance

  • Tier 1 — TT-TG <17 mm (CT) / <14 mm (MRI): Lateralizing vector within normal limits. Isolated physeal-sparing MPFL reconstruction is indicated.
  • Tier 2 — TT-TG 17–22 mm (CT) / 14–18 mm (MRI) — "Gray Zone": Decision requires careful clinical judgment. Presence of high-grade tilt (>20°), positive J-sign, prior surgical failure, or high-demand sport favors combined procedure. The dynamic nature of TT-TG during growth is most relevant here.
  • Tier 3 — TT-TG >22 mm (CT) / >18 mm (MRI): Significant pathological lateralization. Strong indication for combined procedure. Isolated MPFL-R carries high risk of long-term failure.

6. Synthesis and Expert Recommendations

6.1 Key Findings

  1. Recurrent instability is driven by dual pathologies: MPFL insufficiency (proximal) and excessive lateral force from elevated TT-TG (distal).
  2. Isolated physeal-sparing MPFL-R is safe and effective but its efficacy may be limited when the lateral vector remains uncorrected.
  3. The combined procedure provides statistically superior correction of patellar tilt (6.6° vs. 3.9° improvement).
  4. The biomechanical advantage must be weighed against increased surgical complexity, higher complication profiles, and the theoretical risk of iatrogenic patellar malrotation.
  5. There is no definitive evidence that the combined procedure is superior in preventing redislocation (meta-analysis shows no statistically significant difference in recurrent instability rates). [51]
  6. Functional outcomes and return-to-sport rates are comparable between the two approaches.

6.2 Clinical Practice Recommendations

  1. Comprehensive Preoperative Evaluation: Mandatory clinical examination (dynamic tracking/J-sign, laxity, alignment) complemented by MRI (preferred for soft-tissue detail, chondral assessment, and avoiding ionizing radiation).
  2. Modality-Specific TT-TG Thresholds: Adopt the tiered approach with CT- and MRI-specific cutoffs to avoid systematic measurement error.
  3. Combined Procedure for Pathological TT-TG: For patients with TT-TG >22 mm (CT) or >18 mm (MRI) and significant tilt, combined physeal-sparing MPFL-R + R-G should be strongly considered.
  4. Individualized Approach for Gray Zone: Borderline TT-TG cases require global assessment of risk factors, activity level, and dynamic examination findings.
  5. Intraoperative Assessment: After R-G transfer but before final MPFL tensioning, carefully assess patellar tracking and rotation to ensure the distal realignment has not created an abnormal rotational profile.

6.3 Future Research Directives

  1. Prospective Comparative Trials: Urgently needed RCTs directly comparing isolated vs. combined procedures in skeletally immature patients with defined elevated TT-TG.
  2. Inclusion of Rotational Analysis: Future studies must include patellar rotation as a primary radiographic outcome using 3D-CT or weight-bearing MRI protocols.
  3. Long-Term Follow-Up (10+ years): Required to determine if superior tilt correction translates to lower patellofemoral osteoarthritis rates.
  4. Longitudinal Growth Studies: Investigation into the dynamic, potentially non-linear changes of TT-TG during adolescent growth to develop predictive models for surgical timing.
