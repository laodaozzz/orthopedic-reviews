Extended Trochanteric Osteotomy in Revision Total Hip Arthroplasty

Comprehensive Evidence-Based Educational Review · Adapted from Source Document · Senior Resident & Fellow Level

1. Introduction & Historical Evolution

Total hip arthroplasty (THA) is widely celebrated as one of the most successful surgical interventions in modern medicine, providing profound pain relief and functional restoration for patients with end-stage degenerative, inflammatory, and post-traumatic hip arthropathies. However, the exponential rise in primary joint replacements—coupled with a shifting demographic toward younger, more active patients—has precipitated a proportional increase in revision total hip arthroplasty (rTHA). Revision surgery presents formidable anatomical and technical challenges: compromised soft-tissue envelopes, distorted anatomy, significant periprosthetic bone loss, and the necessity of extracting well-fixed implants without inflicting catastrophic iatrogenic damage to host bone.1-4

Historically, accessing the femoral canal and extracting retained components relied on aggressive endosteal burring, forceful retrograde impaction, and extraction devices that transmitted massive stresses to the fragile proximal femur. These maneuvers frequently resulted in cortical perforations, diaphyseal fractures, and irreversible depletion of proximal bone stock.3,12

From Charnley to the Modern ETO

The conceptual foundation for expansile femoral access was established by Sir John Charnley, who routinely utilized a standard transtrochanteric approach involving transverse or oblique osteotomy of the greater trochanter. While this offered unparalleled 360° exposure, nonunion rates reached 20% in revision settings, accompanied by wire breakage, trochanteric migration, and profound abductor dysfunction.1,3

Glassman et al. (late 1980s–1990s) introduced the trochanteric slide osteotomy, maintaining continuity of the vastus lateralis distally and gluteus medius/minimus proximally. This continuous musculo-tendinous unit acted as a tension band, vastly improving union rates compared to Charnley's approach. Glassman's early results in 90 cases demonstrated the utility of this musculoosseous-muscular sleeve.1,7

The contemporary Extended Trochanteric Osteotomy (ETO) represents the zenith of this evolution. Initially described by Wagner as an anteriorly based transfemoral osteotomy, the technique was refined and popularized by Younger et al. (1995). Their critical review of 166 osteotomies with ~4-year follow-up demonstrated a nonunion rate of only 1.2%, firmly establishing the ETO's reliability.3,8

In 2003, Paprosky et al. formalized a laterally based ETO through a standard posterior approach—rapidly becoming the workhorse of modern revision THA, offering high union rates, efficient operative times, and reproducible results.3

Core Philosophy

A controlled, meticulously planned osteotomy that undergoes predictable secondary bone healing is vastly superior to an uncontrolled, chaotic iatrogenic fracture with an unpredictable, and often disastrous, clinical trajectory.12

2. Primary Indications & Patient Selection

The clinical threshold for performing an ETO is dictated by a careful calculus balancing specific anatomical challenges, the nature of in situ implants, host bone quality, and the selected reconstructive strategy.

2.1 Aseptic Loosening & Well-Fixed Implant Extraction Level III

The most frequent indication—accounting for 63.4% of cases in systematic reviews—is aseptic loosening requiring removal of retained femoral implants.13 The ETO is strongly indicated for:

  • Well-fixed, fully porous-coated or extensively coated cementless stems that achieve rigid biologic fixation (osseointegration) over massive surface areas. Blind disruption from a proximal approach frequently causes longitudinal diaphyseal splitting or unrecoverable cortical thinning.3,14
  • Well-fixed cemented stems with deeply retained, well-interdigitated distal cement mantles, cement plugs, or distal pedestals. Attempting to clear tough distal cement blindly carries extraordinarily high risk of false routes or anterior cortical perforation.6,16

The ETO permits direct, brightly illuminated visualization of the entire cement mantle and bone-implant interface, enabling precise disruption with thin osteotomes, Gigli saws, or high-speed pencil-tip burrs without violating medial, anterior, or posterior cortices.3,14

2.2 Correction of Proximal Femoral Deformity Level III

Significant proximal femoral deformity—most notably varus remodeling—is a major indication. When a loose, undersized, or short femoral component toggles within the medullary canal, the proximal femur undergoes gradual varus remodeling, hinging around the distal tip of the failed implant. Paprosky and Martin documented varus deformation in up to 30% of aseptic loosening cases.4

If ignored, attempting to ream and insert a long, straight revision stem inevitably leads to varus malalignment or anterior perforation. The ETO circumvents this: osteotomizing the deformed segment allows straight reaming down the true anatomical axis of the intact distal diaphysis.4,14

2.3 Periprosthetic Joint Infection (PJI) Level I

The ETO has emerged as a critical tool in PJI, particularly during the first stage of two-stage exchange arthroplasty or carefully selected single-stage exchanges.11 The fundamental principle relies on complete removal of all foreign material—retained implants, bone cement, cement restrictors, and biofilm-laden debris. Without expansile exposure, microscopic cement fragments or residual biofilm can serve as persistent nidi for recurrent infection.

Historical Dogma Refuted

Contemporary data decisively refutes the historical concern that ETO in infected fields invites nonunion. Systematic reviews demonstrate that ETO does not negatively impact infection eradication rates, and union rates in septic environments are statistically comparable to aseptic revisions.3,11

2.4 Periprosthetic Fractures Level III

The ETO is invaluable for complex periprosthetic fractures, particularly Vancouver B2 (loose stem, adequate bone) and Vancouver B3 (loose stem, compromised bone). Existing fracture lines can be incorporated into osteotomy design, converting jagged, unpredictable fractures into controlled approaches while enabling safe stem extraction, accurate bypass fixation, and anatomical reduction with rigid cerclage or cable-plate constructs.3

2.5 Complex Primary Arthroplasty Level III

The ETO is occasionally indicated in severe developmental dysplasia of the hip (DDH), specifically Crowe Type IV (high dislocation). Luo et al. demonstrated efficacy in 19 patients (23 hips) with 8–12 cm ETO, achieving excellent exposure, facilitating femoral shortening, significantly lowering nerve palsy risk, and achieving 100% radiographic union at 1 year.1 Cagri Ors et al. analyzed 127 Crowe IV hips treated with transverse subtrochanteric osteotomy and Wagner-cone stem, demonstrating 94.5% 10-year prosthetic survival.1

2.6 Contraindications

Absolute Contraindication

Intent to use a fully cemented femoral prosthesis for definitive reconstruction. Pressurized PMMA cement will extrude through the osteotomy site before polymerization, interposing between bone fragments and almost guaranteeing nonunion.13,17

Relative Contraindication

Severely compromised, paper-thin cortical bone from massive endosteal osteolysis. Cables can "cheese-wire" through the cortex, causing complete fragmentation and catastrophic destruction of the osteotomy fragment.15,17

3. Anatomical & Biomechanical Principles

The ETO's success is predicated on understanding proximal femoral anatomy, vascular biology, and reconstructive biomechanics. The goal is not simply to resect bone, but to successfully mobilize and reintegrate a functional "musculoosseous-muscular sleeve."7

3.1 The Musculotendinous Sleeve & Vascular Preservation

The typical ETO fragment comprises the lateral one-third to one-half of the proximal femoral diaphysis, extending proximally to include the entire greater trochanter.3 Critical soft tissue attachments include:

  • Proximally: Gluteus medius and gluteus minimus (inserting on the greater trochanter)
  • Distally: Vastus lateralis (originating from the vastus ridge and lateral diaphysis)2

Preserving these attachments serves two non-negotiable biological functions:

  1. Vascular preservation: The continuous musculo-tendinous sleeve maintains extramedullary periosteal blood supply via penetrating vascular perforators from the vastus lateralis. In revision settings, the native endosteal supply is obliterated by prior implants, reaming, and cement. Excessive stripping leads directly to avascular necrosis of the fragment, drastically increasing nonunion risk.16
  2. Tension band mechanism: The abductors' proximal pull is counterbalanced by the vastus lateralis' distal pull. If the vastus lateralis is detached, this equilibrium is destroyed, leading to superior migration—"trochanteric escape."3,12

3.2 Biomechanical Impact of the Osteotomy

Robust cadaveric studies have quantified the structural deficit created by an ETO:

  • Zderic, Gueorguiev et al.: ETO reduces torsional strength (torque to failure) by 73% compared to intact controls.22
  • Even after perfect reduction and rigid repair with hardware plus a well-fixed long-stem prosthesis, torsional strength remains ~23% weaker than an intact femur with a similar stem.4
  • A significant, direct relationship exists between host bone mineral density (BMD) and ultimate torque to failure in reconstructed models.23
Clinical Implications

The definitive revision stem must achieve rigid, scratch-fit fixation in intact diaphyseal bone distal to the osteotomy, bypassing the distal extent by a minimum of two cortical diameters (4–6 cm) to dissipate stress risers.3,4

4. Key Surgical Techniques & Approaches

4.1 Laterally Based Osteotomy (Paprosky Method) Most Common

Introduced in 2003, performed through a standard posterior approach, yielding a posterolateral bone fragment.3

  1. Prophylactic cerclage cable placed 1–2 cm distal to the planned osteotomy distal extent—serves as a mechanical "crack-arrester."3,16
  2. Length: Typically 12–16 cm from the greater trochanter tip, calculated preoperatively based on implant length, cement mantle, or distal hardware.5,16
  3. Posterior longitudinal limb executed first with thin-bladed oscillating saw from trochanter tip distally along the posterolateral femur.16
  4. Transverse limb at predetermined distal extent, involving no more than one-third of the diaphyseal circumference. Critical refinement: corners rounded with high-speed pencil-tip burr to dissipate stress risers and prevent fracture.16
  5. Anterior longitudinal limb only partially cut or weakened with serial drill holes—completed via controlled fracture using stacked osteotomes, creating a greenstick-type fracture minimizing devascularization.16
  6. Fragment gently retracted anteriorly on its soft-tissue hinge. Endosteal debridement of the fragment is delayed until closure to protect the delicate vascular network.16

4.2 Anteriorly Based Osteotomy (Wagner & Younger Method)

Primarily anteriorly based, performed through a direct lateral or anterolateral approach, yielding a fragment including the anterior one-third of the proximal femur.3 The vastus lateralis is elevated with careful management of perforating vessels. Posterior cortex is perforated with burr holes or saw, and the fragment is levered anteriorly.3,16

4.3 Modifications for Modern Approaches

  • Direct Anterior Approach (DAA) modification (Morcos et al., 2020): Extended exposure via vastus lateralis split or posterior-directed S-shaped incision; saw cut from anterolateral to posterior cortex; fragment displaced laterally.3
  • Lakstein modification (2011): Direct lateral approach; posterior cut placed posterior to gluteus medius insertion but anterior to short external rotators, theoretically reducing posterior dislocation risk.3

4.4 Emerging Technology: 3D-Printed Patient-Specific Guides

Cadaveric studies have explored 3D-printed cutting guides based on preoperative CT scans. These custom guides conform to the patient's posterior femoral topography, directing the oscillating saw precisely along a pre-calculated trajectory—accurate to within millimeters—potentially improving bone healing likelihood and reducing trochanteric escape risk.24

Note: While promising, 3D-printed cutting guides are not yet standard of care. Evidence is limited to cadaveric biomechanical studies.

5. Fixation Methods & Construct Stability

After stem implantation, the ETO fragment requires rigid, anatomical fixation against host bone and prosthesis to achieve secondary bone healing and prevent proximal migration.2

5.1 Cerclage Cables & Wires

The historical gold standard—still most widely used—involves multifilament braided metal cables or heavy double-stranded Luque wires. Standard protocol: 3–4 cables to secure the construct without excessive periosteal strangulation. One or two cables placed around the diaphyseal segment; at least one placed superior to the lesser trochanter to maximize compressive forces.16

Vulnerabilities: Fatigue failure, fraying, or breakage under cyclical abductor loading; "cheese-wiring" through osteopenic cortex leading to loss of fixation.2,15

5.2 Cable-Plate Constructs & Claw Devices

Sheridan et al. compared cables alone (19 cases) vs. cable-plate construct (11 cases) in acute periprosthetic fractures with ETO. The cable-plate group showed:

  • Mean proximal migration 1.7 mm lower (p < 0.05)
  • Significantly better Modified Harris Hip Scores (p < 0.05)
  • Superior radiographic outcomes (Beals & Tower classification, p < 0.01)20

Spider clamp / trochanteric claw devices feature proximal hooks capturing the greater trochanter superiorly, with a plate extending distally secured by cables. Biomechanically superior against vertical and lateral tension forces.3,15 However, bulkier implants can cause trochanteric bursa irritation—21 patients in one series required plate removal for persistent lateral hip pain, though underlying union was successful.25

5.3 Novel Strategies

Non-metallic suture tapes: Biomechanical cadaveric study evaluated heavy non-metallic suture tapes against steel wires and cables. Under cyclical loading, suture tape showed the highest displacement (up to 5.8 mm at 300 N) and lowest compressive force (303 N) vs. metal cables (1015 N). Current non-metallic tape lacks the mechanical profile for rigid ETO fixation.28

Monocortical screw technique: A periprosthetic monocortical screw is driven into the trochanteric fragment distal to the most proximal cable. As the abductors pull superiorly, the fragment moves until the screw head abuts the cable, physically counteracting vertical shear forces—a minimally invasive solution to trochanteric escape.21

6. Clinical Outcomes & Long-Term Survivorship

6.1 Union Rates & Timelines Level I

Healing of the ETO is highly predictable, facilitated by the massive cancellous surface area, rigid cable compression, and preserved vascularity from the intact vastus lateralis.8

  • Cumulative union rate: 94.0%–98.6% across large meta-analyses13
  • Union timeline: 10.6–15.6 weeks (average ~12 weeks / 3–6 months)13

Stem type influence: Uncemented extensively porous-coated stems achieve union rates up to 99.1%; modular fluted tapered stems ~90.2%. Nonunion rate with uncemented stems is approximately 3× lower than cemented stems.13

6.2 Functional Improvement

Patient-Reported Outcome Measures demonstrate profound improvement:

  • Harris Hip Scores (HHS): Preoperative 45–57 → Postoperative 77–87 (average improvement ~45.5 points)16
  • Merle D'Aubigné & Postel scores: Average improvement from 5.3 to 10.0, indicating marked pain reduction and ambulation gains19

6.3 Long-Term Survivorship Level III

  • Mayo Clinic 10-year data:
    • Survivorship free of revision for aseptic femoral loosening: 97%
    • Survivorship free of any component removal: 91%
    • Survivorship free of reoperation for any reason: 82%
  • 15-year survival free from stem-related failure: ~93.9%35
Key Finding

The deliberate creation of a massive bony defect via the ETO does not compromise the ultimate longevity of the revision construct.35

6.4 ETO in Periprosthetic Joint Infection Level I

ETO union rate in septic patients: 94.8%–96.8%—statistically comparable to aseptic cohorts.13 Infection eradication rates: 91.6%–96.0%.11

A retrospective study comparing single-stage vs. two-stage exchange with ETO showed:

  • Infection clearance: 96% (single-stage) vs. 92% (two-stage)
  • Two-stage procedures experienced higher mechanical complication rates (20% vs. 9%), largely attributable to spacer morbidity and re-operative scar tissue37,39
  • No difference in time to union or overall union rate between patients requiring osteotomy reopening during second-stage surgery vs. those who did not19

7.1 Trochanteric Escape

The most devastating complication—proximal migration of the osteotomized fragment due to fixation failure. The gluteus medius/minimus pull acts continuously; if the vastus lateralis is overly stripped or cables fatigue/fail, rapid superior migration occurs.12,21

  • Expected early settling: Mean ~3 mm before union16
  • Clinically significant escape: >10 mm in ~7% of cases16
  • Consequences: Profound abductor weakness, Trendelenburg limp, intractable lateral hip pain, substantially increased dislocation risk3
  • Management: Complex reoperation with trochanteric claw plate or heavy allograft reinforcement15

7.2 Nonunion & Malunion

Nonunion occurs in 1.2%–7.0% of cases.5 Wieser et al. reported only 4% nonunion with ETO vs. 20% with traditional anterior trochanteric slide osteotomy in revision settings.4

Risk factors: Excessive soft-tissue stripping, thermal necrosis from aggressive sawing, older age, cement interposition (if cemented stem used).4 Many cases represent "stable fibrous union"—fragment does not migrate extensively and patient remains largely asymptomatic, precluding reoperation.15

7.3 Stem Subsidence

Clinically significant subsidence (>5 mm): 6.0%–10.7%; total incidence up to 16.6% in broader meta-analyses.13

Critical factor: Modular fluted tapered stems (achieving secure distal engagement via splines and 2–3° taper) yield significantly lower subsidence rates than porous-coated cylindrical modular stems. In one series, cylindrical modular stems had 85% subsidence rate vs. significantly lower rates with tapered designs.12

8. Postoperative Rehabilitation Protocols

The profound biomechanical insult created by the ETO dictates a highly conservative rehabilitation protocol. The unhealed femur—missing one-third of its cortex and dependent on cables—is mechanically fragile.

Weight-Bearing Protocol

  • Weeks 0–6 (minimum), often 0–12: Strictly protected weight bearing—partial weight bearing (PWB) at 30% body weight, or toe-touch weight bearing (TTWB) using walker or bilateral crutches3,13
  • Weeks 6–12: Progressive advancement to weight bearing "as tolerated" based on clinical comfort and radiographic evidence of healing13

Trochanteric Precautions

  • Prohibited for 6–12 weeks: Active hip abduction exercises and passive adduction stretches (which place gluteus medius/minimus under extreme tension)3
  • Restrictions maintained until undeniable radiographic union observed13
  • Non-compliance drastically increases risk of wire breakage, cheese-wiring, and proximal migration44,45

9. Conclusion

The Extended Trochanteric Osteotomy stands as a pivotal, indispensable advancement in complex revision THA. By transforming unpredictable, destructive blind implant extraction into a controlled, reproducible, expansile exposure, the ETO preserves critical proximal femoral bone stock and ensures functional integrity of the soft-tissue envelope.

Extensive evidence supports its application across a spectrum of etiologies—from extensively porous-coated stem extraction and varus deformity correction to PJI eradication and Vancouver B fracture management. With meticulous surgical execution—emphasizing vastus lateralis vascular pedicle preservation, strategic rounding of osteotomy corners, and rigid fixation via modern cable-plate constructs—the osteotomy yields an exceptional average union rate exceeding 95%.

While the ETO introduces specific mechanical vulnerabilities—a 73% reduction in torsional strength and risk of trochanteric escape—these are effectively mitigated through optimal stem selection (modular fluted tapered designs), prophylactic cerclage, and strict adherence to protected weight-bearing and trochanteric rehabilitation protocols. Ultimately, the ETO empowers surgeons to navigate the most formidable challenges in revision hip surgery, delivering long-term 10- and 15-year survivorship, profound functional improvement, and sustained joint stability.
