Perioperative Antidepressant Use in Orthopedic Surgery

A Pharmacological and Clinical Analysis of Bleeding, Bone Healing, and Postoperative Complications

I. Executive Summary

Depression is a highly prevalent comorbidity among patients presenting for major orthopedic surgery, with estimates of documented depression ranging from 6.4% to over 26% in cohorts for total joint arthroplasty (TJA) and rotator cuff repair. Level II This creates a complex clinical scenario, as extensive evidence indicates that both the underlying psychiatric illness and the pharmacological agents used to treat it—particularly Selective Serotonin Reuptake Inhibitors (SSRIs)—are independent risk factors for poor surgical outcomes.

Key Findings

This report deconstructs pharmacological mechanisms, evaluates clinical evidence for specific complications, and synthesizes data to resolve apparent contradictions and provide actionable clinical guidance.

Six Core Findings

  1. Pharmacological Mechanisms: SSRIs introduce two distinct biological risks. First, by blocking the serotonin transporter (SERT) on platelets, SSRIs deplete intraplatelet serotonin, impairing platelet aggregation and inducing an "aspirin-like" functional defect. Level V — Preclinical Second, preclinical and in-vitro data demonstrate that SSRIs exert a direct, serotonin-independent inhibitory effect on osteoblast differentiation and mineralization. Level V — Preclinical
  2. Bleeding Risk: Systematic reviews and meta-analyses confirm that perioperative serotonergic antidepressant (SSRI/SNRI) use is associated with increased bleeding and blood transfusion. Meta-analysis of TJA patients shows an odds ratio (OR) of 1.78 for transfusion Level I, with other orthopedic studies finding risks as high as adjusted OR 3.71. Level II
  3. Bone Healing & Implant Risk: Patients with fracture nonunions have significantly higher prevalence of SSRI use (26.6% vs. 11%). Level III Proxy data from dental literature shows SSRI users experience a more than two-fold increase in implant failure rate (10.6% vs. 4.6%). Level III
  4. The Confounder: A diagnosis of depression is itself a catastrophic risk factor, independently associated with near-doubling of revision risk (HR 1.73) and PJI (HR 2.23). Level II Untreated patients have the worst functional gains; medically treated patients achieve improvements comparable to non-depressed cohorts. Level III
  5. Re-evaluating Complications: The "Revision Risk Paradox"—where some studies show SSRIs reduce revisions, some increase them, and the largest shows no difference—can be explained by a "net-zero" effect: psychiatric benefit cancels pharmacological detriment. Level II
  6. Clinical Guidance: Current consensus guidelines recommend continuing most psychiatric medications to prevent catastrophic psychiatric relapse. Level V — Consensus Exceptions exist for high-risk healing and compounded bleeding scenarios.

The Three-Tier Risk Model

Tier 1 — Low Risk

Non-depressed patients. Best baseline and best outcomes. Standard surgical protocol applies.

Tier 2 — Moderate Risk

Treated depression. Better than Tier 3 but still inferior to Tier 1. Patients accept pharmacological risks to gain psychiatric benefits.

Tier 3 — Very High Risk

Untreated depression. Worst outcomes: PJI, revision, delirium, poor function. Full behavioral and physiological risks of disease without mitigation.

II. The Serotonergic Mechanism: Hemostasis & Bone Metabolism

To critically evaluate the clinical data, it is essential to understand the two distinct biological mechanisms by which SSRIs interfere with orthopedic outcomes.

A. Mechanism 1: SSRIs and Platelet Dysfunction

The association between SSRI use and increased bleeding is a direct, predictable consequence of the drug's primary mechanism of action. Level V — Pharmacological mechanism

The Role of Serotonin in Hemostasis

Serotonin (5-HT) is a powerful vasoactive molecule critical to hemostasis. Approximately 99% of the body's serotonin is stored within platelets. Platelets cannot synthesize serotonin; they acquire it from plasma via the serotonin transporter (SERT) on the platelet membrane.

SSRI-Induced Platelet Depletion

SSRIs block SERT both in the brain and on platelets. Chronic administration leads to profound depletion of intraplatelet serotonin stores, with reductions in the range of 65% to 90%. This creates a functional platelet defect comparable to aspirin—a mild "anti-platelet" state.

Class Effect

This is a class effect of all serotonergic antidepressants (SSRIs and SNRIs). Magnitude of bleeding risk correlates with SERT affinity. Agents with highest serotonin reuptake inhibition (fluoxetine, paroxetine, sertraline) show the most frequent abnormal bleeding reports.

Functional Consequence: Impaired Aggregation

Stored serotonin acts as a "helper agonist," potentiating aggregation induced by ADP, epinephrine, and collagen. By depleting serotonin, SSRIs blunt this potentiation. In-vitro studies confirm decreased aggregation and ATP release in response to collagen and arachidonic acid, with reduced expression of glycoprotein IIb/IIIa and P-selectin. Level V — In-vitro

B. Mechanism 2: SSRIs and Bone Cell Homeostasis

Direct Osteoblast Inhibition

A 2017 murine model study found that fluoxetine exerts a direct, serotonin-independent inhibitory effect on osteoblast differentiation and mineralization. Level V — Animal model In femur fracture models, chronic fluoxetine use resulted in a significantly smaller and biomechanically weaker bony hard-callus. Most alarmingly, discontinuing the drug on the day of fracture surgery did not restore healing—those animals developed complete non-unions, indicating total arrest of the repair process.

Effects on Osteoclasts and Bone Remodeling

A 2016 study proposed a "dual mode of action" for fluoxetine: peripherally antiresorptive (impairing osteoclast differentiation), but chronic CNS effects trigger increased sympathetic output that increases bone resorption. Level V — Animal model Regardless of the precise balance, the consensus is that SSRIs disrupt bone cell homeostasis, linking chronic use to decreased BMD, osteoporosis, fracture risk, nonunion, and impaired implant osseointegration.

Critical Clinical Paradox

The animal data suggest that discontinuing an SSRI at the time of fracture worsens outcome, causing non-union. A surgeon motivated to stop an SSRI to prevent bleeding may inadvertently increase the patient's risk of long-term healing failure.

III. Perioperative Bleeding and Transfusion Risk

A. Systematic Evidence

Multiple systematic reviews, meta-analyses, and large cohort studies converge on a consistent finding: SSRI use increases bleeding complications and blood transfusion need during and after surgery. Level I

A 2025 meta-analysis of elective THA and TKA (ten comparative cohort studies, 2,098,833 patients) found that perioperative serotonergic antidepressant use was associated with a statistically significant increase in transfusion: OR 1.78 (95% CI 1.11–2.83). Level I

Additional key findings from the source document:

  • A foundational orthopedic study found transfusion risk almost quadrupled (aOR 3.71; 95% CI 1.35–10.18) for serotonergic antidepressant users vs. non-users. Level II
  • In TJA cohorts, SSRI utilization is associated with increased perioperative blood loss (OR 1.476). Level II
  • In hip fracture surgery, ORs for transfusion range from 1.28 to 1.7. Level I — Systematic review
  • In spinal fusion, a 23% increase in blood loss reported for SSRI/SNRI users. Level III
  • One study noted 23% of serotonergic antidepressant patients required transfusion vs. only 4% in the non-serotonergic group. Level III

B. Serotonergic vs. Non-Serotonergic Agents

The platelet-serotonin mechanism implies risk should be confined to SERT-blocking agents. The data strongly supports this:

Critical Differentiation

The same study that found a 3.71-fold transfusion increase for serotonergic antidepressants found that non-serotonergic antidepressants (e.g., bupropion, mirtazapine) had no increased risk (OR 0.74; 95% CI 0.10–5.95). Level II This isolates the risk to the serotonergic mechanism.

C. Procedure-Specific Variability

Despite strong overall signal, evidence is not monolithic. The 2025 meta-analysis noted "considerable heterogeneity" (I² = 97%). Level I

The Spine Surgery Contradiction: While some studies report increased bleeding in spinal fusion, a large 2024 matched-cohort study of 1–3 level lumbar fusions (420 matched pairs) concluded antidepressant use was not associated with increased blood loss or transfusion. Level II

The Bupropion Contradiction: A 2012 spinal surgery study found SSRI/SNRI users had higher blood loss, but also found bupropion (non-serotonergic) users had statistically higher blood loss in anterior/posterior lumbar fusion. Level III This challenges the "serotonin-only" hypothesis.

Possible Explanation: Arthroplasty involves large cancellous bone surfaces with diffuse "ooze" dependent on platelet function—where mild platelet dysfunction is clinically significant. Lumbar fusion bleeding is often from discrete, surgically-controlled sources where cautery/ligation achieves hemostasis regardless of platelet function.

IV. Impact on Bone Healing and Implant Integrity

While bleeding is an acute perioperative risk, the osteoblast inhibition mechanism raises concerns about a more insidious, long-term complication: failure of bone healing, with profound implications for fracture repair, spinal fusion, and cementless orthopedic implant survival.

A. Clinical Evidence: Fracture Nonunion

A 2022 retrospective study analyzed a database of patients with established nonunions. Level III Critical finding: prevalence of SSRI use in the nonunion cohort was 26.6%, significantly higher than the 11% prevalence in the general population. While retrospective design precluded conclusions on "time to union," the strong epidemiological association provides first clinical evidence that the animal-model mechanism may occur in humans.

B. Clinical Evidence: Osseointegration and Implant Failure

Osseointegration—direct structural bonding of bone to implant surface—is fundamentally a form of de novo bone formation dependent on osteoblast and osteoclast function. SSRI-mediated osteoblast inhibition poses a direct threat.

Critical Proxy: Dental Implants

A 2014 retrospective cohort study of 916 dental implants in 490 patients found: Level III

Nonuser failure rate: 4.6%  →  SSRI user failure rate: 10.6% (p = .03)

This more than doubling of failure risk is catastrophic. If even partially translatable to cementless TJA stems, tibial trays, or interbody fusions, it represents a massively under-recognized source of long-term implant failure.

This concern is bolstered by orthopedic-specific data showing THA patients on antidepressants had 1.70 times higher odds of periprosthetic fracture — a complication occurring at the vulnerable bone-implant interface. Level II

V. Deconstructing the Confounder: Illness vs. Medication

The analyses in previous sections are complicated by a powerful confounder: the indication for the medication. Depression is not a passive comorbidity; it is a systemic disease state that is itself a profound independent risk factor.

A. Depression as an Independent Risk Factor

  • Revisions & Infection: Depression associated with 1.73-fold increase in all-cause revision risk and doubling of PJI risk (HR 2.23). Level II
  • Functional Outcomes: Depressed patients present with poorer preoperative HRQoL and higher pain levels. Deficits persist postoperatively with inferior functional scores even after successful surgery. Level II
  • Complications: Depression predicts increased postoperative pain, higher 90-day ED visits/readmissions, and increased postoperative delirium risk. Level II

B. The Mitigating Effect of Treatment

A pivotal 2018 TJA study directly compared three cohorts: Level III

  • Untreated depressed patients: Much smaller gains in physical function scores compared to non-depressed.
  • Medically treated depressed patients: Functional gains statistically similar to the non-depressed cohort.

Similarly, a study on cancer surgery (proxy) found: Level III

  • Non-depressed: lowest cost ($17,551)
  • Treated depressed: moderate cost ($22,086)
  • Untreated depressed: highest cost ($24,897) and highest complication rates
Reframing the Dilemma

The operative question is not "Should we add a risky drug?" but rather "For a high-risk Tier 3 patient, does this drug move them to the more manageable Tier 2?" The evidence suggests the answer is unequivocally yes.

VI. Postoperative Complications — Re-evaluated

Armed with the Three-Tier Risk Model, it is now possible to differentiate between complications driven by the pharmacology of the drug and those driven by the pathophysiology of the illness.

A. The Revision Risk Paradox

The most conflicting data in the literature:

  • Reduced risk: Mayo Clinic study of 20,112 TJA procedures found perioperative SSRI use associated with reduced all-cause revisions (HR 0.77). Level II This likely represents an "effective-treatment" effect—SSRI marks treated depression (Tier 2), mitigating Tier 3 revision risk.
  • Increased risk: Hip fracture study found 26% increased reoperation risk for current SSRI users. Level III Rotator cuff study found significantly increased odds of revision. Level III
  • No difference: Large 2024 matched-cohort TKA study (209,320 pairs) found no clinically notable difference in 5-year revision survival (96.4% vs. 96.9%). Level II
Resolving the Paradox

The "net-zero" finding (2024 TKA study) is likely the true balance: psychiatric benefit (mitigating Tier 3 risks → reducing revisions) is cancelled out by pharmacological detriment (impaired bone healing → increasing revisions). Two opposing forces balance.

B. Periprosthetic Joint Infection (PJI)

Clean separation between illness and drug: Level II

  • Illness-driven: Depression doubles PJI risk (HR 2.23).
  • No drug-driven risk: Perioperative antidepressant use was not associated with PJI. Study of new-onset post-surgical depression also found no difference in PJI between those who started antidepressants and those who did not.

C. Readmissions, Length of Stay, and Pain

  • Readmissions: Antidepressant use consistently associated with higher resource utilization. Primary THA study: 8% 90-day readmission (antidepressant) vs. 4.2% (non-antidepressant). Level III
  • Length of Stay: Contradictory. Some studies associate SSRI use with increased LOS; others find no association. Level III
  • Pain: Unambiguous. Patients on antidepressants consistently report higher pain levels before and after surgery, with higher odds of acute postoperative pain and ED visits for pain. Level II

D. Postoperative Delirium (POD)

  • Illness-driven: Depression is an independent predictor for POD in spine patients. Level II
  • No drug-driven risk: Spine deformity study found no difference in POD between antidepressant users and non-users. Level III Secondary analysis confirmed no association between SSRI use and next-day delirium. Level III

VII. Clinical Recommendations

The evidence presents a complex, multi-variable problem with no risk-free options.

A. Risk-Benefit Calculus

  • Risk of Discontinuation: Abrupt cessation can trigger discontinuation syndrome in up to 20% of patients. Level II More critically, risks full relapse of MDD, moving patient from Tier 2 to catastrophic Tier 3.
  • Risk of Continuation: (1) Clear, manageable, acute risk of increased bleeding/transfusion. (2) Poorly understood but biologically plausible long-term risk of impaired bone healing, nonunion, and implant failure.

B. Guideline Review

  • AAOS: Does not currently provide a formal CPG or Position Statement on perioperative antidepressant management. Not specified
  • SPAQI (2021): Consensus statement recommends continuing most psychiatric medications perioperatively, as psychiatric relapse risk outweighs perioperative drug risks. Level V — Consensus
  • European Society of Medicine: Recommends continuing SSRIs but identifies "compounded risk" scenario (mandatory antiplatelet therapy) where SSRI discontinuation may be warranted, in consultation with psychiatry. Level V — Consensus

C. Stratified Clinical Recommendations

Recommendation 1: Universal Psychiatric Screening

The single greatest modifiable risk is untreated (Tier 3) depression. Most critical intervention: universal preoperative screening to identify and optimize psychiatric care before elective surgery.

Recommendation 2: "Continue" Default

For the majority of patients with stable, treated (Tier 2) depression, continue the SSRI/SNRI through the perioperative period. Risk of psychiatric relapse (Tier 3) is more severe than pharmacological risks. Surgical teams must manage the consequence: increased bleeding risk (blood type & screen, TXA, cell saver).

Recommendation 3: "High-Bleed" Exception

Multidisciplinary discussion warranted for: (a) concurrent mandatory anticoagulants/antiplatelet agents; (b) pre-existing coagulopathies; (c) exceptionally high non-compressible bleeding risk procedures. Consider psychiatrist-led switch to non-serotonergic agent (e.g., bupropion) 4–6 weeks preoperatively.

Recommendation 4: "High-Risk Healing" Exception

For patients where bone healing is the primary surgical goal (nonunion repairs, multi-level fusions, revision arthroplasty with cementless components, patients with multiple implant failure risk factors), counsel on elevated nonunion/failure risk. Consider bone-growth stimulators, biologics, autograft, and enhanced long-term follow-up.

Recommendation 5: Differentiate Drug Classes

Stop grouping all "antidepressants" together. Bleeding risk is clearly linked to serotonergic agents (SSRIs/SNRIs). Bone healing risk is most strongly linked to SSRIs and TCAs. Non-serotonergic agents (bupropion) carry a different, likely lower risk profile, making them viable alternatives in high-risk surgical patients.
