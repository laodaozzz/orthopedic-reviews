Pneumatic Tourniquet Use in Orthopedic Surgery

An Evidence-Based Review of Pathophysiology, Protocols, and Clinical Outcomes

1. Introduction and Historical Context

The pneumatic tourniquet is a fundamental yet paradoxical device in modern orthopedic surgery. Its primary function—to arrest arterial blood flow and create a bloodless surgical field—is simultaneously its greatest utility and its most significant liability. By inducing transient ischemia, it grants unparalleled visualization and reduces intraoperative blood loss, but at a physiological cost: a localized pathological state of hypoxia, acidosis, and mechanical compression, followed by systemic insults from reperfusion.

Historically, non-pneumatic devices (Esmarch bandages) applied uncontrolled pressures, causing nerve palsies and necrosis. The introduction of the pneumatic tourniquet by Cushing in 1904 allowed for pressure regulation. Today, the discourse has shifted from simple utility to optimization and necessity, especially in the ERAS era. High-level evidence from 2020-2025, particularly in total knee arthroplasty (TKA), challenges the dogma that a bloodless field equals superior outcomes. Concurrently, the WALANT technique in hand surgery questions the need for the device in upper extremity procedures.

Key Paradigm Shift

The era of "routine, standardized" tourniquet use (fixed high pressures, indiscriminate application) is ending. It is being replaced by a "selective, personalized" approach driven by physiological evidence and microprocessor technology.

2. Pathophysiology of Tourniquet-Induced Ischemia and Reperfusion

Application of a tourniquet initiates a cascade of progressive metabolic failure and structural injury in two phases: ischemic (inflation) and reperfusion (deflation). Level II

Phase 1: Ischemia (Inflation)

ATP Depletion & Glycolytic Shift: Within minutes, oxygen stores are exhausted. Muscle switches to inefficient anaerobic glycolysis. ATP stores deplete significantly as time approaches 2 hours. Level II

Intracellular Acidosis: Lactate accumulates, causing pH to drop below 6.5 within one hour, disrupting enzymatic function and cell membranes. Level III

Mitochondrial Dysfunction: Ischemia disrupts the electron transport chain and suppresses antioxidant enzymes (MnSOD), priming cells for reperfusion injury. Level II

Gene Expression: Transcriptomic analyses show ~72 genes in skeletal muscle are significantly upregulated within two hours, primarily involved in stress and apoptosis pathways. Level II

Phase 2: Reperfusion (Deflation) - The "Second Hit"

Reactive Oxygen Species (ROS) Generation: Restoration of blood flow floods tissue with oxygen. Dysfunctional ETC and compromised antioxidants convert oxygen into superoxide, causing direct cellular damage via lipid peroxidation. Level II

No-Reflow Phenomenon: Ischemia activates endothelium, causing neutrophil adhesion and migration. This inflammatory infiltrate causes edema and capillary plugging, compromising perfusion. Level II

Systemic Washout: Deflation releases a bolus of lactate, potassium, myoglobin, and cytokines into systemic circulation, manifesting as transient hypotension, hypercapnia, and potential metabolic acidosis. Level II

3. Pressure Settings: The Paradigm Shift to Personalization

For decades, fixed pressures (250 mmHg upper, 300-350 mmHg lower) were standard, exposing patients to unnecessary high pressure and nerve injury risk. The contemporary standard is Personalized Tourniquet Pressure based on Limb Occlusion Pressure (LOP). Level II

3.1 Limb Occlusion Pressure (LOP) Methodology

LOP is the minimum pressure required, with a specific cuff on a specific patient, to stop arterial blood flow distal to the cuff. It accounts for patient blood pressure, limb circumference, tissue compliance, and cuff width. Modern microprocessor-controlled systems measure LOP automatically using a distal photoplethysmographic sensor. Level III

Systematic Review Data: In upper limb surgery, mean inflation pressure using LOP protocols was 169 mmHg (95% CI 144.9–193.6), significantly lower than the standard 250 mmHg. Level II

3.2 Cuff Selection

Cuff width should be greater than half the limb diameter. Wide cuffs (10-14 cm) allow for lower occlusion pressures. Narrow cuffs create high edge-pressure gradients, risking nerve shearing. Variable contour (conical) cuffs are designed for tapered limbs (e.g., thighs) to ensure uniform pressure distribution. Level III

4. Safety Duration and Reperfusion Protocols

While pressure dictates mechanical injury severity, duration dictates ischemic metabolic injury severity. There is no physiological on/off switch; damage accumulates progressively.

4.1 The "Safe" Ischemic Limit

The consensus is that continuous inflation time should ideally not exceed 120 minutes. Level I

  • Physiological Basis: By 2 hours, glycogen stores are depleted, and significant intracellular acidosis is present. Functional recovery remains likely.
  • Beyond 2 Hours: Risk of nerve palsy, post-tourniquet syndrome, and rhabdomyolysis increases non-linearly. BOASt states use beyond 150 minutes is "rarely justified." Level II

4.2 Reperfusion Intervals ("Breathing Period")

When procedure exceeds the safety limit, a reperfusion interval is mandatory.

  • Trigger Point: Initiate after 90-120 minutes of continuous inflation.
  • Duration: Recommended 10-15 minutes. Studies show 5 minutes is insufficient for metabolic recovery. Level II
  • Subsequent Intervals: Subsequent inflation periods should be shorter (e.g., 60-90 min), and breathing periods longer (15-20 min) due to cumulative tissue stress. Level III
  • Re-exsanguination: Crucial upon re-inflation to prevent venous congestion. Level III

4.3 Bilateral Procedures

Stagger deflation of bilateral tourniquets by at least 30-45 minutes to prevent systemic bolus of potassium and acid causing myocardial depression. Level III

5.2 Robotic-Assisted TKA

Robotic cases often run longer, placing them closer to the 120-minute limit. Some surgeons adopt "limited use"—inflating only during bone cuts and cementation (15-20 min) to ensure a dry bone bed for cement interdigitation. Level III

6. Upper Limb Surgery: The WALANT Revolution

In upper extremity surgery, the conversation has shifted from "how to use" to "whether to use" the tourniquet.

6.1 Wide Awake Local Anesthesia No Tourniquet (WALANT)

WALANT uses tumescent lidocaine (1%) with epinephrine (1:100,000) for anesthesia and hemostasis. The historical dogma against digital epinephrine has been debunked; reviews of thousands of cases show zero digital necrosis when phentolamine is available. Level II

Advantages:

  • Patient Satisfaction: Systematic reviews show unequivocally higher satisfaction vs. tourniquet-based anesthesia, primarily by eliminating "tourniquet pain." Level II
  • Intraoperative Assessment: Awake patients can actively flex/extend, allowing real-time verification of tendon repairs and tenolysis. Level III
  • Cost & Efficiency: Enables surgery in minor procedure rooms, bypassing anesthesiologist, fasting, and PACU, with significant cost savings. Level III

6.2 Pneumatic Tourniquet in Upper Limb

When WALANT is not feasible. Standard 250 mmHg is excessive; LOP-based protocols show mean effective pressures of ~169 mmHg. Level II Forearm placement for hand procedures is increasingly favored as it is less painful, though it may tether extrinsic tendons. Level III

Complications range from minor soft tissue injury to life-threatening systemic events.

8. Clinical Implementation Protocol

Synthesized from AORN, BOASt, and AAOS guidelines. Level I

8.1 Preoperative Assessment

  • Contraindications: Absolute (AV fistula, recent bypass, severe PVD). Relative (sickle cell, DVT history).
  • Cuff Selection: Measure circumference; width >50% diameter. Contoured cuff for tapered limbs.
  • Skin Protection: Two layers of wrinkle-free padding. Adhesive drape to seal cuff from prep solution.

8.2 Intraoperative Management

  • Pressure Setting: Measure LOP pre-inflation. Set pressure to LOP + Safety Margin. If LOP unavailable, use SBP formulas (see Table 1).
  • Time Management: Announce "Tourniquet Up" time. Notify surgeon at 60, 90, and 120 minutes. If >120 min, initiate 10-15 min reperfusion interval before re-inflation.

8.3 Postoperative Care

  • Deflation: Deflate rapidly. Remove cuff and padding immediately.
  • Inspection: Inspect skin for blisters/burns/pressure areas. Document in op note.
  • Monitoring: Monitor for hypotension for first 15 minutes post-deflation.

10. Conclusion and Clinical Imperatives

The evidence from 2020 to 2025 supports three definitive conclusions:

  1. Personalization is Mandatory: There is no justification for fixed pressures. Limb Occlusion Pressure (LOP) is the gold standard for minimizing nerve injury and pain. Level I
  2. Time is Tissue: The 120-minute limit is a critical physiological boundary. Surpassing it requires strict adherence to reperfusion protocols. Level I
  3. Utility is Procedure-Specific: In TKA, the tourniquet is no longer mandatory and may hinder ERAS goals; "limited use" is a viable compromise. In hand surgery, WALANT offers a superior, cost-effective alternative that eliminates tourniquet morbidity. Level I

For the modern orthopedic surgeon, the tourniquet remains a powerful tool that must be wielded with deep respect for the physiological cost of ischemia. Adherence to LOP-based protocols and strict duration management maximizes benefits while minimizing the "second hit" of reperfusion injury.
