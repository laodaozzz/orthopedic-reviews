1. Introduction to Patellofemoral Instability

Patellofemoral instability is a multifactorial clinical challenge characterized by subluxation or complete dislocation of the patella from the trochlear groove. It disproportionately affects pediatric and adolescent athletic populations undergoing rapid skeletal growth. Level II

Key Epidemiology (Source Data) Annual incidence of primary patellar dislocation in youth aged 10–17 years: 29 per 100,000. Up to 61% of all primary dislocations occur in the adolescent athletic subset, driven by ligamentous laxity, dynamic valgus collapse, and pathoanatomic risk factors [2].

The medial patellofemoral ligament (MPFL) is the primary soft-tissue stabilizer, providing up to 60% of the total medial restraint against lateral patellar translation during 0°–30° of knee flexion [2]. In virtually all lateral dislocations, the MPFL is attenuated, stretched, or completely ruptured, most frequently at its femoral origin [3].

1.1 Paradigm Shift: ESSKA 2024 Consensus on First-Time Patellar Dislocation (FTPD)

The 2024 European Society of Sports Traumatology, Knee Surgery and Arthroscopy (ESSKA) formal consensus defines FTPD as the first-time event where the patella completely leaves the trochlear groove, confirmed clinically, radiologically, or both [4,5]. While conservative therapy remains appropriate for isolated FTPD without osteochondral lesions, there is a distinct paradigm shift toward early surgical intervention in the presence of severe anatomic risk factors or osteochondral fractures [4]. 2. Skeletally Immature Pediatric Patients

The primary challenge in pediatric MPFLR is the extreme proximity of the native MPFL femoral insertion to the highly active distal femoral physis. Iatrogenic growth plate injury can cause premature physeal closure, leg-length discrepancies, or progressive genu valgum [3]. Level III

In adults, the Schöttle point is the gold-standard radiographic landmark for femoral tunnel placement [12]. In pediatric patients, extensive DRR studies confirm the Schöttle point is consistently located distal to the distal medial femoral physis, with a mean minimum distance of 9.9 mm [14].

Sex-Dependent Variation Females have a Schöttle point 1.31–1.83 mm closer to the physis compared to age-matched males (mean 6.51–6.92 mm in females vs. 7.71–8.56 mm in males) [16]. This demands heightened intraoperative vigilance in young female athletes.

2D Fluoroscopy vs. 3D CT Discrepancy

The distal femoral physis possesses a complex, U-shaped 3D morphology. 2D lateral radiographs superimpose anterior, central, and posterior margins, creating an illusion that the Schöttle point lies on or proximal to the physis. However, 3D CT renderings prove the true cortical position is consistently distal to the physis with a mean clearance of 6.1 ± 1.9 mm [18]. Level III

Current guidelines: femoral tunnel guidewire must be advanced with 15–20° distal and posterior angulation, avoiding any proximal trajectory. Reaming diameter should be ≤ 6 mm; larger diameters increase physeal violation probability to 6% [12,14].

Historically, non-anatomic "sling-based" techniques (adductor magnus tendon, MCL) were used to avoid physeal injury entirely. However, systematic reviews demonstrate alarmingly high failure rates [11,12]:

Critical Evidence Gap Noted in Source Non-anatomic techniques exhibit catastrophic failure rates (up to 38%). Modern anatomic techniques achieve <3.4% redislocation with 0% physeal closure incidence in systematic reviews [3,11].

Patient-reported outcomes (Kujala, Lysholm, Pedi-IKDC) show significant improvement with anatomic reconstruction. Pooled pediatric Kujala scores improve from 59.1 → 84.6 [24]. Systematic reviews report a 0% incidence of premature physeal closure, angular deformity, or leg-length discrepancy with modern anatomic physeal-sparing techniques [3].

The gracilis tendon autograft remains the most frequently utilized (>60% of pediatric cases), favored for its predictable diameter, ease of harvest, and stiffness profile approximating native MPFL failure load (~208 N) [3,26]. Level II

The pedicled quadriceps tendon autograft (quadriceps turndown technique) has gained substantial traction as an anatomy-preserving alternative [27]:

  • A partial-thickness superficial layer (~9 cm × 10 mm) is harvested with distal attachment preserved at the superior patellar pole [28]
  • The free proximal end is reflected 180° distally, rotated 90° medially, and secured to the Schöttle point via epiphyseal anchor [28]
  • Preserves the fan-like medial patellar insertion of native MPFL
  • Avoids all patellar drilling — eliminates risk of iatrogenic patellar fracture [29]
  • Avoids saphenous nerve neuropathy and hamstring weakness [29]

Meta-analyses comparing hamstring vs. quadriceps tendon autografts demonstrate no statistically significant differences in redislocation rates, Kujala scores, or Lysholm scores [30]. Level II

Aggressive bony procedures (tibial tubercle osteotomy, trochleoplasty) are contraindicated in open-physis patients [23]. Alternative strategies include:

  • Profound genu valgum (LDFA < 84°): MPFLR + temporary hemiepiphysiodesis using tension-band "8-plates" spanning the medial distal femoral physis. Growth correction occurs over 12–18 months [32,33]
  • Profound patella alta: Soft-tissue patellar tendon shortening/imbrication combined with MPFLR to distalize the extensor mechanism without violating the tibial apophysis [33]
Conceptual Framework Pediatric patellofemoral instability must be viewed as a dynamic, developmental biomechanical failure requiring ongoing, minimally invasive architectural modulation — not merely a simple ligamentous rupture.

3. Skeletally Mature Adolescents

With complete physeal closure, surgical constraints shift fundamentally. Objectives pivot to: maximizing construct rigidity, restoring biomechanical isometry, executing concomitant osseous realignments, and navigating aggressive RTS protocols.

Isometry ensures the reconstructed ligament maintains physiologic tension throughout ROM: heavy engagement from 0°–30° of flexion (preventing subluxation) and relaxation in deeper flexion as the patella centers in the trochlea [40]. Level III

Non-anatomic femoral placement (proximal/anterior to Schöttle point) causes destructive graft anisometry → over-tensioning in deep flexion → chondral degradation, arthrofibrosis, medial pain, and eventual graft failure [40]. Intraoperative fluoroscopic mapping of the Schöttle point remains non-negotiable.

The native MPFL has a broad, sail-like insertion covering the proximal half to two-thirds of the medial patellar margin [36]. Double-bundle reconstruction is biomechanically superior to single-bundle [26]. Level II

3.5 Return to Sport: Metrics, Deficits & Kinesiophobia

A landmark meta-analysis (23 studies, 930 patients) demonstrated 92.8% overall RTS rate at a mean of 6.7 months, with 71.3% returning to pre-injury level or higher. Recurrent instability: only 1.9%–5.4% [9]. Level I

Alarming Functional Deficits at 6–8 Months Only 44.4% achieved LSI > 90% in isometric quadriceps strength. Only 32.0% passed LSI > 90% on all four hop tests simultaneously [51]. This indicates most adolescent athletes require rehabilitation extending well beyond 8 months.

Psychological readiness is a profound bottleneck: approximately 47% of patients who fail RTS cite purely psychological factors (kinesiophobia) rather than physical limitation [9]. The MPFL-Return to Sport Index (MPFL-RSI) threshold of 55 predicts successful RTS with 90.9% sensitivity and 70.6% specificity [56,57]. Level II

Essential Clearance Criteria (Source-Derived)
  • LSI > 90%: Isometric quadriceps peak torque
  • LSI > 90%: Single hop, triple hop, crossover hop, timed hop
  • Y-Balance anterior reach difference < 4 cm
  • MPFL-RSI score ≥ 55

4. Conclusion

The management of recurrent patellofemoral instability in young patients demands exhaustive understanding of skeletal maturation, 3D articular geometry, and sport-specific biomechanics. The literature supports a strictly bifurcated approach:

Skeletally Immature

  • Physeal preservation is paramount
  • Anatomic epiphyseal/surface techniques: <3.4% redislocation, 0% physeal injury
  • Quadriceps turndown: excellent PROMs, zero patellar fracture risk
  • Growth-guided hemiepiphysiodesis for severe valgus

Skeletally Mature

  • Absolute anatomic restoration & rigid fixation
  • Suture anchors preferred over tunnels (lower pain, fewer revisions)
  • "Menu à la carte": TTO + trochleoplasty for severe dysplasia
  • RTS: 92.8% rate, but only 32% meet all LSI criteria at 6–8 months
  • MPFL-RSI ≥ 55 needed to address kinesiophobia

By seamlessly marrying precise, growth-aware surgical execution with aggressive, multidimensional, and psychologically informed rehabilitation, clinicians can restore complex joint kinematics, eliminate recurrent instability, and return young athletes to peak performance.
