Unstable Distal Clavicle Fractures

Tension Band Wiring vs Plate Fixation — Systematic Review & Meta-Analysis Summary

I. The Clinical Challenge

1.1 Epidemiology & Biomechanics

Distal clavicle fractures account for 10–28 % of all clavicular fractures, typically resulting from a direct high-energy impact to the superolateral shoulder. not specified in source

Instability arises from opposing deforming forces: the trapezius pulls the proximal shaft superoposteriorly, while the weight of the arm, compounded by pectoralis major and latissimus dorsi traction, displaces the small distal fragment inferiorly and anteriorly. Source document, §1.1

The coracoclavicular (CC) ligaments (conoid & trapezoid) are the primary vertical stabilizers. When their attachment to the proximal fragment is disrupted, displacement is unopposed — leading to a reported nonunion rate of 10–44 % with non-operative care. Level III–IV evidence, multiple retrospective series cited in source

⚠ Diagnostic Paradox: The Neer classification exhibits poor to moderate inter- and intra-observer reliability on plain radiographs. Misclassification risks under-treatment (nonunion) or over-treatment (unnecessary surgery). Advanced imaging (MRI) corrected classification in 8.5 % of cases in one study. Level III

II. Surgical Fixation Techniques

2.1 Tension Band Wiring (TBW)

TBW converts superior tensile forces into inferior compressive forces. Two parallel K-wires cross the fracture; a figure-of-eight stainless-steel wire is looped through a proximal drill hole and around the K-wire ends distally. Source document, §2.1

Key trade-off: Trans-acromial K-wire placement maximizes stability but violates the AC joint, risking stiffness and arthrosis. AC-joint–sparing placement sacrifices fixation strength. Source document, §2.1

2.2 Plate Fixation

Hook Plate: A posterior hook engages the acromion undersurface, providing powerful leverage for severely comminuted distal fragments. Subacromial impingement and acromial erosion are near-universal, making routine removal standard — a "two-surgery solution." Level III, multiple comparative studies

Anatomic Locking Plate: Pre-contoured plates with divergent 2.7 mm locking screws maximize purchase in small distal fragments via angular stability, avoiding AC-joint violation and subacromial impingement. When combined with CC ligament augmentation, this approach represents a paradigm shift from mechanical bridging to anatomical reconstruction of the bone–ligament complex. Level III; supported by network meta-analysis

III. Functional Outcomes

3.1 Recovery Trajectory: A Paradox

TBW shows faster early recovery — one prospective study found significantly better forward elevation, abduction, and Oxford shoulder scores at 3 months, attributed to less soft-tissue dissection. However, by 6 months, no significant difference remained. Level II, prospective comparative (Hsu et al. 2011)

Plate fixation shows faster return to high-demand activities. In a retrospective study, 93.7 % of hook-plate patients returned to work within 3 months vs. 60 % of TBW patients; return to sport at 6 months was 81.2 % vs. 40 %. Level III, retrospective comparative (Lee et al. 2010)

Clinical Interpretation: TBW may suit patients prioritizing comfortable early ROM for ADLs; plate fixation better serves manual laborers or athletes needing early high-demand function. The perceived rigidity of plating encourages earlier loading despite restricted terminal motion.

3.2 Final Functional Scores

Meta-analyses consistently favor plate-based methods:

  • Hook plate vs. TBW — significantly higher Constant-Murley score with hook plate (OR in favor). Level I, Asadollahi et al. 2019
  • Network meta-analysis (5 techniques) — CC fixation yielded the best Constant-Murley scores (mean difference +7.11 points vs. TBW); SUCRA probability 96.8 % of being best. Level I, Ye et al. 2018
  • Locking plate ± CC suturing significantly better than K-wire ± TBW. Level I, systematic review 2020

IV. Complication Profiles

4.1 Hardware-Related Complications

  • TBW: Implant dislodgement 17 % (Hsu 2011), overall complication rate 30 % (Lee 2010), including loss of reduction and wire breakage. Superficial infection rate 10–15 %. Level III
  • Hook Plate: Subacromial erosion 26 % (Hsu 2011); reoperation for removal approaches 100 %. Level III
  • Locking Plate: More favorable profile — occasional screw loosening or peri-implant fracture; routine removal rarely needed. Level III

4.2 Nonunion & Infection

TBW: reported nonunion 5 % vs. hook plate 0 % in one study. Trend toward higher nonunion with less rigid fixation across meta-analyses. Level III (Al-Kaduh et al. 2024); trend supported by Level I Ye et al. 2018

V. Evolving Paradigms

5.1 CC Ligament Augmentation

Modern philosophy treats these injuries as disruptions of the superior shoulder suspensory complex, not isolated bone injuries. Heavy non-absorbable sutures, synthetic tapes, or suspensory fixation devices (TightRope®, Dog Bone™) recreate CC function, neutralizing deforming forces and protecting fixation hardware. Level III, supported by Level I network meta-analysis

The 2018 network meta-analysis found stand-alone CC fixation yielded the best Constant-Murley scores; the 2020 review confirmed locking plate ± CC augmentation superior to TBW. Level I

5.2 Emerging Technologies

  • Minimally invasive & arthroscopic techniques — suspensory devices via small incisions; arthroscopy-assisted coracoid passage reduces neurovascular risk. Source document, §5.2
  • Double-plate vertical fixation — orthogonal plating (superior + anterior) for highly comminuted fractures provides multi-planar stability. Source document, §5.2
  • Modified hook plate application — drilling the hook directly into the acromion to avoid subacromial conflict. Case report level, source §5.2

VI. Recommendations & Algorithm

6.1 Evidence-Based Decision Algorithm

1

Diagnosis & Classification

High-quality radiographs (including Zanca view). If fracture pattern or displacement is equivocal → obtain CT or MRI to avoid misclassification. Level III, supported by 8.5 % reclassification rate with MRI

2

First-Line: Anatomic Locking Plate ± CC Augmentation

Recommended for the vast majority of Neer II / V fractures. Best balance of stable fixation, superior function, and lowest complication profile. Level I (Ye 2018, SUCRA 93.8 %)

3

Second-Line / Salvage: Hook Plate

Reserve for severe distal comminution or exceptionally small fragment where screw purchase is impossible. Counsel patient pre-operatively about impingement symptoms and planned second surgery for removal. Level III

4

Reconsider TBW

Given higher complication rates and inferior function, TBW should be largely a historical technique. Justifiable only in resource-constrained settings or very specific low-demand scenarios. Level I (Asadollahi 2019, Ye 2018)

6.2 Future Research Priorities

  • Large-scale multicenter RCT: locking plate + CC augmentation vs. hook plate
  • Long-term studies on post-traumatic AC joint arthritis across fixation methods
  • Reliability studies of the Neer classification and cost-effectiveness of routine advanced imaging
