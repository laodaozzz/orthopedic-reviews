1 Pathobiomechanical Principles

The Thoracolumbar Junction

The thoracolumbar junction (T10–L2) is the most common site for traumatic spinal fractures, accounting for approximately 60% of all such injuries. I

This predilection arises from the junction's unique biomechanical role: it is a transitional zone between the rigid, kyphotic thoracic spine (stabilized by the rib cage and coronally oriented facet joints) and the more mobile, lordotic lumbar spine (designed for flexion and extension). This abrupt change in stiffness creates a fulcrum where axial and flexion-compression forces from high-energy trauma are concentrated.

Three-Column Model (Denis)

Table 1 — Denis Three-Column Model of Spinal Stability
ColumnAnatomical StructuresClinical Significance
Anterior Anterior longitudinal ligament, anterior annulus fibrosus, anterior half of vertebral body Primary load-bearing column; compression injuries first affect this column
Middle Posterior half of vertebral body, posterior annulus, posterior longitudinal ligament (PLL) Critical determinant of instability; retropulsed fragments threaten the spinal canal
Posterior Pedicles, facets, laminae, spinous processes, posterior ligamentous complex (PLC) Tension band; PLC disruption signals major instability

Neurological Significance

The spinal canal at this level houses the conus medullaris (terminating ~L1–L2) and the cauda equina. Injuries here carry neurological deficits in 27–55% of cases. I Treatment goals are twofold: restore mechanical stability and decompress/protect neural structures.

Rationale for Combined PKP + PPSF

Burst fractures (AO Type A3/A4) involve failure of both anterior and middle columns, producing vertebral body height loss, kyphotic deformity, and potential canal compromise. Historically, this required morbid two-stage open surgeries (posterior fixation + anterior column reconstruction).

The combined PKP-PPSF approach achieves single-stage, three-column reconstruction through a minimally invasive posterior corridor:

  • PPSF provides immediate posterior/middle column stability ("internal brace"), creating a safe environment for kyphoplasty.
  • PKP restores anterior column height via balloon inflation and bone cement buttress.
  • The anterior cement support unloads the posterior instrumentation, protecting against fatigue failure and ensuring long-term correction maintenance. II
Key Synergy: This symbiotic biomechanical relationship is the core innovation — each technique compensates for the other's limitations when used in isolation for burst fractures.

C — Rotation Translation/rotation C (all) Contraindicated — too unstable for percutaneous reduction not indicated

Specific Indications for Combined PKP-PPSF

  • Fracture Type: Unstable burst fractures (AO Type A3/A4) without severe neural compression requiring open decompression II
  • Radiographic Criteria:
    • Loss of anterior vertebral body height > 50%
    • Local kyphotic angle > 25–30°
    • Progressive deformity on follow-up imaging
  • Patient Factors: Elderly/osteoporotic patients (cement augments screw purchase); polytrauma patients (reduced surgical stress) II

Contraindications

Do NOT use this technique when:
  • Significant neurological deficits requiring open laminectomy/corpectomy
  • Severe translational fracture-dislocations (AO Type C)
  • Complete destruction of the posterior vertebral wall
  • Active systemic infection, osteomyelitis, or bacteremia
  • Uncorrectable severe coagulopathy

3 Patient Evaluation & Decision Framework

Initial Assessment

All patients are managed per ATLS protocols. Spinal precautions (log-rolling, immobilization) are mandatory until stability is confirmed. A detailed neurological exam is performed and documented using the ASIA Impairment Scale. I

Advanced Imaging

  • CT (Gold Standard for Bone): Multiplanar reconstructions detail fracture pattern, comminution, canal compromise, and pedicle integrity I
  • MRI (Essential for Soft Tissue): Evaluates PLC integrity (high signal on T2/STIR sequences) and directly visualizes the spinal cord/nerve roots I

4 Surgical Technique: Step-by-Step Workflow

Table 4 — Surgical Workflow Summary
PhaseObjectiveKey StepsPearls & Pitfalls
Patient Positioning Passive reduction via ligamentotaxis; optimal imaging Prone on radiolucent frame (Jackson table); bolsters under chest & iliac crests; abdomen free Avoid abdominal compression → reduces epidural venous pressure. Confirm AP & lateral fluoroscopic views before draping. II
Stage 1: Kyphoplasty Restore VB height; create anterior cement buttress Bilateral transpedicular access → balloon tamp insertion & inflation (often >150 psi) → high-viscosity PMMA cement injection under live fluoroscopy Needle trajectory must remain within pedicle. Monitor balloon inflation to prevent endplate breach. Inject cement to fill anterior 2/3; halt if leakage toward posterior wall. II
Stage 2: Pedicle Screw Fixation Posterior tension band; lock in reduction Percutaneous guidewires → cannulated screws one level above & below → rod insertion (subcutaneous tunnel) → reduction maneuvers → final tightening Consider intermediate screws at fracture level (6-screw construct) for increased stiffness. Contour rod for lordosis. Confirm alignment on fluoroscopy. II

Construct Design Options

  • Short-Segment Fixation (SSF): 4-screw construct (one level above, one below) — most common II
  • Intermediate Screw Augmentation: 6-screw construct adding screws at the fracture level — significantly increases stiffness, reduces implant failure rates II
Meta-analytic evidence (Li et al.): Intermediate screw augmentation yields statistically significant improvements in postoperative Cobb angle, less correction loss over time, and lower implant failure rate vs. standard 4-screw fixation. II

6 Technological Innovations

Navigation & Robotics

  • 3D Intraoperative Navigation (O-arm): CT-like imaging in the OR coupled with real-time instrument tracking significantly improves screw placement accuracy and reduces radiation exposure vs. conventional fluoroscopy. II
  • Robotic-Assisted Surgery: Platforms (Mazor X, ExcelsiusGPS, Cirq) enable preoperative CT-based planning, intraoperative registration, and robotic arm guidance for screw trajectory. Meta-analyses confirm higher accuracy, reduced radiation, and less bleeding. II
  • Future Direction: Integration of AI/deep learning may enable semi-autonomous surgical tasks with superhuman precision. Emerging
