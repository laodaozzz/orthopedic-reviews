Evidence-Based Management of Proximal Humerus Surgical Neck Fractures in Patients with Seizure Disorders

Comprehensive Interactive Educational Summary — Synthesized from Peer-Reviewed Literature

1. Introduction & Epidemiological Context

Proximal humerus fractures (PHFs) account for 4–6% of all fractures and rank as the third most common non-vertebral osteoporotic fracture in the elderly, following distal radius and proximal femur fractures. Level III

Population-based data reveal an overall incidence of approximately 82 per 100,000 person-years, with a marked gender disparity: 114/100,000 in females vs. 47/100,000 in males. A significant seasonal winter peak is attributable to environmental hazards (ice/snow) causing falls on outstretched hands. Level III

The Epileptic Subpopulation

Patients with epilepsy and seizure disorders represent a uniquely high-risk cohort. Unlike the general population—where the protective "parachute reflex" during falls predominates—epileptic patients sustain fractures from violent, involuntary, and asymmetric muscle contractions inherent to generalized tonic-clonic seizures. Level III

This results in:

  • Highly comminuted, severely displaced fractures or posterior fracture-dislocations Level IV
  • Iatrogenically compromised bone microarchitecture from chronic antiepileptic drug (AED) therapy Level II
  • Markedly elevated risk of catastrophic postoperative complications, including seizure-induced hardware failure Level IV
Critical Paradigm Shift: Standard orthopedic trauma protocols are frequently insufficient and potentially hazardous in epileptic patients. Management demands individualized, multidisciplinary care spanning neurological optimization, augmented surgical fixation, and customized postoperative immobilization.

2. Biomechanics & Injury Morphology

2.1 The Inverted Pendulum Model (General Population)

In non-epileptic individuals, the most common mechanism involves the "parachute reflex"—falling on an outstretched hand with the arm in ~70° forward flexion, 30° internal rotation, and 20° sagittal angulation. Level III

This mechanism predominantly produces:

  • Shield-type injuries (~38% of falls)
  • Displaced greater tuberosity fractures (19%)
  • Direct impact falls cause undisplaced humeral head splits (up to 53%)

2.2 Intrinsic Muscular Trauma in Seizures

During a generalized tonic-clonic seizure, the injury mechanism is entirely intrinsic. The internal rotators (latissimus dorsi, pectoralis major, subscapularis, teres major) are significantly stronger than external rotators. Level IV

This muscular imbalance forces the arm into adduction, forward flexion, and extreme internal rotation, driving the humeral head against the posterior glenoid rim—acting as an "anvil"—generating extreme compressive and shearing forces. Level III

Pathognomonic Injury Profile: The defining injury in epileptic patients is the posterior shoulder fracture-dislocation, almost exclusively associated with seizures, electrical shock, or extreme high-velocity trauma. This creates a reverse Hill-Sachs lesion, significantly complicating joint stability. Level III

2.3 Seizure-Specific Fracture Morphologies

  • Valgus Impaction & Head Splits: Fracture line extends across the medial anatomical neck; metaphysis driven away from head in severe valgus Level III
  • Shield-Type Fractures: Destructive three-part pattern with cartilaginous head sheared from tuberosity "shield," forced postero-inferiorly, compromising vascular supply Level III
  • Butterfly Fragments: Segment of greater tuberosity remains attached to lesser tuberosity via bicipital groove; intact groove may mitigate AVN risk by limiting arcuate vessel damage Level III

A high-resolution analysis demonstrated an overwhelming association between pre-existing cortical thinning and fracture occurrence: Odds Ratio = 7.766 (95% CI 4.760–12.669, p<0.0001). Mean bone density and cortical thickness in the central humeral head are the strongest predictors of overall bone strength. Level III

3. Pharmacological Impact of Antiepileptic Drugs on Skeletal Integrity

Long-term AED therapy is the most significant independent risk factor for systemic bone mineral density (BMD) loss, osteomalacia, and secondary osteoporosis. Level II

3.1 Cytochrome P450 Induction & Vitamin D Catabolism

Enzyme-inducing AEDs (EIAEDs)—including phenytoin, carbamazepine, phenobarbital, and oxcarbazepine—potently upregulate hepatic CYP450 enzymes, accelerating catabolism of 25-hydroxyvitamin D into inactive metabolites. This triggers:

  1. Intestinal calcium malabsorption
  2. Secondary hyperparathyroidism with compensatory PTH elevation
  3. Accelerated osteoclastic bone resorption → reduced cortical thickness
  4. Severe cases → osteomalacia with mineralization defects Level II

3.2 Clinical Sequelae

Patients on long-term AEDs have a 2- to 3-fold increased risk of non-traumatic fractures. Up to 80% of chronic inpatients with epilepsy show abnormally low BMD. Duration of therapy directly correlates with severity. Level II

Clinical Implication: A 45-year-old epileptic patient presenting with a surgical neck fracture may possess the biomechanical bone quality of an 80-year-old with advanced senile osteoporosis. Level II

4. Operative vs. Non-Operative Management

4.1 The General Consensus: Non-Operative Outcomes

Landmark RCTs—most notably the PROFHER trial and the NITEP trial—have challenged the trend toward surgical fixation. For displaced two-part surgical neck fractures in patients ≥60 years, no statistically significant difference exists between operative and non-operative cohorts for long-term functional outcomes or quality of life. 4.2 The Epileptic Exception

Why Conservative Management Frequently Fails: Conservative care relies on the periosteal sleeve, joint capsule, and rotator cuff as a stabilizing "hinge." During a post-injury seizure, violent contraction of the internal rotators completely obliterates this soft-tissue envelope, leading to recurrent dislocation, massive secondary displacement, and non-union. Level IV

A minimally displaced surgical neck fracture can rapidly convert into a devastating ischemic four-part fracture-dislocation following a single tonic-clonic episode in the early weeks of non-operative treatment. Therefore, surgical stabilization must be heavily considered for epileptic patients, provided their neurological status can be medically optimized. Level IV

5. Advanced Surgical Fixation Strategies

5.1 Locking Plate vs. Intramedullary Nailing

The PHILOS (Proximal Humerus Internal Locking System) plate uses threaded screw heads that lock directly into the plate, creating a fixed-angle construct. This is critical in osteoporotic bone where standard screws strip and pull out. Level III

Intramedullary nails (IMN) offer a load-sharing alternative positioned closer to the mechanical axis. Level III

5.2 Fibular Strut Allograft Augmentation

Loss of medial calcar support is the single most significant cause of construct failure. Without it, cyclical loading or seizure overload causes varus collapse and screw cut-out. The fibular strut allograft is the most thoroughly validated augmentation technique. Level II

Technique: The fibular strut is inserted into the humeral shaft medullary canal, impacted proximally across the surgical neck to buttress subchondral bone. Locking screws are driven through the dense fibular allograft, creating a unified "plate-and-strut" scaffold. Level III

In an analysis of 693 patients across 21 studies: screw penetration rate reduced to 3.7%, reoperation rates 1.9%–4.4% at 1.55 years mean follow-up, average Constant-Murley Score 81.8. Level II

5.3 Tension Band & Hawkins Wiring Techniques

When bone stock is too degraded for even locking screws, tension band wiring provides a biologically respectful alternative based on the Pauwels principle: placing material along the tension side converts destructive tensile forces into stabilizing compressive forces. Level III

Key advantages: minimal periosteal stripping, preservation of the retrograde blood supply via the anterior humeral circumflex artery (arcuate branches), and reduced AVN risk. Level III

The Hawkins wiring technique employs a 6.5-mm AO cancellous lag screw from shaft into head, followed by two heavy 18-gauge wires (or FiberWire sutures) in a figure-of-eight pattern. In cohorts averaging 71 years: mean forward flexion 160°, external rotation 46°, internal rotation to the T10 vertebra. Level IV

6. Reconstructive Salvage: Arthroplasty in the Epileptic Shoulder

6.1 Historical Limitations

Unconstrained anatomical implants (TSA, hemiarthroplasty) were historically considered contraindications in epileptic patients. They rely on balanced rotator cuff tension and cannot withstand seizure-generated shear forces, leading to unacceptable dislocation, glenoid loosening, and rapid erosion rates. Level III

6.2 Constrained Fixed-Fulcrum Reverse Total Shoulder Arthroplasty (FF-RSA)

The constrained fixed-fulcrum reverse prosthesis (e.g., Bayley-Walker) features:

  • Large-pitched, HA-coated titanium uncemented screw for glenoid fixation
  • 22 mm CoCrMo alloy head with "snap-fit" constrained articulation into UHMWPE liner
  • Intrinsic mechanical multidirectional stability with ~60° permitted motion in any direction Level III

7. Perioperative Neurological Optimization

The perioperative period is an extreme vulnerability window. Injury trauma, hospitalization stress, NPO fasting, sleep deprivation, and anesthetic agents collectively lower the seizure threshold. Level III

7.1 Preoperative Protocols

  • Mandatory neurologist/epileptologist consultation Level III
  • Strict uninterrupted continuation of baseline AED regimen
  • Oral AEDs administered on morning of surgery with a small sip of water despite NPO status
  • If enteral route unavailable: immediate IV bioequivalent conversion (e.g., IV phenytoin, levetiracetam, sodium valproate) Level III
Key Tenet: Abrupt disruption of chronic AED therapy is the leading precipitating cause of perioperative status epilepticus. Level III

7.2 Anesthetic Considerations

CYP450-inducing AEDs create pharmacokinetic cross-tolerance with anesthetic agents, often requiring significantly higher weight-adjusted doses of induction agents, paralytics, and analgesics. Level III

Tranexamic Acid (TXA): Higher cardiac-surgery doses (50–100 mg/kg) are associated with seizure risk via GABA receptor antagonism. Lower orthopedic doses (10–20 mg/kg) generally do not significantly increase risk, but extreme caution and individualized risk-benefit analysis are required in known epileptic patients. Level III

7.3 Acute Postoperative Seizure Management

First-line abortive therapy: IV benzodiazepines (lorazepam or diazepam). If IV access is lost: intramuscular, buccal, intranasal, or rectal benzodiazepines within the first 2–5 minutes. Rapid intervention is critical to prevent progression to established status epilepticus, which carries 10–40% short-term mortality. Level III

8. Postoperative Immobilization & Phased Rehabilitation

8.1 The Gunslinger Brace

A standard sling immobilizes the arm in internal rotation and adduction. If a seizure occurs, the massive internal rotators fire forcefully, perfectly recreating the original injury mechanism and guaranteeing catastrophic failure. Level III

The "Gunslinger Brace" (rigid abduction/external rotation orthosis) locks the shoulder in 30–60° abduction, neutral-to-slight external rotation, 90° elbow flexion. This provides an unyielding mechanical abutment: during a seizure, the rigid frame physically blocks the internal rotators from completing their destructive arc, dispersing kinetic energy across the trunk. Level III

8.2 Phased Rehabilitation Protocol

Phase I: Protective Immobilization Weeks 0–4

Continuous gunslinger brace use. Non-weight-bearing (≤1–2 lbs). Early pendulum exercises are strictly delayed/prohibited. Focus on grip, wrist, and isolated elbow ROM. Absolute prohibition of active/passive internal rotation. Level III

Phase II: Early Passive Motion Weeks 4–6

Brace for daytime only. Supervised passive ROM: forward elevation max 90–140°, external rotation max 40°. No active motion permitted. Level III

Phase III: Active-Assisted Motion Weeks 6–12

Brace discontinued. Active-assisted ROM (wall pulleys, supine wand exercises). Progression contingent on radiographic evidence of stable bridging callus. Level III

Phase IV: Strengthening & Functional Return Weeks 12–24+

Progressive isotonic/isometric resistance training. Unrestricted activity delayed to 5–9 months, contingent on seizure disorder control and medication compliance. Level III

Critical Warning: A single missed AED dose during the first six weeks can instantly destroy a perfectly executed reconstruction, necessitating complex revision surgery. Level IV

9. Evidence Gaps & Limitations

  • No dedicated RCTs exist comparing operative vs. non-operative management specifically in epileptic patients with PHFs. Current recommendations are extrapolated from general population trials and retrospective surgical cohorts.
  • Fibular strut augmentation data (693 patients, 21 studies) are derived from mixed osteoporotic populations, not exclusively seizure cohorts.
  • FF-RSA outcomes are based on relatively small institutional cohorts with medium-term follow-up (Source 54–55).
  • Rehabilitation protocol variability is substantial across published literature; epileptic-specific protocols are largely expert consensus and case-series derived. Level IV
  • TXA seizure thresholds in orthopedic dosing remain incompletely characterized in epileptic populations.

Quick Decision Framework

Non-Operative Considered If:

  • Minimal displacement
  • Well-controlled seizures
  • Low functional demands

Surgery Strongly Favored If:

  • Active seizure disorder
  • Displaced fracture
  • Medial calcar deficiency

Arthroplasty Indicated If:

  • 4-part head-split fracture
  • Reverse Hill-Sachs >40%
  • Chronic neglected instability
